Compton effecteffect

A photon hits an electron — like a billiard ball hitting another ball. After the collision, the photon changes direction and loses energy, its wavelength increases. This shift is direct proof that light has momentum, like particles.
💡 The Compton effect convinced the scientific world that light is not only a wave but also a stream of particles; this brought Compton the Nobel Prize in 1927.
In 1923, Arthur Compton discovered that X-ray radiation scattered on graphite has two components: with the original wavelength and with an increased one. The shift depended on the scattering angle and did not depend on the substance. This was explained by elastic scattering of a photon on a free electron: part of the energy is transferred to the electron, and the photon's wavelength increases according to the formula Δλ = (h/mc)(1–cos θ). It is as if a tennis ball hit a stationary basketball — the first slows down and bounces off, while the second moves slightly.

How it works

The effect is used in gamma spectroscopy to measure the energy of high-energy photons from the energy of scattered electrons. In astrophysics, Compton scattering, including the inverse effect, shapes the X-ray spectra of accretion disks of black holes and pulsars.

💡 The Compton wavelength of the electron — approximately 2.4 picometers — is the minimum scale at which a point electron ceases to be point-like: when trying to localize it with greater precision, electron-positron pairs are created.
The Compton effect is a quantum-relativistic elastic scattering of a photon on a stationary or weakly bound electron, leading to an increase in the photon's wavelength by an amount determined by the scattering angle: Δλ = λ_C(1–cos θ), where λ_C = h/(m_e c) is the Compton wavelength of the electron.

Discovery

Arthur Holly Compton, working at Washington University in St. Louis, in 1922–1923 performed experiments on scattering of monochromatic X-ray radiation on targets of graphite and other light elements. He recorded a shift in the wavelength of scattered radiation, which was perfectly described by his derived formula based on the laws of conservation of energy and momentum using Einstein's photon model. The data obtained immediately convinced the physics community of the reality of light quanta, and in 1927 Compton was awarded the Nobel Prize.

How it works

The effect is used in Compton spectrometers for non-destructive testing of materials, in Compton cameras for medical imaging (single-photon emission computed tomography). In astrophysics, the inverse Compton effect (high-energy electrons transfer energy to photons) explains the X-ray emission from active galactic nuclei and the cosmic microwave background. Limits of applicability: the formula is valid for a free electron; when scattering on bound electrons in atoms, the line profile becomes complicated by Doppler broadening and binding effects.

Caveats

When scattered on bound electrons (Compton effect on atomic shells), the line profile becomes complicated due to Doppler broadening and binding.; An accurate description of multiple Compton scattering requires solving the transport equation, which is computationally complex.; At energies comparable to the electron mass, quantum electrodynamic corrections become significant, including radiative corrections to the Klein–Nishina cross section.

\Delta \lambda = \frac{h}{m_e c}(1 - \cos\theta)
Δλ is the change in photon wavelength, h is Planck's constant (6.62607015×10⁻³⁴ J·s), m_e is the electron mass (9.10938356×10⁻³¹ kg), c is the speed of light in vacuum (299792458 m/s), θ is the photon scattering angle.
\lambda_C = \frac{h}{m_e c}
λ_C is the Compton wavelength of the electron (≈ 2.426×10⁻¹² m), a fundamental constant that determines the length scale at which quantum-relativistic effects become significant for the electron.
