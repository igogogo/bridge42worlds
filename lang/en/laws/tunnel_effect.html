A particle leaks through a wall if it is thin enough — like water through a crack in stone. Flash memory and scanning microscopes work exactly like that.

In practice: Tunnel diodes, flash memory, scanning tunneling microscopes, radioactive alpha decay, quantum computers.
In 1928, George Gamow, Ronald Gurney and Edward Condon independently explained alpha decay: a particle inside the nucleus does not have enough energy to escape according to classical laws, but thanks to the wave properties of matter, there is a small probability of finding it outside — as if it passed through a wall without breaking it. Later, the effect found application in electronics: in 1957, Leo Esaki created the tunnel diode, where current flows through the forbidden zone of a semiconductor.

How it works

Alpha particles fly out of the nucleus during radioactive decay, overcoming the nuclear barrier. In a scanning tunneling microscope, electrons jump from the tip to the sample, mapping the relief of atoms. In flash memory, electrons tunnel through a thin oxide, storing information.

💡 In a USB flash drive, electrons tunnel through an oxide layer just a few nanometers thick, changing the cell charge — that's how your files are stored.
In the quantum world, a particle does not bounce off an obstacle like a ball off a concrete slab but can pass through. The probability of such passage is higher the thinner the barrier and the smaller the difference between the particle's energy and the barrier height. The effect arises from the wave nature of matter (particles behave as waves), which allows 'penetration' into forbidden regions.

💡 Thanks to the tunnel effect, our Sun shines: at core temperatures insufficient classically to overcome the Coulomb barrier, protons still fuse due to quantum tunneling.
The tunnel effect is a quantum mechanical phenomenon of a particle passing through a potential barrier whose energy exceeds the particle's kinetic energy. In classical physics, such a particle would reflect, but in quantum mechanics, the wave function exponentially decays inside the barrier and can have a non-zero amplitude beyond it. The transmission coefficient T for a rectangular barrier is approximately expressed by the formula: \( T \approx \exp\left(-\frac{2d}{\hbar}\sqrt{2m(V_0 - E)}\right) \), where \( d \) is the barrier width, \( m \) is the particle mass, \( V_0 \) is the height of the potential barrier, \( E \) is the particle energy, \( \hbar \) is the reduced Planck constant. The effect underlies many micro- and nanoelectronic devices, as well as nuclear processes.

Discovery

In 1927, Friedrich Hund first discovered tunneling while analyzing a double potential well. In 1928, George Gamow, and independently Ronald Gurney and Edward Condon, applied the concept to explain alpha decay. Later, in 1957, Leo Esaki invented the tunnel diode, demonstrating practical use of the effect in semiconductors, for which he received the Nobel Prize.

How it works

The tunnel effect manifests at scales on the order of the de Broglie wavelength of the particle. As the particle mass and barrier width decrease, the tunneling probability increases. It is used in scanning tunneling microscopes, tunnel diodes, flash memory cells, and in nuclear fusion in stars (overcoming the Coulomb barrier). Limits of applicability: requires quantum description; not observed in the macroscopic world due to exponential suppression for large masses and thicknesses.

Caveats

Creating controllable tunnel junctions for quantum computers with minimal decoherence.; Increasing the stability and lifespan of flash memory under repeated tunneling.; Understanding tunneling in strong fields and many-particle systems (e.g., during atomic ionization).
