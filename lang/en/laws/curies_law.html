Curie's lawlaw

In a paramagnet, atomic magnets are like dancers on a scorching dance floor: the higher the temperature, the more chaotic their movements, and aligning them in a common direction is impossible.

In practice: Based on this law, magnetic thermometers for ultra-low temperatures (below 1 K) have been developed, where traditional sensors are powerless.
In 1895, while heating oxygen, palladium, and paramagnetic salts, Pierre Curie discovered a pattern: the hotter the sample, the weaker it responds to a magnetic field. The reason is the opposition between the external field, which aligns atomic magnetic moments, and thermal motion, which disorders them. At low temperatures, the field 'overpowers' the thermal chaos, but as it heats up, disorder wins, and magnetization drops. Formally, the dependence is simple: χ = C/T.

How it works

As long as the sample is cold, most atomic magnets line up nicely along the field, producing noticeable magnetization. As temperature rises, thermal vibrations increasingly break this alignment, and magnetization decreases. This effect is used in magnetic thermometers for cryogenic temperatures and is accounted for when designing MRI devices, where heating of components can weaken the magnetic field.

💡 If Curie's law worked down to absolute zero, the susceptibility would become infinite — an infinitely small field would cause infinite magnetization. In reality, at very low temperatures, quantum effects and interactions between atomic magnets come into play, smoothly transitioning to the Curie–Weiss law.
Curie's law explains why the magnetization of weakly magnetic substances (paramagnets, such as aluminum or platinum) decreases when heated. Their magnetic susceptibility (the ability to enhance an external magnetic field) is inversely proportional to absolute temperature: χ = C/T. T is the temperature in kelvins, and C is the Curie constant, unique to each material. Imagine: atomic magnets, like miniature compass needles, strive to align with the field, but thermal jostling gets in the way. The hotter it is, the stronger the jostling, and the weaker the net magnetization.

💡 The Curie constant can be expressed through microscopic parameters: C = N μ₀ μ² / (3 k_B), where N is the number of magnetic ions, μ is their moment, μ₀ is the magnetic constant, k_B is Boltzmann's constant. Classical treatment (Boltzmann) gives Curie's law for localized moments, but quantum Fermi–Dirac statistics for conduction electrons eliminates the temperature dependence, leading to Pauli paramagnetism.
Curie's law is an empirical law of paramagnetism: the magnetic susceptibility χ of an ideal paramagnet is inversely proportional to absolute temperature T: χ = C/T, where C is the Curie constant. For magnetization in weak fields, M = (C/T) B holds. The law is valid for systems of non-interacting localized magnetic moments in fields far from saturation, and at temperatures above possible magnetic ordering points.

Discovery

Pierre Curie in 1895, systematically investigating the magnetic properties of substances over a temperature range up to 1400°C, formulated this law, showing that for paramagnets, susceptibility decreases with heating, unlike diamagnets, where it is constant. In 1905, Paul Langevin provided a statistical-mechanical explanation, linking the Curie constant to the ions' magnetic moment. Subsequently, Pierre Weiss generalized the law by introducing a correction for inter-moment interaction, leading to the molecular field theory and the description of ferromagnetism.

How it works

Curie's law is used for calibrating low-temperature thermometers, determining magnetic moments of ions in crystals, and analyzing magnetic phase transitions. Boundaries of applicability: only for paramagnets with negligibly weak exchange interaction, in weak fields, and at temperatures above possible ordering. For noticeable interaction, the Curie–Weiss law is used; at very low temperatures or in strong fields, quantum models are necessary.

Caveats

Does not describe ferro-, antiferro-, and ferrimagnetism, where exchange interaction leads to spontaneous ordering.; Violated near absolute zero due to quantum degeneracy effects.; For metals, the paramagnetism of delocalized electrons obeys Pauli paramagnetism with an almost constant susceptibility, not Curie's law.

χ — magnetic susceptibility, C — Curie constant (depends on the material), T — absolute temperature in kelvins.
