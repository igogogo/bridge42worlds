Newton's second lawlaw

The harder you push a cart, the faster it accelerates; but a heavy concrete slab will accelerate less with the same effort.

In practice: When designing elevators, the traction force of the cable is necessarily calculated to accelerate the cabin with the required acceleration, knowing its mass and passengers.
Newton's second law quantitatively relates force, mass, and acceleration. It was also published in 'Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy' (1687) and became the heart of classical dynamics. Historically, Newton built on the work of Galileo and Kepler. In the modern formulation, the law is often written in terms of momentum change: force equals the derivative of momentum with respect to time. Momentum (mass times velocity) is a measure of the quantity of motion. If mass is constant, F = m·a, but in general (e.g., a rocket losing fuel) the impulse form is correct.
💡 During the launch of a Soyuz rocket, the thrust of the engines creates an acceleration of up to 4 g, and the cosmonauts feel four times heavier.
Acceleration (how quickly speed changes) is directly proportional to force and inversely proportional to mass. Force (e.g., a push) is the cause of motion change; mass (heaviness) shows how hard it is to do. If you apply the same force to a light ball and a heavy weight, the ball will accelerate more.

How it works

A cyclist pedaling applies force — the bicycle accelerates. A truck with more mass picks up speed much slower than a car with the same engine.

💡 In modern amusement park rides like catapults, complex hydraulic systems create forces that accelerate a capsule to 100 km/h in 2 seconds; engineers calculate the g-forces to avoid damaging the passenger's spine.
Newton's second law states: in an inertial reference frame, the rate of change of momentum of a point mass is equal to the force acting on it. Mathematically: F = dp/dt, where p = mv. With constant mass, this is equivalent to F = m dv/dt = ma. The law holds for Newtonian mechanics at speeds much less than light; in special relativity, relativistic momentum is required. Newton's key contribution was the explicit separation of kinematics (description of motion) and dynamics (causes of motion).

Discovery

The concept of force was discussed even by Archimedes, but the mathematical form of the force-motion connection was sought for a long time. Galileo Galilei established that speed is proportional to the time of fall. Johannes Kepler hypothesized that planets move under a force from the Sun. Isaac Newton in the famous 'Principia' (1687) gave a clear formulation, calling force 'an action exerted upon a body to change its state of rest or uniform rectilinear motion'.

How it works

It is used everywhere in engineering to calculate loads, motion of mechanisms, material strength. In celestial mechanics, it allows calculating orbits under gravity. In biomechanics, it explains why an impact is stronger if the braking time is reduced (force impulse). Limitations: does not work at speeds close to light speed and in strong gravitational fields where general relativity is required; in the microcosm, the Schrödinger equation or quantum mechanics serves as an analog.

Caveats

At relativistic speeds, mass depends on speed, and the equation F = ma is incorrect; one must use F = dp/dt with relativistic momentum p = γmv.; In quantum mechanics, the concept of trajectory is blurred, and force is not a convenient quantity; energy and the Hamiltonian operator become primary.

\vec{F} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}
F is the force vector; dp/dt is the derivative of momentum p with respect to time t; momentum p = mv (m is mass, v is velocity).
\vec{F} = m \vec{a}
F is force; m is mass; a is acceleration (change in speed per unit time).
