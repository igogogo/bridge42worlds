A sufficiently massive dying star will inevitably collapse into a singularity — a point of infinite density. Gravity is so strong that no forces can stop the compression.

In practice: Justification of the inevitability of black holes in astrophysics: predicts information loss and gravitational collapse; guides the search for quantum gravity.
In 1965, Roger Penrose used methods of global differential geometry to prove: if in spacetime the energy dominance condition holds (gravity always attracts) and there exists a closed trapped surface (a surface from which even light rays converge), then a singularity inevitably arises — the edge of the causal past where geodesics terminate. This meant that singularities are not an artifact of symmetry, but a generic feature of gravitational collapse. The theorem was the first rigorous statement about singularities in GR without assuming symmetry. Later, Hawking and Penrose extended it to the cosmological singularity.
💡 Penrose's theorem 'guarantees' black holes: it states that if a star has collapsed, then a singularity is inevitable, like falling into a bottomless abyss.
The Penrose singularity theorem states that once a trapped surface forms inside a collapsing star (a surface from which even light cannot escape outward), the collapse inevitably ends in a singularity (a point of infinite curvature where the laws of physics break down). It's like trying to compress a sponge: if there's nowhere else to compress, it will tear. But with gravity, spacetime tears to infinite density.

How it works

Once a star compresses inside its event horizon, according to the theorem, nothing can prevent the formation of a singularity at the center. Even if the matter disappears, gravity will create a singularity. This is mathematical proof of the inevitability of black holes and the Big Bang. In practice, it explains why dark mass accumulates in the centers of galaxies: black holes are the end point of massive star evolution.

💡 Penrose's topological trick with the boundary of the causal future is so elegant that it is often taught as an example of applying differential geometry in physics.
The Penrose singularity theorem (1965) is the first singularity theorem in general relativity not requiring spherical symmetry. Formulation: Let spacetime \((M, g)\) satisfy: 1) the energy dominance condition (\(R_{\mu\nu} v^\mu v^\nu \ge 0\) for any timelike vector \(v^\mu\)); 2) global hyperbolicity (a Cauchy hypersurface exists); 3) contains a closed trapped surface (a two-dimensional surface on which converging null geodesics have negative divergence). Then in \(M\) there exists an inextendible timelike or null geodesic — a singularity.

Discovery

Roger Penrose in 1965, in the paper 'Gravitational Collapse and Space-Time Singularities,' made a breakthrough, showing that singularities are not a pathology of symmetric solutions but an inevitable consequence of collapse. He introduced the concept of a trapped surface and used a topological argument with the boundary of the causal future. Later, in collaboration with Stephen Hawking, the singularity theorems were extended to the cosmological context, proving the existence of the Big Bang. Penrose's work earned him the 2020 Nobel Prize.

How it works

The theorem states that under realistic conditions, gravitational collapse always generates a singularity — a boundary of spacetime beyond which the theory breaks down. This justifies the inevitability of black holes and the need for quantum gravity to describe singularities. Applications: black hole astrophysics, cosmology (the Big Bang singularity). Limitations: requires energy conditions that may be violated at the quantum level; the assumption of global hyperbolicity is unrealistic for quantum gravity.

Caveats

The energy dominance condition can be violated by quantum effects (e.g., during inflation).; The theorem does not describe the nature of the singularity: the curvature might not be infinite but merely incomplete.; Does not answer what lies 'beyond' the singularity: quantum gravity is needed.; Global hyperbolicity is a strong topological assumption that does not hold in some quantum models.

R_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu \ge 0
\(R_{\mu\nu}\) is the Ricci tensor of spacetime, \(k^\mu\) is the tangent vector to a null geodesic (lightlike vector, \(k^\mu k_\mu = 0\)). The inequality expresses the attractive condition of gravity: light rays are always attracted by matter.
