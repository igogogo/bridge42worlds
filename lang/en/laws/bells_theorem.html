Bell's theoremtheorem

Entangled particles behave as if connected by an invisible thread. Measuring one instantly determines the state of the other, no matter how far apart they are. Bell's theorem proves that this is not a trick: no prearranged plan can explain such correlation without faster-than-light interaction.
💡 Imagine two suspects who give identical answers during interrogation, even without conspiring. If they had prearranged, the correlation of their answers could not exceed a certain limit — Bell's inequality. Experiments show this limit is violated.
In 1964, John Stewart Bell, a physicist at CERN, posed the question: can quantum mechanics be supplemented with hidden variables to restore determinism and locality? He derived inequalities for the measurement correlations of entangled particles. Local realistic theories must satisfy them, while quantum mechanics predicts violation. Experiments by Alain Aspect and others confirmed the quantum predictions: local realism is untenable.

How it works

Bell inequalities are tested on pairs of photons. Entangled photons fly apart, and in remote laboratories their polarization is measured at different angles. Statistical analysis of correlations shows whether they exceed the classical limit. If so, it means the particles truly interact non-locally. This forms the basis of quantum cryptography — an eavesdropper cannot listen in without being detected because they would disrupt the correlations.

💡 Bell himself was convinced that experiment would confirm quantum mechanics. For a long time his work remained in the shadows, and serious tests began only in the 1970s. For the experimental confirmation of the violation of Bell's inequalities, Alain Aspect, John Clauser, and Anton Zeilinger received the Nobel Prize in 2022.
Bell's theorem is a fundamental result about the impossibility of local hidden variables. John Stewart Bell in 1964 showed that any theory satisfying the principles of locality and realism imposes constraints on the measurement correlations of entangled particles. Quantum mechanics violates these constraints. For a system of two particles and measurements in directions a, a', b, b', the CHSH inequality holds: |E(a,b) — E(a,b')| + |E(a',b) + E(a',b')| ≤ 2. Quantum predictions give a value up to 2√2 ≈ 2.828. Numerous experiments, starting with the work of Alain Aspect in the 1980s, have confirmed the violation, ruling out local hidden variables.

Discovery

John Stewart Bell, a CERN employee, published in 1964 an article 'On the Einstein Podolsky Rosen paradox', in which he derived inequalities to test locality. The starting point was the Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen paradox (1935), which asserted the incompleteness of quantum mechanics. The first experimental tests were conducted by John Clauser and Stuart Freedman in 1972, but the decisive experiments, closing most loopholes, were performed by Alain Aspect (1982). Later work by Anton Zeilinger and others strengthened the conclusion. For these achievements, Aspect, Clauser, and Zeilinger were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2022.

How it works

Bell inequalities are used to test the foundations of quantum mechanics and in quantum technologies: quantum key distribution (providing provable security), generation of truly random numbers, quantum teleportation. Limits of applicability are related to experimental loopholes: not all particles are detected (detection loophole), freedom of choice of settings may be limited, superdeterministic predetermination is possible. A completely loophole-free test remains technically challenging, but progress continues.

Caveats

Experimental loopholes: detection, freedom of choice, superdeterminism — no experiment has yet closed them simultaneously and absolutely.; The theorem does not rule out non-local hidden variables, such as the de Broglie–Bohm theory.; Violation of Bell's inequality does not prove faster-than-light information transfer; it only means the absence of local realism.

|E(a,b) - E(a,b')| + |E(a',b) + E(a',b')| \leq 2
E(a,b) is the expectation value of the product of the measurement outcomes (±1) for analyzer orientations a and b; a, a', b, b' are different measurement directions for the two particles. Violation of the inequality means that the predictions of quantum mechanics cannot be reproduced by a local realistic theory.
