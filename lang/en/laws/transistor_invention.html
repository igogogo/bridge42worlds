invention of the transistorinvention

A tiny electronic switch with no moving parts, fitting in the billions on a fingertip.

In practice: The basis of all microprocessors, memory, and amplifiers — from smartphones to satellites.
At the end of 1947 at Bell Labs, a team led by William Shockley, along with John Bardeen and Walter Brattain, created the first working point-contact transistor. For this discovery, they received the 1956 Nobel Prize. The operating principle is based on the properties of semiconductor p-n junctions: the current through the junction can be controlled by the injection of minority charge carriers.
💡 The first transistor was assembled on a piece of germanium with gold contacts pressed by a spring, and looked like a jumble of metal plates and paper clips. It was the size of a large coin, whereas today transistors in processors are smaller than a virus.
The transistor works like a water faucet for electric current: a weak input signal controls a strong flow at the output. In a bipolar transistor, a small base current opens the path for a large current between collector and emitter. This allows signals to be amplified and logical states ('0' and '1') to be switched. The invention of the transistor in 1947 replaced bulky, fragile, and hot vacuum tubes.

How it works

Transistors are used in audio amplifiers, in power switches, and most importantly, as elementary cells of digital logic. Their miniaturization down to nanometer sizes led to modern microelectronics: your phone's processor contains over 10 billion transistors.

💡 Walter Brattain, one of the creators of the point-contact transistor, according to legend, was so annoyed when they wouldn't let him patent the design that he exclaimed: 'So I can invent anything on a kitchen stove!' — and indeed, the first transistor largely resembled a homemade contraption.
The transistor is a semiconductor device capable of amplifying, generating, and switching electrical signals. It is based on the properties of p-n junctions and the control of charge carrier concentration (electrons and holes). A bipolar junction transistor (BJT) is a three-layer structure (npn or pnp), where the collector current I_C depends exponentially on the base-emitter voltage and is approximately proportional to the base current: I_C ≈ β I_B. A field-effect transistor (FET) is controlled by the gate voltage, which creates a conducting channel.

Discovery

The first point-contact transistor was invented by John Bardeen and Walter Brattain on December 23, 1947, at Bell Labs under the leadership of William Shockley. Shockley developed the p-n junction theory in 1948 and proposed the bipolar planar transistor. In 1954, the silicon transistor appeared (Texas Instruments). In 1959, Robert Noyce and Jack Kilby independently created the integrated circuit, combining many transistors on a single chip, which marked the beginning of the microelectronics era.

How it works

Modern transistors are predominantly MOSFETs (metal-oxide-semiconductor), where the channel current is controlled by the gate voltage. Miniaturization (Moore's Law) has reached 3 nm technologies, but it faces quantum effects (tunneling through the gate dielectric) and the problem of heat dissipation. Limits of applicability: at the nanoscale, charge discreteness and quantum entanglement begin to manifest.

Caveats

Overcoming the miniaturization limit of MOSFETs: what next?; Transition to transistors based on new principles (tunnel, spintronic).; Reducing static power consumption in nanometer chips.

I_C = \beta I_B
I_C — collector current (output current); I_B — base current (controlling current); β — current gain (typical values from 20 to 1000). The relation shows how a small base current controls a large collector current.
