The complementarity principle is a fundamental tenet of quantum mechanics, introduced by Niels Bohr to resolve wave-particle duality. It postulates that a complete description of a micro-object requires two mutually exclusive classes of experimental setups: one reveals wave behavior (interference), the other — particle behavior (localization). Mathematically, complementarity is often expressed through the inequality for path distinguishability and interference visibility: V² + D² ≤ 1, where V — contrast of interference fringes, D — reliability of particle path identification. The principle is not a consequence of measurement imperfection, but reflects quantum-mechanical non-commutativity of observables, as in the Heisenberg uncertainty relation.

Discovery

Niels Bohr developed the principle in 1927 during the famous debates with Albert Einstein, who insisted on the incompleteness of quantum mechanics. Bohr argued that classical concepts of particle and wave are adequate only separately, and their combination requires additional perspectives. John Archibald Wheeler later proposed the delayed-choice thought experiment, showing that the decision of whether to observe a wave or a particle can be made after the photon has passed through the apparatus. In the 1970s, Wootters and Zurek gave a quantitative form of complementarity, which allowed experimental testing of the boundary between wave and particle behavior.

How it works

The complementarity principle underlies quantum cryptography technologies, where any attempt at eavesdropping inevitably disturbs the state and is detected by the disruption of interference. Limits of applicability: for macroscopic objects, complementarity is practically indistinguishable due to rapid decoherence (loss of quantum properties through interaction with the environment), but formally it always holds. In quantum computing, the principle is used for error correction through redundant encoding.

Caveats

Are there hidden variables making complementarity only apparent?; Applicability to gravity at the quantum level remains an open question.; The measurement problem in quantum cosmology, where there is no external observer.
