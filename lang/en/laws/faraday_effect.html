How a magnetic field twists light: a beam passing through magnetized glass changes the orientation of its waves — like a gymnast spinning a ribbon caught in a vortex.

In practice: Allows building optical isolators in lasers and measuring magnetic fields in the interstellar medium.
In 1845, Michael Faraday sought a connection between light and magnetism and discovered that heavy glass placed in a strong magnetic field changes the polarization of transmitted light. This became the first proof of the unity of electromagnetism. Essentially, the magnetic field makes the medium anisotropic for light — as if light were forced to go up a spiral staircase where one turn is steeper than the other.

How it works

It is encountered in fiber optics with magnetic sensors, and in astrophysics for measuring magnetic fields of galaxies.

💡 Faraday had been trying to find the effect for several years unsuccessfully until he took super-transparent lead glass from a chandelier. It turned out that ordinary glass gave too weak a rotation.
Light is an electromagnetic wave (oscillations of electric and magnetic fields) moving in a certain plane. When such a beam passes through a transparent substance in a magnetic field, this plane rotates. The reason is that the magnetic field causes electrons in atoms to rotate differently for left and right circular polarizations of light. As a result, one circular polarization slows down, the other speeds up, and the combined wave emerges with a rotated direction of oscillation.

How it works

It is encountered in fiber optics with magnetic sensors, and in astrophysics for measuring magnetic fields of galaxies.

💡 The Verdet constant for most materials is extremely small; for example, for water at room temperature and a wavelength of 589 nm, it is about 1.3×10⁻⁵ angular minutes per gauss-centimeter. To obtain a noticeable rotation, either large fields or long samples are required — thus, on astrophysical scales, the effect accumulates over thousands of light-years.
The Faraday effect is a magneto-optical phenomenon consisting of the rotation of the plane of polarization of linearly polarized light as it passes through a medium in the presence of a longitudinal magnetic field. The rotation angle θ is given by θ = VBL, where V is the Verdet constant (a material parameter depending on wavelength and temperature), B is the magnetic induction, L is the interaction length. Physically, the effect is caused by a difference in phase velocities for right- and left-circularly polarized components of the light wave due to the Zeeman effect in the medium.

Discovery

Discovered by Michael Faraday in 1845 after years of trying to detect the influence of magnetism on light. Experimenting with samples of heavy flint glass (lead glass) placed between the poles of a powerful electromagnet, he noticed the rotation of the plane of polarization. This phenomenon, along with the discovery of electromagnetic induction, formed the basis of the concept of the unified nature of electricity, magnetism, and light, later developed by James Clerk Maxwell.

How it works

Used in optical isolators (valves) to prevent back-reflections in laser systems, circulators, and modulators. In astrophysics, measurement of Faraday rotation from pulsars and radio sources is used to map magnetic fields in the interstellar and intergalactic medium. Limits of applicability: limited by medium transparency and saturation in strong fields; applicable from radio to ultraviolet radiation.

Caveats

Accuracy of measuring weak cosmic magnetic fields is limited by background noise and plasma inhomogeneities.; High requirements for purity of crystalline media to create effective isolators.
