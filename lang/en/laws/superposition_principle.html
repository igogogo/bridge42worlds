superposition principleprinciple

A quantum particle can be in several states at once — like a coin tossed in the air, which hasn't landed yet and so is neither heads nor tails, but a mixture of them. This cocktail of possibilities collapses into a concrete result only upon measurement.
💡 The most famous example of superposition is Schrödinger's cat: in a thought experiment, it sits in a box with a poison mechanism and is simultaneously alive and dead until you open the lid.
History: in 1924, Louis de Broglie suggested that particles have wave properties, and in 1926, Erwin Schrödinger created wave mechanics, where the state of a particle is described by a wave function. The superposition principle states that any two possible wave functions can be added with complex weights to obtain a new allowed state. Advanced analogy: imagine a musical chord — the pressed piano keys create a sound that is the sum of pure notes. Similarly, an electron in an atom is not on one orbit but is smeared over several simultaneously, forming hybrid clouds.

How it works

All of quantum chemistry (formation of molecules), the operation of quantum computers, and quantum cryptography are based on superposition. For example, the hydrogen molecule arises because the electron 'splits' itself between two protons.

💡 Thanks to superposition, a quantum computer can explore many solutions in parallel, but extracting the answer is not easy: upon measurement, the entire rich pattern collapses into one result.
The principle of superposition of states is one of the central postulates of quantum mechanics. It states: if a quantum system can be in states described by wave functions ψ₁ and ψ₂, then it can also be in the state ψ = c₁ψ₁ + c₂ψ₂, where c₁ and c₂ are arbitrary complex numbers satisfying the normalization condition |c₁|²+|c₂|²=1. Upon measurement of a physical quantity, the probability of obtaining the value corresponding to state ψ₁ is |c₁|². The principle is mathematically expressed through the linearity of the Schrödinger equation and the vector representation of states in Hilbert space: |ψ⟩ = c₁|ψ₁⟩ + c₂|ψ₂⟩.

Discovery

The roots of the principle go back to the wave hypothesis of Louis de Broglie (1924), who suggested that each particle corresponds to a wave. Erwin Schrödinger in 1926 constructed the wave equation, whose solutions were expressed through a superposition of harmonic states. In the same year, Max Born proposed the probabilistic interpretation of the wave function, after which the superposition principle acquired probabilistic meaning. Paul Dirac in 1930 generalized the formalism to any quantum systems, introducing bra-ket notation and the concept of Hilbert space, where any linear combination of admissible state vectors is also admissible.

How it works

The principle underlies all of quantum physics: it explains the interference of particles at two slits, the structure of molecules through hybridization of orbitals, the operation of quantum computers (qubits in superposition of |0⟩ and |1⟩). It also gives rise to quantum entanglement, when the state of two particles is inseparable into individual states. Limitation: superposition persists only until the act of measurement (state reduction). At macroscopic scales, decoherence quickly destroys quantum correlations, so Schrödinger's cat cannot be observed.

Caveats

Measurement problem: why and how exactly does one state get chosen from a superposition?; Decoherence as a process of irreversible loss of quantum coherence upon interaction with the environment; Is there a fundamental limit to the scale of superposition? (experiments with macromolecules); Nonlinear modifications of quantum mechanics (e.g., spontaneous collapse models) and their verification
