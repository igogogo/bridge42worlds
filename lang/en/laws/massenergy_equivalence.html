mass–energy equivalenceequation

The world's most famous formula — E=mc² — says that mass and energy can turn into each other. Even at rest, any object stores colossal energy: as if every pebble were a compressed spring ready to release. A little mass yields enormous energy because the speed of light (c) is very large, and its square is an astronomical number. This is how stars and nuclear reactors work.
💡 The Sun converts about 4 million tons of its mass into light every second! We live thanks to that.
In 1905, Albert Einstein, in his paper 'Does the inertia of a body depend upon its energy content?', derived the relation E=mc² as a consequence of special relativity. It means: the rest mass of a body is a measure of its internal energy. An advanced analogy: imagine a bank account in Swiss francs and dollars; they seem like different currencies but are always convertible at a fixed rate. So mass and energy are two currencies of nature with exchange rate c².

How it works

The formula explains why atomic nuclei weigh slightly less than the sum of protons and neutrons: the mass defect turns into binding energy. Nuclear power and atomic bombs are based on this. In colliders, particles are accelerated near the speed of light, and their energy becomes much greater than mc².

💡 Even an ordinary spring, when compressed, becomes a tiny bit heavier — exactly by the amount its energy increased! But the effect is so small it's impossible to weigh.
The full relativistic relationship between energy, momentum, and mass: E² = (pc)² + (mc²)². For a body at rest (p=0) it reduces to E = mc², signifying equivalence of rest mass and energy. It unifies the conservation laws of mass and energy into one, asserting their interconvertibility. Derived from Lorentz transformations and Einstein's postulates.

Discovery

Albert Einstein published the final formulation in 1905, though ideas about the connection between mass and electromagnetic energy were expressed earlier by Henri Poincaré in 1900. Experimental confirmations came with the discovery of nuclear reactions and mass defect in the 1930s. Today it has been verified with extremely high precision in accelerators.

How it works

Foundation of nuclear and particle physics: allows calculation of energy release in fusion and fission reactions, production of particle-antiparticle pairs. Limitations: applicable in inertial reference frames; in general relativity, energy becomes part of the energy-momentum tensor. The concept of mass becomes more complicated in curved spacetime.

Caveats

The nature of rest mass and its connection to the Higgs field.; The equivalence of inertial and gravitational mass in the context of dark energy.; The cosmological constant problem as vacuum energy.
