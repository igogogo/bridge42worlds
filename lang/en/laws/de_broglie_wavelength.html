de Broglie formulaequation

Everything around us — not just light, but also matter particles, such as electrons — behaves like waves! Every moving particle has its own wavelength, calculated using the de Broglie formula. For large objects, this wavelength is vanishingly small, so we don't see a ball bending around corners. But for tiny particles, it is large enough to exhibit wave properties.
💡 If you throw a baseball, its de Broglie wavelength would be billions of times smaller than an atomic nucleus — no diffraction would be noticeable. But for a slowly moving electron, it can be the size of a molecule!
In 1924, the French prince-physicist Louis de Broglie, in his doctoral dissertation, put forward a revolutionary idea: if waves can behave like particles (photons), then particles might also have a wave nature. He suggested that any moving particle is associated with a wave of length λ = h/p, where p is momentum. This hypothesis was brilliantly confirmed in 1927 when electrons demonstrated diffraction and interference — typical wave phenomena.

How it works

Electron microscopes use beams of electrons with a very short de Broglie wavelength, allowing them to see details thousands of times smaller than in an optical microscope. Neutron diffraction helps study crystal structures. Even atomic orbitals in quantum chemistry are standing matter waves around the nucleus!

💡 At de Broglie's thesis defense, one committee member asked if there was any experimental evidence for the idea; the answer was that not yet, but Einstein himself said: 'He has lifted a corner of the great veil.' And so wave mechanics was born.
The de Broglie hypothesis postulates that any particle with momentum p has a wavelength λ given by λ = h/p, where h is Planck's constant. For a non-relativistic particle of mass m moving at speed v, λ = h/(mv). This idea led to the creation of wave quantum mechanics and the Schrödinger equation, where the wave function describes 'probability waves'.

Discovery

Louis de Broglie in 1924 suggested the universality of wave-particle duality, extrapolating the results of Planck and Einstein for light to all particles. In 1927, Clinton Davisson and Lester Germer experimentally discovered diffraction of slow electrons on nickel, and George Thomson in thin metal films. These experiments confirmed the hypothesis, and Erwin Schrödinger soon developed the mathematical apparatus to describe de Broglie waves.

How it works

The de Broglie formula is used to calculate diffraction gratings in electron and neutron optics, estimate quantum effects (tunneling, interference) during microparticle motion, and in scattering theories. In the macroscopic limit (m large), λ becomes negligibly small, consistent with classical mechanics. For ultra-relativistic particles, a relativistic expression for momentum is required.

Caveats

The exact physical meaning of the de Broglie wave remains a subject of debate: is it a probability wave or a material wave?; The original formula is not invariant under Lorentz transformations and requires a covariant formulation.; How to describe the 'wave' for an extended body consisting of many particles without losing coherence?
