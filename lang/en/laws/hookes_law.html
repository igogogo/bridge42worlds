Hooke's lawlaw

A spring responds proportionally to stretching: pull twice as far, twice the force.

In practice: Car shock absorbers, spring scales, orthopedic mattresses — all use Hooke's law. Even bones and tendons in our body obey it under moderate loads.
In 1660, Robert Hooke, experimenting with springs for clocks, formulated the rule: 'As the force, so the stretch' — Ut tensio, sic vis. He announced his discovery in 1678 in the form of an anagram ceiiinosssttuv, fearing plagiarism. After deciphering it later, the world learned the law of elasticity. Solids are networks of atoms linked by 'springs' of electromagnetic forces; for small displacements these bonds are linear, giving rise to a restoring force. A tuning fork, a string, a steel bridge in windy weather — all are examples of a harmonic oscillator generated by Hooke's law.

How it works

The scale in a grocery store: the spring inside stretches under the weight of the goods, and we read the mass on the dial. Keyboard keys return to their original position thanks to the elasticity of silicone domes. Ancient catapults worked on the same principle of elastic deformation of ropes.

💡 The word 'elasticity' doesn't quite capture the essence: elasticity is a property, while Hooke's law is a quantitative measure. Asphalt seems hard, but it also deforms elastically under car wheels — otherwise roads would crumble.
Hooke's law describes elasticity: the more you deform an elastic body (stretch a spring, compress a rubber band), the greater the resistance force that arises. The force is directly proportional to the displacement (how far it is moved from rest) and is directed opposite to it. If you overdo it, the body will break or remain deformed — the law stops working.

How it works

💡 The spring constant k is measured in newtons per meter in SI, but its relation to fundamental constants: for a microscopic spring made of a single atom, k ~ E·a, where E is Young's modulus and a is the interatomic distance. This allows us to 'feel' the elasticity of individual chemical bonds.
Hooke's law in the classical formulation for a spring: F = −k x, where F is the elastic force, x is the displacement vector (from the equilibrium position), and k is the stiffness. The minus sign indicates that the force is directed opposite to the displacement. In a generalized form for isotropic media, the stress tensor σ is linearly related to the strain tensor ε through Young's modulus E: σ = E ε (for uniaxial tension). The law holds only in the region of elastic deformations, before the proportional limit is reached.

Discovery

Robert Hooke, an English naturalist and architect, discovered the law of proportionality between force and deformation in 1660 while working on improving spring clocks. However, he did not publish it immediately, fearing competition, and only in 1676 placed an anagram in 'Lectures on the Spring'. The deciphering 'Ut tensio, sic vis' ('As the stretch, so the force') appeared in 1678. Interestingly, Hooke accused Huygens of plagiarizing the clock mechanism, but ultimately the law became his main contribution. Later, Thomas Young and Cauchy generalized the theory of elasticity to continuous media, introducing the concepts of stress and strain.

How it works

Hooke's law is the foundation of elasticity theory, used in construction, mechanical engineering, and seismology. It determines the behavior of solids under small loads, allowing calculation of deformations in bridges, skyscrapers, and machine components. Exceeding the yield point leads to plasticity or fracture. At the nanoscale, the discreteness of the atomic structure and quantum effects can modify the linear law.

Caveats

Not all materials are linear even in the elastic region (rubber, biological tissues are described by nonlinear models).; Viscoelasticity: under prolonged loading, many polymers and glasses 'flow', obeying not pure elasticity.; At high frequencies (e.g., in ultrasonic vibration), internal friction and inertia break the simple linear relationship.

F — elastic force (vector, N), exerted by the deformed body; k — stiffness coefficient (positive constant, N/m), characterizing the elastic properties of a specific spring; x — displacement vector (from equilibrium, m). The minus sign shows that the force is directed opposite to the displacement.
\sigma = E \epsilon
σ — mechanical stress (Pa), force per unit cross-sectional area; E — Young's modulus (modulus of elasticity, Pa), a fundamental material characteristic (for steel E ≈ 2.1×10¹¹ Pa, for rubber E ~ 10⁶ Pa); ε — strain (dimensionless), ε = ΔL/L₀, where ΔL is the change in length, L₀ is the original length.
