invention of the blue LEDinvention

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A blue crystal, shining through the dark corners of semiconductor physics to give us white light.

In practice: Thanks to the blue LED, we have energy-efficient LED lighting, phone and TV screens with full color gamut, and laser projectors.
The path to the blue LED took several decades. The main difficulty: obtaining a semiconductor with a bandgap of about 3.4 eV (corresponding to blue light) and doping it p-type (adding impurities to create hole conductivity). Shuji Nakamura at the Japanese company Nichia first created high-quality GaN films on a sapphire substrate and developed a thermal annealing method to activate magnesium acceptors, obtaining p-GaN. In 1993, he demonstrated a bright blue LED, and later — the first blue laser.

How it works

Blue LEDs combined with a phosphor (a special coating that converts part of the blue light into yellow) produce white light that illuminates our homes and streets. They consume many times less energy than incandescent bulbs and last tens of thousands of hours.

💡 Shuji Nakamura was awarded the Nobel Prize in 2014, but his company Nichia initially paid him a bonus of just $$200 for the invention, after which a long legal battle ensued.
For a long time, scientists could make red and green LEDs, but blue remained elusive — a material with a wide bandgap was needed that could efficiently emit blue light and operate long-term. In the 1990s, Shuji Nakamura, using gallium nitride (GaN), managed to grow a crystal of the required quality and build a bright blue LED based on it. This completed the RGB triad (red, green, blue), allowing colors to be mixed to produce white light.

💡 To grow high-quality GaN crystals, Nakamura personally modified a commercial MOCVD reactor by changing the gas-flow geometry — without it, his attempts invariably ended in failure. This 'Nakamura reactor' now stands in a museum.
The invention of the blue LED is based on the physics of wide-bandgap group III-nitride semiconductors (GaN, InGaN). Under forward bias of a p-n junction, electrons recombine with holes, emitting photons with energy close to the bandgap E_g. For blue emission with λ ~ 450 nm, E_g ≈ 2.76 eV is required, but due to quantum size effects in InGaN quantum wells, layers with varying indium concentration are actually used, allowing wavelength tuning. Key challenges: obtaining monocrystalline GaN without defects, p-type doping (long unsuccessful), and creating high-quality electrical contacts.

Discovery

In the 1970s, Jacques Pankove (RCA) obtained the first blue emission from GaN, but without p-doping. In 1986, Isamu Akasaki and Hiroshi Amano first created p-GaN using electron-beam irradiation. Shuji Nakamura, working independently at Nichia, in 1993 applied thermal annealing and developed a structure with a double heterostructure InGaN/GaN, which led to a commercially successful blue LED. In 2014, Akasaki, Amano, and Nakamura received the Nobel Prize 'for the invention of efficient blue light-emitting diodes which has enabled bright and energy-saving white light sources'.

How it works

Blue LEDs serve as the foundation for solid-state lighting (LED lamps) and displays (OLED, microLED). They are also used in laser diodes for Blu-ray drives (λ=405 nm) and Li-Fi systems (visible light communication). Limitations: efficiency still drops at high current densities ('droop'), and there are degradation issues during prolonged operation.

Caveats
