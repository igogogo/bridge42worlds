Bekenstein-Hawking entropyequation

Black holes seem like bottomless pits where everything falls in and disappears. But they have a special memory — entropy, a measure of disorder. The more falls in, the larger the area of their 'surface' — the event horizon — becomes. The Bekenstein-Hawking formula links this area to entropy: it's enormous! Imagine a black hole is an ideal draft document that can't be read from the outside, but the amount written can be judged by the size of the crumpled paper.
💡 Stephen Hawking initially argued with Bekenstein and even made a bet, but later helped derive the exact formula himself! The story of the two geniuses reads like a scientific detective novel.
In the 1970s, Jacob Bekenstein, pondering black hole thermodynamics, proposed that the area of the event horizon is the entropy. Otherwise, a hot teapot falling into a hole would violate the second law of thermodynamics. Stephen Hawking refined the coefficient to 1/4 and united quantum mechanics with gravity. An advanced analogy: just as a hologram stores a three-dimensional image on a flat film, a black hole encodes information about the infalling matter on its surface.

How it works

The formula S = kA/(4l_P²) is used in cosmology and black hole physics. It explains why black holes can evaporate (Hawking radiation) and underlies the holographic principle — the idea that our universe might be a projection.

💡 The entropy of a black hole with the mass of the Sun is about 10⁷⁷ times larger than the entropy of the Sun itself! This makes black holes the most 'informational' objects in the universe.
The Bekenstein-Hawking entropy S = (k_B c³ A)/(4 G ħ) = k_B A/(4 l_P²), where A is the area of the black hole's event horizon, l_P is the Planck length. In Planck units, S = A/4. It links thermodynamics, quantum mechanics, and general relativity, generalizing the second law of thermodynamics: the total entropy of matter and black holes never decreases.

Discovery

Jacob Bekenstein realized in 1973 that without attributing entropy to black holes, the second law of thermodynamics would be violated when matter falls in. He proposed proportionality to horizon area. Stephen Hawking, initially skeptical, in 1974 used quantum field theory in curved spacetime to derive that black holes radiate and independently confirmed the 1/4 factor. Their work gave birth to modern black hole thermodynamics.

How it works

Used in astrophysics, for calculating the information capacity of black holes, in string theory, and in AdS/CFT correspondence. Applicable to any equilibrium black holes, but is semiclassical: quantum corrections become significant in the final stages of evaporation. For extremal black holes, entropy is zero, requiring separate interpretation.

Caveats

The microscopic nature of black hole degrees of freedom (the entropy problem).; The information paradox: whether information is preserved during evaporation.; Entropy in the extremal limit and quantum corrections.
