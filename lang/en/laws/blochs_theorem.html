Bloch's theoremtheorem

📊 0 Related articles

An electron in a crystal dances to the rhythm imposed by the atoms: its wave function repeats from cell to cell, only slightly shifting the phase, like a pattern in a kaleidoscope. Bloch's theorem states that the electron state is the product of a traveling wave and a periodic function.

In practice: The entire band theory of solids rests on Bloch's theorem. Without it, it is impossible to understand why some materials conduct electricity and others do not, and to design a transistor, LED, or laser.
In 1928, Felix Bloch, working on his dissertation under Heisenberg, pondered: how to describe an electron in a crystal where the atoms are arranged periodically? The answer turned out to be surprisingly elegant: the wave function must be 'Bloch-like' — the product of a plane wave and a function repeating with the lattice spacing. This naturally led to the idea of energy bands: some energy intervals are allowed, others forbidden. Thus band theory was born, explaining the difference between metals, dielectrics, and semiconductors.

How it works

Calculations of the band structure of all crystalline materials are based on Bloch's theorem. It explains electrical conductivity, optical properties, and allows engineers to design semiconductor devices: transistors, microchips, solar cells, and LEDs.

💡 Bloch left his mark in various fields: there are 'Bloch walls' in magnetism and the 'Bloch sphere' for qubits, but his theorem on waves in a crystal is a separate masterpiece.
Inside a crystal, atoms stand perfectly aligned, like columns. An electron, picking its way through them, cannot move freely. Felix Bloch understood: its wave function must be rhythmic — like a melody, where each verse repeats the motif, but with a phase shift. Mathematically, this is written as the product of a traveling wave and a function repeating with the lattice period. Such a rhythm automatically splits possible energies into bands — some allowed, others forbidden. Thanks to this we know why copper conducts electricity, and diamond does not.

💡 In the 1990s, Bloch's idea penetrated optics: in photonic crystals, light behaves similarly, giving rise to photonic band gaps. This led to the creation of optical waveguides and next-generation lasers.
Bloch's theorem is a fundamental consequence of the translational symmetry of a crystal: for any periodic potential V(r+R)=V(r), solutions of the stationary Schrödinger equation can be represented as ψ_k(r) = e^{i k·r} u_k(r), where u_k has the periodicity of the Bravais lattice. The quantity k is the quasi-wave vector, defined up to a reciprocal lattice vector. From this directly follows the band structure of the spectrum.

Discovery

In 1928, Felix Bloch, completing his dissertation under Werner Heisenberg in Leipzig, rigorously proved that the electron wave function in a periodic potential must have the specified form. This immediately led to an understanding of the band nature of the spectrum and became the key to the theory of metals and insulators. Later the theorem was generalized to the case of an external magnetic field (Rama-Showen theorem), as well as to systems with strong spin-orbit coupling and topological phases.

How it works

The theorem is strictly valid only for ideal infinite crystals. Impurities, boundaries, defects, and thermal vibrations break translational symmetry, and Bloch states cease to be proper. Nevertheless, for most real materials, the Bloch approximation works excellently. To account for electron-electron correlations, one must go beyond the single-electron picture; modern methods include density functional theory and dynamical mean field theory.

Caveats

The presence of defects and impurities breaks strict periodicity; Strong electron correlations (Mott insulators) require going beyond the Bloch picture; The theorem is not applicable to amorphous materials and glasses; Calculation of the real band structure of complex compounds is impossible without powerful numerical methods
