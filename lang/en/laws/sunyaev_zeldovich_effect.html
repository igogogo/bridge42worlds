Sunyaev–Zeldovich effecteffect

📊 30 Related articles

Hot gas in a galaxy cluster heats up the echo of the Big Bang: photons of the CMB, colliding with plasma electrons, steal bits of energy from them.

In practice: An independent method to measure distances to galaxy clusters and study the large-scale structure of the Universe.
In 1969, Soviet astrophysicists Rashid Sunyaev and Yakov Zeldovich predicted that hot intergalactic gas in galaxy clusters should leave an imprint on the cosmic microwave background. Plasma electrons, accelerated to enormous speeds, transfer energy to photons via inverse Compton scattering. The effect is like a 'fog' of electrons that slightly tints the background light. This allows us to see galaxy clusters at any distance, since the surface brightness of the effect does not depend on distance.

How it works

Astronomers observe this distortion and use it to determine the gas pressure in the cluster, its mass, and its distance, independent of redshift.

💡 The Sunyaev-Zeldovich effect was one of the first methods to confirm the existence of dark energy, when it was used to measure distances to distant clusters and compare them with the expansion model.
The cosmic microwave background is a faint microwave light left over from the Big Bang, uniformly filling the cosmos. Passing through huge clouds of hot gas in galaxy clusters, some photons collide with fast electrons. Electrons, having high energy (temperatures of millions of degrees), transfer some of this energy to photons, increasing their frequency. As a result, in the direction of the cluster, the spectrum of the CMB is slightly distorted—it becomes slightly 'hotter' at certain wavelengths.

How it works

Astronomers observe this distortion and use it to determine the gas pressure in the cluster, its mass, and its distance, independent of redshift.

💡 In the Bullet Cluster, overlaying maps of the SZ effect and gravitational lensing directly separated the distributions of ordinary and dark matter during a cluster collision, which became a classic proof of dark matter's existence.
The thermal Sunyaev–Zeldovich effect is a spectral distortion of the cosmic microwave background (CMB) caused by inverse Compton scattering of CMB photons by hot electrons (T_e ~ 10⁷–10⁸ K) within galaxy clusters. This scattering gives low-energy CMB photons additional energy, leading to a characteristic change in brightness temperature: below ~218 GHz there is a deficit, above—an excess. The magnitude of the effect is described by the Compton y-parameter y = (σ_T/(m_e c^2)) ∫ n_e k_B T_e dl, where σ_T is the Thomson scattering cross-section. The kinetic SZ effect additionally shifts the frequency due to the Doppler shift.

Discovery

Predicted in 1969 by Rashid Sunyaev and Yakov Zeldovich in the context of studying the interaction of the CMB with hot intergalactic plasma. Nearly two decades later, in the early 1980s, the effect was first detected in clusters (e.g., the Coma cluster) using radio telescopes. It was further developed with the launch of space observatories WMAP and Planck, which provided detailed SZ effect maps, and with ground-based instruments like SPT and ACT. Sunyaev and Zeldovich also described the kinetic version of the effect, related to the bulk motion of the cluster.

How it works

Used in cosmology to detect and study galaxy clusters at high redshifts, estimate their mass and structure. Because the surface brightness of the SZ effect does not depend on distance (unlike apparent brightness), it serves as a powerful tool for cosmological tests, including constraints on dark energy parameters and cosmological models. The applicability is limited by the need to accurately separate the SZ signal from primary CMB anisotropy, as well as from dust and synchrotron emission.

Caveats

Extracting the weak SZ signal against the primary CMB fluctuations requires highly sensitive multi-frequency observations.; Signal contamination by dust emission from the clusters themselves and foreground/background galaxies.; The need to model the gas pressure profile for accurate mass determination—non-Gaussianity of the distribution introduces uncertainty.
