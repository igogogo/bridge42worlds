Faraday's law of electromagnetic inductionlaw

A magnet entering a coil seems to push electrons, making them run along the wire — an induction current arises. A changing magnetic field creates a vortex electric field.

In practice: Electric generators at all power plants, transformers, induction cooktops, and wireless chargers operate on this principle — converting motion into electricity.
Michael Faraday in 1831 long sought a connection between magnetism and electricity. His ingenious experiment with two windings on one iron ring showed: when current was switched on in the first coil, a momentary surge appeared in the second. He described it as 'converting magnetism into electricity.' Later, James Clerk Maxwell put the law into rigorous mathematical form. The essence: any change in a magnetic field over time generates a vortex electric field, even in a vacuum.
💡 If you drop a strong magnet into a copper pipe, it falls surprisingly slowly — induced currents (eddy currents) according to Lenz's law slow it down, as if an invisible hand is holding it.
A moving magnet has an invisible area around it (magnetic field). If you insert it into a coil of wire (coil) or remove it, an electric voltage (electromotive force, EMF) briefly appears in the wire. The math here is simple: the voltage is greater the faster the 'flux' changes (the number of magnetic lines passing through the coil). The minus sign in the formula is Lenz's clue: induction current always opposes the change that caused it.

How it works

A bicycle dynamo: a magnet spins near a coil, the magnetic flux constantly changes, and the light bulb glows. In a wall outlet, alternating current comes through a transformer, where one coil changes the field and induces current in another, stepping down or up the voltage.

💡 Faraday, lacking formal mathematical education, recorded his ideas in the form of imaginative lines of force, which later inspired Maxwell to create field theory. The famous phrase 'convert magnetism into electricity' comes from his laboratory notebook.
The law states: the electromotive force (EMF) of induction ε in a closed loop equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux Φ_B through a surface bounded by the loop: ε = - dΦ_B/dt. The equivalent differential form is one of Maxwell's equations: rot E = -∂B/∂t. The minus sign reflects Lenz's rule: the induced current creates a magnetic field opposing the change in the original flux, which is a consequence of energy conservation.

Discovery

In the 1820s, Hans Christian Ørsted discovered that an electric current creates a magnetic field. Michael Faraday took on the reverse problem and after years of experiments, in 1831 observed a momentary current in the secondary winding when the primary circuit was closed. The results were reported to the Royal Society. Independently, Joseph Henry in the USA discovered the phenomenon in the same year but published later. James Clerk Maxwell included the law in his system of equations (1861–1862), adding the displacement current term for symmetry.

How it works

The operating principle of generators, transformers, magnetic sensors, hard disk recording. Limits of applicability: works in classical electrodynamics up to relativistic speeds, but at the quantum limit, accounting for fluctuations and charge discreteness requires a more detailed description.

Caveats

For single atoms, flux quantization is necessary (Aharonov-Bohm effect); In superconductors, Lenz's law is violated due to zero resistance; For very rapid field changes, displacement currents must be accounted for
