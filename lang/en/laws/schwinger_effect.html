Schwinger effecteffect

📊 0 Related articles

In a superstrong electric field, the void bursts, creating pairs of particles out of nothing — like a superheated liquid boiling at a critical temperature. The Schwinger effect describes this quantum breakdown of the vacuum.

In practice: Direct observation requires fields billions of times stronger than laboratory ones, but in graphene, where the effective mass of electrons is small, it is possible to simulate a similar breakdown, promising a breakthrough in terahertz electronics.
Julian Schwinger, one of the creators of quantum electrodynamics, realized in 1951: if the electric field exceeds a fantastic value of about 10^18 V/m, the vacuum becomes unstable. Quantum fluctuations — virtual particle pairs — will be torn apart by the field and become real. Schwinger derived a formula showing that pair production is similar to tunneling: exponentially small in a weak field and catastrophically increasing at the critical value. This phenomenon is a close relative of Hawking radiation and particle production in the early Universe.

How it works

The effect explains what can happen near magnetars or in the focus of ultra-powerful lasers. In the laboratory, its analogue is created in graphene: due to the negligible effective mass of charge carriers, the critical field is reduced by orders of magnitude, allowing the study of quantum breakdown on a table.

💡 Physicists hope to 'boil' the vacuum with next-generation X-ray lasers, and for now reproduce a mini-Schwinger in graphene, obtaining an avalanche of electrons and holes.
The quantum vacuum is not emptiness but a sea of virtual pairs that continuously appear and disappear. If a field of monstrous strength, about 10^18 volts per meter, is applied, it manages to pull the pair apart, turning virtual particles into real electron and positron. Julian Schwinger calculated the threshold field and the probability of such an event. The weaker the field, the more negligible the probability — just as water barely evaporates at 30°C, but boils violently at 100°C.

💡 In 2020, in a graphene p-n junction, an avalanche of electron and hole creation was observed, the current-voltage characteristic of which exactly matched Schwinger's prediction — possibly the first laboratory observation of the effect.
The Schwinger effect is the non-perturbative production of electron-positron pairs from the vacuum in a constant electric field with a strength exceeding the critical value E_c ≈ 1.32×10^18 V/m. The production rate is proportional to exp(−π E_c / E). Physically, this is a tunneling transition from the Dirac sea. In quantum chromodynamics, an analogous chromoelectric effect is responsible for quark confinement.

Discovery

Julian Schwinger predicted the effect in 1951 in a paper on gauge invariance and vacuum polarization. Searches are being conducted in experiments with powerful lasers (SLAC, ELI) and at heavy ion colliders: in 2022, the STAR collaboration reported a possible observation of Schwinger breakdown in peripheral gold ion collisions, where fields briefly reached 10^20 V/m.

How it works

The effect occurs when the field work over the Compton wavelength of the electron exceeds 2m_e c^2. At E ~ E_c, vacuum polarization becomes nonlinear, and the back-reaction of the created particles must be taken into account. Reaching the critical field in the laboratory requires colossal intensity; schemes with next-generation colliders are promising. Astrophysical candidates are the vicinities of magnetars. In solid-state physics, an analogue is realized in Dirac materials (graphene) due to the small effective mass of charge carriers.

Caveats

It is technically impossible to create a constant field with the strength E_c in a laboratory volume; The non-stationarity and inhomogeneity of real fields (laser, in collisions) complicate the isolation of the pure effect; The back-reaction of the created particles on the field requires a self-consistent kinetic theory; Solid-state analogues (graphene) only qualitatively reproduce Schwinger production, but have a different microscopics

E_c = \frac{m_e^2 c^3}{e\hbar}
E_c — critical electric field strength (Schwinger limit), m_e — electron mass (≈9.109×10⁻³¹ kg), c — speed of light in vacuum (≈2.998×10⁸ m/s), e — elementary charge (≈1.602×10⁻¹⁹ C), ħ — reduced Planck constant (≈1.0546×10⁻³⁴ J·s)
P \propto \exp\left(-\frac{\pi m_e^2 c^3}{e\hbar E}\right)
P — probability of creating an electron-positron pair per unit volume per unit time (for a constant uniform field), m_e, c, e, ħ — as above, E — actual electric field strength
