Imagine you're standing on a platform and a train with a siren rushes past. When the train approaches, the siren sounds higher pitched, and when it moves away, lower. That is the Doppler effect! The frequency of sound changes due to the motion of the source. The same happens with light: if a star is moving toward us, its light 'blueshifts', and if it moves away, it 'reddens'. This is how scientists learn where distant galaxies are moving.

How it works

The effect appears everywhere: in sound — an ambulance siren changes pitch; in light — spectral lines of distant objects shift. This is the basis of Doppler spectroscopy: by measuring the line shift, the radial velocity of a star or exoplanet is calculated. It also works in sonar, medical ultrasound, traffic police radars, and even household motion sensors.

The Doppler effect is a physical phenomenon consisting of a change in the frequency (or wavelength) of oscillations registered by a receiver due to the relative motion of the source and the receiver. The classical effect is described for waves propagating in an elastic medium (sound), while for electromagnetic waves a relativistic formula is used. Formally: observed frequency f' = f · (c ± v_obs)/(c ∓ v_src) for mechanical waves; for light in vacuum, f' = f √((1-β)/(1+β)) for radial recession. The effect underlies the determination of radial velocities of stars, Doppler broadening of spectral lines, and the discovery of the expansion of the Universe.

Discovery

In 1842, Austrian physicist Christian Doppler theoretically described the dependence of frequency on the motion of the source and observer for sound and light in his work 'On the Color of Double Stars'. He mistakenly believed that all stars are white and their color is determined by motion. In 1845, Dutchman Buys Ballot experimentally confirmed the effect for sound using a locomotive and musicians. For light, the effect was first detected by William Huggins in 1868 by measuring a shift in the spectral lines of Sirius. The relativistic formula was derived by Albert Einstein in 1905 as a consequence of special relativity.

How it works

The effect arises because wave crests reach the receiver at different intervals during their mutual movement. In radar, a transmitted pulse reflects off a moving target — the speed is calculated from the frequency shift. In astrophysics, Doppler shift of spectral lines is the main method for measuring radial velocities. Limits of applicability: for sound — speeds much less than the speed of sound, otherwise shock waves occur; for light — the relativistic formula works at any speed up to the speed of light. At speeds close to the speed of light, transverse Doppler shift due to time dilation becomes significant.
