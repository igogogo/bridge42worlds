Ohm's lawlaw

📊 12 Related articles

Electric current is like water in a hose: higher voltage — stronger pressure, higher resistance — weaker flow.

In practice: In any electrical device, the calculation of the resistance of a heating element or fuse uses Ohm's law. Without it, you cannot adjust speaker volume or select wire gauge for wiring.
Georg Ohm in 1826 experimentally investigated how metals conduct electricity. At that time, it was believed that current is a flow of a mysterious fluid. Ohm established a clear mathematical relationship: current I = U / R, where R is a characteristic of the conductor. He presented the result in the book 'The Galvanic Circuit Investigated Mathematically', but colleagues received the work coldly. Recognition came only a few years later. Today Ohm's law is the ABC of electrical engineering: in any microchip, transformer, LED strip, it is essential.

How it works

In a hair dryer, the heating coil has high resistance: current heats it, delivering power. The diameter of power line cables is huge to reduce resistance and energy loss to heat. In wet weather, insulation deteriorates, resistance drops, current increases — because of this, the RCD trips.

💡 The human body is a conductor. The resistance of dry skin is ~100 kΩ, while wet skin drops to 1 kΩ. The danger of electric shock increases dramatically after a shower.
The law says: the current strength (how many charges run per second) is directly proportional to the voltage (electrical 'pressure') and inversely proportional to resistance (the obstacle to flow). The higher the voltage, the more charges flow; the higher the resistance, the less. In a 220-volt outlet, with a kettle resistance of 20 ohms, the current is 11 amps.

💡 The letter R for resistance did not become standard immediately. Ohm used the symbol r for 'Reduktion' (German for 'reduction'). In early translations, it was replaced with S from 'Strom', but later returned to R.
Ohm's law in integral form: the current I through a conductor segment is directly proportional to the voltage U and inversely proportional to the electrical resistance R: I = U / R. Often written as U = I R. In differential form for continuous media, current density j is proportional to electric field strength E: j = σ E, where σ is specific conductivity. This is an empirical law valid for many materials under constant conditions, but not fundamental — it emerges from approximations of microscopic conduction theory.

Discovery

Georg Simon Ohm, a German physicist, in 1825–1826 conducted a series of meticulous experiments with galvanic cells and metal wires of different lengths and cross-sections. Using a modified Coulomb torsion balance to measure current, he discovered a linear dependence. In 1827, his book 'Die galvanische Kette, mathematisch bearbeitet' was published, where he introduced the concepts of electromotive force, current strength, and resistance. Contemporaries, including Georg Hegel, mocked the 'useless fantasies', but by the 1840s the law gained universal recognition. James Clerk Maxwell later integrated it into the unified theory of electromagnetism.

How it works

Ohm's law forms the basis of analyzing electrical circuits — from household appliances to superconducting magnets. Its limits: materials with a nonlinear current-voltage characteristic (semiconductors, diodes, gas discharges) do not obey simple proportionality. Also, at high frequencies or temperatures near absolute zero, effects appear that require quantum corrections.

Caveats

In nanoscale conductors and molecular circuits, resistance is quantized, and Ohm's law in the classical form is violated.; For superconductors below the critical temperature, resistance disappears — current flows without voltage, contradicting U = I R.; In memory shape materials (memristors), the relationship between current and voltage depends on history, turning the circuit into an analog computer.

I = \frac{U}{R}
I is current (amperes, A), the amount of electric charge passing through the cross-section of a conductor per unit time; U is voltage (volts, V), the potential difference across the conductor; R is electrical resistance (ohms, Ω), a measure of the conductor's opposition to current.
\mathbf{j} = \sigma \mathbf{E}
j is current density vector (A/m²), current per unit cross-sectional area; σ is specific electrical conductivity (S/m), the reciprocal of resistivity ρ (σ = 1/ρ); E is electric field strength vector (V/m).
