The second law of thermodynamics says that in nature everything tends to disorder. Imagine a room: you can tidy it up, but without effort it will become messy again. This law states: entropy (a measure of chaos) in a closed system never decreases. Heat always flows from hot to cold, and it is impossible to completely convert heat into useful work without losses.

How it works

The law explains why a refrigerator heats the room, why the efficiency of heat engines is always less than 100%, and why the universe cools after the Big Bang. It underlies chemical reactions, biological evolution, and even processes in black holes (the generalized second law of Bekenstein-Hawking).

💡 Precisely because of this law, a perpetual motion machine of the second kind is impossible: part of the energy always dissipates as heat that cannot be recovered.
In 1824, Sadi Carnot, studying steam engines, laid the foundation of thermodynamics. Later, Rudolf Clausius and Lord Kelvin rigorously formulated the second law: heat cannot spontaneously flow from a cold body to a hot one, and complete conversion of heat into work in a cyclic process is impossible. Entropy, introduced by Clausius, measures the dissipation of energy. A good analogy is a deck of cards: if you throw it on the table, the cards scatter, and gathering them in the original order is practically impossible without intervention.

Clausius formulation (1850): heat cannot spontaneously flow from a colder body to a hotter one. Kelvin-Planck formulation: it is impossible to construct a cyclically operating machine whose sole effect is the conversion of heat received from a reservoir into work. Entropy S is a state function, its increment dS ≥ δQ/T, where equality holds for reversible processes. In isolated systems, ΔS ≥ 0.

Discovery

In 1824, Sadi Carnot, in his work 'Reflections on the Motive Power of Fire', laid the foundations of thermodynamics by introducing the concept of an ideal cycle. In 1850, Rudolf Clausius gave the first formulation and introduced the concept of entropy. In 1851, Lord Kelvin (William Thomson) proposed an alternative formulation. Later, Max Planck and others generalized the law, linking it to molecular statistics.

How it works

Applied everywhere: from designing heat engines to climate prediction and cosmology. Limits: fluctuations in small systems can temporarily violate the increase in entropy, but statistically it holds. For open systems, entropy can decrease locally at the expense of the environment.

Caveats

Loschmidt's paradox (contradiction with the reversibility of microscopic equations); Connection with the arrow of time; Applicability to quantum systems and black holes (generalized second law)
