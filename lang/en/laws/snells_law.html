Snell's law of refractionlaw

A light ray 'breaks' at the boundary between media: in glass it slows down and bends, like a skier going from loose snow onto hard crust.

In practice: Manufacture of eyeglass lenses, camera lenses, microscopes, and telescopes; fiber optic cables for the internet; explanation of rainbows and mirages.
Dutchman Willebrord Snellius in 1621 recorded tables for angles and found a law relating sines. Later Descartes published it in 1637. The essence: the 'slower' light is in a medium, the more it refracts. Analogy: a column of soldiers marching diagonally from asphalt onto sand — the rank stepping on sand slows down, and the line turns.
💡 Thanks to total internal reflection — the reverse effect — light can run along a curved stream of water, just like through a wire.
Light in different transparent media (air, water, glass) travels at different speeds. Because of this, when entering another medium, its direction changes — the ray refracts. The angle of incidence (measured from the perpendicular to the surface) and the angle of refraction are related via refractive indices (numbers showing how much the medium slows light). Snell's law: n1⋅sin(angle of incidence) = n2⋅sin(angle of refraction).

How it works

A spoon in a glass of tea appears broken; a lens in glasses focuses light on the retina; fiber optics transmit a signal by repeatedly reflecting the beam from the walls.

💡 Snellius used triangles: he measured not angles but segments, and noticed the constant ratio of their lengths — these were the prototypes of sines.
Snell's law: n1 sinθ1 = n2 sinθ2, where n1 and n2 are the absolute refractive indices of the media, θ1 is the angle of incidence, θ2 is the angle of refraction between the ray and the normal. When entering an optically denser medium (n2 > n1), the ray approaches the normal. It is a consequence of Fermat's principle (principle of least time).

Discovery

Willebrord Snellius experimentally discovered the law in 1621, using geometric constructions. The first printed account was given by René Descartes in 1637 in his treatise 'Dioptrics'. Pierre Fermat later derived the law from the principle of least time, laying the foundations of geometric optics.

How it works

Used to calculate ray paths in lenses, prisms, waveguides. The law is valid for linear isotropic media and monochromatic radiation. It breaks down in anisotropic crystals (birefringence), in nonlinear optical effects, and at boundaries with significant diffraction.

Caveats

Does not describe birefringence in crystals (Iceland spar); At extremely high intensities, nonlinear refraction arises, depending on the field; Boundary effects at nanoscales require taking into account the wave nature of light

n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2
n₁, n₂ — refractive indices of the first and second media (dimensionless), θ₁ — angle of incidence (rad or deg), θ₂ — angle of refraction (rad or deg). The refractive index equals the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum c to the phase velocity in the medium v: n = c/v.
