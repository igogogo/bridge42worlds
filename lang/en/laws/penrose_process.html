Penrose processeffect

A rotating black hole can give up energy while slowing down. A particle that dives into the ergosphere flies out with more energy than it had, by stealing the hole's rotation.

In practice: Theoretically explains how quasars and active galactic nuclei can tap into monstrous energy; in perspective — hypothetical power plants on black holes.
In 1969, Roger Penrose showed that a rotating black hole has a region outside the horizon — the ergosphere — where some of its rotational energy can be extracted. The mechanism: a particle, entering the ergosphere, moves along a complex orbit and splits into two. One of them can escape to infinity with energy greater than that of the original particle, while the second falls into the hole, carrying away negative energy (according to distant clocks) and opposite angular momentum. As a result, the black hole slows down, and the surplus energy goes to the ejected particle. The maximum efficiency of the process can reach 20.7% for a hole with critical spin. This explains how supermassive black holes in quasars can generate colossal radiation fluxes.
💡 The Penrose process is a cosmic analogue of energy 'theft': as if you threw a ball at a propeller and it bounced back faster, slowing down the propeller.
The Penrose process is a way to extract energy from a rotating black hole. A particle enters the ergosphere (the region around the hole where everything must rotate), splits into two fragments; one falls into the hole with negative energy (carries away rotation), and the second flies out with more energy than the original. The black hole thereby slows down, losing mass and angular momentum. Analogy: throwing a stone into a whirlpool so that part of the water's rotation is transferred to the stone, making it fly out faster.

How it works

Requires a rotating (Kerr) black hole with an ergosphere — the region between the event horizon and the static limit, where no body can remain at rest but must rotate in the direction of the hole's spin. Energy is extracted from the ergosphere via 'negative' trajectories: the infalling particle carries away angular momentum opposite to the spin, reducing the hole's energy. In nature, it may power ultra-powerful jets from galactic centers.

💡 The idea came to Penrose at a railway station: he was pondering particles falling into a whirlpool and imagined that if the breakup happens inside the ergosphere, one could 'rob' the black hole.
The Penrose process is a relativistic mechanism for extracting energy from a Kerr black hole by means of a particle entering the ergosphere. In Boyer–Lindquist coordinates in the ergosphere, the timelike Killing vector becomes spacelike, allowing particles to have negative energy relative to infinity. The energy \(E\) of a particle on a geodesic with 4-momentum \(p_\mu\) is \(E = -p_\mu \xi^\mu\), where \(\xi^\mu\) is the timelike Killing vector at infinity. When a particle with \(E_0\) splits into two with \(E_1\) and \(E_2\), energy conservation gives \(E_0 = E_1 + E_2\). If \(E_2 < 0\) and this particle is captured by the black hole, then \(E_1 = E_0 - E_2 > E_0\). The maximum efficiency is determined by the event horizon and the spin parameter \(a\).

Discovery

Roger Penrose proposed the mechanism in 1969 in the paper 'Gravitational Collapse: The Role of General Relativity' and later in 1971 with Floyd. The idea relied on Kerr's solution (1963) for rotating black holes. Penrose proved that the ergosphere allows energy extraction, reducing the hole's mass and angular momentum. Later, the process was generalized to charged and superradiant effects (Zel'dovich, Starobinsky). The process played a key role in understanding astrophysical jets and black hole thermodynamics.

How it works

The process is possible only for Kerr (rotating uncharged) and Kerr–Newman (charged rotating) black holes. Energy is extracted from the ergosphere — the region between the event horizon and the static limit. Particles with negative energy can exist only in this region. In practice, energy can be transferred to electromagnetic fields or accretion disks, producing relativistic jets. Limits: the process stops when the black hole ceases to rotate (Schwarzschild limit); it requires high aiming precision.

Caveats

Instability: small perturbations can destroy finely tuned orbits.; Quantum analogs: in quantum field theory, superradiance limits extraction.; Efficiency is limited: maximum 20.7% for an extreme hole; for real accretion disks, the efficiency may be different.; No direct observational confirmation of the Penrose process specifically has been found, although jets are observed.
