The area of a black hole's event horizon does not decrease over time — as if the black hole were a balloon that can only inflate but never deflate.

In practice: It establishes that when black holes merge, the new hole will be larger, and when matter falls in, its surface area grows — this is used for calculations in gravitational-wave astronomy.
Stephen Hawking proved this theorem in 1971, based on general relativity and the assumption that matter cannot violate energy conditions. The event horizon area A = 16π G² M² / c⁴ (for a non-rotating black hole of mass M) can only increase, ΔA ≥ 0. This striking resemblance to the second law of thermodynamics later led to the idea of black hole entropy and inspired Bekenstein.

How it works

When two black holes collide, the burst of gravitational waves carries away some energy, but the event horizon still swells. LIGO and Virgo detectors observe such mergers and indirectly confirm the theorem.

💡 The theorem does not forbid a black hole from 'losing weight': it can evaporate via Hawking radiation, but the horizon area formally does not decrease due to quantum effects — entropy leaks out along with the radiation.
Hawking's area theorem states that the total area of the event horizon (the boundary from which even light cannot escape) of all black holes never decreases. If something falls into a black hole, its area increases. If two black holes merge, the area of the new one is larger than the sum of the areas of the original ones. This closely resembles the second law of thermodynamics (the entropy of a closed system does not decrease).

💡 In 2021, the LIGO-Virgo collaboration detected event GW190521, where two intermediate-mass black holes merged into one, with a remnant mass of about 142 solar masses, consistent with the theorem — the area increased by nearly 30%.
Hawking's area theorem (1971) is a rigorous statement within classical general relativity: the sum of the areas of all cross-sections of black hole event horizons never decreases over time, provided the weak energy condition holds (energy density is non-negative in any reference frame). For a static Schwarzschild black hole, the horizon area is A = 4π (2GM/c²)² = 16π G² M² / c⁴, and for a Kerr (rotating) black hole, A = 8π ( G M / c² ) ( G M / c² + √( (G M / c²)² - (J / M c)² ) ), where J is the angular momentum.

Discovery

Stephen Hawking formulated the theorem in 1971 using methods of global differential geometry. Earlier, Jacob Bekenstein, inspired by the thermodynamic analogy, proposed that the horizon area is proportional to entropy. Hawking initially criticized the idea but later combined it with his theorem and discovered Hawking radiation.

How it works

The theorem is applied in black hole astrophysics: it restricts possible outcomes of collisions (allowing prediction of the minimal mass of the final black hole) and serves as a basis for calculations in numerical relativity. The classical theorem holds as long as quantum effects are neglected; with Hawking radiation, the area can decrease, and then it is generalized into the generalized second law — the sum of the horizon entropy and matter entropy does not decrease.

Caveats

The theorem is violated when accounting for quantum evaporation of black holes; Verification is needed in extreme regimes, such as near-extremal mergers; A complete theory of quantum gravity must explain the fate of information encoded on the horizon
