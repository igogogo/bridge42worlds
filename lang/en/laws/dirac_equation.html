Dirac equationequation

The Dirac equation is a mathematical instruction that describes the behavior of electrons at very high speeds, close to the speed of light. If the ordinary Schrödinger equation works for slow particles, the Dirac equation is like a racing car, capable of taking into account both the speed and the spin of particles. It predicted that every particle has a counterpart from the anti-world — an antiparticle. That is how physicists learned about the existence of positrons.
💡 Dirac initially did not believe in antiparticles, but when a few years later the positron was discovered in cosmic rays, he was called "genius madness." Dirac himself said that the equation turned out to be smarter than its creator.
In 1928, Paul Dirac combined quantum mechanics and special relativity, obtaining an equation for a relativistic electron. The main highlight is that the wave function is no longer a scalar, but a set of four components — a spinor. They can be thought of as describing an electron with two possible spins and its antiparticle. Dirac seemed to open the door to the Looking Glass: negative energies, which were previously discarded, gained real meaning — the Dirac sea, full of electrons, from which holes sometimes break out — positrons.

How it works

The Dirac equation explains the fine structure of the hydrogen spectrum, takes into account the spin and magnetic moment of the electron naturally, without artificial additives. It also describes particles with zero mass — neutrinos, and formed the basis of quantum electrodynamics. Without it, we would not understand how semiconductor lasers work and why antimatter exists.

💡 Dirac was looking for a linear relativistic equation and came to 4×4 matrices, which are now called Dirac matrices. They say he was so engrossed that he walked around a tree in the mornings, thinking about symmetry.
The Dirac equation is a relativistic wave equation for a free fermion with spin 1/2. It is a first-order partial differential equation in time and space, covariant under Lorentz transformations. In its most compact covariant form, it is written as (iγ^μ ∂_μ − m)ψ = 0, where γ^μ are Dirac matrices, ψ is a four-component spinor field (Dirac spinor), m is the particle mass. The equation unites the principles of quantum mechanics and special relativity, predicts the existence of antiparticles, and automatically includes the particle's spin.

Discovery

Paul Dirac proposed the equation in 1928, trying to overcome the limitations of the Klein–Gordon equation, which gave negative probabilities. He demanded that the equation be first order in time, like the Schrödinger equation, while also being relativistically invariant. The use of matrices made it possible to obtain an equation linear in momentum. Initially, negative energies were a problem, which Dirac interpreted as a filled "sea" of electrons, and holes in that sea as positrons, thus predicting antimatter. The positron was discovered by Carl Anderson in 1932, confirming the theory.

How it works

The Dirac equation is applied in quantum electrodynamics, solid-state physics to describe Dirac fermions in graphene, in neutrino theory (Wolfgang Pauli predicted the neutrino based on the Dirac equation), and in all processes with relativistic electrons. Limits of applicability: the equation describes free particles; interactions are introduced by gauge addition of fields. At energies comparable to the particle mass, quantum field corrections must be taken into account. The equation does not account for strong and weak interactions.

Caveats

The concept of the "Dirac sea" runs into difficulties for bosons and does not explain the origin of antiparticles in modern quantum field theory.; Quantization of the equation leads to a theory with a complex vacuum and requires a normal ordering procedure.; Unification with gravity remains an open problem, since the equation does not include the metric of spacetime.; When going to many particles, problems with causality and locality arise, solved within quantum field theory.
