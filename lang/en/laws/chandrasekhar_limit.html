Chandrasekhar limiteffect

A white dwarf is a star the size of Earth, but with the mass of the Sun. It resists gravity with the help of quantum pressure from electrons. The Chandrasekhar limit is the mass at which electrons 'give up', and the star collapses into a neutron star or black hole. Roughly speaking, 1.4 solar masses.
💡 It is because of this limit that exploding white dwarfs have become cosmic 'beacons' by which the expansion of the Universe is measured.
In 1930, the twenty-year-old Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar, on a ship from India to England, realized that the speed of electrons in a very dense white dwarf approaches the speed of light. Combining special relativity and quantum statistics, he derived the maximum mass. An analogy: it is like a spring that stiffens the harder you compress it, but breaks under too much load.

How it works

In a white dwarf, electrons fill all the lowest energy levels (Pauli principle). When compressed, they acquire enormous kinetic energy, creating pressure. If the mass exceeds the limit, even relativistic electrons cannot hold off collapse, and the core becomes a neutron star.

💡 Chandrasekhar on that voyage was ahead of official science, but faced fierce criticism from Arthur Eddington, who believed that nature must prevent collapse.
The Chandrasekhar limit is the maximum mass of a star for which the pressure of degenerate electron gas, calculated using relativistic laws, can balance gravitational contraction. Expressed as M_Ch ≈ 1.4 M_☉ for carbon-oxygen white dwarfs.

Discovery

Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar in 1930, while sailing to Cambridge, solved the equations of stellar structure taking into account special relativity and Fermi-Dirac quantum statistics. He found that above a certain mass, equilibrium is impossible. His conclusions were ridiculed by Eddington, but later, in 1983, Chandrasekhar received the Nobel Prize for this and other work.

How it works

It is applied to understand stellar evolution, classify white dwarfs, estimate the age of star clusters, and as standardization of type Ia supernovae in cosmology. Limitation: in close binary systems, accretion can cause the white dwarf to exceed the limit, triggering a thermonuclear explosion.

Caveats

The exact value of the limit depends on the chemical composition and the electron interaction model; In strong magnetic fields and rotation, the limit can change; General relativity is not taken into account in the classical derivation, which requires corrections for neutron stars

M_{\text{Ch}} = \frac{\omega_3^0 \sqrt{3\pi}}{2} \left(\frac{\hbar c}{G}\right)^{3/2} \frac{1}{(\mu_e m_H)^2}
ω_3^0 is the Lane-Emden constant for a polytrope with index n=3 (approximately 2.018), ℏ is the reduced Planck constant (ℏ ≈ 1.0546×10⁻³⁴ J·s), c is the speed of light (c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s), G is the gravitational constant (G ≈ 6.674×10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²), μ_e is the mean molecular mass per electron (for fully ionized carbon μ_e = 2), m_H is the mass of the hydrogen atom (m_H ≈ 1.673×10⁻²⁷ kg).
M_{\text{Ch}} \approx 1.4 \, M_{\odot}
M_☉ is the solar mass (M_☉ ≈ 1.989×10³⁰ kg), numerical value about 1.4 solar masses.
