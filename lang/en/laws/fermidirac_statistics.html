Fermi–Dirac statisticsprinciple

176 Related articles
Imagine a party where each guest demands their own unique chair and cannot stand neighbors on the same seat. Fermions (electrons, protons) behave exactly like that: at most one particle can occupy a quantum state. Fermi–Dirac statistics describes how these 'individualists' distribute themselves over energies: at low temperature they fill the lowest levels first, just as guests would occupy all chairs in the front row before moving to the back rows.

How it works

The behavior of electrons in metals: they fill the conduction band up to the Fermi energy. Because of this, some materials conduct electricity, others are insulators. In astrophysics, white dwarfs exist thanks to the pressure of degenerate electron gas, and neutron stars — the pressure of degenerate neutrons. In semiconductors, this statistics explains the dependence of carrier concentration on temperature and doping.

💡 It is precisely because electrons obey this statistics that atoms do not collapse and the world around us has a definite structure. In white dwarfs, only the pressure of the degenerate electron gas, described by Fermi–Dirac statistics, keeps the star from collapsing into a black hole.
In 1926, Enrico Fermi and Paul Dirac, building on the Pauli exclusion principle, independently derived the distribution for particles with half-integer spin. Fermions cannot occupy the same quantum state more than once — like passengers on a train where each seat is strictly individual. The distribution function shows the probability that a level with energy E is occupied, and at zero temperature it becomes a step function: all states below the Fermi energy are filled, above — empty. As temperature rises, the step 'blurs'.

How it works

The behavior of electrons in metals: they fill the conduction band up to the Fermi energy. Because of this, some materials conduct electricity, others are insulators. In astrophysics, white dwarfs exist thanks to the pressure of degenerate electron gas, and neutron stars — the pressure of degenerate neutrons. In semiconductors, this statistics explains the dependence of carrier concentration on temperature and doping.

💡 In 1927, Fermi applied his statistics to electrons in metals, laying the foundation of band theory, and Dirac showed that antisymmetric wave functions naturally lead to this distribution. Interestingly, at very high temperatures, Fermi–Dirac statistics transitions to the classical Boltzmann distribution, but with an 'avoidance effect' of each other.
Fermi–Dirac statistics determines the equilibrium distribution of identical fermions (particles with half-integer spin) over energy levels in an ideal gas at thermodynamic equilibrium. The distribution function: f(E) = 1 / (e^{(E-μ)/(kT)} + 1), where f(E) is the probability of occupation of a state with energy E, μ is the chemical potential, k is Boltzmann's constant, T is absolute temperature. The Pauli principle is expressed in that the maximum occupation of each state is one, and as T → 0 the function becomes a step function (Fermi step).

Discovery

In 1925, Wolfgang Pauli formulated the exclusion principle. In 1926, Enrico Fermi and Paul Dirac independently constructed the statistics for particles obeying this principle. Fermi applied it to electrons in metals, and Dirac connected it with the antisymmetry of wave functions. Further development occurred in the works of Eugene Wigner, Felix Bloch, and others, becoming the foundation of quantum statistical physics and solid-state physics.

How it works

It is used to describe the electronic properties of metals and semiconductors (band theory), degenerate gas in white dwarfs and neutron stars (equation of state). Limits of applicability: ideal Fermi gas without interactions; for strong interactions or non-equilibrium conditions, quantum field theory or kinetic equations must be employed. It does not describe correlations beyond the statistics of independent particles.

Caveats

For strongly interacting fermions (e.g., in quark-gluon plasma), the statistics require generalizations.; It does not account for quantum entanglement, which becomes significant in ultracold systems and when modeling solids.; In disordered systems (glasses, amorphous semiconductors), deviations from the equilibrium distribution arise, requiring more complex models.
