Rutherford scattering formulalaw

📊 0 Related articles

Bombarding gold foil with alpha particles is like firing peas at a hidden bowling ball: the rare ricochet reveals the location of the 'nucleus'. Rutherford's formula calculates how many particles will bounce off at a given angle.

In practice: With its help Rutherford discovered the atomic nucleus, and today the method of Rutherford backscattering controls the thickness and composition of nanofilms in microchips.
Ernest Rutherford, skeptical of the complex structure of the atom, tasked Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden with bombarding gold foil with alpha particles. Everyone expected the particles to pass through, only slightly scattering. But Marsden noticed: about one in 8000 particles bounced back! Rutherford was amazed and later compared it to a 15-inch shell ricocheting off tissue paper. He realized that almost all the mass of the atom is concentrated in a tiny nucleus. In 1911, he derived a formula relating the number of scattered particles to angle, energy, and nuclear charge. This was a purely classical result, perfectly explaining the experiment; only later were quantum corrections needed for light nuclei.

How it works

The formula applies to any charged particles in a Coulomb field. It is the basis of Rutherford backscattering spectrometry (RBS), which is used to non-destructively determine the composition and thickness of coatings, check the quality of films in microelectronics, and calculate doses in radiation therapy.

💡 Rutherford recalled: 'I did not believe in backscattering until Geiger showed me the counter. Then I realized we had hit upon the nucleus.'
Imagine you are throwing tennis balls into a fog where a bowling ball is hidden. Almost all balls will fly past, only slightly changing trajectory. But those that fly straight at the ball will bounce back. That is exactly how Geiger and Marsden saw the rare alpha particles reflected by the gold foil. Rutherford explained this with a formula: the probability of strong deflection increases with the nuclear charge and decreases with the particle energy. The nucleus is a super-dense 'pea' inside an almost empty atom.

It is the basis of Rutherford backscattering spectrometry (RBS), which is used to non-destructively determine the composition and thickness of coatings, check the quality of films in microelectronics, and calculate doses in radiation therapy.

💡 In 1927, H. Mott generalized Rutherford's formula to the case of identical particles (e.g., α-α scattering), which allowed the proof of Pauli spin statistics.
Rutherford's formula is the classical differential cross-section for elastic Coulomb scattering of a non-relativistic point particle on a stationary point nucleus. In the center-of-mass system: dσ/dΩ = (Z₁Z₂e²/(16π ε₀ E))² · 1/sin⁴(θ/2). It is applicable when the energy is high enough to neglect electron screening, but low for relativistic and nuclear effects. At high energies or for light nuclei, quantum mechanical corrections (Mott formula) and relativistic generalizations are required.

Discovery

Analyzing the experiments of Geiger and Marsden (1909–1911), Rutherford concluded that the atom contains a positively charged nucleus with a radius of ~10⁻¹⁵ m, concentrating almost all the mass. He derived the formula for elastic scattering in the field of a central force 1/r², which ideally matched the data. This disproved Thomson's model ('plum pudding'). Subsequently, the formula became the basis of Rutherford backscattering spectrometry (RBS) for non-destructive analysis of the composition and thickness of thin films.

How it works

The formula works for energies from a few keV to several MeV; at lower energies, electron screening is significant, at higher energies, relativistic effects. For 5 MeV alpha particles on gold, it works excellently. The cross-section integral diverges at small angles due to the long-range nature, so in practice a minimum scattering angle is introduced. At very small impact parameters, nuclear forces come into play.

Caveats

The total cross-section is formally infinite due to the long-range Coulomb potential; For light nuclei and high energies, the quantum mechanical Mott formula is required; Relativistic corrections and electron screening alter the picture at low energies; At very small impact parameters, nuclear forces and tunneling come into play

\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega} = \left(\frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{16\pi\varepsilon_0 E}\right)^2 \frac{1}{\sin^4(\theta/2)}
dσ/dΩ — differential scattering cross-section (probability of scattering into solid angle dΩ), Z₁ and Z₂ — charge numbers (number of protons) of the incident particle and target nucleus, e — elementary charge (≈1.602×10⁻¹⁹ C), ε₀ — electric constant (≈8.854×10⁻¹² F/m), E — kinetic energy of the incident particle, θ — scattering angle from the original direction
