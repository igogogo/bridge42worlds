Gas molecules are like restless schoolchildren in a gym: the more they are cornered (volume reduced) or made to run faster (heated), the more often they hit the walls — that's how pressure rises.

In practice: From inflating tires to weather forecasting: the relationship between pressure, volume, and temperature governs engines, balloons, and breathing.
In 1834, Émile Clapeyron combined the experimental laws of Boyle, Charles, and Gay-Lussac into the single equation pV = nRT. This elegant formula describes the equilibrium state of an ideal gas — a model where molecules are considered point-like and non-interacting. It works as a 'switch' between the volume, pressure, and temperature of a gas, allowing prediction of the behavior of real gases under ordinary conditions.

The equation links four quantities: pressure (force per area), volume (the space the gas occupies), temperature (a measure of the molecules' energy), and amount of substance (number of molecules). An ideal gas is a theoretical model where particles have no volume of their own and do not interact at a distance, undergoing only elastic collisions. The equation of state pV = nRT establishes the relationship between macroscopic parameters. It is derived from the kinetic theory of gases, where pressure is expressed through the average kinetic energy of molecules: p = (2/3) n ⟨E_k⟩. The internal energy of an ideal gas depends only on temperature: for a monatomic gas U = (3/2) nRT. The law is the limiting case for real gases at low pressures and high temperatures.

Discovery

The experiments of Robert Boyle (1662) and Edme Mariotte showed the inverse proportionality of pressure and volume (pV=const at constant T). Jacques Charles (1787) and Joseph Gay-Lussac (1802) established the dependence of volume on temperature. Amedeo Avogadro in 1811 hypothesized that equal volumes of gases contain equal numbers of molecules under the same conditions. Émile Clapeyron in 1834 combined these laws into a single equation, later generalized by Dmitri Mendeleev with the inclusion of the universal gas constant.

How it works

Widely applied from internal combustion engine calculations to atmospheric modeling. Limits of applicability: the equation is not applicable at high densities and low temperatures, when intermolecular interactions and the finite size of molecules become significant — there, Van der Waals equations with corrections are used.

Caveats

Does not account for quantum effects and degeneracy at ultra-low temperatures; Does not describe phase transitions; For polyatomic gases, internal energy is more complex due to rotational and vibrational degrees of freedom
