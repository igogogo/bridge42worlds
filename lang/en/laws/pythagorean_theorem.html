The Pythagorean theorem is the foundation of geometry: in a right triangle, the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the legs. It is attributed to Pythagoras (6th century BC), although evidence from Babylon shows earlier familiarity. The first rigorous proof was given by Euclid in the 'Elements'. In physics, it determines the distance between points, the interval in relativity theory, and the norm of a vector in quantum mechanics.

The Pythagorean theorem is a geometric theorem stating that in the Euclidean plane, for a right triangle with legs a, b and hypotenuse c, the relation a² + b² = c² holds. In n-dimensional Euclidean space, the squared distance ds² = dx₁² + ... + dx_n² defines the metric. In Riemannian geometry and general relativity, the metric is generalized to ds² = g_ij dx^i dx^j, where g_ij is the metric tensor describing curved spacetime. In Hilbert space, the inner product that defines probability amplitudes is based on the same quadratic structure.

Discovery

The theorem has been known since ancient times. Babylonian mathematicians around 1800 BC used triples of integers satisfying the relation to construct right angles. The school of Pythagoras (6th–5th centuries BC) is credited with the first proof and giving the theorem philosophical significance. Euclid in the 'Elements' (c. 300 BC, Book I, Proposition 47) gave a classical geometric proof. In the non-Euclidean geometries of Lobachevsky and Bolyai, the theorem no longer holds, and in the 20th century it was generalized to curved manifolds and became the foundation of the metric tensor in general relativity.

How it works

The theorem is constantly applied in physics: distance calculations in mechanics, determining the interval in special relativity, constructing the metric in general relativity. In quantum mechanics, the norm of state vectors, calculated Pythagorean-way, ensures conservation of total probability. Limits of applicability: it holds strictly only in flat Euclidean space; in curved manifolds it works locally in infinitesimal neighborhoods. In Minkowski spacetime, the signs in front of squares differ, leading to null intervals for lightlike paths.

Caveats

In non-Euclidean geometries, the theorem does not hold; its role is taken by local metric relations.; In spaces with indefinite metric (as in special relativity), the sum of squares can be negative, requiring the concept of signature.; Historical priority is disputed: ancient Egyptians and Babylonians knew triples long before Pythagoras.
