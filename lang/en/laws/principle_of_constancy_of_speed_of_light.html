Light is a true sprinter of the Universe, always running at the same speed. No matter whether you are chasing a flashlight beam or rushing towards it, its speed will remain unchanged for you — about 300,000 km/s. This is unlike the everyday world: if you walk on a moving escalator, your speed adds to the speed of the steps. With light, this trick doesn't work! It is this strange property that leads to time dilation and length contraction in relativity.

The principle of the constancy of the speed of light in all inertial frames of reference is the cornerstone of the special theory of relativity, formulated by Albert Einstein in 1905. Historically, it was approached by Maxwell's equations (the speed of electromagnetic waves turned out to be independent of the frame of reference) and the failure of the Michelson–Morley experiment to detect the ether. Einstein postulated: every observer, measuring the speed of light in a vacuum, will obtain c ≈ 3.0·10⁸ m/s, regardless of how fast they move relative to the source.

The principle of the constancy of the speed of light in a vacuum states that the speed of light c = 299,792,458 m/s is the same in all inertial frames of reference and does not depend on the motion of the source. The rigorous formulation is given in Einstein's special theory of relativity: the Minkowski spacetime metric ds² = c²dt² - dx² - dy² - dz² is invariant under Lorentz transformations, which mix the space and time coordinates. This implies the Lorentz invariance of the quantity c.

Discovery

The first indications of the constancy of the speed of light came from Maxwell's equations (1861), from which c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) was calculated. Attempts to detect the ether wind through the Michelson–Morley experiment (1887) yielded a null result. Hendrik Lorentz and Henri Poincaré developed the preparatory mathematical apparatus, but it was Albert Einstein in 1905 who elevated the constancy of c to a fundamental postulate, discarding the ether and building the theory of relativity.

How it works

The principle underlies all modern high-energy physics and astrophysics. It is necessary for the correct operation of particle accelerators, satellite navigation systems, and understanding the evolution of the Universe. Limits of applicability: special relativity does not account for gravity, so in strong fields (near black holes or at extreme densities) general relativity is required, where the speed of light is locally constant, but its global behavior is more complex.

Caveats

Unification with quantum mechanics: how to build a theory in which spacetime is quantized?; Possible violation of Lorentz invariance at Planck scales (quantum gravity hypotheses).; The horizon problem: in the expanding Universe, distant objects recede faster than light, which does not contradict the principle locally, but complicates global description.
