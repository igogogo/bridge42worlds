Wheeler-DeWitt equationequation

At the quantum level, the universe obeys a balance equation where the energy of matter and the curvature of space completely cancel each other out, leaving an eternal 'now' without the passage of time.

In practice: Helps to find a quantum description of the Big Bang and to understand how three-dimensional space emerges from quantum foam.
In quantum gravity, time disappears as a fundamental quantity. The Wheeler–DeWitt equation is a direct result of applying the laws of quantum mechanics to Einstein's theory of gravitation. John Wheeler and Bryce DeWitt formalized quantum theory in the so-called canonical approach in 1967. It turned out that the wave function of the universe (the amplitude of all possible geometries) is independent of time — it is a static eigenvalue equation where the Hamiltonian operator yields zero. That is, at the deepest level, there is no evolution: all moments of time 'are present' simultaneously. Analogy: a vast library where, instead of books, three-dimensional slices of space are kept, and the equation is the rule binding them into a single four-dimensional framework.
💡 Because time is absent from the equation, some physicists jokingly call it 'the equation where nothing happens.'
The Wheeler–DeWitt equation is the main equation of quantum cosmology. It describes the entire universe as a single quantum state (a set of probabilities of various space geometries) and states that the total energy of the gravitational field and matter is always zero. Time does not enter this equation — it is 'frozen,' as in a still picture. Analogy: a book with variants of three-dimensional worlds, where each page is a possible shape of space; the equation demands that the sum of energies on each page be zero.

How it works

Arises from attempts to unify quantum mechanics (the behavior of particles at the micro level) and general relativity (the geometry of space-time). Applied in models of the early universe, where space-time was quantum. In practice, it helps theorists calculate the probabilities of various scenarios for the birth of the universe, for example, from quantum tunneling.

💡 Although the equation is traditionally attributed to Wheeler and DeWitt, Wheeler himself called it 'the equation without time,' and DeWitt jokingly called it 'the equation that hates me' due to its mathematical difficulties.
The Wheeler–DeWitt equation: \(\hat{H} \Psi = 0\) is the central equation of canonical quantum gravity (geometrodynamics). It is derived from the ADM formalism (Arnowitt, Deser, Misner) by imposing quantum constraints on the Hamiltonian constraint. Here \(\hat{H}\) is the Hamiltonian operator acting on the wave functional \(\Psi[h_{ij}]\) which describes the quantum state of the gravitational field. The equation demands that physical states be invariant under time reparameterization, which eliminates coordinate time from the theory — the problem of time in quantum gravity. Solutions are sought in superspace — the infinite-dimensional space of all three-dimensional geometries.

Discovery

In 1967, John Archibald Wheeler and Bryce DeWitt published a paper deriving the equation for the quantum wave function of the universe. They relied on earlier work by Paul Dirac on quantizing constrained systems and on the ADM formalism of 1962. Wheeler actively developed the concept of 'quantum foam' and space-time at Planck scales, while DeWitt gave the idea a rigorous mathematical form. The equation became the starting point for quantum cosmology and the work of Hartle–Hawking, Vilenkin, and others.

How it works

Used to describe the quantum state of the universe as a whole, especially in the regime of strong gravitational fields where classical general relativity is inapplicable (near singularities). Allows the calculation of the wave function of the universe and the prediction of probabilities for various initial conditions for cosmological inflation. Limits of applicability: Planck scale of lengths and energies; the problem of interpreting time and choosing an internal time variable remains unsolved; the equation is difficult to solve for the general case.

Caveats

The equation contains singularities in superspace for degenerate metrics.; Ambiguity in defining time (the problem of time): how to recover dynamics from a static equation.; Technical difficulty: regularization and quantization of an infinite-dimensional configuration space are required.; Lack of observable predictions: verification requires access to Planck energies.
