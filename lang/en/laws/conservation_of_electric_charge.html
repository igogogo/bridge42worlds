The law of conservation of electric charge states that the algebraic sum of electric charges in a closed system does not change over time. It is an empirical law confirmed with the highest precision, and in modern physics, it is derived as a consequence of the gauge symmetry of the electromagnetic interaction (U(1) symmetry) via Noether's theorem. Mathematically, the law is expressed by the continuity equation relating charge density ρ and current density J.

Discovery

The idea of conservation of electricity arose in the work of Benjamin Franklin in 1747. He introduced the concept of positive and negative charge and postulated that during friction, one body transfers an 'electric fluid' to another. Michael Faraday in the 1830s experimentally proved the constancy of charge in electrolysis: the mass of substance released at an electrode is proportional to the passed charge. In the early 20th century, the law gained theoretical justification thanks to Emmy Noether's theorem, which linked it to the gauge invariance of electrodynamics.

How it works

The law is applied in all areas of physics and engineering where electrical phenomena occur. It underlies Kirchhoff's first rule (the sum of currents at a node is zero), explains the impossibility of annihilation of a single electron without a positron, and also guarantees that batteries, generators, and lightning only transfer charge, not create it. No violation of the law has been detected in any experiment, including high-energy particle collisions, although theoretically under extreme conditions (e.g., black hole evaporation), the creation of charged particles is possible but balanced.

Caveats

Strict charge conservation may be violated in some grand unified theories predicting proton decay.; The Schwinger effect allows pair production from vacuum in strong electric fields without an external source, but with total charge conservation.; The asymmetry between the amount of matter and antimatter in the Universe remains unexplained under exact charge conservation.
