holographic principleprinciple

All information in a volume of space fits on its surface — like the entire content of a book encoded on the cover.

In practice: Reinterprets the nature of black holes, inspires concepts of quantum computers and holographic memory.
In the 1970s, Jacob Bekenstein suggested that black holes have entropy proportional to the horizon area, and Stephen Hawking derived the exact formula S = A/(4Gℏ) (in units k_B=c=1). In 1993, Gerard 't Hooft realized the generality of this idea: if the black hole entropy is maximal for a given volume, then any system cannot contain more information. Leonard Susskind formalized the principle: the number of fundamental states (degrees of freedom) in a region of space is bounded by the area of the boundary in Planck units. The AdS/CFT correspondence became an exact realization of the holographic idea.
💡 According to the holographic principle, the visible Universe could be a giant hologram recorded on the cosmological horizon.
The holographic principle says: the maximum amount of information (or entropy — a measure of uncertainty) in any region of space is proportional to the area of its boundary, not the volume. This is astonishing: the three-dimensional world seems to be 'projected' from a two-dimensional screen. The bound comes from black hole physics: their entropy S equals the horizon area A divided by 4 times the square of the Planck length lₚ (Planck length is the quantum limit of distance, lₚ ≈ 1.6×10⁻³⁵ m). The formula S ≤ A/(4lₚ²) is a strict upper bound for any system.

How it works

In practice, this means that nature is fundamentally two-dimensional, and the third dimension is an illusion. This explains why information is not lost in black holes, and provides new approaches to unify quantum mechanics with gravity.

💡 The holographic principle inspired a hypothetical cosmological model of the 'holographic Universe', where our three-dimensional space is merely an emergent projection from a two-dimensional horizon, and time is a thermodynamic illusion.
The holographic principle is the hypothesis that all the contents of spacetime can be fully described by a theory on its boundary. The fundamental bound: S ≤ A/(4l_P²) (or in natural units S = A/4), where S is entropy, A is the area of the surface enclosing the system, l_P = √(ħG/c³) is the Planck length. It follows from the analysis of black holes: their entropy (Bekenstein–Hawking) scales with area, not volume, and they have the maximum entropy for a given volume (generalized second law of thermodynamics). The principle underlies the AdS/CFT duality and, perhaps, is the key to quantum gravity.

Discovery

Jacob Bekenstein (1972) linked black hole entropy with horizon area, and Stephen Hawking (1974) computed the 1/4 coefficient, discovering Hawking radiation. Gerard 't Hooft (1993) formulated the holographic principle as a resolution of the information loss paradox in black holes. Leonard Susskind (1995) developed it into a full program, introducing the holographic bound. Juan Maldacena (1997) provided a concrete realization via AdS/CFT. In the 21st century, the principle is actively applied in quantum information theory and entropic gravity.

How it works

The principle is applied in quantum gravity theories to resolve black hole paradoxes: information is preserved on the horizon. Boundaries: for static asymptotically flat spacetimes it is precisely defined; for cosmological horizons (de Sitter) there are modifications. Limitation — a complete holographic theory for a realistic Universe has not yet been built. In quantum cosmology, the holographic principle imposes constraints on inflationary models.

Caveats

How does the hologram work in de Sitter space (with a positive cosmological constant)?; What are the fundamental carriers of information on the holographic boundary?; How does the holographic principle explain three-dimensional gravity — through entropic forces or something else?
