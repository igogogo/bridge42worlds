Schrödinger equationequation

In the quantum world, particles behave like waves of probability. The Schrödinger equation is a mathematical rule that tells how these waves change over time. It does not indicate the exact location of a particle but gives probabilities: for example, where an electron is most likely to appear. It's like a weather forecast that predicts where rain is most likely.
💡 Without the Schrödinger equation, there would be no modern semiconductors, lasers, or even quantum computers — all of these work on quantum principles.
In 1926, Erwin Schrödinger, inspired by de Broglie's hypothesis of matter waves, proposed an equation governing the wave function ψ. It became a cornerstone of quantum mechanics along with Heisenberg's matrix mechanics. Imagine a guitar string: its vibrations are described by a wave equation, but the Schrödinger equation contains an imaginary unit and a potential, so the waves of particles are probability waves.

How it works

The equation explains the structure of atoms, chemical bonds, and the behavior of electrons in solids. It allows calculating energy bands in semiconductors and creating transistors, LEDs, and lasers. It is also indispensable in quantum chemistry for modeling molecules.

💡 Schrödinger himself did not accept the probabilistic interpretation and proposed the cat-in-a-box paradox to show the absurdity of quantum superposition, but quantum mechanics passed all experimental tests.
The Schrödinger equation is a postulate of non-relativistic quantum mechanics. Time-dependent equation: iħ ∂ψ/∂t = Ĥψ, where ψ is the wave function (state vector), Ĥ is the Hamiltonian operator. Stationary equation: Ĥψ = Eψ, describes states with definite energy. The wave function contains all information about the system, and |ψ|² gives the probability density of finding a particle.

Discovery

Erwin Schrödinger published the equation in 1926, building on the work of Louis de Broglie (1924) on matter waves. The first success was explaining the spectrum of the hydrogen atom. Soon after, Max Born gave a statistical interpretation of the wave function, and John von Neumann developed a rigorous mathematical formalism in Hilbert space. The Schrödinger equation is still widely used, although for relativistic particles the Dirac equation is used.

How it works

Applied for calculating energy levels of atoms and molecules, band structure of crystals, dynamics of quantum systems. Limits: not applicable at speeds close to the speed of light (replaced by the Dirac equation), does not describe particle creation and annihilation (for this, second quantization and quantum field theory are used).

Caveats

Measurement problem: wave function collapse and transition from quantum to classical; Connection with general relativity (quantum gravity); Interpretation of the wave function (realism, Copenhagen, many-worlds, etc.)
