It is impossible to make a perfect copy of an unknown quantum secret — any attempt to peek at it will ruin it.

In practice: Protects quantum communication channels from eavesdropping: any intrusion leaves inevitable traces, making quantum cryptography absolutely secure.
The no-cloning theorem was proved in 1982 independently by William Wootters and Wojciech Zurek, and also by Dennis Dieks. It immediately explained why in the quantum world, communication through entanglement does not allow faster-than-light information transfer: the received signal is an unknown state that cannot be copied for repeated verification. The prohibition turned out to be a fundamental limitation born from the linearity of quantum mechanics. Thanks to it, quantum information is fundamentally different from classical information.

How it works

In quantum computers, this means that information is protected from copying: you cannot secretly duplicate data. Quantum cryptography uses this property to create secret keys, and any attempt to intercept them is immediately noticed by the legitimate party as anomalies.

💡 Although perfect cloning is forbidden, one can build 'quantum copying machines' that create imperfect copies with a certain accuracy. They have almost reached the theoretical limit, but will never reach 100%.
If you try to copy the exact quantum state of a particle (all its invisible information), you first have to measure it. But measurement always changes the state — it destroys the original. Therefore, perfect copying without knowing what exactly is being copied is impossible. It is like trying to make an exact mold from sand without touching the original.

How it works

In quantum computers, this means that information is protected from copying: you cannot secretly duplicate data. Quantum cryptography uses this property to create secret keys, and any attempt to intercept them is immediately noticed by the legitimate party as anomalies.

💡 Irony of fate: in the same issue of Nature where the no-cloning theorem was published, there was an article about the first successful cloning of a mammal — a mouse. The quantum world turned out to be stricter.
The no-cloning theorem states that there is no unitary evolution operator U that, for an arbitrary unknown quantum state |ψ⟩ and a fixed initial state of an auxiliary system |e⟩, would produce the transformation U(|ψ⟩⊗|e⟩) = |ψ⟩⊗|ψ⟩. The proof relies on the linearity of quantum mechanics: if such an operator existed, it would violate the superposition principle for sum states. Consequence: no measurement method can perfectly duplicate an unknown state, making quantum information immune to undetectable copying.

Discovery

In 1982, William Wootters and Wojciech Zurek in a short note in Nature showed that cloning an unknown quantum state contradicts linearity. In the same year, Dennis Dieks published a similar result. The formulation quickly became a cornerstone of quantum information theory. Subsequently, the theorem was generalized to mixed states and entangled systems, and also formed the basis for proving the security of quantum cryptography protocols such as BB84.

How it works

The theorem is actively applied in quantum computing and quantum communication. It guarantees that an eavesdropper cannot undetectably intercept quantum keys, since any attempt to copy photons will introduce errors. In quantum computing, it prevents error correction by simple copying, forcing the use of sophisticated codes. Limits of applicability: the theorem holds for closed systems with unitary evolution; in open systems one can only talk about approximate cloning.

Caveats
