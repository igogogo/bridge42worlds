The analytic continuation of the exponential function into the complex domain reveals a deep connection between algebraic operations and geometric transformations. Euler's formula e^(iθ) = cosθ + i sinθ establishes an isomorphism between complex numbers on the unit circle and rotations in ℝ², and the identity e^(iπ) + 1 = 0 demonstrates the unity of the additive and multiplicative worlds.

Discovery

Leonhard Euler in the 1740s experimented with series and discovered that substituting ix into the series of e^x yields two familiar series. In 1748, he published this result in 'Introductio in analysin infinitorum'. Before him, Roger Cotes had derived similar relations, but it was Euler who realized the full power of the complex exponential. Later, the formula became the key to creating complex analysis.

How it works

Euler's formula is used to represent oscillations in physics and engineering: from describing electromagnetic waves to fast Fourier transform algorithms. Its limits of applicability arise when moving to non-commutative algebras, such as quaternions, where the exponential does not decompose simply into trigonometric functions.
