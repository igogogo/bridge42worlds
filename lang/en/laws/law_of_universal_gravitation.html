law of universal gravitationlaw

Imagine that all bodies attract each other like magnets, but much weaker. The more massive the objects and the closer they are, the stronger the attraction. It is this force that keeps us on Earth and makes the planets revolve around the Sun.
💡 If gravity disappeared, we would fly into space, and the Earth would stop moving along its orbit.
Isaac Newton formulated the law in 1687, unifying terrestrial and celestial mechanics. He built on the work of Galileo and Kepler, as well as discussions with Hooke. The law states: any two bodies attract with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. We feel this force as weight, and it governs the motion of planets and tides. Imagine a trampoline: a heavy ball depresses the surface, and a light ball rolls toward it — that's how gravity works according to Einstein, but Newton's law gives a simple and accurate formula for weak fields.

How it works

The law manifests at all scales: from a falling apple to the orbits of galaxies. It is used to calculate satellite trajectories, predict tides, and even discover new planets (e.g., Neptune). In mechanical engineering and construction, it helps calculate loads. However, under very strong gravity (near black holes) or at enormous speeds, it is replaced by the general theory of relativity.

💡 The law of universal gravitation was so accurate that it predicted the existence of Neptune from deviations in Uranus's orbit.
Law of universal gravitation: every particle in the Universe attracts every other particle with a force directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers. Vector form: F = -G m1 m2 r / |r|^3. Gravitational constant G ≈ 6.67430×10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg². This is a fundamental interaction dominating at macroscopic distances.

Discovery

Isaac Newton formulated the law in 1687 in 'Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica', generalizing terrestrial and celestial observations. Galileo studied falling bodies, Kepler studied planetary motions, and Robert Hooke suggested ideas about a force inversely proportional to the square of distance, but Newton provided a rigorous mathematical form.

How it works

Used for calculating orbital mechanics, gravitational maneuvers of spacecraft, and determining masses of celestial bodies. Limits of applicability: does not work in strong gravitational fields (replaced by Einstein's equations) and at the microscale where quantum effects must be considered.

Caveats

Does not explain the nature of gravity; Does not work near black holes; Incompatible with quantum mechanics

F = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
F — force of attraction, G — gravitational constant, m1 and m2 — masses of the bodies, r — distance between their centers
