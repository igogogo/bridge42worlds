Ampere's lawlaw

Current in a wire generates a circular magnetic field — like water rotating around a drain.

In practice: Calculation of magnetic fields in electric motors, generators, and transformers, design of MRI magnets.
In 1820, after Ørsted's experiment, André-Marie Ampère developed a theory of current interaction within a week. He deduced that the circulation of the magnetic field along any closed path equals the current passing through the loop (multiplied by the constant μ0). Analogy: the stronger the traffic flow on a ring road, the more powerful the air vortex above it, and the wind swirls along the roadside. Later, Maxwell generalized the law, adding the displacement current.
💡 Using Ampere's law, one can 'weigh' the current — with a current balance, the ampere is determined with high accuracy.
When charged particles run along a conductor (electric current), a magnetic field forms around the conductor (a special form of matter causing attraction of magnets). This field wraps around the wire in invisible rings. The greater the current, the stronger the field. Ampere's law relates these quantities: the closed loop around the wire multiplied by the field strength is directly proportional to the current.

How it works

Examples: magnetic lifting crane for scrap metal, electric door lock, deflection of a compass needle when an electrical appliance is turned on.

💡 Ampere devised an ingenious method of measuring current — he passed it through a spiral and determined the interaction of the turns by the spring extension.
Ampere's law in integral form: ∮ B·dl = μ0 I_enc. Here the integral is taken over a closed loop, B is magnetic induction, dl is an element of loop length, I_enc is the total current through a surface bounded by the loop. For vacuum and steady currents. In differential form: ∇×B = μ0 J, where J is current density. Both expressions are equivalent, but the second describes the field at each point.

Discovery

In 1820, Hans Christian Ørsted noticed that a current-carrying wire deflects a magnetic needle. André-Marie Ampère, inspired by this, presented a report with a quantitative law just a week later. In 1861, James Clerk Maxwell included the law in his equations and added the displacement current, correcting a contradiction with charge conservation.

How it works

The law is actively used to calculate magnetic fields in symmetric configurations: straight wire, solenoid, toroid. Limits of applicability: only for steady currents; when electric fields change over time, the displacement current must be considered. In the presence of magnetic materials, the relationship becomes more complex.

Caveats

The original law does not describe the vortex magnetic field in a capacitor during charging; Requires symmetry for analytical integration; In nonlinear magnetic media, B and H are related in a complex manner
