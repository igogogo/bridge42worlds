Two photons entering a half-silvered mirror from different sides always exit together, as if glued.

In practice: Ultra-precise synchronization in optical clocks and resolution enhancement in quantum microscopy thanks to photon 'sticking'.
Discovered in 1987 by Chung Ki Hong, Zhe-Yu Ou, and Leonard Mandel, this effect revealed strange quantum behavior: two identical photons meeting at a beam splitter always stick together. A beam splitter is like a half-silvered mirror, reflecting half the light and transmitting half. From a classical perspective, sometimes the photons would end up in different detectors, but in the quantum world, interference of probability amplitudes completely excludes that outcome. This is a vivid demonstration that photons are indistinguishable particles obeying Bose–Einstein statistics, which favors occupying the same state. The original experiment used parametric down-conversion to create photon pairs and revealed a sharp dip in coincidence counts.

How it works

The effect appears in experiments with single-photon sources. For example, if two photons from such sources are sent to a beam splitter (half-silvered mirror), the detectors at the outputs will never click simultaneously when the photons are perfectly identical. This is used to test the degree of indistinguishability of photons and in quantum communication for security.

💡 The 'Hong–Ou–Mandel dip' is so sensitive that it allows measuring delays of just a few femtoseconds (millionths of a billionth of a second), turning it into a tool for ultrafast synchronization.
In our ordinary world, if two particles strike a half-silvered mirror from opposite sides, each can be reflected or transmitted independently, sometimes ending up on different sides. But for identical photons (particles of light with identical properties, such as color and arrival time), quantum rules forbid that: they always exit together. This happens because photons are bosons (particles inclined to bunch up in the same state), and their quantum waves interfere (overlap like ripples on water, sometimes reinforcing, sometimes canceling) in such a way that the splitting option completely vanishes.

💡 In 2023, the Hong–Ou–Mandel effect was successfully demonstrated not only with photons but also with phonons — quanta of sound — in optomechanical systems, opening new paths for hybrid quantum technologies.
The Hong–Ou–Mandel effect is a two-photon quantum interference in which two indistinguishable photons incident on different inputs of a 50:50 beam splitter always leave together through one output. This 'bunching' is a consequence of the bosonic nature of photons and the superposition of probability amplitudes, leading to destructive interference for the outcome with one photon in each output. The effect is the simplest non-classical interference and a cornerstone of linear optical quantum computing.

Discovery

The effect was first experimentally observed in 1987 by Chung Ki Hong, Zhe-Yu Ou, and Leonard Mandel at the University of Rochester. They investigated two-photon interference using photon pairs generated by spontaneous parametric down-conversion. In the experiment, they varied the path length of one photon, introducing a time delay, and measured the coincidence count rate, which showed a characteristic dip to zero at zero delay, confirming full quantum interference. This built upon earlier theoretical work by Robert Hanbury Brown and Richard Twiss on boson bunching, but added a unique two-particle interference signature.

How it works

The effect is used to characterize single-photon sources and implement quantum logic gates. It is critical for quantum teleportation, quantum key distribution, and linear optical quantum computing. The degree of photon indistinguishability is quantified by the visibility of the Hong–Ou–Mandel dip. The effect requires mode matching: photons must be indistinguishable in frequency, polarization, spatial mode, and arrival time.

Caveats

Perfect photon indistinguishability in practice is difficult due to temporal and spectral instabilities of single-photon sources.; The effect is sensitive to losses and noise in optical circuits, complicating the scaling of quantum computing.; The interpretation of the effect within hidden variable theories and realist theories is still debated in the context of the foundations of quantum mechanics.

|1,1\rangle \xrightarrow{BS} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|2,0\rangle - |0,2\rangle)
|1,1\rangle — initial state with one photon in each of the two inputs; BS — 50:50 beam splitter; |2,0\rangle and |0,2\rangle — states with two photons in the first or second output. This superposition means that the photons always end up together, but which output is random.
P_{coin}(\tau) = \frac{1}{2}\left[1 - e^{-\frac{(\Delta\omega \tau)^2}{2}}\right]
P_{coin} — probability of two detectors clicking simultaneously (coincidences); \tau — time delay between the arrival of photons at the beam splitter; \Delta\omega — frequency bandwidth of the photons (characterizes their spectral purity). For \tau=0 and perfect indistinguishability, P_{coin}=0.
