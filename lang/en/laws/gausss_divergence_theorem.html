Gauss's theorem (divergence theorem) is a fundamental statement of vector calculus: the integral of the divergence of a vector field F over a volume V equals the flux of this field through the closed surface ∂V bounding the volume: ∮_{∂V} F·dS = ∫_V (∇·F) dV. In physics, it appears as Gauss's law for electrostatics (∮ E·dA = Q/ε₀) and for gravity (∮ g·dA = -4πGM), linking field fluxes with sources (charges, masses). The theorem applies to smooth vector fields in any dimension and serves as a bridge between differential and integral forms of conservation laws.

Discovery

Carl Friedrich Gauss used this result in 1813 in the theory of magnetic potentials, and in 1828 Mikhail Ostrogradsky gave the first general proof. George Green also derived related theorems. Gauss introduced the theorem into physics, formulating the law for electric fields in 1835. Later, James Clerk Maxwell included Gauss's law in his system of equations, and in the 20th century, the theorem found application in relativity and quantum field theory.

How it works

Gauss's theorem allows calculating fields of high symmetry (spheres, cylinders, planes) without complex integration. It is used in engineering calculations of electrostatic shields, gravitational lenses, and in fluid dynamics. Limitation: requires differentiability of the field and smoothness of the surface; for regions with discontinuities (e.g., on charged plates), boundary conditions are used.
