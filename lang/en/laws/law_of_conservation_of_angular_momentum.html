Every rotating object has a property called angular momentum. It depends on mass, rotation speed, and distance from the axis. If no external twisting forces are applied, this property does not change. When a rotating figure decreases the distance to the axis (arms to the body), the rotation speed must increase to conserve angular momentum.

Law of conservation of angular momentum: in a closed system, the vector sum of the angular momenta of the particles does not change over time. Mathematically: dL/dt = τ_ext, where τ_ext is the resultant external torque. If τ_ext = 0, then L = const. Angular momentum of a single particle: L = r × p.

Discovery

Johannes Kepler formulated his second law (1609) — the radius vector of a planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times, which is a consequence of conservation of angular momentum. Isaac Newton in the 'Principia' derived the conservation from the laws of motion and gravity. In 1915, Emmy Noether proved a general theorem linking symmetries and conservation laws, in particular, the isotropy of space to the conservation of angular momentum.

How it works

Used everywhere: from designing gyrostabilized platforms to analyzing elementary particle collisions. Boundaries: the classical form is valid as long as quantum and relativistic corrections can be neglected. In general relativity, global conservation of angular momentum is not always well-defined due to the absence of global symmetry.

Caveats

In general relativity, the law has no global formulation, only a local one; The electron spin paradox: with a rotational interpretation of spin angular momentum, causality is violated; Quantum fluctuations of the vacuum can transfer angular momentum, changing the state of microsystems

\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}
L — angular momentum vector (kg·m²/s), r — radius vector from the chosen point to the particle (m), p — momentum of the particle (kg·m/s), × — cross product.
L = I \omega
I — moment of inertia about the axis of rotation (kg·m²), ω — angular velocity (rad/s).
