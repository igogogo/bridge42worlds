law of conservation of energylaw

Energy does not disappear and does not appear out of nothing — it only changes costumes, turning from one form to another.

In practice: Without this law, power plants would not work: the chemical energy of coal turns into heat, then into electricity. In every battery and solar panel, its action is present.
In the mid-19th century, several scientists independently concluded that heat is not a mysterious fluid but a form of particle motion. Julius Mayer, observing the color of sailors' blood, noticed a connection between the body's work and heat. James Joule, through precise experiments, measured how much heat mechanical work produces. Hermann von Helmholtz formalized it into a coherent theory. The law of conservation of energy states: in an isolated system, total energy — the sum of kinetic, potential, thermal, and other forms — is constant. It's like a family budget: income and expenses can change form, but the total amount in the account is preserved if no external source intervenes.
💡 Thanks to this law, we know exactly: the flap of a butterfly's wing does not disappear without a trace, but dissipates in the chaos of thermal motion of air.
The law says: in a closed system (where nothing enters from outside and nothing leaves), the total amount of energy is always the same. Energy can change from one form to another — like water poured from a jug into a glass remains water. A swing: at the top point it is motionless and full of potential energy (position energy), at the bottom it rushes — kinetic energy (motion energy) is maximum, and the sum of these two energies is constant if friction is ignored.

How it works

This law explains why it is impossible to create a perpetual motion machine: some energy always turns into heat. A light bulb heats up because not all electrical energy turns into light. When a stone falls, potential energy of height becomes kinetic, and on impact — sound and heat.

💡 According to one hypothesis, the total energy of the Universe is zero: the positive energy of matter is exactly compensated by negative gravitational energy. This elegant assumption has not yet been proven.
The law of conservation of energy is a fundamental principle of physics, stating that the total energy of a closed system remains constant over time. Energy is neither created nor destroyed, but only transformed from one form to another. In classical mechanics, this is expressed as the constancy of the sum of kinetic and potential energy: E = K + U = const. In thermodynamics — by the first law: ΔU = Q − W, where the change in internal energy equals the heat added minus the work done by the system. Noether's theorem links this law to symmetry: the invariance of physical laws with respect to time translation leads to energy conservation.

Discovery

The idea of the indestructibility of 'vis viva' (mv²) goes back to Leibniz in the 17th century. However, until the mid-19th century, heat was considered a special substance — caloric. Physician Julius Mayer in 1842 was the first to formulate the mechanical equivalent of heat, observing people in the tropics. James Joule in 1843–1847 experimentally proved the conversion of mechanical work into heat. Hermann von Helmholtz in 1847 gave a mathematical formulation for all types of energy. Emmy Noether in 1918 revealed the deep connection between symmetries and conservation laws. Albert Einstein extended the law to the relativistic domain, including mass-energy equivalence (E=mc²).

How it works

The law is applied in all branches of physics and engineering: from calculating planetary trajectories to designing heat engines and nuclear reactors. Its boundaries are indicated by general relativity, where gravitational field energy is non-local, and quantum mechanics, where energy fluctuations are possible on short intervals (uncertainty principle). However, in the macroscopic world and in closed systems it is absolute.

Caveats

The problem of dark energy: the accelerating expansion of the Universe requires an energy source with negative pressure, whose nature is unknown.; In general relativity, the law of energy conservation is not local in nature, causing debates about its universality.; Quantum fluctuations of energy at Planck length scales raise the question of global energy conservation in the early Universe.

E = K + U = \text{const}
E is total mechanical energy; K is kinetic energy (energy of motion), for a point mass K = mv²/2, where m is mass, v is velocity; U is potential energy (position energy, depending on the system configuration, e.g., in gravity U = mgh, where g ≈ 9.8 m/s² is the acceleration of free fall, h is height).
\Delta U = Q - W
ΔU is the change in internal energy of the system; Q is the amount of heat added to the system; W is the work done by the system against external forces (in some conventions, the sign may be different). For an isolated system, Q = 0, W = 0, therefore ΔU = 0, i.e., internal energy is constant.
