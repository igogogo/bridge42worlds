In a vacuum, two closely spaced metal plates attract each other, as if emptiness itself pushes them together. This is not magic, but a consequence of quantum fluctuations.

In practice: In nanomachines, the Casimir force can cause moving parts to stick, so engineers must compensate for it.
In 1948, the Dutch physicist Hendrik Casimir, based on quantum electrodynamics, predicted that two perfectly conducting parallel plates in a vacuum would attract each other. The vacuum, it turns out, is not empty: it is a sea of virtual photons continuously popping in and out of existence. Between the plates, only certain wavelengths can exist, like standing waves on a fixed string. The smaller number of allowed modes inside compared to the outside space creates a net pressure that pushes the plates together. The force strongly depends on distance: halve the gap, and the force increases 16-fold. The first precise measurement of the effect was conducted in 1997.

How it works

In everyday life, the Casimir force is too weak to notice, but at the scale of microchips, it can cause parts to clamp. It is also studied in laboratories to investigate the quantum vacuum, and ultrasensitive sensors are being developed based on this principle.

Even in complete emptiness, quantum mechanics says that energy cannot be strictly zero. There is always a faint jitter of electromagnetic fields that appear and vanish, like a fizzing foam. The Casimir effect is a physical force arising from the modification of zero-point energy of the quantized electromagnetic field between two closely spaced uncharged conducting surfaces. Formally, it is derived by summing the allowed electromagnetic modes between the plates and subtracting the infinite vacuum energy of free space, yielding a finite attractive force per unit area. This effect proves that vacuum fluctuations have measurable consequences and is a pure consequence of quantum field theory.

Discovery

Hendrik Casimir derived the effect in 1948 while working on colloidal solutions at Philips Research Laboratories. He initially discussed the idea with Niels Bohr, who suggested a connection to zero-point energy. Casimir, together with Dirk Polder, first explained the force between a perfectly conducting plate and an atom, and then generalized it to two plates. The first quantitative measurements were performed by Marcus Sparnaay in 1958, but decisive precise experiments became possible later with the advent of atomic force microscopy and MEMS technologies.

How it works

The Casimir force is significant at submicron distances and affects the operation of micro- and nanoelectromechanical systems (MEMS/NEMS), where it can cause stiction. It is also used in precision force measurements, tests of fundamental physics (e.g., searches for extra dimensions), and studies of quantum friction. The effect is limited to conditions of nearly parallel plates and distances much smaller than the thermal wavelength.

Caveats

Calculating the force for real materials with finite conductivity and roughness requires complex numerical methods.; At very small distances (<100 nm), the perfect conductor approximation breaks down, and corrections for the plasma frequency of metals are needed.; Is it possible to create a stable repulsive configuration for levitation? Experiments are still limited.; How does gravity affect vacuum energy? The Casimir effect may be a key to the cosmological constant problem.
