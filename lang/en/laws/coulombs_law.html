Coulomb's lawlaw

Coulomb's law is a rule that explains with what force electric charges attract or repel. Imagine you have two magnets: if you bring them together with like poles, they repel, and with opposite poles, they attract. The same with charges: like (plus-plus or minus-minus) repel, unlike (plus-minus) attract. The force depends on the magnitude of the charges and the distance between them: the larger the charges and the closer they are, the stronger the interaction.
💡 Coulomb's law is surprisingly similar to Newton's law of universal gravitation! In both cases, the force depends on distance in the same way: inversely proportional to the square of the distance. Only in gravity the force always attracts, while in electricity it can also repel.
Coulomb's law was discovered by French engineer Charles Coulomb in 1785 using a torsion balance. He established that the force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of their magnitudes and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. An interesting analogy: imagine a fountain where water jets spray in all directions. The intensity of the spray at a distance decreases inversely with the area of the sphere, i.e., the square of the distance. Electric interaction 'sprays out' similarly: it weakens with increasing distance by the same law.

How it works

This law is the foundation of all electrostatics. It explains why electrons are held around the nucleus in a hydrogen atom, how capacitors work, and why we feel a spark when taking off a sweater. For example, in hydrogen, the attractive force between a proton and an electron is computed by Coulomb's law, giving an estimate of the atom's size.

💡 Coulomb used the same torsion balance that Henry Cavendish used to measure the gravitational constant. In fact, Coulomb's law became the first precise quantitative law in electricity and served as the foundation for the future theory of electromagnetism.
Coulomb's law is a fundamental law of electrostatics describing the interaction force between two stationary point electric charges in vacuum. Strict formulation: the magnitude of the electrostatic force between two point charges in vacuum is directly proportional to the product of the magnitudes of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. The force is directed along the line joining the charges, with like charges repelling and unlike charges attracting. This law is one of the basic ones in classical electrodynamics and is generalized by Maxwell's equations.

Discovery

The law was formulated by Charles-Augustin de Coulomb in 1785 based on experiments with a torsion balance. Earlier, a similar dependence for magnetic interactions was suggested by John Robison and Henry Cavendish, but Coulomb was the first to publish results for electric charges. Later, the law was incorporated by James Clerk Maxwell into his system of equations, becoming part of classical electrodynamics. The experimental accuracy of the law has been confirmed to a high degree, and its form is analogous to Newton's law of gravitation.

How it works

Coulomb's law applies to point charges or spherically symmetric charge distributions in electrostatics. It allows calculation of electric fields and potentials, underlies capacitors, motion of charged particles in accelerators, and many other phenomena. In atomic physics, it describes the attractive force between the nucleus and electrons, although quantum mechanics is needed for an accurate description of atoms. Limits of applicability: the law is valid in inertial reference frames for stationary charges; when charges move, magnetic effects and retardation must be considered.

Caveats

Coulomb's law requires the concept of a point charge, which is an idealization; real charges have sizes and can polarize.; At very small distances (less than ~10^-15 m) quantum effects appear, and Coulomb's law ceases to be exact.; In a medium, interaction is weakened due to polarization, requiring the introduction of dielectric permittivity.; Compatibility with relativity: for moving charges, a transition to a covariant formulation within electrodynamics is necessary.
