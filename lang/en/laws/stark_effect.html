Stark effecteffect

An electric field shifts and splits the colors of atoms, just as tuning a radio shifts the frequency. Optical modulators in fiber optics work on this principle.

In practice: Optical modulators, semiconductor research, quantum-confined structures, spectral analysis of electric fields in plasma.
In 1913, German physicist Johannes Stark discovered that in a strong electric field, hydrogen lines split and shift. Unlike the Zeeman effect, where the magnetic field breaks rotational symmetry, the electric field directly polarizes the atom. Classical physics could not explain the magnitude of the splitting; success came with quantum theory, where for hydrogen the shift depends linearly on the field (linear Stark effect). In multi-electron atoms, the interaction is usually quadratic.
💡 Near high-voltage power lines, electric fields are so strong that they cause a measurable shift in the spectral lines of air glow — the Stark effect can be seen in the lab without touching the wires.
When an atom is placed in an external electric field, its energy levels shift and can split into several close ones. This happens because the electric field deforms the atom's electron cloud (polarizes it), creating a small dipole that interacts differently with the field depending on the electron's state. The stronger the field, the more noticeable the shift.

How it works

In fiber-optic communication lines, an electrical signal controls the shift of levels in a crystal, changing light absorption or refraction. In astrophysics, the Stark broadening of lines in stellar atmospheres is used to estimate the density of charged particles.

💡 One method of trapping ultracold molecules is based on the Stark effect: molecules are 'locked' in an alternating electric field trap thanks to the induced dipole moment.
The Stark effect is the change in energy levels of atoms under the influence of an external electric field. For hydrogen-like atoms in the linear approximation, the energy shift of a level with principal quantum number n and parabolic quantum numbers n₁, n₂, m (n = n₁ + n₂ + m + 1) is: ΔE = (3/2) e a₀ n (n₁ - n₂) E, where e is the elementary charge (≈ 1.602×10⁻¹⁹ C), a₀ is the Bohr radius (≈ 5.292×10⁻¹¹ m), E is the electric field strength. For non-hydrogenic systems, a quadratic Stark effect is usually observed due to the lack of degeneracy in orbital angular momentum.

Discovery

Johannes Stark discovered the effect in 1913 by observing the splitting of hydrogen Balmer series lines in a strong electric field. The quantum mechanical explanation was given by Paul Epstein and Karl Schwarzschild in 1916, using separation of variables in the Schrödinger equation in parabolic coordinates. Subsequently, the effect became an important tool for plasma diagnostics and the development of optoelectronic devices.

How it works

The effect is used for light modulation in electro-optic modulators, where applied voltage controls absorption in semiconductor structures (quantum-confined Stark effect). In spectroscopy, Stark broadening of lines determines the concentration of charged particles in plasma. Limits of applicability: in strong fields, ionization occurs (fields above 10⁸ V/m), in weak fields, the effect is masked by Doppler broadening.

Caveats

Accurate calculation of the quadratic Stark effect for complex atoms requires accounting for electron correlations.; In inhomogeneous fields (e.g., near electrode surfaces), interpretation becomes more complicated.; The dynamic Stark effect in alternating fields is the basis of laser cooling, but modeling for multilevel systems remains computationally challenging.

\Delta E = \frac{3}{2} e a_0 n (n_1 - n_2) E
