Imagine a stove: the stronger you heat it, the more heat it radiates — that's intuitive. But the Stefan–Boltzmann law says that the radiated power increases not just a lot, but as the fourth power of temperature! That is, if you heat an iron from 300°C to 600°C (twice as hot on the absolute scale), it will radiate 16 times more heat. Analogy: it's like having a garden hose — you turn the tap slightly, and the water pressure increases dramatically. So it is with light and heat from a heated body.

How it works

Have you ever warmed your hands over a campfire? The hotter the fire, the stronger the heat sensation — the law in action. In science, it is used in astrophysics to determine the luminosity of stars from their temperature and radius: knowing the star's temperature (e.g., from Wien's law) and its size, you can calculate how much energy it radiates, and compare with the observed brightness. Also, pyrometers — devices that measure temperature by radiation — are based on this law, as well as the thermal design of spacecraft.

💡 Thanks to this law, we understand why the Sun, while not so large by cosmic standards, shines more than 400 times brighter than its entire surface would if it had the temperature of a human body! If the Sun cooled to 4000°C instead of the current 5500°C, we would receive half as much light — life on Earth would be different. And an incandescent light bulb shines dimly precisely because its filament only heats up to 2500°C — if it were like the Sun, it would instantly evaporate.
The Stefan–Boltzmann law was established empirically by Josef Stefan in 1879 from the analysis of experiments by Dulong and Petit, and five years later Ludwig Boltzmann gave a theoretical derivation from thermodynamics and Maxwell's electromagnetic theory, linking radiation pressure to energy density. The essence is simple: the total energy emitted by a unit area of a black body per unit time is proportional to the fourth power of its absolute temperature. This power-law dependence works throughout the Universe — from a candle flame to giant stars.

And Boltzmann, who loved atomism, showed that this law is a consequence of light pressure, which was a bold idea for the time.
Stefan–Boltzmann law: the total radiant exitance (integrated flux of radiation per unit area) of a black body is j = σ T^4, where σ ≈ 5.67×10⁻⁸ W/(m²·K⁴) is the Stefan–Boltzmann constant. The law was empirically found by J. Stefan in 1879 and theoretically justified by L. Boltzmann in 1884 based on classical thermodynamics and electrodynamics, using the relationship between radiation pressure and energy density. In modern physics, the law is derived from Planck's distribution by integrating over all frequencies, which automatically gives an expression for σ in terms of fundamental constants.

Discovery

Irish physicist John Tyndall conducted measurements of thermal radiation in the 1860s, which led Stefan to analyze the data of Dulong and Petit. Josef Stefan, an Austrian physicist of Slovenian origin, published a note in 1879 in which he claimed that the radiation from a platinum wire is proportional to the fourth power of its absolute temperature. Ludwig Boltzmann, impressed by the insight, undertook a rigorous proof: in 1884, he considered radiation as a working substance in a Carnot cycle and derived the law from the second law of thermodynamics, using the light pressure discovered by James Clerk Maxwell. Later, in 1900, Max Planck, in deriving his famous formula, confirmed and generalized this law, showing that σ can be expressed through Planck's constant, the speed of light, and Boltzmann's constant.

How it works

The Stefan–Boltzmann law is widely applied in astrophysics to determine the luminosities of stars, as well as to calculate the energy balance of planets, including Earth: knowing the solar constant and albedo, one can estimate the planet's equilibrium temperature. In engineering, the law is used in designing furnaces, steam boilers, as well as in infrared sensors and thermal imagers. Limits of applicability: the law holds strictly only for black bodies or gray bodies with a constant emissivity; for real bodies, a correction factor (emissivity) is introduced. At very high temperatures, when effects of general relativity become noticeable (e.g., near black holes), the calculation of energy release requires modifications.

Caveats

The law is derived for thermodynamically equilibrium radiation; it is not applicable in non-equilibrium conditions (e.g., laser radiation).; The fourth-power dependence means a very rapid increase in radiation with temperature: small errors in temperature measurement lead to large errors in flux calculations.; For real bodies, it is necessary to know the spectral emissivity, which itself can depend on temperature and surface condition, complicating accurate measurements.
