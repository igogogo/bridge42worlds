Pauli exclusion principleprinciple

The Pauli exclusion principle is a rule of the quantum world that forbids two particles from being in the same state at the same time. Imagine a theater where each seat has a unique row and seat number. Fermion spectators (such as electrons) cannot sit in the same seat together: each needs its own. Thanks to this, atoms do not collapse, and chemical elements differ from each other.
💡 If it weren't for the Pauli principle, all electrons in an atom would gather in the lowest orbital, and then there would be no chemistry, no life — all atoms would be like inert gases.
In 1925, Wolfgang Pauli formulated the exclusion principle to explain the structure of atomic spectra. The essence is simple: in a quantum system, two particles with half-integer spin (fermions) cannot be in the same quantum state. Behind the ornate formulation lies a deep idea: quantum states can be imagined as "apartments" with different sets of "keys" (quantum numbers), and no more than one fermion can be placed in one apartment. This is radically different from the behavior of bosons, which can "crowd" into one state.

How it works

The principle works wherever there are fermions: in atoms, metals, white dwarf stars. In an atom, electrons fill orbitals sequentially, and this defines the Periodic Table. In metals, thanks to the Pauli principle, electrons are distributed by energy, creating degenerate gas pressure that resists the compression of white dwarfs.

💡 Despite the name "exclusion principle," it does not prohibit but rather prescribes a pattern. Pauli did not immediately understand what mathematical property explains this prohibition; later it turned out that the wave function of a system of fermions must be antisymmetric.
The Pauli exclusion principle (Pauli's exclusion principle) is a fundamental principle of quantum mechanics stating that two or more identical fermions (particles with half-integer spin) cannot simultaneously occupy the same quantum state within a single quantum system. Mathematically, this is expressed by the antisymmetry of the wave function: when two fermions are swapped, the wave function changes sign. For bosons, by contrast, the wave function is symmetric. The principle explains the structure of electron shells of atoms, the properties of metals and neutron stars, and also underlies Fermi–Dirac statistics.

Discovery

The principle was proposed by Wolfgang Pauli in 1925 based on the analysis of atomic spectra. He noticed that the distribution of electrons among shells could be explained by assuming that electrons are forbidden to occupy already occupied states, characterized by the fourth quantum number — spin. Later, in 1940, Pauli proved the connection of the principle with spin and statistics — the spin-statistics theorem. The principle was further developed in the works of Erwin Schrödinger and Paul Dirac, who linked it to the antisymmetry of the wave function.

How it works

The Pauli exclusion principle applies to all fermions, including electrons, protons, neutrons, and quarks. It determines the filling of electron shells, explains the chemical properties of elements, the electrical conductivity of metals, superconductivity, and the behavior of degenerate gas in astrophysical objects. Limits of applicability: the principle is strict for fundamental fermions; in composite systems, effective bosons may arise that do not obey the prohibition, such as Cooper pairs in superconductors.

Caveats

Why the exclusion principle applies specifically to fermions — this is related to the spin-statistics theorem, but its fundamental origin remains a subject of debate.; In systems with strong interaction, quasiparticles with different statistics can form, for example, anyons in the fractional quantum Hall effect.; The exclusion principle makes neutron stars stable, but upon collapse into a black hole it is violated, indicating its limitations in singularities.; Quantum entanglement and nonlocality can lead to apparent violations of particle distinguishability, which requires a subtle analysis.
