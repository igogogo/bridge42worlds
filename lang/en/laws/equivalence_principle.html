equivalence principleprinciple

Being in an elevator falling in a vacuum is no different from free flight in weightlessness. You will not feel your own weight in both cases. The equivalence principle says: locally, gravity and acceleration are indistinguishable — that very 'happiest moment' in Einstein's life.
💡 Aircraft for training cosmonauts fly along a ballistic trajectory and allow passengers inside to experience 20–30 seconds of real weightlessness — this is a direct consequence of the equivalence principle.
History: a brilliant insight came to Einstein in 1907: if a person is freely falling, they do not feel their own weight — as if gravity had disappeared. This thought experiment led to the equivalence principle: locally, it is impossible to distinguish a homogeneous gravitational field from acceleration. In other words, inertial and gravitational masses are always equal, so a bird in an elevator that has snapped its cable floats in weightlessness. Analogy: if you are on a space cruise liner rotating to create artificial gravity, any experiment inside the ship cannot distinguish centrifugal force from real gravity.

How it works

The principle explains why an orbital station does not fall to Earth: it is constantly 'falling past' the planet, moving by inertia, and people inside are in free fall — this is a pure realization of equivalence. Inertial navigation systems operate on this principle, and GPS accounts for gravitational time dilation.

💡 Modern experiments confirm the principle to within one part in a trillion: in the satellite experiment MICROSCOPE (2017), the equality of masses was tested to a record level of 10⁻¹⁵.
The equivalence principle is the main postulate of general relativity, asserting the equivalence of gravity and acceleration. In its weak form: the so-called weak equivalence principle (WEP) states that the trajectory of a freely falling test body does not depend on its internal composition and structure, which is equivalent to the equality of inertial and gravitational mass: m_i = m_g. Einstein's equivalence principle (EEP) extends it: in any local freely falling reference frame, all non-gravitational laws of physics take the form predicted by special relativity. The strong principle (SEP) adds that the same holds for gravitational laws (except gravity itself).

Discovery

Galileo Galilei in the early 17th century showed that all bodies fall with the same acceleration (ignoring air resistance). Isaac Newton in 1687 noted the proportionality of gravitational force to acceleration through the introduction of two types of mass, but considered it a coincidence. Albert Einstein in 1907, reflecting on a man falling from a roof, realized that free fall locally eliminates weight, and formulated the equivalence principle as a basis for extending relativity to gravity. This led him to general relativity (1915). Later, the principle was repeatedly tested: from Eötvös experiments with torsion balances to the MICROSCOPE satellite.

How it works

The equivalence principle explains phenomena such as gravitational redshift, the bending of starlight near the Sun, and time dilation in a gravitational field. It is essential for the operation of satellite navigation systems (GPS, GLONASS), where relativistic corrections to clock rates are taken into account. Applicability is strictly local: in sufficiently large regions of spacetime, tidal forces appear, revealing the non-uniformity of the gravitational field.

Caveats

Is the equivalence principle a separate postulate or a consequence of GR?; Is the strong principle violated in theories with varying coupling constants (dilaton models)?; Search for a 'fifth force' — a possible deviation from the universality of free fall at very small distances; Problem of reconciling the equivalence principle with quantum field theory (quantum gravity)

m_i = m_g
m_i is the inertial mass, determining resistance to acceleration (Newton's second law), m_g is the gravitational mass, determining the force of gravitational attraction (law of universal gravitation). Experimentally, they are equal with high precision, so all bodies fall equally.
a = g
a is the acceleration of a body in the Earth's gravity field (or any gravitating body), g is the acceleration of free fall (approximately 9.8 m/s² at the Earth's surface). Acceleration does not depend on the mass and composition of the body.
