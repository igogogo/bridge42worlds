Spectral theoremtheorem

The spectral theorem is a mathematical way of 'breaking down' a complex object into simple, independent parts. Imagine you have an incomprehensible noise, and you decompose it into a sum of pure sounds of different volumes and frequencies. Similarly, any operator (a special kind of mathematical function) can be represented as a set of its eigenvalues and directions. In quantum mechanics, it is the eigenvalues of an operator that give the possible measurement results of a physical quantity — for example, the energy levels of an electron in an atom.
💡 The theorem got its 'spectral' name because its ideas first arose in the study of absorption and emission spectra of light: each color corresponds to its own frequency, and atoms as operators 'output' a discrete set of frequencies — exactly like the eigenvalues of a matrix.
In the early 20th century, mathematicians (Hilbert, Riesz, von Neumann) generalized a well-known fact from linear algebra: a symmetric matrix can be diagonalized, i.e., represented as a sum of projectors onto its eigenvectors multiplied by eigenvalues. The spectral theorem asserts that the same is true for a much broader class of operators in infinite-dimensional spaces — self-adjoint operators on a Hilbert space. It's as if you could tune a piano so that each key corresponds to a strictly defined note, and when you press any combination, you hear exactly the sum of those notes, without dissonance.

How it works

Quantum mechanics: the energy operator (Hamiltonian) via the spectral theorem gives the stationary states of an electron in an atom — Balmer series, Lyman series, etc. Any signal can be decomposed into a frequency spectrum (Fourier transform) — this is a special case of the spectral theorem for the shift operator. In data analysis, principal component analysis diagonalizes the covariance matrix, revealing the most important directions of variability.

💡 John von Neumann, at just 26 years old, gave a rigorous formulation of the spectral theorem for unbounded operators in 1929, forever changing the face of quantum physics. It is said that he did it almost playfully, between developing game theory and participating in the Manhattan Project. And the spectral theorem itself hides in every computer JPEG compression, since the Fourier transform is its direct consequence!
The spectral theorem states that every self-adjoint (Hermitian) operator A, acting on a separable Hilbert space H, admits a spectral decomposition: A = ∫_{σ(A)} λ dE(λ), where σ(A) is the spectrum (the set of eigenvalues and the continuous spectrum), and E(λ) is the spectral measure (a projection-valued function). In the finite-dimensional case: A = ∑_i λ_i P_i, where λ_i are eigenvalues, P_i are orthogonal projectors onto the corresponding eigenspaces.

Discovery

The origins of the theorem lie in the works of David Hilbert on integral equations (1904–1910). Ernst Schmidt introduced the notion of eigenfunctions. John von Neumann in 1929 gave the modern axiomatic formulation for unbounded operators, grounding the mathematical apparatus of quantum mechanics. In parallel, Stone proved the theorem on unitary representations of groups (1932), closely related to spectral theory.

How it works

A fundamental tool of quantum mechanics: each observable (energy, momentum, spin) is represented by a self-adjoint operator, and its spectral decomposition gives the possible measurement results and probabilities. In analytic mathematics, it is used to solve partial differential equations (separation of variables), in dynamical systems theory — for stability analysis. Limits: applicable only to normal or self-adjoint operators; for non-normal operators (e.g., in hydrodynamics), one uses the Jordan form or pseudospectra.

Caveats

For non-self-adjoint operators, the spectrum can be extremely unstable to perturbations, leading to phenomena not described by the spectral theorem.; In quantum field theory, operators are often not properly defined self-adjoint operators, requiring workarounds (renormalization group).; Computing the spectral measure for specific complex systems (e.g., in chaotic billiards) is an actively developing field.

A = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \lambda_i P_i
A is the self-adjoint operator (matrix); λ_i are real eigenvalues; P_i are orthogonal projectors onto eigenspaces, satisfying P_i P_j = δ_{ij} P_i and ∑_i P_i = I (identity operator).
