Any correctly posed question about a quantum system breaks down into a set of unambiguous answers, like a chord into pure notes. The spectral theorem allows you to decompose measurement to the ground.

In practice: It is precisely what allows predicting quantum measurement results, building stable search engine algorithms, and compressing images into JPEG.
Born in the depths of functional analysis through the efforts of David Hilbert and John von Neumann in the 1920s-30s, the spectral theorem became a bridge between algebra and quantum mechanics. It states that any Hermitian operator (named after Charles Hermite), describing an observable quantity, can be diagonalized: represented as a sum (or integral) over its spectral projections, each corresponding to a specific instrument value. If you have a set of color filters, then any beam can be decomposed into pure colors and then reconstructed by mixing them. The spectral theorem does the same in infinite-dimensional spaces, guaranteeing that for any observable there is a 'set of filters'—spectral projectors—allowing the decomposition of a quantum state into components with definite values.

How it works

MRI works because the energy levels of hydrogen nuclei are eigenvalues of a spin operator, and the spectral theorem guarantees precise separation of signals from different tissues. In JPEG and MP3 compression, the signal is decomposed into a basis; without the mathematical guarantee of completeness and orthogonality provided by the theorem, the decomposition would be unreliable.

💡 Without the spectral theorem, the Schrödinger equation would have remained a mathematical abstraction—it is precisely this that links the electron's wave function to the real spectrum lines we see in a rainbow or in the light of distant stars.
In quantum mechanics, any property—energy, coordinate, momentum—is described by a mathematical object called an operator (a rule that transforms one function into another). The spectral theorem says: if the operator is 'honest' (self-adjoint, i.e., its action is symmetric), it can be represented as a set of allowed values—the spectrum—and directions—eigenvectors—along which these values are measured. It's like breaking a chord into individual notes: a complex vibration is represented as a sum of simple harmonics. In the quantum world, such a procedure gives the possible instrument readings and the probabilities of obtaining them.

The spectral theorem is a central result of functional analysis, generalizing matrix diagonalization. For a bounded self-adjoint operator A on a Hilbert space H, it asserts the existence of a spectral family E(λ)—a projection-valued measure on the real axis—such that A = ∫ λ dE(λ). In quantum mechanics, this means that any observable corresponds to a self-adjoint operator, and its measurement yields one of the spectrum's eigenvalues; immediately after measurement, the system transitions to the corresponding eigenstate (projection postulate). Physical meaning: the wave function, expanded in the operator's eigenfunctions, determines the probability amplitudes of possible measurement outcomes.

Discovery

In 1906, David Hilbert introduced the concept of spectrum for integral equations and proved the first versions of the theorem for bounded operators. In the 1920s, John von Neumann generalized it to unbounded self-adjoint operators, creating a rigorous mathematical foundation for quantum mechanics (1932, 'Mathematische Grundlagen der Quantenmechanik'). In parallel, Marshall Stone in 1932 gave a proof for unitary operators. The ideas of spectral decomposition date back to the work of Joseph Fourier (1822) and Henri Poincaré, but the modern form owes to von Neumann and André Weil.

How it works

Applications span quantum physics, where the eigenvalues of the Hamiltonian determine the energy levels of atoms and molecules; control theory and signal processing (singular value decomposition); quantum chemistry (molecular orbital method). Limitations: the theorem guarantees the existence of spectral decomposition, but finding eigenvectors for complex systems can be computationally infeasible (exponential growth of dimensionality). In quantum field theory, operators are often unbounded, and self-adjointness issues are nontrivial.

Caveats

For operators in state spaces with continuous spectrum, the projection-valued measure becomes more complex: the eigenfunctions are generalized (do not belong to the Hilbert space), requiring rigged spaces.; Defining eigenstates for non-self-adjoint operators (e.g., describing dissipative systems) goes beyond the standard theorem.; In quantum gravity, the problem of time: the role of the spectral theorem in the absence of an external classical time remains a subject of discussion.
