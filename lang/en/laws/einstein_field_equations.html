How it works

In 1915, Albert Einstein, after several years of intense work and collaboration with mathematicians, presented the final equations of general relativity. Independently, they were derived by David Hilbert. Rigorous formulation: the equations establish a relationship between the Einstein curvature tensor G_{μν} and the energy-momentum tensor T_{μν}, with the possible inclusion of the cosmological constant Λ. Geometry tells matter how to move, and matter tells geometry how to curve.

Discovery

Albert Einstein developed general relativity from 1907 to 1915, attempting to reconcile special relativity with Newtonian gravity. In the crucial stage, he collaborated with mathematician Marcel Grossmann. In the autumn of 1915, Einstein presented the final form of the equations at the Prussian Academy of Sciences. David Hilbert almost simultaneously derived them from a variational principle. The first exact solution was found by Karl Schwarzschild in 1916. Later, Georges Lemaître used the equations for a cosmological model of an expanding universe, and in 1998, the discovery of accelerated expansion confirmed the importance of the cosmological constant Λ.

How it works

It applies in all cases where gravity is strong or high precision is required: calculating orbits in strong fields (perihelion of Mercury), black hole dynamics, gravitational lensing, evolution of the universe (cosmology), prediction of gravitational waves (detected by LIGO in 2015). The equations apply from the scale of astrophysical objects to the entire universe. In the classical limit of weak fields, it reduces to Newton's law of universal gravitation. Limitations: they do not account for quantum effects, which requires a theory of quantum gravity.

Caveats

Problem of quantum gravity: incompatibility with quantum mechanics at Planck scales.; Dark matter and dark energy: require introducing unknown components into T_{μν} or modification of Λ.; Singularities: solutions of the equations often lead to infinite curvature (black holes, Big Bang), indicating the incompleteness of the theory.; Problem of the cosmological constant: huge discrepancy between the theoretical value of Λ from quantum field theory and the observed value.
