gravitational lensingeffect

Massive bodies, such as galaxies, curve the space around them, like a heavy ball lying on a stretched sheet. Light rays passing nearby bend around this "dent," changing direction. As a result, we see distorted or even multiple images of distant objects — for example, a galaxy may appear as a luminous arc. This effect helps astronomers "weigh" galaxy clusters and find dark matter.
💡 Sometimes a gravitational lens creates a perfect ring — an Einstein ring. Imagine looking into a huge cosmic eye! Such rings can be almost perfectly circular if the lens, source, and observer are aligned exactly in a line.
Gravitational lensing was predicted by Einstein's general theory of relativity in 1915 and first confirmed during a solar eclipse in 1919: stars near the solar disk shifted by a tiny angle. The essence of the effect is that any mass concentration deforms spacetime, and light follows geodesic lines — the shortest paths in curved geometry. The more massive the lens and the closer the ray passes to it, the stronger the deflection.

How it works

There are three types of lensing: strong (multiple images, arcs, and rings from massive clusters), weak (barely perceptible distortion of the shapes of distant galaxies, allowing mapping of dark matter), and microlensing (brightening of a star when a massive, compact object passes in front of it). Lensing has become a powerful tool of cosmology.

💡 The first extragalactic gravitational lens — the double quasar Q0957+561 — was discovered in 1979. It turned out that the same bright nucleus produces two images separated by a huge angle due to a massive galaxy along the line of sight.
Gravitational lensing is an astrophysical phenomenon described by the equations of Einstein's general theory of relativity. Rigorous formulation: in the weak-field approximation, the deflection angle of a light ray by a point mass is given by α = 4GM/(c²r). For extended bodies, the lens equation is applied, linking the position of the source and images through the Einstein angular radius θ_E = √(4GM/c² · D_ls/(D_l D_s)). In the case of alignment, multiple images or an Einstein ring arise.

Discovery

Albert Einstein predicted the bending of light by gravity in 1915, and it was confirmed by Arthur Eddington in 1919. In 1937, Fritz Zwicky first proposed using galaxy clusters as gravitational lenses to study distant objects. Later, in the 1970s, advances in observational technology allowed the discovery of the first lenses, and Vera Rubin's work on galaxy rotation and dark matter made weak lensing a standard method in cosmology.

How it works

Gravitational lensing is used to study the distribution of dark matter (weak lensing), identify exoplanets (microlensing), and study distant galaxies thanks to brightness enhancement. Limitations of the method are related to the need for accurate modeling of the lens potential, possible parameter degeneracy, and the difficulty of separating lensed objects from chance alignments.

Caveats

Parameter degeneracy: from the shape of images it is difficult to uniquely reconstruct the mass distribution.; Microlensing requires very precise photometry and long-term monitoring.; Systematic errors in weak lensing due to asphericity and tidal effects.; Difficulty of modeling complex caustic structures in strong lenses.
