The virial theorem is a fundamental relation of statistical mechanics and classical dynamics, linking the time-averaged kinetic energy of a system of particles to the average value of the virial of forces. In general form: 2⟨T⟩ = ⟨∑_k F_k · r_k⟩. For systems with potential energy U ∝ r^{n+1} (where n = -2 for gravity or Coulomb's law) we get 2⟨T⟩ = -⟨U⟩. It holds for stable, bounded systems that have evolved sufficiently long.

Discovery

Rudolf Clausius introduced the concept of the 'virial' (from Latin vis — force) in 1870 and proved the theorem for a gas of molecules. In 1933, Fritz Zwicky applied it to the Coma cluster and found that galaxy velocities require 400 times more mass than visible — this was the first indication of dark matter. Subsequently, the theorem was generalized to relativistic systems and quantum mechanics.

How it works

Widely applied in dynamics of stellar systems, interstellar medium physics, and cosmology. It allows estimating masses of gravitationally bound objects without detailed knowledge of orbit distribution. Limits of applicability: the system must be isolated or in a stationary state; non-stationary processes (mergers, accretion) require modifications.

Caveats

Sensitivity to tidal effects and external gravitational fields — for not fully isolated systems.; Need for careful accounting of projection effects when determining radial velocities in astronomy.; Relativistic generalization for strong fields (neutron stars, black holes) is still debatable.
