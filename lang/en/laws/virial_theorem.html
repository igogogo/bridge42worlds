Stars in galaxies whirl around the center like children on a carousel. The virial theorem links the speed of stars to the force of gravity: if a galaxy weighed exactly as much as what is visible, the stars would have to fly apart. Fritz Zwicky first noticed the discrepancy and proposed invisible 'dark' mass.
💡 If you compress a gas ball by half, according to the virial theorem its temperature will rise — this is how protostars heat up until they ignite thermonuclear fusion.
For a system of particles bound by distance-dependent forces, the virial theorem establishes a simple relation between the average kinetic and potential energies: 2⟨T⟩ = -⟨U⟩ for gravitational or Coulomb potential. Derived by Rudolf Clausius in 1870 for heat, it gained astrophysical meaning after the work of Fritz Zwicky, who used it to discover a lack of visible mass in galaxy clusters.

How it works

The theorem is indispensable in astrophysics: from measured velocities of stars or gas particles one calculates the total mass of galaxies and clusters. In plasma physics it connects pressure and gravity inside stars. Even the dynamics of globular clusters and X-ray gas in clusters follow virial equilibrium.

💡 Clausius introduced the virial (from Latin vis — force) as a function of the form ∑ r·F. Today, in numerical simulations of the evolution of the Universe, verification of virial equilibrium serves as a test for code correctness: if after a galaxy collision it is violated, there is an error in the calculations.
The virial theorem is a fundamental relation of statistical mechanics and classical dynamics, linking the time-averaged kinetic energy of a system of particles to the average value of the virial of forces. In general form: 2⟨T⟩ = ⟨∑_k F_k · r_k⟩. For systems with potential energy U ∝ r^{n+1} (where n = -2 for gravity or Coulomb's law) we get 2⟨T⟩ = -⟨U⟩. It holds for stable, bounded systems that have evolved sufficiently long.

Discovery

Rudolf Clausius introduced the concept of the 'virial' (from Latin vis — force) in 1870 and proved the theorem for a gas of molecules. In 1933, Fritz Zwicky applied it to the Coma cluster and found that galaxy velocities require 400 times more mass than visible — this was the first indication of dark matter. Subsequently, the theorem was generalized to relativistic systems and quantum mechanics.

How it works

Widely applied in dynamics of stellar systems, interstellar medium physics, and cosmology. It allows estimating masses of gravitationally bound objects without detailed knowledge of orbit distribution. Limits of applicability: the system must be isolated or in a stationary state; non-stationary processes (mergers, accretion) require modifications.

Caveats

Sensitivity to tidal effects and external gravitational fields — for not fully isolated systems.; Need for careful accounting of projection effects when determining radial velocities in astronomy.; Relativistic generalization for strong fields (neutron stars, black holes) is still debatable.

2 \langle T \rangle = \langle \sum_k \mathbf{F}_k \cdot \mathbf{r}_k \rangle
⟨T⟩ — time-averaged kinetic energy of the particle system, F_k — net force acting on the k-th particle, r_k — radius vector of the k-th particle, sum over all particles of the system; angle brackets denote averaging over a sufficiently long time interval.
2 \langle T \rangle = - \langle U \rangle
⟨T⟩ — average kinetic energy, ⟨U⟩ — average potential energy of gravitational (or Coulomb) interaction; the relation holds for stable bound systems obeying the inverse-square law.
