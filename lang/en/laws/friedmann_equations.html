Imagine that the Universe is a giant balloon with galaxies living on its surface. The Friedmann equations are instructions for how this balloon inflates or deflates over time. They say: 'If you know how much matter and energy is inside, you can predict how fast the balloon expands, and even find out whether it will expand forever or collapse back.' These equations are the foundation of modern ideas about the Big Bang.

How it works

The equations work in cosmology, describing the evolution of a homogeneous and isotropic Universe. For example, according to modern data, the Universe is expanding with acceleration, which in the equations reflects the presence of dark energy (lambda term). They are used to calculate the age of the Universe, its composition, and the geometry of space.

💡 Alexander Friedmann himself was a mathematician and meteorologist. He loved solving complex equations and even participated in launching stratospheric balloons. His boldness in applying general relativity to the entire Universe initially displeased Einstein, who at the time believed in an eternal and unchanging Universe.
In 1922, Russian physicist Alexander Friedmann found non-stationary solutions to Einstein's equations, showing that the Universe can expand or contract. Imagine a herd of antelope fleeing a watering hole: their speed depends on how frightened they are (energy density) and on the features of the landscape (curvature of space). The equations relate the rate of change of the scale factor of the Universe to matter density, curvature, and the cosmological constant. They became the mathematical foundation of the Big Bang theory.

💡 Friedmann published his work in the journal Zeitschrift für Physik, and later the Belgian priest Georges Lemaître independently obtained the same solutions and linked them to observations of galactic redshifts, laying the foundation for Hubble's law.
The Friedmann equations are a system of two differential equations derived from Einstein's equations under the assumption of homogeneity and isotropy of the Universe (Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric). The first equation relates the square of the Hubble parameter H = ȧ/a to the energy density ρ, spatial curvature k/R^2, and cosmological constant Λ: H^2 = (8πGρ/3) − (kc^2/a^2) + (Λc^2/3). The second equation describes the acceleration: ä/a = −(4πG/3)(ρ + 3p/c^2) + Λc^2/3. They determine the dynamics of the scale factor a(t). Meaning: the geometry and evolution of the Universe are dictated by its content — matter, radiation, and dark energy.

Discovery

Alexander Friedmann in 1922 first found non-stationary solutions of general relativity, predicting the expansion of the Universe. Einstein initially rejected them but later acknowledged their correctness. Georges Lemaître independently derived the same equations in 1927 and was the first to link them with astronomical observations, pointing out a linear relationship between speed and distance. In 2011, Saul Perlmutter, Brian Schmidt, and Adam Riess proved accelerated expansion, confirming the role of the Λ term.

How it works

The Friedmann equations serve as the basis for the standard ΛCDM cosmological model. They allow, knowing the current parameters (Hubble constant, fractions of matter, radiation, and dark energy), to reconstruct the history of the Universe from the Big Bang to the future. The limit of applicability is scales greater than ~100 Mpc, where the Universe is indeed homogeneous; on smaller scales, the structure is inhomogeneous, and perturbations must be taken into account.

Caveats

The cosmological constant problem: why is the observed Λ so small compared to the predictions of quantum field theory?; The nature of dark matter and dark energy: the equations indicate their existence, but we do not know what they are.; The unknown initial conditions of the Big Bang: what set the initial singularity and was there one?; Possible inhomogeneity on large scales: could the accelerated expansion be due to a local void?
