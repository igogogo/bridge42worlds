law of conservation of momentumlaw

Momentum is like money in a closed system: you can move it from pocket to pocket, but the total does not disappear. Motion does not vanish, but is only redistributed among bodies.

In practice: Reactive motion: a rocket flies forward, throwing out combustion products backward. Automotive crash tests: deformation zones stretch the collision over time, reducing peak forces on passengers.
The law was formulated by Newton in the Principia (1687) based on the work of Wallis, Wren, and Huygens on collisions. It states: if no external forces act on a system, total momentum is conserved. The reason is the homogeneity of space: the laws of physics do not depend on where you are, and from this symmetry (by Noether's theorem) follows conservation of momentum. Analogy: on perfectly smooth ice, a skater who throws a backpack will move in the opposite direction — without external friction, the motion of the system's center of mass does not change.
💡 In open space, if an astronaut throws a wrench away from themselves, they will start drifting slowly in the opposite direction, while the wrench flies forward. Retrieving the wrench without assistance is impossible — momentum is already divided.
Momentum is a measure of motion: mass times velocity (p = m·v). In a closed system (where there are no external forces, e.g., in space far from planets), the total momentum of all bodies remains constant. When billiard balls collide, their total momentum before and after the collision is the same, although each ball changes its speed and direction.

How it works

A figure skater pushes off the barrier: she gains momentum in one direction, while the rink together with the Earth — negligibly small due to the huge mass — in the other. In a firework, the gunpowder gases fly downward, and the rocket upward. When shooting, the recoil of the weapon is a consequence of the law: the bullet and the rifle receive equal but opposite momentum.

💡 In 2005, the Deep Impact probe shot a 370-kilogram copper impactor at comet Tempel 1 at a speed of 10.2 km/s. The transferred momentum was so small compared to the comet's mass (~10^13 kg) that its orbit changed by only a fraction of a millimeter per second — conservation law in action on cosmic scales.
The principle of conservation of momentum: in an inertial reference frame, the total momentum of a closed system of bodies does not change over time. For a system of material points, ∑pᵢ = const if the sum of external forces is zero. From it follow the laws of impact, reactive motion, and it explains recoil. At the micro level, it is also conserved in quantum mechanics — through the momentum operator. In the relativistic case, the 4-vector of energy-momentum is used, but the law remains fundamental. Together with the laws of conservation of energy and angular momentum, it forms the basis of classical dynamics.

Discovery

In the mid-17th century, John Wallis, Christopher Wren, and Christiaan Huygens independently derived rules for elastic and inelastic collisions, introducing the concept of 'quantity of motion'. René Descartes earlier considered mv as a measure of motion, but mistakenly thought it was a scalar. Isaac Newton in the Principia (1687) generalized these results and linked conservation of momentum to the third law. Emmy Noether in 1918 proved that the law is a consequence of the homogeneity of space — a symmetry inherent in our Universe.

How it works

Used in ballistics (calculating trajectories of projectiles), astronautics (Tsiolkovsky rocket equation), and the design of passive safety features in cars. Limits of applicability: the system must be closed — in practice, friction and environmental resistance need to be compensated. At speeds close to the speed of light, relativistic momentum p = γmv is used. In the quantum domain, momentum is an operator, and the law holds for the wave functions of an isolated system.

Caveats

Accounting for momentum with variable mass (rocket, watering hose) requires solving the Meshchersky differential equation.; In relativistic mechanics, momentum is not proportional to velocity, complicating the calculation of particle collisions in accelerators.; Quantum momentum does not commute with position, so particle trajectories are blurred, although the average momentum is conserved.

p = m v
p — momentum of the body (kg·m/s); m — mass of the body (kg); v — velocity of the body (m/s)
m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2
