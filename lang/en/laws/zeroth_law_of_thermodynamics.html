zeroth law of thermodynamicslaw

If a cup of hot tea touches a table, and the table touches a handle, the handle's temperature will eventually equal the tea's temperature. Thermal equilibrium is contagious.

In practice: A thermometer works precisely because it reaches equilibrium with the object being measured, and we read the common temperature.
Thermodynamics begins with the zeroth law because it was recognized later than the first and second, but turned out to be more fundamental. It was formulated by Ralph Fowler in 1931. The essence: thermal equilibrium is transitive. If coffee in a thermos and the air in the room have the same temperature, and the air in the room has the same as a book on the shelf, then the coffee and the book also 'agree' on temperature.
💡 Thermometers appeared long before formal thermodynamics. Galileo created a thermoscope as early as 1593, not knowing that it embodied the zeroth law.
The zeroth law of thermodynamics states: if two objects each have the same temperature as a third, then they have the same temperature as each other. Temperature is a measure of how 'hot' a body is, and it equalizes upon contact. This principle allows us to measure temperature with a thermometer: we wait until the thermometer heats or cools to the temperature of the patient or room.

How it works

All thermometers — mercury, electronic, infrared — rely on the zeroth law. Even when you touch a forehead and decide if there's a fever, your hand compares heat with the body.

💡 The name 'zeroth law' arose almost as a joke: the first three laws were already numbered 1-3, and the fundamental principle was left unnumbered. They had to introduce a zeroth one to avoid shifting the established numbering.
The zeroth law of thermodynamics postulates: if two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third, then they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This transitivity of equilibrium serves as the basis for introducing temperature as a state function and for the correctness of all temperature measurements.

Discovery

Although the idea of temperature is as ancient as observations of cooling, the formalization of the law occurred in the 20th century. Ralph Fowler in 1931, while discussing the principles of statistical mechanics, called this postulate 'zeroth' because it logically precedes the first and second laws, formulated earlier. The term quickly caught on.

How it works

The law underlies all thermometry and calorimetry. Without it, the concept of temperature would be undefined. Limits of applicability: the law holds for macroscopic systems in equilibrium. In non-equilibrium processes, for nanoscale objects, or in strong gravitational fields, refinements may be needed.

Caveats

The law assumes that thermal equilibrium is a state where macroscopic parameters do not change. However, quantum fluctuations in the microworld call into question the existence of strict equilibrium for small systems.; The definition of temperature is not entirely unambiguous in the presence of long-range forces or in curved spacetime.
