first law of thermodynamicslaw

Energy does not appear from nowhere and does not disappear without a trace — it only changes from one form to another. The first law of thermodynamics is like a bank account: the change in your savings equals the sum of deposits minus withdrawals. The same principle in physics: the change in internal energy of a system equals the heat added minus the work done.
💡 The human body is a heat engine: all the energy from food eaten is spent on muscle work, heat release, and stored in fat. You never get more energy than you consumed.
Discovery history: in the mid-19th century, Julius Mayer and James Joule experimentally proved that heat is a form of energy, and determined the mechanical equivalent of heat. Rudolf Clausius in 1850 mathematically expressed the law of conservation of energy for thermodynamic systems, introducing the concept of internal energy. The law states: energy neither disappears nor is created, but only transforms. An analogy with a hydraulic system: internal energy is the water level in a tank, heat is the liquid poured in, work is the liquid drained. If you pour in and drain the same amount, the level remains unchanged.

How it works

The law governs all thermal processes: car engines, refrigerators, power plants, metabolism of living cells. For example, in a steam turbine, heat from fuel combustion is converted into mechanical work, but part inevitably goes to heating the environment.

💡 It is the first law of thermodynamics that puts an end to perpetual motion machines of the first kind: it is impossible to build a machine that performs work without energy input.
The first law of thermodynamics is a fundamental law of conservation of energy for thermodynamic systems. Strict formulation: in any process occurring in an isolated system, the total energy of the system remains constant. For a closed system, the change in internal energy ΔU equals the sum of heat Q transferred to the system and work W done on the system (depending on the sign convention). Mathematically: ΔU = Q - W, where W is the work done by the system. In differential form: dU = δQ - δW, where dU is an exact differential, while δQ and δW are process functionals.

Discovery

The law was formed by the efforts of several scientists in the mid-19th century. Julius Robert von Mayer in 1842 first formulated the principle of conservation of energy based on observations of blood color in the tropics. James Prescott Joule in 1843-1845 experimentally measured the mechanical equivalent of heat, proving that heat is a form of energy. Hermann von Helmholtz in 1847 generalized the law to all forms of energy. Rudolf Clausius in 1850 introduced the concept of internal energy and gave the first rigorous mathematical formulation for heat engines.

How it works

The law is the basis of all technical thermodynamics: calculation of engine cycles, refrigerators, chemical reactions. It is applicable to macroscopic systems in a state of thermodynamic equilibrium or close to it. Limits: for relativistic processes, the mass-energy equivalence (E=mc²) must be taken into account; for microscopic systems, quantum fluctuations are important.

Caveats

The need for complete accounting of all forms of energy, including vacuum energy and dark energy; Paradoxes related to gravitational field energy in GR; Violations at quantum gravity scales, where the concepts of temperature and work may be undefined; Applicability to strongly non-equilibrium systems, where entropy is not defined

\Delta U = Q - W
ΔU is the change in internal energy of the system, Q is the amount of heat transferred to the system, W is the work done by the system against external forces. The minus sign corresponds to the convention that positive work expands the system and decreases its internal energy.
dU = \delta Q - \delta W
dU is the exact differential of internal energy (path-independent), δQ and δW are inexact differentials of heat and work, depending on the process path.
