spin–statistics theoremtheorem

In the quantum world, particles are divided into unsociable individualists and sociable party-goers — that's what the spin–statistics theorem states. A particle's character is rigidly tied to its spin: half-integer spin — 'don't like neighbors' (fermions, like electrons), integer spin — 'the more the merrier' (bosons, like photons).
💡 If electrons were bosons, all atoms would collapse into uniform lumps, and the Periodic Table would disappear.
In 1940, Wolfgang Pauli rigorously proved what was known empirically: particles with integer spin obey Bose–Einstein statistics (their wave function is symmetric under exchange), while those with half-integer spin obey Fermi–Dirac statistics (antisymmetric). The deep reason lies in relativistic quantum field theory, where locality and causality dictate this connection. Imagine an apartment building: in each apartment, only one family with a grumpy head (fermion) can live, but if sociable neighbors (bosons) move in, they happily crowd into one apartment.

How it works

The theorem explains why electrons in atoms occupy different orbitals (Pauli principle), and photons can accumulate in one state (laser effect). It is also responsible for the stability of white dwarfs (degenerate electron pressure) and for superconductivity (Cooper pairs as bosons).

💡 In a 3D universe, the exclusion principle holds for fermions, but in two-dimensional systems, exotic 'anyon' statistics are possible — neither bosonic nor fermionic.
The theorem states that in any relativistic quantum field theory with Lorentz invariance and microphysical causality, particles with integer spin are bosons (obey commutation relations, symmetric statistics), and those with half-integer spin are fermions (anticommutation relations, antisymmetric statistics). In other words, the spin–statistics connection is inevitable for preserving positivity of energy and the absence of signals outside the light cone.

Discovery

Wolfgang Pauli (1940) played a key role in the proof, building on earlier work by Markus Fierz (1939). Pauli showed that without accounting for the spin–statistics connection, quantum electrodynamics would give negative probabilities and causality violation. Later, the generality of the theorem was confirmed by Lüders and Zumino (1958) within the framework of axiomatic field theory.

How it works

In practice, the theorem determines which fields can be used to describe matter (fermionic Dirac fields) and force carriers (bosonic gauge fields). It underlies all quantum statistics, from atomic structure to early universe cosmology. Limits of applicability: strictly holds in 3+1-dimensional spacetime for free particles and under the condition of Lorentz invariance.

Caveats

In systems with broken Lorentz invariance (e.g., in condensed matter), exceptions are possible, as the theorem is not applicable.; The existence of anyons in two-dimensional systems (fractional quantum Hall effect) indicates that in dimensions below 3+1, the spin–statistics connection may weaken.; It remains an open question whether the theorem can be derived without using quantum field theory, for example, from topological considerations.

(-1)^{2s} = \begin{cases} +1, & \text{integer spin (bosons)} \\ -1, & \text{half-integer spin (fermions)} \end{cases}
s is the spin quantum number (eigenvalue of the spin operator in units of Planck's constant ħ ≈ 1.054571817×10⁻³⁴ J·s); for bosons s = 0, 1, 2, … , for fermions s = 1/2, 3/2, … . The +1 sign denotes symmetry of the wave function (Bose–Einstein statistics), –1 denotes antisymmetry (Fermi–Dirac statistics).
