When measuring a mirror's position with a laser beam, you inevitably push it with photons, blurring the result. The standard quantum limit is the minimum possible error, dictated by the laws of the quantum world, when you try to simultaneously know both the coordinate and momentum of an object.

In the 1970s, Vladimir Braginsky, analyzing the limits of measurement precision in macroscopic quantum systems, introduced the concept of the standard quantum limit. It stems from the Heisenberg uncertainty principle, but applies to continuous measurements. If you illuminate a mirror with a laser to track its position, quantum fluctuations in the photon number create radiation pressure that randomly displaces the mirror. The total coordinate error has a minimum, known as the SQL. Interestingly, this limit can be circumvented using quantum non-demolition measurements and squeezed states of light.

💡 The standard quantum limit is sometimes confused with the Heisenberg limit, but the latter is more fundamental and relates to parameter estimation, while the SQL is a limit for a specific type of measurement noise.
The standard quantum limit (SQL) for continuous coordinate measurements is defined by the minimum power spectral density of the measurement noise of the coordinate x of a free mass m, which cannot be less than √(ħ/(2mΩ²)) in the optimal measurement limit. More precisely, for a freely evolving particle, the coordinate error accumulated over the measurement time τ is bounded below as Δx SQL = √(ħτ/m). This limit relates the uncertainty in coordinate measurement to the back-action caused by quantum fluctuations of the measuring device. The SQL is not a fundamental quantum limit in the sense that it can be surpassed using quantum-correlated or squeezed states of the probe field, as proposed by Braginsky and realized in LIGO experiments using squeezed light.

Discovery

Vladimir Braginsky in 1967 first pointed out the quantum sensitivity limit in gravitational wave antennas. Further development of the SQL theory is associated with the work of Carlton Caves, who formalized the concept of the standard quantum limit in the 1980s. Experimental surpassing of the SQL was achieved at LIGO in collaboration with Caroline McKenzie and others, where using squeezed states of light improved sensitivity by 3 dB. Key contributions to understanding and overcoming the SQL were also made by Yu. I. Vorontsov and Kip Thorne.

How it works

SQL limits the precision of interferometric measurements, such as gravitational wave detectors, atomic force microscopes, and optical traps. For free masses, the limit depends on the measurement time: Δx SQL ~ √(ħτ/m). For a harmonic oscillator, there is a resonant regime where the SQL is reached when measuring amplitude. Overcoming the SQL is possible using quantum non-demolition measurements (measuring only one quadrature without disturbing the other) or squeezed states. Modern LIGO interferometers already operate below the SQL for certain frequency bands.

Caveats

The SQL is not an absolute prohibition but rather indicates a boundary achievable with coherent states; squeezed states allow it to be surpassed.; For velocity measurements, the limit is formulated differently and can be circumvented using variational measurements.; In systems with strong nonlinearity or with feedback, additional quantum effects may occur that are not described by the simple SQL.
