Kepler's first lawlaw

Planets do not circle the Sun in perfect circles — their path resembles a slightly flattened circle, an ellipse. The Sun does not sit at the center of the ellipse but is offset to one of two special points — the foci. In winter the Earth is slightly closer to the star, in summer — farther away. The difference in distance is small, but it is precisely what makes the planet accelerate and decelerate. The same elliptical dance is performed by satellites around planets and even exoplanets around distant stars.
💡 Mars gave Kepler a real quest: eight years of calculations and about forty attempts to fit its orbit to a circle failed. Having discarded the perfect circle, he obtained an ellipse — and the discrepancy with observations reduced to a couple of arcminutes. Thus the first law was born, along with the habit of not trusting old dogmas.
In the early 17th century, Johannes Kepler, processing many years of positional data from Tycho Brahe, discovered that the orbit of Mars does not lie on a circle. After many months of calculations, he dared to replace the circle with an ellipse and placed the Sun at one of the foci. An ellipse is a curve, the sum of distances from any point of which to two foci is constant. The flatness is given by the eccentricity e: for e = 0 we get a circle, for 0 < e < 1 — an ellipse. The point closest to the Sun is perihelion, the farthest is aphelion. The first law is quantitatively expressed by the polar equation of the ellipse, where r is the distance, a is the semi-major axis, θ is the angle from the direction of perihelion. The law became the empirical foundation of celestial mechanics and was later derived by Newton from the law of universal gravitation.

How it works

The law works wherever two bodies interact gravitationally and the mass of the central body significantly exceeds the mass of the satellite. It describes the orbits of planets, asteroids, comets around the Sun, the motion of satellites around planets, and the orbits of exoplanets in binary systems. Knowing the shape of the ellipse, astronomers calculate the distance to a celestial body at any moment in time. Engineers use the first law to calculate interplanetary trajectories: a flight to Mars or Jupiter is a transition from one elliptical orbit to another. Deviations from the ellipse due to the influence of other planets are small, but they are taken into account for precise navigation.

💡 The eccentricity of Earth's orbit fluctuates with a period of about 100,000 years due to the gravitational influence of Jupiter and Saturn. These Milankovitch cycles affect ice ages. And among exoplanets, extreme ellipses occur: for example, HD 20782 b has an eccentricity reaching 0.97 — the planet sometimes 'dives' almost into the star, then flies far beyond the orbit of Mars.
Kepler's first law is an empirical regularity of planetary motion, published in 1609 in the work 'Astronomia Nova'. Strict formulation: each planet revolves around the Sun in an elliptical orbit, with the Sun at one of the foci. Mathematically, the orbit is given by the equation of an ellipse in polar coordinates: r = a(1−e²)/(1 + e cos θ), where the semi-major axis a determines the size, eccentricity e — the elongation, and the angle θ (true anomaly) is measured from the direction of perihelion. The ellipse is also characterized by the focal parameter p = a(1−e²). The law describes unperturbed motion in the two-body problem and follows from the conservation of energy and angular momentum in the Newtonian gravitational potential. It applies to all objects in the Solar System and to exoplanetary systems with a dominant central mass.

Discovery

Johannes Kepler, working in Prague with the most precise observational data of his time from Tycho Brahe, focused on the mysterious behavior of Mars. After years of attempts to describe its motion with a combination of circles (epicycles), he encountered a discrepancy of 8 arcminutes — too large to dismiss as an error. Having abandoned the dogma of perfect circles, Kepler tried several oval curves and finally chose the ellipse. He placed the Sun at one of the foci, not at the center. The result was instantly confirmed by all positions of Mars. Thus in 1609 the first of three laws was born, later generalized by Newton and becoming the starting point of celestial mechanics.

How it works

The law is used to calculate ephemerides — positions of planets on the celestial sphere. In combination with the second law, it gives the complete kinematics of orbital motion. The orbit is determined by six elements, among which a and e are key. Modern applications include mission design (gravity assists, Hohmann transfers), analysis of light curves and radial velocities of exoplanets, and study of the dynamics of star clusters. The limit of applicability is weak gravitational fields and speeds far from the speed of light. Near black holes or in systems with strong relativistic corrections, the Kepler ellipse is replaced by a precessing ellipse of general relativity (Einstein effect).

Caveats

The law is exact only for the two-body problem; in real systems there are gravitational perturbations from other planets, the oblateness of the central body, and relativistic effects.; Within the framework of general relativity, the orbit is not closed — the perihelion slowly rotates, which is especially noticeable for Mercury.; The law does not provide a dynamic reason for ellipticity — it follows from the initial conditions of the planetary system's formation.; For bodies of comparable mass (binary stars), both objects move around a common center of mass, and the notion of 'focus' requires clarification.

r = \frac{a(1-e^2)}{1 + e\cos\theta}
r is the distance from the focus (Sun) to the planet at a given moment; a is the semi-major axis of the ellipse; e is the eccentricity (0 ≤ e < 1); θ is the true anomaly, the angle between the direction to perihelion and the current radius vector.
e = \frac{c}{a}
c is the distance from the center of the ellipse to the focus; a is the semi-major axis. When e = 0, the orbit is circular; as e → 1, the ellipse becomes increasingly elongated.
