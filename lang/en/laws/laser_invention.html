The idea of the maser (the microwave predecessor) belongs to Charles Townes, who, together with Arthur Schawlow, proposed in 1958 to extend the principle to light. The first working ruby laser was built by Theodore Maiman in 1960. Since then, lasers have become ubiquitous: from simple pointers to industrial cutting and scientific spectroscopy.

How it works

The laser beam transmits energy over long distances with almost no spreading. Thanks to this, we have fast internet via optical fiber, precise rangefinders (lidars), and even laser vision correction.

💡 The laser is so precise that it was used to measure the distance from Earth to the Moon with millimeter accuracy, by bouncing a beam off a mirror left by the Apollo mission astronauts.
A laser is a device that amplifies light through stimulated emission (photons 'push' atoms to emit identical photons, creating an avalanche of identical light particles). To obtain a powerful directional beam, the active medium is placed between two mirrors that repeatedly bounce the light back and forth until the gain exceeds the losses. The result is coherent radiation of a single frequency and phase.

The invention of the laser (Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation) is based on the phenomenon of stimulated emission, predicted by Albert Einstein in 1917. The physical principle: by creating a population inversion (a state where there are more atoms at the upper energy level than at the lower one), a passing photon with energy equal to the level difference triggers an avalanche of secondary photons with the same phase, frequency, and direction. For light generation, feedback is necessary — a resonator made of two mirrors, one of which is partially transparent. The threshold condition: the gain per pass must compensate for losses at the mirrors and in the medium.

Discovery

The roots of the idea — Einstein in 1917 (stimulated emission). In 1954, Charles Townes and his students built an ammonia maser. In 1958, Townes and Arthur Schawlow published a theoretical paper on the possibility of a laser. Theodore Maiman on May 16, 1960, at Hughes Research Laboratories, demonstrated the first solid-state ruby laser. Later came gas lasers, semiconductor lasers, and in the 1990s — quantum cascade lasers.

How it works

Modern lasers are classified by wavelength (from infrared to X-ray), mode (continuous, pulsed), and power. They are used in medicine (laser scalpel), transportation (laser communication), industry (welding, cutting), and science (laser cooling of atoms). The main limitation is thermal distortion of the active medium at high powers and the need for pumping.

Creating lasers with the shortest possible pulses (attoseconds).; Improving the efficiency of semiconductor lasers in the far-infrared range.; Developing powerful and compact coherent X-ray sources.
