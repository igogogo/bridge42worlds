Hubble's lawlaw

Hubble's law is a simple rule: the farther a galaxy is from us, the faster it is receding. Imagine that you are standing in the center of an exploding firecracker — the sparks fly apart, and those farther away move faster. That is exactly how the Universe behaves after the Big Bang: galaxies are carried away from each other, and this is the expansion of the Universe.
💡 Edwin Hubble, without knowing it, discovered the law while looking through the most powerful telescope at the time at Mount Wilson Observatory. Surprisingly, the first to notice this pattern in the data was not Hubble, but the Belgian priest Georges Lemaître, who even published it earlier, but his work went unnoticed.
In 1929, Edwin Hubble discovered that distant galaxies are receding from us at a speed proportional to their distance: v = H_0 · d. Analogy: imagine you are baking a raisin cake in the oven. The dough rises, and the raisins move apart — the farther apart two raisins are, the faster the distance between them grows. This law confirmed the expansion of the Universe and allowed its age to be estimated at approximately 13.8 billion years.

How it works

The law works for galaxies on all scales where cosmological expansion dominates over local gravitational interactions. It is used to measure distances to distant objects, determine the Hubble parameter, and map the large-scale structure of the Universe.

💡 The value of the Hubble constant is still being refined, and modern methods give slightly differing results: about 67 km/s/Mpc from the cosmic microwave background and about 73 km/s/Mpc from supernovae. This 'Hubble tension' is one of the main mysteries of cosmology.
Hubble's law is an empirical law stating a linear relationship between the recession velocity of a galaxy v and its distance d: v = H_0 d, where H_0 is the Hubble constant (current value around 70 km/s/Mpc). The law is a consequence of the expansion of a homogeneous and isotropic Universe, predicted by the Friedmann equations, and does not require that we are at the center of expansion — it looks the same from any point.

Discovery

In the 1910s, Vesto Slipher measured the redshifts of spiral nebulae, interpreted as recession velocities. In 1927, Georges Lemaître, based on this data and distances, derived a linear relationship. In 1929, Edwin Hubble, using his measurements of distances to galaxies via Cepheids and redshifts, published the famous 'velocity-distance' diagram, confirming the law. Milton Humason assisted with the observations.

How it works

The law is applied in cosmology to determine the scale factor and distances within the Lambda-CDM model. It is valid for velocities much less than the speed of light, where relativistic corrections can be neglected. For very distant objects, a more precise relativistic formula is used. It also requires accounting for the peculiar velocities of galaxies.

Caveats

The exact value of H_0: contradiction between local and global methods (Hubble tension).; Is H_0 constant over time? In fact, it is not a constant but a parameter that changes over the course of the Universe's evolution.; Applicability on very small scales: in galaxy clusters, the law is violated due to gravitational binding.; The need for more accurate accounting of systematic errors in distance measurements.
