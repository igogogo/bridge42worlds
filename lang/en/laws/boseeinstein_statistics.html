Bose–Einstein statisticsprinciple

If fermions are guests who absolutely refuse to share a seat, then bosons are the life of the party: they happily crowd into the same state. Bose–Einstein statistics describes such sociable particles (photons, helium-4 atoms). At very low temperatures, they can all 'freeze' at once into the lowest energy state — this is called a Bose–Einstein condensate, where many particles behave as one giant wave.
💡 The Bose–Einstein condensate was predicted by Einstein in 1925, but it was produced in the laboratory only 70 years later — in 1995, scientists cooled clouds of rubidium atoms to millionths of a degree above absolute zero, and thousands of atoms merged into a single quantum state.
In 1924, the Indian physicist Satyendra Nath Bose sent Einstein his paper, where he re-derived the blackbody radiation law by treating photons as indistinguishable 'wave-particles'. Einstein translated the article, published it, and developed the idea: any particles with integer spin must obey a similar distribution, and at low temperatures they 'condense' into the zero state. The average number of bosons in a state with energy ε is given by a formula very similar to the fermionic one, but with a minus sign in the denominator, which means bosons do not avoid, but rather 'attract' each other quantum-statistically.

How it works

Photons in a box: the spectrum of the cosmic microwave background is beautifully described by the Bose distribution. Superfluid helium: at temperatures around 2 K, liquid helium-4 flows without friction due to Bose condensation. Modern experiments with ultracold atoms allow creating 'atom lasers' and studying quantum turbulence. Even in particle physics, bosonic statistics account for lasers and superconductivity (via Cooper pairs, which behave as bosons).

💡 Satyendra Nath Bose got the idea during a lecture: he noticed that the derivation of Planck's law was based on semi-classical assumptions and decided to derive it by treating photons as fully quantum objects. Einstein was so impressed that he not only translated the paper from English to German but immediately sent it to press, and then wrote two more papers predicting condensation.
Bose–Einstein statistics describes the equilibrium distribution of identical bosons (particles with integer spin) over energy levels. The average number of particles in state i: ⟨n_i⟩ = 1 / (e^{(ε_i-μ)/(kT)} - 1), where ε_i is the energy of the state, μ is the chemical potential (for photons and phonons μ = 0), k is Boltzmann's constant, T is temperature. When (ε_i-μ) → 0, the distribution diverges, leading to macroscopic occupation of the ground state — Bose–Einstein condensation.

Discovery

In 1924, Satyendra Nath Bose derived Planck's law by assuming photons are identical and indistinguishable, and introduced a new way of counting microstates. Albert Einstein generalized the approach to particles with mass and integer spin, predicting Bose condensation (1925). The condensate was first obtained experimentally in 1995 by Eric Cornell, Carl Wieman (rubidium atoms) and Wolfgang Ketterle (sodium atoms).

How it works

It is used to describe thermal radiation (blackbody spectrum is a special case), superfluidity of helium-4, Bose–Einstein condensates of dilute gases. Limits: ideal Bose gas without interactions; accounting for interactions requires mean field (Gross–Pitaevskii equation) or more complex methods. At high temperatures, it transitions to the classical Boltzmann distribution, but with a 'bunching' effect.

Caveats

Interactions between bosons in a condensate (e.g., in helium) greatly complicate the theory and require going beyond the ideal gas.; For systems with long-range interactions or strong coupling, no exact analytical solution exists.; The statistics do not apply to fermions – one must construct a theory of Bose condensation of Cooper pairs, as in superconductivity.

\langle n_i \rangle = \frac{1}{e^{(\varepsilon_i - \mu)/(k T)} - 1}
⟨n_i⟩ is the average number of bosons in a state with energy ε_i; μ is the chemical potential (for systems with variable particle number, for photons and phonons μ = 0); k is Boltzmann's constant, k ≈ 1.380649×10⁻²³ J/K; T is absolute temperature.
