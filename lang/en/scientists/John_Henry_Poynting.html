👨‍🔬 John Henry Poynting

1852–1914 · electrodynamics, physics, astrophysics
British physicist, best known for his work on Poynting's theorem and the Poynting vector, which describe the flow of energy in an electromagnetic field. His research laid the foundation for understanding electromagnetic energy transfer and influenced the development of electrical engineering and physics. He also contributed to the study of light pressure and predicted the Poynting–Robertson effect, which explains the motion of dust particles around stars.

Biography

John Henry Poynting was born on 9 September 1852 in Monton, England. He studied at Owens College, Manchester, and Trinity College, Cambridge, where he became a fellow. From 1880, he worked as a professor of physics at Mason Science College (later the University of Birmingham), where he established a strong physics laboratory. He collaborated with J. J. Thomson and other renowned scientists. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society of London in 1888 and awarded the Royal Medal in 1905. He died on 30 March 1914.

Key discoveries

  • Poynting's theorem and the Poynting vector (description of the energy flow of the electromagnetic field, a fundamental concept in electrodynamics)
  • Poynting–Robertson effect (the drag on microscopic particles in the Solar System caused by solar light pressure)
  • Measurements of the gravitational constant and investigations into the elasticity of materials
  • Work on light pressure and the transfer of momentum by radiation
💡 Poynting was keen on astronomy and even built his own observatory in his garden; his interest in celestial mechanics led to the prediction of the cosmic dust effect.
