👨‍🔬 Ernest Rutherford

1871–1937 · Nuclear physics, Radioactivity, Atomic physics
Ernest Rutherford was a New Zealand-British physicist, 'father of nuclear physics'. He discovered alpha and beta radiation, and proposed the planetary model of the atom with a nucleus. Rutherford first achieved an artificial nuclear reaction, converting nitrogen into oxygen. His work laid the foundation for understanding atomic structure and nuclear forces.

Biography

Rutherford studied at Canterbury College (New Zealand) and the University of Cambridge (Cavendish Laboratory). He worked as a professor at McGill University (Canada) and the University of Manchester, then returned to Cambridge as director of the Cavendish Laboratory.

Key discoveries

  • Discovery of alpha and beta radiation (1899) — types of radioactive decay.
  • Planetary model of the atom (1911) — nucleus at the center, electrons orbiting around, based on gold foil experiments.
  • First artificial nuclear reaction (1919) — bombardment of nitrogen with alpha particles producing oxygen.
💡 Rutherford won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry (1908), not physics, which surprised him. He said: 'All science is either physics or stamp collecting.'
Quote: "In science there is only physics; all the rest is stamp collecting."
