Karl Schwarzschild

! scientist · # tag · @ author
1873-1916 · astrophysics, theoretical physics, general relativity
Karl Schwarzschild is a German astrophysicist who found the first exact solution to Einstein's equations of general relativity, describing the gravitational field around a spherical mass. This solution predicted the existence of an event horizon — a boundary beyond which light cannot leave a body — and laid the foundation for the theory of black holes.

Biography

Born in Frankfurt am Main into a wealthy Jewish family. Showed exceptional abilities: published his first scientific paper at the age of 16. Studied at the Universities of Munich and Strasbourg. Was director of the Potsdam Astrophysical Observatory. At the outbreak of World War I, he volunteered for the front. While in active service, he received the new theory from Einstein and within a few days derived his famous solution, sending it by letter. Soon died of pemphigus. Einstein presented his work to the Prussian Academy of Sciences, calling it outstanding.

Key discoveries

  • Schwarzschild metric and black holes: the first exact solution to Einstein's field equations for a point mass, which predicted the existence of the Schwarzschild radius — a critical size at which an object becomes a black hole.
  • Schwarzschild effect in photometry: discovered and investigated the phenomenon of reciprocity failure in photographic emulsion, which improved the accuracy of astronomical measurements of star brightness.
  • Theory of stellar atmospheres: developed radiation transfer models, laying the foundations of modern astrophysics.
💡 Schwarzschild derived the solution to Einstein's equations while in the trenches of the Eastern Front, suffering from a fatal illness. In a letter to Einstein, he wrote: 'As you see, the war has been kind to me, despite the heavy fire...' His work was completed just a few months before his death but became an immortal contribution to science.
Quote: "When gravity becomes sufficiently great, light cannot leave the star — it becomes invisible. Today we call this a black hole."
