👨‍🔬 Enrico Fermi

1901–1954 · Nuclear physics, Quantum statistics, Particle physics
Italian-American physicist, the 'architect of the nuclear age'. He built the first nuclear reactor, thus initiating controlled nuclear chain reaction. Developed Fermi-Dirac statistics for particles with half-integer spin. Made fundamental contributions to the theory of beta decay, introduced the concept of the weak interaction. Exceptionally successfully combined theoretical and experimental work.

Biography

Born in Rome. Studied at the University of Pisa. Worked in Göttingen and Leiden, then professor at the University of Rome. In 1938, due to anti-Semitic laws, emigrated to the USA, worked at Columbia University and the University of Chicago. Winner of the 1938 Nobel Prize in Physics for the discovery of new radioactive elements.

Key discoveries

  • Theory of beta decay — explained the emission of an electron from the nucleus, introducing a new particle, the neutrino, and described the weak interaction.
  • Fermi-Dirac statistics — developed a statistical description of systems of particles with half-integer spin (fermions) obeying the Pauli exclusion principle.
  • First nuclear reactor (Chicago Pile-1, 1942) — built and commissioned the world's first nuclear reactor, demonstrating a controlled chain reaction.
  • Discovery of transuranium elements — neutron bombardment of uranium led to the creation of new elements, for which he received the Nobel Prize (later it turned out that nuclear fission had been observed).
💡 Fermi was known for his ability to quickly make accurate estimates, called 'Fermi problems' — for example, estimating the energy of a nuclear explosion by eye from the movement of pieces of paper.
Quote: "Where is everybody? (Fermi paradox about extraterrestrial intelligence)."
