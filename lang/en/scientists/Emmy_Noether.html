👨‍🔬 Emmy Noether

1882–1935 · Mathematics, theoretical physics
Emmy Noether — an outstanding German mathematician who revolutionized mathematics and theoretical physics. Her most famous work is Noether's theorem, which links the symmetries of physical systems to conservation laws. This theorem became a cornerstone of modern physics, especially in general relativity and quantum field theory. Despite obstacles as a woman in academia, she made fundamental contributions to abstract algebra and was renowned for her original thinking.

Biography

Born in Erlangen to mathematician Max Noether. She studied languages and planned to become a teacher, but later turned to mathematics. Studied at the University of Erlangen, where initially she only audited lectures because women were not allowed to be full students. Later moved to the University of Göttingen, where she worked with David Hilbert and Felix Klein. Due to discrimination, she was long unable to obtain a position, teaching under Hilbert's name. In 1933, being Jewish, she emigrated to the USA, where she taught at Bryn Mawr College and collaborated with the Institute for Advanced Study. She died in 1935 after an operation. Recognition came to her largely posthumously.

Key discoveries

  • Noether's theorem: establishes an equivalence between continuous symmetries of a physical system and corresponding conserved quantities. For example,
  • from the homogeneity of time follows conservation of energy,
  • from the homogeneity of space follows conservation of momentum. The work became the foundation for understanding conservation laws in general relativity and quantum mechanics.
💡 Despite being denied a paid position early in her career, she conducted seminars officially listed under Hilbert's name, and once even gave a lecture under his name when Hilbert himself was ill. Einstein called her 'the most significant creative mathematical genius thus far produced since the higher education of women began.'
Quote: "My methods are methods of work and thinking; that is why they have spread so imperceptibly everywhere."
