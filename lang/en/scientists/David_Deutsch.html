👨‍🔬 David Deutsch

1953- · quantum physics, quantum computing, philosophy of science
British theoretical physicist who laid the foundations of quantum computing. He formulated the concept of a universal quantum computer and showed how quantum parallelism can solve problems inaccessible to classical machines. A strong advocate of the many-worlds interpretation of quantum mechanics, Deutsch argues that quantum computers draw their power precisely from parallel universes. His work blurs the line between physics, computer science, and philosophy.

Biography

Born in Haifa, Israel. Studied physics at Cambridge, received his doctorate from Wolfson College, Oxford. Began thinking about quantum computing in the 1970s, and in 1985 published the revolutionary paper 'Quantum Theory, the Church–Turing Principle and the Universal Quantum Computer'. He is a visiting professor at Oxford University and one of the founders of the Centre for Quantum Computation. Author of popular books 'The Fabric of Reality' and 'The Beginning of Infinity', in which he expounds his vision of science and progress.

Key discoveries

  • Formulation of the concept of a universal quantum computer and quantum gates, showing that quantum systems can perform fundamentally different kinds of computations.
  • Development of the many-worlds interpretation as a necessary basis for understanding quantum computing.
💡 Deutsch considers the quantum computer a literal window into parallel worlds: in his view, computation is distributed across the multiverse. His book 'The Beginning of Infinity' asserts that knowledge has no limits and that with sufficient scientific advancement, any problems are solvable.
Quote: "The most important thing to understand about quantum mechanics is that parallel universes are not just a mathematical abstraction, but reality."
