👨‍🔬 Shuji Nakamura

1954 – настоящее время · Optoelectronics, Semiconductor physics, Materials science
Japanese and American engineer, inventor of the high-brightness blue light-emitting diode, which revolutionized lighting technologies and made possible modern energy-efficient displays and lasers. His story is an example of scientific audacity and perseverance: he developed the blue LED despite skepticism from his superiors, working alone with homemade equipment.

Biography

Shuji Nakamura was born in Ehime Prefecture, Japan. He graduated from the University of Tokushima with a degree in electronic engineering. In 1979, he began working at the small company Nichia Chemical Industries, where he worked on semiconductors. In the 1990s, with no budget and no support, he single-handedly created the technology for growing gallium nitride (GaN) crystals for blue LEDs. He later moved to the United States and became a professor at the University of California, Santa Barbara. In 2014, he received the Nobel Prize in Physics together with Isamu Akasaki and Hiroshi Amano for the invention of efficient blue LEDs.

Key discoveries

  • High-brightness blue LED: developed a method for producing a p-layer in gallium nitride through thermal annealing, which allowed the creation of a bright blue LED. This led to the emergence of energy-efficient white lighting (in combination with a phosphor).
  • Blue semiconductor laser: was the first to create a laser diode based on GaN, paving the way for Blu-ray discs, laser projectors, and medical equipment.
  • Industrial MOCVD technology: improved the metal-organic chemical vapor deposition method for growing high-quality nitride crystals, which became a standard in optoelectronics.
💡 Nakamura sued his former company Nichia for compensation, eventually receiving about $$8 million – far less than the company earned from his invention. He calls himself a 'weird inventor' and continues to work on creating even more efficient lasers.
Quote: "I was just doing my job, not thinking about the Nobel Prize. My goal was to do what no one had done."
