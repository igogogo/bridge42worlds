Fritz Zwicky

! scientist · # tag · @ author
1898-1974 · astrophysics, cosmology, rocketry
Swiss-American astrophysicist, renowned for his bold and prophetic ideas. He was the first to point out the existence of dark matter and neutron stars, predicted gravitational lensing, and the possibility of supernovae as standard candles. His eccentric character and aggressive style often caused rejection, but time has confirmed his correctness.

Biography

Born in Varna (Bulgaria) into a Swiss-Czech family. He graduated from the Swiss Federal Institute of Technology in Zurich (ETH). In 1925, he moved to California and joined Caltech, where he worked for most of his life. During World War II, he consulted on rocket programs and received the Presidential Medal of Freedom. He was known as a fierce polemicist.

Key discoveries

  • Dark matter: in 1933, he applied the virial theorem to the Coma Cluster and showed that the visible mass is insufficient to hold the galaxies together; he introduced the term 'dark matter'.
  • Neutron stars and the term 'supernova': together with Walter Baade, he proposed that supernovae form during the collapse of ordinary stars into neutron stars, and gave them their modern name.
  • Gravitational lensing: he pointed out that massive objects can bend light rays and predicted that galaxies could serve as lenses for observing distant objects.
  • Morphological analysis: he developed a universal method for solving complex problems by systematically examining all possible solutions, used in engineering and management.
💡 Zwicky often called his colleagues "spherical bastards" because they "look stupid from any angle." He also pointed his finger directly in opponents' faces and once claimed that his ideas were so simple that only a genius could understand them. Despite this, many of his predictions form the basis of modern astrophysics.
Quote: "A true scientist must be a lone wolf, going his own way."
