👨‍🔬 Paul Dirac

1902-1984 · theoretical physics, quantum mechanics, quantum field theory
British theoretical physicist, one of the founders of quantum mechanics. He formulated the Dirac equation, which united special relativity and quantum theory, and predicted the existence of antimatter. His work laid the foundation for quantum electrodynamics and field theory.

Biography

Born in Bristol, England. Studied electrical engineering at the University of Bristol, then mathematics at Cambridge. Completed his doctoral thesis under Ralph Fowler. At age 30, became the Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at Cambridge (a position once held by Isaac Newton). Nobel Prize in Physics in 1933 jointly with Erwin Schrödinger. After retiring, moved to Florida, where he taught at Florida State University. Fellow of the Royal Society, member of the Order of Merit. Known for his extreme taciturnity and literal perception of the world.

Key discoveries

  • Derivation of the Dirac equation (1928), which describes the behavior of relativistic fermions and predicts the existence of antiparticles (the positron was discovered in 1932).
  • Development of Fermi-Dirac statistics for particles with half-integer spin.
  • Introduction of the Dirac delta function and bra-ket notation, which became standard tools of quantum mechanics.
  • Foundational work on quantum electrodynamics and the theory of magnetic monopoles.
💡 Dirac was known for his extreme reticence. Once after a lecture a student said, 'Professor Dirac, I didn't understand the last equation.' Dirac simply remained silent. When reminded of the question, he replied, 'That was not a question, it was a statement.' He also remained unmarried for a long time, but one day met the sister of a colleague and unusually for him said, 'You are my better half.' They lived together for the rest of his life.
Quote: "The beauty of equations is more important than their agreement with experiments. If an equation is not beautiful, it is probably wrong."
