👨‍🔬 Joseph von Fraunhofer

1787-1826 · optics, spectroscopy, astrophysics, instrumentation
German optician and physicist who turned spectroscopy into an exact science. His main achievement is the discovery of dark lines in the solar spectrum (Fraunhofer lines) and the invention of the diffraction grating, which made it possible to measure light wavelengths with high precision for the first time. His work laid the foundations of astrophysics and spectral analysis.

Biography

Born in Straubing, Bavaria, to a poor glazier's family. Orphaned at age 11 and forced to become an apprentice. Thanks to a workplace accident and the intervention of the future King Maximilian I, he gained the opportunity to study. He taught himself mathematics and optics. Worked at the Optical Institute in Benediktbeuern, where he created the best telescopes and microscopes in the world. He had no formal higher education but became an honorary doctor of the University of Erlangen and a member of the Bavarian Academy of Sciences. The Fraunhofer Society, one of the largest applied research organizations in Europe, is named after him.

Key discoveries

  • Discovery and systematic description of Fraunhofer lines in the solar spectrum (1814), which later became key to studying the chemical composition of stars.
  • Invention of the diffraction grating and development of methods for its precision fabrication, enabling precise measurement of light wavelengths.
  • Creation of high-precision optical instruments (achromatic lenses, heliometer) used for the first stellar parallax measurements.
💡 Fraunhofer died at age 39 from heavy metal poisoning accumulated over years of working with glass. His craftsmanship was so high that telescopes of his manufacture remained unsurpassed for many decades after his death. Fraunhofer's grave in Munich is decorated with an image of the solar spectrum featuring his famous lines.
Quote: "Work and patience turn sand into glass, and glass into a window to the Universe."
