👨‍🔬 Antoine Lavoisier

1743–1794 · Chemistry, Pneumatic chemistry, Thermodynamics
Antoine Lavoisier was a French chemist, considered the father of modern chemistry. He discovered and named oxygen, disproved the phlogiston theory, and formulated the law of conservation of mass in chemical reactions. Lavoisier also contributed to the systematization of chemical nomenclature and proved that water consists of hydrogen and oxygen. His work laid the foundations of quantitative chemistry and led to the understanding of combustion and respiration processes.

Biography

Lavoisier was born in Paris into a wealthy family. He studied law but became interested in science. He worked at the Royal Academy of Sciences and was also a tax collector. He was guillotined during the French Revolution.

Key discoveries

  • Discovery of oxygen (1778) — showed that oxygen is an element necessary for combustion and respiration, and named it.
  • Law of conservation of mass (1789) — experimentally proved that the mass of substances does not change during chemical reactions.
💡 Lavoisier was executed during the revolution despite his scientific achievements; the judge declared: 'The Republic has no need of scientists.'
Quote: "Nothing is lost, nothing is created, everything is transformed."
