👨‍🔬 J. J. Thomson

1856–1940 · Atomic physics, Electromagnetism
English physicist who discovered the electron, the first elementary particle. He proved that cathode rays consist of negatively charged particles and measured their charge-to-mass ratio. He proposed one of the first atomic models (the "plum pudding" model). His work paved the way for electronics and atomic physics.

Biography

Born near Manchester. He studied at Owens College and the University of Cambridge (Trinity College). Professor at the Cavendish Laboratory, where he created a powerful school of experimental physicists. Nobel Prize in Physics laureate in 1906. His students included Rutherford, Bohr, Oppenheimer and others.

Key discoveries

  • Discovery of the electron (1897) — in experiments with cathode rays, he proved that they are a stream of particles, which he called electrons, and measured their charge-to-mass ratio.
  • Isotopes — discovered that the element neon consists of atoms with different masses, opening the existence of stable isotopes.
  • Thomson atomic model — represented the atom as a uniformly positively charged sphere with embedded electrons.
  • Leadership of the Cavendish Laboratory — transformed it into a world center of physics, which trained a generation of Nobel laureates.
💡 J.J. Thomson's son, George Paget Thomson, also won a Nobel Prize in Physics for the discovery of electron diffraction.
Quote: ""Physics is everything; everything else is stamp collecting" (paraphrasing Rutherford)."
