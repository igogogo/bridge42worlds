👨‍🔬 John Bardeen

1908–1991 · Condensed matter physics, Quantum physics, Electronics
John Bardeen is the only scientist to have won the Nobel Prize in Physics twice. He received the first prize in 1956 for the invention of the transistor with William Shockley and Walter Brattain. The second in 1972 for the creation of the theory of superconductivity (BCS theory) with Leon Cooper and Robert Schrieffer. His work laid the foundation for all modern electronics and quantum condensed matter physics. Bardeen also contributed to quantum field theory and semiconductor physics.

Biography

Bardeen was born in Madison, Wisconsin. He earned a bachelor's degree in electrical engineering from the University of Wisconsin and a doctorate in mathematical physics from Princeton. He worked at Bell Labs, where he created the transistor, and then as a professor at the University of Illinois.

Key discoveries

  • Invention of the transistor (1947) — a key element of all modern electronics, enabling miniaturization of computing devices.
  • BCS theory of superconductivity (1957) — explained how some materials conduct electricity without resistance at low temperatures using Cooper pairs of electrons.
💡 Bardeen was so modest that he often refused interviews and disliked publicity, despite two Nobel Prizes.
Quote: "Science is not a collection of facts, but a way of thinking."
