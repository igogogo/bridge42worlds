👨‍🔬 Leonhard Euler

1707–1783 · Mathematics, Physics, Astronomy, Mechanics, Optics
Leonard Euler made fundamental contributions to mathematics and physics. His formula e^(ix) = cos x + i sin x, linking trigonometric functions with the exponential function, became a cornerstone of complex analysis. It allowed the unification of approaches to solving differential equations, describing wave processes and electrical circuits. Euler also developed the foundations of the calculus of variations, laid the foundations of graph theory (the Königsberg bridge problem) and introduced many modern mathematical notations.

Biography

Studied at the University of Basel under Johann Bernoulli. Worked at the St. Petersburg Academy of Sciences (1727–1741 and from 1766) and the Berlin Academy (1741–1766). Collaborated with Christian Goldbach and other scientists. Was an honorary member of many academies, including the Royal Society of London. Awarded the Order of St. Stanislaus and a prize of the Paris Academy of Sciences.

Key discoveries

  • Euler's formula: e^(ix) = cos x + i sin x, connecting complex exponentials with trigonometric functions, fundamental for complex analysis and physics.
  • Solution of the Basel problem: finding the sum of the reciprocals of squares (∑ 1/n² = π²/6), which was a triumph of analysis.
  • Euler characteristic of polyhedra: formula V − E + F = 2, which laid the foundations of topology.
  • Introduction of notations f(x), e, i, π and others, widely used to this day.
💡 Euler continued to work actively even after total loss of vision, dictating to assistants more than 400 articles and books, which constitutes almost half of his scientific legacy.
Quote: "The art of asking questions is more important than the ability to solve them."
