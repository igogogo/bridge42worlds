👨‍🔬 George Gabriel Stokes

1819–1903 · Mathematics, Physics, Hydrodynamics, Optics
George Gabriel Stokes made significant contributions to mathematical physics and fluid mechanics. His name is associated with Stokes' theorem, which unifies several integral theorems and is widely applied in electrodynamics and hydrodynamics. He also formulated the Navier-Stokes equations, describing the motion of viscous fluids, and discovered the phenomenon of fluorescence (Stokes shift).

Biography

Graduated from Cambridge University (Pembroke College). From 1849 to 1903 he held the post of Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at Cambridge. Collaborated with William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) in the fields of hydrodynamics and optics. Was president of the Royal Society (1885–1890), awarded the Copley Medal (1893).

Key discoveries

  • Stokes' theorem: generalizes Green's and Ostrogradsky-Gauss theorems, linking the integral of the curl of a vector field over a surface to the contour integral over its boundary.
  • Navier-Stokes equations: fundamental equations of motion of a viscous fluid, underlying modern hydrodynamics.
  • Stokes' law for the drag force on a sphere in a viscous fluid: F = 6πμrv.
  • Stokes shift in fluorescence: explained that the wavelength of emitted light is longer than the wavelength of absorbed light.
💡 Stokes was not only an outstanding scientist but also actively participated in politics: he was elected as a Member of Parliament for Cambridge University from 1887 to 1892.
