👨‍🔬 Robert Hooke

1635-1703 · physics, architecture, biology, microscopy
English naturalist, architect, and inventor, one of the most versatile scientists of his time. He formulated the law of elasticity, guessed the inverse-square law of gravity, and introduced the term "cell". His microscopic investigations opened a new world. For a long time, his achievements were underestimated due to his rivalry with Newton.

Biography

Born on the Isle of Wight, England. He was educated at Oxford, where he worked as an assistant to Robert Boyle. He became the first Curator of Experiments of the Royal Society, later its Secretary. After the Great Fire of London, he participated in the city's reconstruction as an architect. He was forced to engage in bitter priority disputes with Newton and Huygens.

Key discoveries

  • Hooke's Law (law of elasticity): he established that the deformation of a solid body is proportional to the applied force, expressing this with the famous anagram 'ceiiinosssttuv' (Latin: ut tensio, sic vis).
  • Hypothesis of the inverse-square law of gravity: in correspondence with Newton (1679–1680), he suggested that the force of gravity decreases with the square of the distance, which Newton later used.
  • Cellular structure of organisms: he was the first to use the word 'cell' after observing cork through a microscope, which became the basis of cell theory.
  • Foundational microscopy: in his book 'Micrographia', he published detailed drawings of insects, plants, and materials, showing for the first time the complexity of the microworld.
💡 Hooke was not only a scientist but also a talented architect; among his projects are the Monument to the Great Fire and the dome of the Greenwich Observatory. He also invented the balance spring for watches, but due to a dispute with Huygens, he did not obtain a patent.
Quote: "There is nothing so small as to escape our inquiry; hence a new world is revealed, visible through the microscope."
