John Archibald Wheeler

1911–2008 · General relativity, Astrophysics, Quantum gravity
American theoretical physicist who revived general relativity. Coined the terms 'black hole' and 'wormhole', actively developed the concept of gravitational lensing, studied the collapse of massive stars and the nature of time. His work laid the foundation for modern black hole physics and quantum gravity.

Biography

Born in Jacksonville, Florida. Received his doctorate from Johns Hopkins University. Spent most of his career at Princeton, mentoring a generation of future Nobel laureates, including Richard Feynman. Participated in the Manhattan Project. Laureate of the Wolf Prize, Einstein Medal, and other awards. Known for aphoristic statements about physics.

Key discoveries

  • The term 'black hole' and mathematical description of its properties; 'wormhole' — a hypothetical tunnel in spacetime.
  • Theory of gravitational lensing: showed that massive bodies can bend light, creating multiple images of distant sources. Today this effect is a primary tool for searching for dark matter and studying galaxies.
  • Concept of 'spacetime foam' at Planck scales, foreshadowing many ideas in quantum gravity.
💡 Wheeler was so fascinated by gravity that he joked: 'If I hadn't become a physicist, I probably would have been a worm to crawl through wormholes.' His teaching style combined rigorous mathematics with poetic imagery and produced dozens of famous students.
Quote: "Time is what clocks measure. But what is a clock without change? Any measurement of time is a counting of changes."
