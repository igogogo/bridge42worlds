Georges Lemaître

1894 – 1966 · Cosmology, Astrophysics, Mathematics
Belgian Catholic priest and cosmologist who first proposed the theory of the expanding Universe and the 'primeval atom' model, later called the Big Bang. He derived the law later named after Hubble and estimated the expansion constant two years before Hubble's publication. His ideas reconciled scientific cosmology with philosophy.

Biography

Georges Lemaître was born in Charleroi, Belgium. He studied civil engineering, but after serving in World War I, he became a priest and became interested in physics. He received his doctorate from MIT under the supervision of Arthur Eddington and Harlow Shapley. In 1927, while solving Einstein's equations, he concluded that the Universe is non-static and expanding. His work went unnoticed until Einstein called it 'beautiful.' He later taught at the Catholic University of Louvain. He was a member of the Pontifical Academy of Sciences.

Key discoveries

  • Theory of the expanding Universe: independently of Friedmann and earlier than Hubble, he showed that the Universe is non-static and expanding, with galaxies receding at a speed proportional to distance (Lemaître-Hubble law).
  • Primeval atom hypothesis: he proposed that the Universe arose from a super-dense clump of matter that disintegrated, giving rise to space and time – the basis of the Big Bang theory.
  • Calculation of the Hubble constant: in 1927, based on redshift data, he estimated the expansion rate, close to the modern value.
💡 When Pope Pius XII tried to use Lemaître's theory to support a theological doctrine, the scientist sharply objected, stating that cosmology does not need divine intervention. Einstein, who initially rejected the non-static model, apologized to Lemaître after a lecture at the Solvay Congress.
Quote: "Scientific knowledge does not contradict faith, because they consider reality from different points of view."
