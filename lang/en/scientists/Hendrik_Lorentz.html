👨‍🔬 Hendrik Lorentz

1853 – 1928 · Theoretical physics, Electrodynamics, Optics
Dutch physicist, one of the creators of classical electron theory, precursor to the theory of relativity. He developed the Lorentz transformations, which underlie the special theory of relativity, and explained the Zeeman effect, for which he received the Nobel Prize. His work on the electrodynamics of moving media paved the way for abandoning the concept of the ether.

Biography

Hendrik Antoon Lorentz was born in Arnhem, Netherlands. He studied at Leiden University, where he later became a professor. Founder of electron theory, he introduced the concept of discrete charges and their interaction with the electromagnetic field. In 1902, he shared the Nobel Prize in Physics with Pieter Zeeman for their research on the influence of magnetism on radiation (the Zeeman effect). He chaired the Solvay Congresses, and was friends and collaborated with Einstein. Foreign member of the Royal Society of London and many academies.

Key discoveries

  • Lorentz transformations: mathematical formulas describing the change of coordinates and time when switching between inertial reference frames. They became a key element of Einstein's special theory of relativity, explaining the constancy of the speed of light.
  • Explanation of the Zeeman effect: using electron theory, he showed how a magnetic field splits the spectral lines of atoms, confirming the existence of electrons and their role in radiation.
  • Electron theory of matter: he hypothesized that all electromagnetic phenomena are associated with the motion of charged particles (electrons), laying the foundations of classical electrodynamics.
💡 Lorentz was known for his extraordinary modesty and regularly corresponded with Einstein, who called him 'the greatest mind I have ever met.' In 1912, he declined a position at the University of Berlin to remain in Leiden.
Quote: "Happy is he who could know the causes of things."
