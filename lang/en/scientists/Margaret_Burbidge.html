👨‍🔬 Margaret Burbidge

1919-2020 · astrophysics, observational astronomy
British-American astrophysicist who played a key role in understanding quasars and the formation of elements in stars. She led the research that proved the extragalactic nature of quasar 3C 273. As a woman in science, she faced discrimination — she was repeatedly denied access to telescopes. Together with her husband Geoffrey Burbidge, William Fowler, and Fred Hoyle, she created the epochal B²FH paper explaining stellar nucleosynthesis. She became the first female director of the Greenwich Observatory.

Biography

Born in Davenport, England. Studied astronomy at University College London, where she also earned her doctorate. Worked at Yerkes Observatory of the University of Chicago, then at Caltech, where she was often listed as her husband's 'assistant' to obtain observing time. Later — professor at the University of California, San Diego, and director of the Center for Astrophysics and Space Sciences. President of the American Astronomical Society and the American Association for the Advancement of Science. Recipient of the National Medal of Science and the Gold Medal of the Royal Astronomical Society.

Key discoveries

  • Co-authorship of the B²FH paper, explaining the synthesis of chemical elements in stars, including the formation of carbon and helium.
  • Determination of the redshift and distance of quasar 3C 273 using spectroscopy, proving its cosmological nature.
  • Contribution to the study of galaxy rotation and mass associated with dark matter.
💡 Margaret Burbidge consistently refused awards intended only for women, considering it segregation. In 1971, she declined the Annie J. Cannon Award of the American Astronomical Society, stating that such awards only reinforce discrimination.
Quote: "The universe is full of magical things, patiently waiting for our wits to grow sharper."
