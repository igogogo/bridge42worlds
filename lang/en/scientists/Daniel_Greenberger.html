👨‍🔬 Daniel Greenberger

1932– · Quantum mechanics, Quantum information, Fundamental foundations
American theoretical physicist, known as one of the discoverers of the Greenberger–Horne–Zeilinger (GHZ) state — a multipartite entangled state that reveals radical contradictions between quantum mechanics and local realism without Bell inequalities. GHZ states have become a workhorse resource in quantum computing and communications.

Biography

He studied at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, received his PhD from the University of Illinois. He worked at the University of California, Berkeley, and the City College of New York, where he chaired the department. Collaborated with Anton Zeilinger. Winner of the Majorana Prize.

Key discoveries

  • GHZ state: in 1989, together with Michael Horne and Anton Zeilinger, proposed a thought experiment with three or more particles, demonstrating the nonlocality of quantum mechanics in an "all or nothing" fashion.
  • Multipartite entanglement: explored the unique properties of GHZ states, which are used for quantum teleportation, superdense coding, and error correction.
  • Neutron interferometry: contributed to the quantum mechanics of neutrons, testing fundamental principles.
💡 Greenberger started as a neutron physicist and only later became interested in quantum paradoxes. The GHZ state is sometimes called "Einstein's nightmare," because it undermines classical concepts even more than the EPR paradox.
Quote: ""Quantum entanglement is not just a strangeness; it is the language in which Nature speaks to us about its wholeness.""
