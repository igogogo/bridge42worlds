Superconductivity from Chaos: How Defects in Graphene Give Birth to Quantum Order ✦

Original: "Disorder-induced superconductivity in graphene"
· Jannes van Poppelen, Tomas Löthman, Annica M. Black-Schaffer
arXiv:2607.02267 · 2026-07-02 · CC BY 4.0 · 5 min · Superconductivity Disordered Systems
Disorder in monolayer graphene induces globally coherent superconductivity at vanishingly small attraction due to quantum geometry.
Abstract

It is shown that superconductivity can emerge purely from disorder, as demonstrated in monolayer graphene. In the clean limit, graphene's semimetallic nature requires prohibitively strong electronic interactions to achieve superconductivity. Introducing a low concentration of vacancies or hydrogenation creates a large density of low-energy states that readily induce superconductivity. For conventional s-wave pairing, disorder generates a finite order parameter for arbitrarily weak attractive interactions; global phase coherence is established not through isolated islands, but thanks to a finite superfluid weight of purely geometric origin. Similarly, hydrogenation yields a finite critical temperature and non-zero superfluid weight. For unconventional nearest-neighbor pairing, usually destroyed by disorder, superconductivity exhibits quantum-critical behavior, yet phase coherence is maintained at weak interactions, displaying mixed d-wave symmetries.

Context

Superconductivity is the brightest example of a macroscopic quantum phenomenon, where electrons form a Bose-Einstein condensate of Cooper pairs, obeying Bose-Einstein statistics. The classic BCS theory, created by John Bardeen and colleagues, describes this phase transition in pure metals. However, in two-dimensional systems like graphene, the situation is complicated: at the charge neutrality point, the density of states tends to zero, and fantastically large attractive forces are needed for pairing. Electrons in graphene behave as massless fermions, obeying a relativistic equation, which was realized early on by Paul Dirac and later developed in the Fermi liquid theory of Lev Landau. Recent breakthroughs in twisted multilayer graphene have shown that creating flat bands with high density of states is key. This work proposes an alternative path: disorder engineering. Random vacancies or chemisorbed hydrogen create a peak in the density of states near the Fermi energy, much like a flat band but born from chaos. The question is whether such an inhomogeneous medium can support global phase coherence—and the answer, obtained via numerical simulation, was affirmative.

Methods

To describe the electronic structure of disordered graphene, a tight-binding model on a lattice of 5000 carbon atoms with periodic boundary conditions was used. Vacancies were modeled by removing sites, and hydrogenation by adding orbitals with finite energy and hybridization. Superconducting pairing was investigated in two channels: conventional s-wave (on-site attraction) and non-trivial d-wave (on nearest-neighbor bonds). To find the order parameter, the Bogoliubov–de Gennes equations were solved in a mean-field formalism, using a quantum field approach, and the critical temperature was determined from the linearized gap equation using a rational pole expansion. The quantum geometry of the normal state was computed via the quantum metric, and the superfluid weight and BKT temperature from the linear response and the Nelson–Kosterlitz criterion. An important role was played by the superposition character of defect-state wavefunctions, which exhibit a multifractal structure, analyzed through the singularity spectrum. All calculations were performed on graphics processors, ensuring interferometric precision in diagonalizing large matrices.

Results

It turned out that even small defect concentrations (1–10%) radically reduce the threshold interaction for superconductivity. If in pure graphene the critical coupling constant U_c≈2.3t, then with 4% vacancies, a superconducting order parameter emerges at U/U_c∼0.2 and grows linearly with U, characteristic of flat-band superconductivity. The spatial distribution of the gap is highly inhomogeneous: it is concentrated around defects, but due to the algebraic decay of multifractal states, it permeates the entire sample. Global phase coherence is established thanks to a finite superfluid weight D_s, which, as shown, has a purely geometric origin—the conventional (dispersive) contribution is absent. The value of D_s correlates with the quantum metric of the normal state and reaches a maximum at a vacancy concentration of about 12%. The BKT temperature T_BKT computed from the Nelson–Kosterlitz criterion reaches tens of kelvin already at U/U_c∼0.2 and grows with defect concentration. For hydrogenation, the results are qualitatively similar, but with smaller gap and T_BKT values due to partial breaking of chiral symmetry and a shift of the density-of-states peak. In the case of d-wave pairing, quantum-critical behavior is observed: superconductivity arises only at finite interaction, but the threshold rapidly decreases with increasing defect concentration. The pairing symmetry is a mixture of d_{x2-y2} and d_{xy} components with a suppressed relative phase, indicating nematicity induced by disorder. In all cases, a crucial role is played by the quantum entanglement of electrons in Cooper pairs, obeying Fermi-Dirac statistics in the normal phase and bosonic in the condensate, and the BKT transition is governed by the balance of energy and entropy of vortices in accordance with the second law of thermodynamics.

Implications

The discovery demonstrates that disorder is not an enemy, but a creator of quantum correlated phases. It lays the foundations for a new paradigm of 'superconductivity from chaos,' applicable to a broad class of two-dimensional materials. In particular, it becomes clear that the traditional requirement of high crystallinity can be relaxed, and controlled defect creation can become a tool for designing superconductors with desired properties. The concept of geometric superfluid weight, previously considered exotic for flat bands in moiré systems, now extends to disordered media. Furthermore, the observed multifractality of the order parameter indicates a deep connection between Anderson critical states and superconductivity, calling for further theoretical understanding.

Future development

In the future, this direction could lead to the creation of superconducting devices based on monolayer graphene with defects, using electrostatic doping for precise tuning. Further research may include realistic modeling accounting for lattice reconstruction and multi-orbital effects, as well as the search for optimal types of disorder. It is also important to study the competition with magnetic phases that may arise from the same defect states. Experimental verification of the predictions will require samples with controlled concentrations of vacancies or hydrogen adatoms, measurements of the tunneling gap and transport properties. Another promising path is the application of quantum-thermodynamic approaches to optimize the transition temperature.

Impact

The field of condensed matter physics and quantum materials will gain a new class of disordered superconductors, and technology will potentially have a simple path to integrating superconducting elements into flexible electronics and sensors based on graphene.

Next steps

Immediate next steps include taking into account structural relaxation of vacancies, multi-orbital corrections, and competition with magnetism, as well as experimental confirmation of the predicted effects in tunneling spectroscopy and superfluid response measurements.

Key open problems

The work is directly connected to unsolved questions in physics: how can disorder generate, rather than destroy, quantum coherence? Where is the boundary between an Anderson localized state and a superconducting condensate? And what are the fundamental limits on the superconducting transition temperature in disordered systems? Answers to these are important for understanding high-temperature superconductivity and quantum phase transitions.
