Diamond qubit based on a nickel-vacancy center with all-optical control and millisecond quantum memory ✦

Original: "A transition-metal qubit in diamond with all-optical control and millisecond quantum memory"
· I. M. Morris, T. Alberth, L. Crooks, T. Lühmann, D. J. Twitchen, S. Pezzagna, J. Meijer, S. S. Nicley, J. N. Becker
arXiv:2607.02258 · 2026-07-02 · CC BY · 3 min
Quantum Physics Other Condensed Matter Atomic Physics
A single nickel-vacancy center in diamond realizes a fully optically controlled spin qubit with coherence over a millisecond at 1.65 K, paving the way to compact and scalable nodes for quantum networks.
Abstract

Quantum networks demand qubits with simultaneous efficient optical access, coherent control, and long-lived quantum memory — merging these capabilities in a single scalable platform remains a key challenge. Transition metal defects in diamond offer a novel route thanks to spin-orbit protected ground-state coherence, all-optical control, and near-infrared emission. On a single nickel-vacancy center (NiV⁻), a fully optically controlled spin qubit with coherence times exceeding 1 ms at 1.65 K, compatible with compact closed-cycle cryocoolers, is demonstrated. Raman Rabi oscillations and Ramsey interferometry are realized, and all-optical dynamical decoupling boosts coherence from T₂* = 371 ns to T₂(CPMG-4) = 1.27 ms. These results establish NiV⁻ as a practical spin-photon interface.

1

Context

Quantum networks exchanging entangled states require interfaces where a spin stores quantum information and a photon carries it over distance. Loophole-free tests of Bell's theorem (Bell, Aspect) confirmed the feasibility of such networks, and experiments with NV⁻ centers in diamond confirmed their realizability. However, NV⁻ suffers from a low Debye-Waller factor and sensitivity to the Stark effect due to lack of inversion symmetry, causing spectral diffusion. SiV⁻, while symmetric, requires millikelvin temperatures or strain engineering because of its small spin-orbit splitting. Transition metals, with their d-orbitals, offer a new lever: hybridization with carbon dangling bonds rearranges the electronic structure, creating a long-lived, phonon-protected spin memory with direct optical access.

Methods

Setup: a single NiV⁻ center, electro-stabilized in a p-i-p junction inside diamond with natural ¹³C abundance (1.1%), placed in a closed-cycle helium cryostat at 1.65 K and a magnetic field of 150 mT (tilted 54.7° from the defect axis, inducing Zeeman splitting). Excitation and fluorescence collection were performed via a confocal microscope with a cryogenic objective. Optical fields for pumping, Raman control, and readout were generated by an electro-optic modulator. After optically initializing the spin into |↓⟩ with >99% fidelity, coherent control was achieved via two-photon Raman transitions far detuned from resonance (Δ/2π = 250 MHz), suppressing scattering. Sequences used: Ramsey interferometry to estimate T₂*, Hahn spin echo, and Carr-Purcell-Meiboom-Gill (CPMG) multi-pulse dynamical decoupling to suppress decoherence from the nuclear spin bath. The state was read out by quantum measurement of resonance fluorescence.

Results

At the optical transitions, a dark superposition of spin levels — coherent population trapping (CPT) — was formed, with a width of 0.91 MHz at zero power, yielding a lower bound of T₂* ≈ 350 ns. Direct Ramsey measurements refined T₂* = 371 ns. Spin echo extended the coherence time to T₂,echo = 125 μs, and a CPMG-4 sequence to T₂ = 1.27 ms; the decay shape (power exponent n≈2) indicates dominance of low-frequency noise from ¹³C nuclei. The temperature dependence of T₂,echo showed that at 1.65 K this reversible noise dominates, rather than fast phonon processes: at 3.3 K the coherence drops to 3.2 μs, and the decay exponent approaches 1, characteristic of Markovian phonon broadening. Thus, millisecond memory is achieved without isotopic purification or ultralow temperatures — a compact closed-cycle cryostat suffices.

Implications

The result demonstrates that d-orbital hybridization is a powerful tool for spectroscopic defect design: it separates the tasks of phonon protection (large spin-orbit splitting) and optical control (singlet excited state) within a single microscopic system. NiV⁻ becomes a prototype for a new class of diamond qubits that require no compromise between coherence and optical interface. A numerically predicted coherence limit of ~30 ms at 1.65 K with ¹²C isotopic enrichment outlines a path to quantum repeater nodes with record performance.

Future development

Next steps are clear: isotopic purification of diamond (¹²C) will reduce nuclear spin noise, and integration into nanophotonic resonators will enhance light-matter interaction and enable conversion to telecom wavelengths. Moreover, transition metals from Ti to Cu open a wide chemical space to search for defects with even more advantageous property combinations, such as an orbital-singlet ground state that completely eliminates phonon decoherence.

Impact

The technology directly impacts the creation of quantum repeaters and distributed quantum computing, as well as improves prospects for quantum cryptography and solid-state spin-based sensing.

Next steps

Planned are the growth of diamond with reduced ¹³C content, embedding centers into photonic crystal structures, and investigating other transition metals (Co, Fe) to optimize lifetime and optical oscillator strength.

Key open problems

The article is directly connected to the problem of scalable quantum networks (creating reliable spin-photon interfaces) and overcoming decoherence in solid-state qubits caused by the nuclear spin environment.
