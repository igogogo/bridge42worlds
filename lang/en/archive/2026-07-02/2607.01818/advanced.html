Gravitational Waves from Primordial Black Holes: Low-Frequency and High-Frequency Signals as a Unified Probe of the Early Universe ✦

Original: "Gravitational Waves from Primordial Black Holes: Connecting Low-Frequency Scalar-Induced Signatures to High-Frequency Binary Mergers"
· Ashu Kushwaha
arXiv:2607.01818 · 2026-07-02 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · Cosmology General Relativity HEP Phenomenology
The same primordial curvature perturbations produce two types of gravitational waves—a low-frequency stochastic background and a high-frequency signal from mergers, paving the way for multi-band gravitational-wave astronomy.
Abstract

The formation of primordial black holes requires a significant amplification of curvature perturbations. This process leaves a dual gravitational-wave imprint: a low-frequency stochastic background of scalar-induced waves and a high-frequency signal from the mergers of binary systems composed of these black holes. For a monochromatic mass function, a model-independent relation between the two observable signals is established, based on constraints linking the abundance of primordial black holes to the power spectrum. It is shown that within both spheroidal and ellipsoidal collapse scenarios, the latter yields a much stronger background signal. Analysis of the merger signal reveals a direct correspondence between the frequency of the scalar-induced peak and the innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO) frequency of the binary system. The peak of the total merger spectrum is linked to the ISCO by a universal relation: the peak frequency is always 1.79 times higher than the ISCO frequency, regardless of masses. Thus, a unified approach connects gravitational-wave channels separated in frequency, allowing the same initial fluctuations to be probed in widely spaced bands.

Context

The study of primordial black holes is one of the most intriguing bridges between early Universe physics and modern astrophysics. Their formation requires amplified primordial density perturbations, which, upon horizon entry during inflation, can collapse into black holes. Such amplified perturbations inevitably generate second-order gravitational waves—a scalar-induced background carrying information about fluctuation spectra on scales inaccessible to the cosmic microwave background. Concurrently, PBHs themselves form binary systems that emit gravitational waves upon merging. Until now, these two channels were considered separately, but their common origin from the same primordial perturbations dictates a tight connection. This paper quantifies that connection for the first time within a unified framework, opening up the possibility of testing the Big Bang theory in entirely new frequency bands.

Methods

The study employs the Press–Schechter formalism to calculate the fraction of collapsing matter during PBH formation, accounting for the nonlinear relation between density contrast and primordial curvature. A narrow log-normal curvature power spectrum is used, yielding a monochromatic PBH mass function. Constraints on the spectrum amplitude are taken from previous PBH abundance studies. The scalar-induced gravitational wave spectrum is computed at second order in perturbation theory during the radiation-dominated era, with suppression after mode horizon exit. For the merger background, a merger rate formula is used with a suppression factor accounting for binary PBH interactions with the environment, and the single-merger energy spectrum is taken from phenomenological approximations that combine inspiral, merger, and ringdown phases. A key step was deriving an analytical relation between the peak SIGW frequency and the innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO) frequency of the merging pair.

Results

It is shown that the ellipsoidal collapse scenario predicts an SIGW signal about an order of magnitude stronger compared to spherical collapse, due to a higher collapse threshold. For the merger background, accounting for rate suppression reduces the gravitational-wave amplitude by up to three orders of magnitude, depending on the PBH mass and f_PBH parameter. The main result is a model-independent relation f_ISCO ≈ 3.4×10^20 (f_SIGW/Hz)^2, connecting the low- and high-frequency bands. For example, an SIGW signal in the pulsar timing array band (nanohertz) corresponds to PBH mergers in the LIGO band (hundreds of hertz), while a signal in the LISA band corresponds to mergers in the kilohertz range. Visualization of the ensemble of spectra for 50 PBH masses demonstrates that the amplitude envelope at ISCO frequency provides a unified description of the source family.

Implications

The derived connection transforms disparate observational channels into a coherent tool for testing inflation physics and dark matter. If future detectors register a stochastic background at low frequencies, one could predict the amplitude and frequency of the high-frequency merger signal, and vice versa. This is especially valuable for regimes where direct detection of high-frequency gravitational waves (above 10 kHz) is still technically unfeasible. Moreover, comparing spherical and ellipsoidal collapse predictions provides a method to distinguish between PBH formation models.

Future development

The proposed formalism naturally generalizes to broad power spectra that produce extended PBH mass functions, and to the case of rotating black holes. It is also possible to include a third channel—gravitational waves from Hawking evaporation of light PBHs—which would add a third characteristic frequency scale to the model. Advances in numerical simulations of cosmological perturbations and mergers will refine predictions.

Impact

The results will impact several areas: dark matter physics (constraints on the PBH fraction), inflation theory (reconstructing the power spectrum on small scales), and gravitational-wave astronomy (multi-band observation strategies).

Next steps

Immediate next steps include accounting for PBH spin, constructing joint constraints from LIGO/Virgo, LISA, and PTA data, and comparisons with alternative background sources, such as phase transitions.

Key open problems

The work directly addresses the nature of dark matter—whether it is at least partially composed of primordial black holes—and the challenge of measuring the primordial power spectrum on scales inaccessible to the cosmic microwave background, a key goal of inflationary cosmology. Additionally, the link between PBH mergers and background radiation offers a means to indirectly detect high-frequency gravitational waves that would otherwise remain unobservable.
