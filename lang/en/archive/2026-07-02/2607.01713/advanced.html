From Computer Prediction to Lab Synthesis: How to Find 25 Stable Materials Out of 894 ✦

Original: "Predicting Novel Stable Materials for Experimental Synthesis"
· Yuqi An, Sihong Zhu, Joseph Montoya, Xingyu Guo, Zhenbin Wang
arXiv:2607.01713 · 2026-07-02 · CC BY · 4 min · Materials Computational Physics
Hierarchical screening based on thermodynamics and dynamics leaves only a few percent of predicted structures truly ready for synthesis.
Abstract

A hierarchical screening scheme for synthesis candidates is proposed. At the first stage, from 894 compounds previously claimed as stable, after removing duplicates and checking against full phase diagrams in the PBE approximation, 298 remained. Next, dynamic stability was evaluated using universal machine-learning interatomic potentials: harmonic phonons and finite-temperature molecular dynamics reduced the set to 166 structures. Thermodynamic refinement with the SCAN method left 109, and the final priority list of 25 candidates was compiled based on decomposition enthalpy and chemical space completeness. This protocol turns stability predictions into realistically achievable synthetic targets.

Context

Back in his famous lecture, Richard Feynman talked about simulating physics on computers, and today we stand on the threshold of the era of predictive materials science. The rapid development of computational chemistry and machine learning has created the illusion that a new material can be discovered simply by pressing a button. Indeed, algorithms find millions of stable atomic combinations, but experimentalists face a harsh reality: obtaining the predicted compound in the lab is incredibly difficult. The reason is that calculations often rely on simplified models and incomplete data, ignoring the second law of thermodynamics, which requires considering all possible structures. Without dynamic verification—whether the crystal will maintain its shape when heated—the prediction remains a gamble. This gap between computer prediction and real synthesis has become the main bottleneck in materials science.

Methods

The authors built a three-level sieve, reminiscent of special forces selection: candidates undergo tests of increasing difficulty. First, from the CAMD database, 603 unique structures were selected and their stability was recalculated using numerical simulation based on the PBE functional (electron density approximation). Here, the key tool is the energy above the convex hull, showing how 'unfavorable' the compound is compared to known ones. The second stage—dynamic stability: using the universal machine learning potential MACE, phonon spectra were calculated (imaginary modes are a sure sign of instability) and molecular dynamics was run on a nanosecond timescale at 1000 K. The final filter is recalculation with the more accurate SCAN functional, which halves the errors in formation energies.

Results

The very first screening was staggering: out of 603 unique materials, only 298 (49%) remained thermodynamically stable on the full phase diagram. The reason lies in competing phases—the addition of previously unknown vanadium bromide VBr3 shifted VBr5 from stable to metastable, raising its energy by 29 meV/atom. Dynamic tests, accounting for entropic effects, shrank the list to 166: 25 structures failed harmonic oscillations, and 107 showed instability in molecular dynamics. Notably, a third of materials behaved inconsistently—phonon stability did not guarantee stability under heating, and vice versa. SCAN refinement left 109 finalists. Finally, considering decomposition enthalpy and chemical space completeness highlighted 25 candidates with a score above 0.80—true diamonds in a pile of sand.

Implications

This work shifts the paradigm: from now on, any predicted material must undergo not only thermodynamic but also dynamic scrutiny. The proposed pipeline dramatically increases confidence in computer design, bringing it closer to the needs of experimentalists. For synthetic chemists, this means a clear list of the 25 most promising targets—instead of thousands of speculative options. For fundamental science, the protocol underscores the importance of the Pauli exclusion principle and the Schrödinger equation, which underpin DFT calculations, and demonstrates how quantum thermodynamics manifests at the nanoscale. Potential applications range from superconductors to quantum computers and quantum information transmission, where crystal lattice stability is critical. The reliability of these calculations rests on the mathematical heart of quantum mechanics, discovered by Erwin Schrödinger. Experimental verification of such structures will require advanced spectroscopy methods.

Future development

The next twist is incorporating variable cell shape in molecular dynamics to catch phase transitions requiring volume changes. The development of universal ML potentials, for instance, accounting for charge states, will make predictions even more accurate and allow tackling ternary and quaternary systems. Integration of such a pipeline into automated laboratories, where a robotic synthesizer will receive a pre-validated list of compounds, is not far off.

Impact

The methodology will directly impact the creation of new batteries, catalysts, and quantum devices, where every extra millielectronvolt of energy determines the material's fate.

Next steps

The immediate task is the synthesis of the 25 proposed compounds by leading experimental groups, followed by structural validation. Simultaneously, expanding databases of competing phases and training ML potentials on a broader class of chemical elements are needed.

Key open problems

The study strikes at the heart of an unsolved problem in condensed matter physics—why most ab initio-stable phases do not form in experiments. It also raises questions about the limits of the PBE functional and the role of dynamic chaos in disrupting ordered structures.

🎯 Out of 894 initial materials, only 25 passed all tests—that's less than 3%, comparable to the percentage of gold in seawater. Nature seems to deliberately hide the most stable forms.
