Rings of Light: How to Find Invisible Matter by Straightening the Picture ✦

Original: "Polar coordinate transformations for machine learning based dark matter subhalo detection in strong gravitational lenses"
· Jacob M Campbell, Simon Dye, Emma Chapman, Adam Moss
arXiv:2607.02663 · 2026-07-02 · CC BY · 1 min · Galaxies Cosmology
By turning a ring into a straight line, scientists taught a computer to better detect invisible clumps of dark matter.
Abstract

Dark matter is invisible, but it bends the light of distant galaxies, creating arcs. Tiny clumps of dark matter inside galaxies make these arcs slightly uneven. Scientists figured out how to 'unroll' the arc into a straight line—like flattening an orange peel—and showed it to a neural network. This found about 15% more clumps. This trick helps hunt for dark matter at almost no extra cost.

The gravity of galaxies bends light from distant stars — this is gravitational lensing. Sometimes the light gathers into rings, described by Einstein. Hidden within these rings is dark matter: it's five times more abundant than ordinary matter, and without it, galaxies would fall apart (as shown by Vera Rubin and Fritz Zwicky). Tiny clumps of dark matter, like bubbles under a sticker, betray its composition by their number.

Clumps are invisible and only slightly jitter the bright arcs. Images from Hubble are analyzed by machine learning programs, but they divide the frame into squares, while the ring is round. The fix is simple: mathematically unroll the ring into a straight band — like peeling a label off a jar. After this straightening, the program finds 15% more clumps.

Strikingly, the accuracy gain reached 50% when the program had no prior training. It's like solving a puzzle in your native language rather than in a mirror reflection.

Upcoming telescopes (Euclid) will deliver thousands of such rings. By comparing the number of clumps with computer simulations (accounting for light stretched by the expansion of space), we will learn whether dark matter is cold (many clumps) or warm (few).

The Einstein angle is the radius of the gravitational ring. The more massive the lens and the closer it is to the line of sight, the larger it is. Tiny subhalos, like invisible satellites, slightly deform the perfect circle, creating microscopic kinks—by these we detect them.
