Prison Beyond the Horizon: Why Super-Earths Will Never Let Intelligence Escape into Space ✦

Original: "Multistage Rocket Optimization, Geophysics, and the Spacefaring Envelope of Habitable Super-Earths"
· Sanjoy M. Som
arXiv:2607.02691 · 2026-07-02 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min
Exoplanets Instrumentation
Even a Voyager-sized probe can't break free from a planet heavier than 11.5 Earth masses — scientists prove chemical rockets are powerless against such gravity.
Abstract

Scientists introduced the concept of 'cosmic suitability' — a planet's ability to allow launching a 1000 kg payload into space using chemical rockets. The model showed: for small planets, atmospheric drag is key; for super-Earths, gravity. With a limit of ~100 F-1 engines, a planet heavier than ~11.5 Earth masses makes chemical escape nearly impossible, confirming the ~10 mass cutoff. Many rocky exoplanets could be eternal prisons for their inhabitants. Earth, meanwhile, sits right at the lower edge of what's possible — we just got lucky.

1

The race for biosignatures — molecular ghosts of distant biospheres — is gaining momentum. Astronomers are discovering more exoplanets in the 'habitable zones' where liquid water is possible. But one question rarely makes it into research: could a mind born on these worlds ever physically leave its cradle? A new study provides a chilling answer: for planets more massive than 11.5 Earths, chemical rockets are helpless — they become cosmic prisons.

Imagine a blossoming island in an endless ocean. The soil is fertile, life teems, but the shores are sheer cliffs and the tidal currents mercilessly dash any boat back against the rocks. The gravity of a massive planet is that same invisible warden, locking a civilization in place. The study's authors wove together geophysics (from plate tectonics to the magnetic dynamo inherited from the protoplanetary disk) and atmospheric dynamics with rocket engineering to construct a 'space-sail envelope.' They accounted for the density profile of a nitrogen-oxygen mix and even the reliability of multistage launchers. It turns out the atmosphere isn't the main foe. On planets more than four times Earth's mass, its drag barely matters. The true guardian at the gate is gravity itself.

Picture this: a rocket as heavy as the Great Pyramid of Giza — 400,000 tons — and a hundred F-1 engines roaring in unison, just to send a one-ton probe starward from a planet 10 times heavier than Earth. It's a flying skyscraper, teetering on pillars of fire.

The roots go back to Isaac Newton. Escape velocity rises with mass: \(v_{esc} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}}\). For Earth it's 11.2 km/s; for a super-Earth of 10 M⊕ it's over 25 km/s. The Tsiolkovsky equation \(\Delta v = I_{sp} g_0 \ln\frac{m_0}{m_f}\) is merciless: every extra km/s demands exponentially more fuel. F-1 engines burn hydrogen and oxygen, birthing an infernal torch. But when you stack too many, the structure can't support itself. The limit of a hundred F-1s is reached at 11.5 M⊕ — beyond that lie only dreams of technologies a hypothetical civilization might never possess.

Remarkably, these figures nearly match earlier calculations by Hippke, obtained by a completely different method. Two independent approaches converge on the verdict: chemical escape snaps shut like a drawn string.

Curiously, Earth itself is only 3.7 times lighter than this fateful threshold — we teeter on the knife-edge of cosmic freedom. This finding upends classical notions of habitability. A warm puddle of water gets a technological dimension. Many worlds perfectly suited for life may turn out to be 'golden cages' — brimming with biosignatures, yet mute to radio telescopes. The Great Filter, perhaps, stands at every second star. For future technosignature hunts, this means: aim for planets below the critical mass. Astronomy enters an era where the question isn't just 'Is there life there?' but 'Can it cry out into the void?' And the answer, sadly, may be bitter.

🎯 To send astronauts to the Moon, it took the 3,000-ton Saturn V. But to launch a modest 1-ton probe to the stars from a super-Earth just 10 times heavier than ours, you'd need to stack the Great Pyramid of Giza (~400,000 tons) and equip it with a hundred F-1 engines — a veritable flying skyscraper, lifting off the ground only for a moment.
