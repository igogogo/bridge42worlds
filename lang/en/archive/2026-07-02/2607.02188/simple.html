How to 'Weigh' the Memory of a Quantum Particle ✦

Original: "Entropy of Non-Abelian Anyons from Slow Quasiparticle Dynamics in Quantum Hall Interferometers"
· Eran Sela, Mitali Banerjee
arXiv:2607.02188 · 2026-07-02 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min · Mesoscale
Scientists turned an interferometer into ultra-sensitive scales to measure the hidden complexity of anyons — a step towards error-free quantum computers.
Abstract

In the fractional quantum Hall state at ν=5/2, particles are born with extra entropy tied to their quantum dimension d, which is the square root of two (a measure of the particle's complexity). Direct measurement is tricky. Scientists have devised an interferometer where the charge of a microscopic antidot reveals itself through jumps in the interference phase. This nonlocal approach extracts the entropy at intermediate temperatures.

Hidden in thin semiconductor films are anyons — traveling particles that remember their path. Their memory was predicted by Frank Wilczek, and Alexei Kitaev showed how to use it to build a quantum computer that is immune to errors. But first, one needs to measure the anyon's entropy — a measure of its hidden complexity, or memory capacity. The higher it is, the more information the particle can hold. Physicists proposed weighing the anyon on ultra-sensitive electrical 'scales' — an interferometer. When a particle jumps into a trap inside the device, the current starts to tremble, changing intensity in jumps. This is like scales that oscillate more strongly the more facets the diamond being weighed has. By analyzing the statistics of these jumps, scientists recover the charge (just one quarter of an electron’s charge!) and the number of hidden states. This is how quantum sensing works — a delicate measurement without destruction. Strikingly, for the most promising type of anyons, the entropy equals exactly √2 ≈ 1.414 — a mathematical constant from geometry, rarely seen in particle physics. Computer simulations (numerical modeling) confirm: for the '5/2' state, the entropy of a single anyon is about 0.35 of Boltzmann's constant — almost half that of an electron. The entire process is immune to quantum decoherence, so quantum measurements become a practical verification tool. Experimental confirmation of these numbers will be long-awaited proof of the existence of anyons with memory and will pave the way to topological computers.

🎯 The anyon's entropy (0.35 of Boltzmann's constant) is almost half that of an electron — as if nature archives information, packing it into these particles with particular economy.

🎬 In the future, such 'scales' could become a routine inspection tool in the manufacturing of quantum chips.

\Delta S = k_B \log d
The change in entropy upon adding a non-Abelian anyon is proportional to the logarithm of its quantum dimension d.
\Delta S = k_B \int_{n_{g1}}^{n_{g2}} \frac{\partial \langle N \rangle}{\partial T} d n_g
Integrating the derivative of the average charge with respect to temperature gives the total entropy change upon varying the gate voltage.
