Astronomers have for the first time found the four-carbon sugar erythrulose in a cold molecular cloud near the Galactic center, proving that life's building blocks are synthesized directly in space.

Erythrulose—a sugar familiar to self-tanning enthusiasts—floats in a cloud near the Galactic center. In frosty silence, on icy dust grains, molecules self-assemble into the patterns of life. Chemistry without test tubes, on a galactic scale. Fred Hoyle dreamed that clouds sow life; now we find sugared pollen inside them. Perhaps our biochemistry began from an alien recipe.

🎯 Erythrulose is the active ingredient in self-tanners, giving skin a bronze tint. Now we know this substance floats in the cloud G+0.693, 27,000 light-years away from us.

🎬 In 'The Black Cloud,' Fred Hoyle imagined interstellar clouds as almost intelligent beings. Today we find sugars in them—real building blocks of biochemistry—and the fiction gains factual footing: clouds can indeed spread a prebiotic 'seed' throughout the Galaxy.
