In icy clouds, C₁₁H₉⁺ ions are like bees: they carry carbon fragments, stitching aromatic rings—harbingers of life.

In the hearts of cold clouds, where near-absolute zero reigns, carbon ions swarm like bees. They graft methyl groups onto aromatic rings, creating stable cations—seeds of cosmic organics. Perhaps this is how the universe embroiders molecular patterns that lead to life.

🎯 Acenaphthene from coal tar served for centuries as a raw material for dyes. Now its 'cosmic relatives' help us understand how carbon complexity is born in interstellar darkness.

🎬 Remarkably, PAHs as building blocks of prebiotic chemistry echo Fred Hoyle’s novel ‘The Black Cloud’—where an interstellar cloud itself was alive. Reality is no less fantastical: clouds are teeming with complex organics.
