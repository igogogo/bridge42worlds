Physics helps neural networks find lensed gravitational waves ✦

Original: "Identifying lensed gravitational waves with physics-informed posterior learning"
· Tian-Yang Sun, Xiao Guo, Jing-Fei Zhang, Xin Zhang
arXiv:2607.03885 · 2026-07-04 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min
General Relativity Cosmology Instrumentation HEP Phenomenology
A hybrid method combines neural network estimation of source parameters and direct waveform analysis to separate lensed mergers from overlaps of independent events.
Abstract

A ranking method based on physically motivated posterior learning is proposed for identifying gravitationally lensed gravitational-wave signals. It exploits geometric-optics consistency: lensing alters amplitudes, arrival times, and Morse phase shifts while preserving the source’s intrinsic phase evolution. The posterior distribution for the detector-frame chirp mass and symmetric mass ratio is estimated using a simulation-trained neural network and combined with direct waveform shape features. Training is performed on generalized simulations; test lenses — point mass, singular isothermal sphere, ellipsoid, and shear-perturbed models — are reserved for out-of-sample evaluation. For an observationally motivated binary black hole population, the combined ranking boosts detection efficiency from 20.8% to 35.2% at a 1% false alarm threshold calibrated on a control sample of unrelated mergers. The network signal-to-noise ratio required for 50% efficiency decreases from 45.3 to 33.5, corresponding to a 1.35-fold increase in equivalent distance.

Context

Gravitational lensing of gravitational waves is a powerful tool: repeated images of a single merger allow weighing compact lenses, searching for clumps of dark matter, and measuring cosmological distances. However, overlapping unrelated black hole mergers within a short time window can mimic a lensed signal, complicating the search. Event ranking is needed that does not require a pre-known lens model. This work addresses exactly that challenge, opening the door to routine searches for lensed waves in LIGO data.

Methods

The authors construct a three-stage neural network. The first stage is a direct classifier based on a convolutional network, operating on time series from two detectors. The second stage is a neural posterior estimator of common source parameters in the phase plane (logarithm of detector-frame redshifted chirp mass and symmetric mass ratio). This estimator is trained only on lensed examples, extracting information about source consistency. The third stage is a fusion classifier that combines waveform feature representation with an unordered set of posterior samples via an attention mechanism. Training is performed on synthetic data with arbitrary image parameters (number from 2 to 5, random delays, magnification factors, and Morse phase shifts). Validation is carried out on four physical lens models: point mass, singular isothermal sphere (SIS), singular isothermal ellipsoid (SIE), and SIE with external shear. Importantly, these models were not used in training, testing the generalization capability.

Results

Averaged over all test lenses and signal-to-noise ratios (SNR), the fusion classifier achieved an AUC ROC of 0.844 compared to 0.785 for the direct method, and at a strict 0.1% false alarm threshold, recall rose from 0.020 to 0.055. Tied to a realistic black hole mass distribution (from observational data), detection efficiency at 1% false alarms improved from 20.8% to 35.2%, and the required network SNR for 50% efficiency dropped from 45.3 to 33.5—equivalent to a 1.35-fold increase in survey distance. The greatest gains occur at intermediate SNRs and for SIE/SIE+Shear type lenses, where simple waveform metrics are less reliable. The tail of false alarms is caused by bright, temporally compact, and partially mass-consistent overlaps that "fool" the direct classifier, but the additional common-source test filters out most of them.

Implications

The work demonstrates that a physical principle—the preservation of phase evolution under lensing—can be effectively embedded in machine learning without an explicit lens model. This turns the uncertainty of the posterior parameter distribution into a useful classification feature. The method opens up prospects for automatic lens searches in growing gravitational-wave catalogs, where manual analysis becomes impossible. Moreover, the approach is physically transparent: it relies on the fundamental property of gravitational lensing in the geometric optics approximation, facilitating the interpretation of decisions.

Future development

Further development includes incorporating spins, precession, and wave optics effects (diffraction on small lenses), as well as connecting information about sky position (detector response). As the detector network expands (LIGO-India, KAGRA), the consistency of sky coordinates will become a stricter criterion. Applying the method to real O4 data and future observing runs, with threshold calibration on a realistic overlap population, is also promising.

Impact

The results will impact observational cosmology (standard sirens), compact object astrophysics, dark matter searches, and tests of general relativity. The method can also be adapted for data from other gravitational-wave detectors, including space-based ones (LISA).

Next steps

It is necessary to calibrate false alarms on the population of events from current LIGO/Virgo catalogs and assess sensitivity to lenses of various masses. It is also important to investigate robustness against non-stationary noise and glitches.

Key open problems

The article is directly connected to the problem of identifying lensed gravitational waves in realistic data, where unequivocally confirmed events are still lacking. This is part of a broader challenge—using gravitational lensing to probe the distribution of dark matter, the nature of compact objects, and test cosmological models. Additionally, the work intersects with questions of machine learning reliability in astrophysics: how to ensure that the classifier relies on physical principles rather than simulation artifacts.
