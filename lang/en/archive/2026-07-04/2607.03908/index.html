Chemical Magic: How a Hydrogen Layer Creates Altermagnetism and Record Magnetoresistance ✦

Original: "Surface Functionalization Enables Two-Dimensional Altermagnetism and Giant Tunnel Magnetoresistance"
· Zhou Cui, Ziye Zhu, Bowen Hao, Xunkai Duan, Xuan Zhou, Yali Xie, Huali Yang, Baisheng Sa, Runwei Li, Tong Zhou
arXiv:2607.03908 · 2026-07-04 · CC BY 4.0 · 2 min
Materials
Chemical modification of a FeSe monolayer turns an ordinary antiferromagnet into an altermagnet with record tunneling magnetoresistance.
Abstract

Chemical treatment of one side of a monolayer of iron selenide turns it from an ordinary antiferromagnet into an altermagnet — a material where magnetic moments are compensated, but electrons with different spins still separate depending on the direction of motion. The key is in breaking certain symmetries (inversion and mirror) while preserving rotational symmetry, which gives d-wave spin splitting. Calculations show that such structures in tunnel junctions exhibit record tunneling magnetoresistance (up to 1870%) thanks to spin and direction filtering. This opens a simple chemical route to spintronic devices without stray fields.

1

In the grand symphony of magnetism, a duo long reigned: boisterous ferromagnetism and silent antiferromagnetism, where spin-soloists cancel each other out. But recently, a third voice joined the ensemble—altermagnetism. Imagine a conductor making the orchestra play soundlessly, yet each instrument carries its own part: an altermagnet separates electrons by spin without creating magnetization. The trouble is that in the flat world of two-dimensional materials, such a talent was almost unheard of—until chemists stepped in, wielding the tool of surface functionalization.

The FeSe monolayer, known for its record superconductivity above 100 K, proved to be an ideal platform. Simply attaching hydrogen atoms to just one side shatters the strict symmetry like a cracked mirror.

Numerical simulations show: a single surface hydrogenation, oxidation, or fluorination breaks inversion and mirror symmetries, transforming mundane spin degeneracy into exquisite d-wave splitting. In the hydrogenated iron-selenide monolayer (Fe₂Se₂H), pairs of Weyl point-antipodes with opposite Chern numbers are born, and spin-orbit coupling opens a topological gap. Thus begins the subtle dance of quantum fields: the wave-particle duality of electrons acquires precise spin polarization—they are not just particles, but waves with perfectly tuned orientation.

Transport calculations in structures like Au/Fe₂Se₂H/barrier/Fe₂Se₂H/Au revealed giant tunneling magnetoresistance—1870%. That’s almost 20 times higher than modern commercial magnetic tunnel junctions. When the Néel vectors in the two layers are parallel, the spin filters are aligned, and current flows freely; when antiparallel, the channel is blocked. Like a lock obedient to a chemical key, the device controls the flow of spin-polarized electrons. The Pauli principle, forbidding two electrons from occupying the same state, here turns into selective transmission: one channel is a high-speed freeway, the other a solid wall.

Looking ahead, chemical engineering can be extended to other antiferromagnetic monolayers. Moreover, combining with FeSe’s innate superconductivity opens a path to hybrid devices where altermagnetic and superconducting correlations intertwine in novel quantum states. This discovery is already changing the rules of the game: instead of passively searching for altermagnets, scientists gain an active method to construct them, and the record magnetoresistance promises spintronic memory and logic with previously unattainable density and speed.

🎯 The FeSe monolayer is a record-holder among chalcogenides: its superconducting temperature surpasses 100 K. Altermagnets, theoretically described just a few years ago, had until now eluded direct observation in two-dimensional form due to intricate symmetry requirements.
