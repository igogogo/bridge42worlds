Inflation in Generalized Entropic Cosmology and f(Q)-Gravity from ACT Data ✦

Original: "ACT-DR6 consistent inflation in generalised entropic cosmology and $$f(Q)$$ gravity"
· Shin'ichi Nojiri, Sergei D. Odintsov
arXiv:2607.06582 · 2026-07-04 · CC BY · 3 min · General Relativity Cosmology HEP Theory
Entropic cosmology and f(Q)-gravity allow us to build inflationary models that agree with the latest data from the ACT telescope.
Abstract

Within the framework of generalized entropic cosmology and its equivalent f(Q)-gravity, inflationary models have been built that are consistent with the latest ACT DR6, Planck, and BAO data. Using a reconstruction scheme, explicit inflationary solutions satisfying observational constraints have been obtained. For f(Q)-gravity with a scalar field, inflation compatible with ACT is achieved for a broad class of f(Q) through the choice of potentials. Cosmology based on generalized entropy (encompassing Tsallis, Rényi, Barrow, and other entropies) has been investigated, and the corresponding Friedmann equations derived. By utilizing the correspondence between generalized entropy and f(Q)-gravity, generalized entropic inflation passing the ACT DR6 test is obtained. It is demonstrated that for any such entropy, ACT-consistent inflation is possible with a suitable scalar potential. The results hint at the existence of a unified entropic mechanism governing the early Universe.

Context

The latest data from the ACT (Atacama Cosmology Telescope) refined the spectral index of primordial perturbations n_s, pushing its median value upward compared to previous Planck measurements. This forces a rethink of many inflationary models and prompts the search for new theoretical frameworks that can explain the observations. One promising direction is modified theories of gravity and entropic cosmology, which connects gravity to thermodynamics, following the ideas of Bekenstein, Hawking, and Guth. Understanding the fundamental entropy that governed the early universe might shed light on the nature of inflation and quantum gravity.

Methods

The authors use f(Q)-gravity, a theory based on the non-metricity of spacetime, where gravity is described not by curvature but by the non-metricity scalar Q. To obtain inflationary solutions, they apply the reconstruction method: instead of solving equations for a given f(Q), they find the form of f(Q) that reproduces the desired evolution of the Hubble parameter H(t). Two parameterizations of H are considered: one smoothly evolving from the inflationary stage to a radiation-dominated phase, and one in terms of e-foldings. To account for radiation production at the end of inflation, effective source terms are introduced. Then, via the previously established correspondence between f(Q)-gravity and generalized entropy, the obtained f(Q) models are translated into the language of entropic cosmology. A key element is the use of the first law of thermodynamics applied to the cosmological horizon, where horizon entropy is linked to the Bekenstein–Hawking entropy and its generalizations.

Results

The result is explicit f(Q) functions that yield inflation with parameters n_s ≈ 0.976 and r ≈ 0.012, precisely matching the combined ACT, Planck, and BAO data. For instance, one model is described by the expression f(Q) = (1/(2κ²)) ( –2Q + 24H₀² + 4(6H₀²)^{1/2}(–Q)^{1/2} Arctan[(–Q/(6H₀²))^{1/2}] – C(–Q)^{1/2} ) with an additional source term modeling radiation creation. Another model, based on e-foldings, leads to f(Q) expressed through an incomplete beta function. The corresponding generalized entropies, derived from these f(Q) models, show a sharp increase near the inflationary plateau, reflecting the rapid decrease of vacuum energy during inflation. These entropies extend the classical results of Bekenstein and Hawking. It is shown that without a scalar field, only a limited class of entropies gives realistic inflation, but introducing a scalar field with a suitable potential yields ACT-consistent inflation for nearly any generalized entropy.

Implications

This work links observational data to deep principles: the thermodynamic nature of gravity and the microstructure of spacetime. It demonstrates that modified gravity and entropic approaches are not just mathematical exercises but can be tested and constrained by real observations. The highlighted class of entropies may point to the microscopic statistics of states in the early universe and serve as a bridge to quantum gravity.

Future development

The topic could evolve towards studying reheating in entropic cosmology without an inflaton. The absence of a scalar field makes particle production after inflation nontrivial: one possible mechanism could be quantum effects in the expanding universe or thermodynamic fluctuations of the horizon. Detailed study of the spectra of produced particles, primordial gravitational waves, and baryogenesis in such models could provide new observational signatures.

Impact

The results will influence the interpretation of CMB data, the theory of the early universe, and quantum cosmology. They also stimulate the development of alternative gravity theories and their phenomenological predictions.

Next steps

Next steps include a detailed calculation of the reheating process in models without an inflaton, analysis of cosmological perturbations, and prediction of the spectrum of relic gravitational waves for the proposed entropy models.

Key open problems

The research directly addresses the problem of quantum gravity and the microscopic nature of spacetime. It also touches on the problem of initial conditions for inflation and the origin of entropy in the observable universe.
