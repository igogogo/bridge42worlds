Quantum Tunneling Mpemba Effect: Paradoxical Cooling in Open Double-Well Potentials ✦

Original: "Quantum tunneling Mpemba effect"
· Hisao Hayakawa, Satoshi Takada
arXiv:2607.03845 · 2026-07-04 · CC0 · 4 min · Statistical Mech Quantum Physics
A hot quantum system can cool faster than a cold one thanks to tunneling and boundary absorption — this is the new quantum tunneling Mpemba effect.
Abstract

The quantum tunneling Mpemba effect in a symmetric double well with boundary sinks has been studied. Using non-Hermitian decomposition of the effective Hamiltonian, relaxation without quenches was investigated. It is proven that the nonmonotonicity of the coefficient a2(Ti) with respect to the initial temperature Ti is a universal topological property governed by the Sturm–Liouville theorem and invariant under system size changes. The peak arises from a geometric coincidence of localized states and extended non-Hermitian decay channels. Anomalies in S(t,Ti) and D(t,Ti) require a separation of scales: above-barrier escape must be much faster than tunneling decay (Γ2 >> Γ0, Γ1). The approach unites classical boundary dissipation with open quantum dynamics, opening possibilities for engineering nonequilibrium states via boundary losses.

Context

The Mpemba paradox, where hot water freezes faster than cold, has long sparked debate, but in the past decade it found rigorous explanation within non-equilibrium statistical mechanics. In classical systems, boundaries play a key role: the effect 'likes to hit the wall'. In parallel, the quantum Mpemba effect is being actively studied, but until now there has been no clear link between coherent tunneling and spatial boundary losses. The new study fills this gap by formulating the problem in continuous coordinate space using Schrödinger quantum mechanics and a non-Hermitian Hamiltonian.

Methods

The authors considered a single particle in a symmetric double-well potential with a complex absorbing potential (CAP) near the boundaries. Within the framework of von Neumann open quantum systems, instead of the standard Lindblad equation, they used a non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian H_eff = H0 − iW, where W(x) is a smooth spatial absorption profile. Then the evolution of the surviving part of the ensemble is described by the non-Hermitian Schrödinger equation iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = H_eff ψ. Thanks to superposition of states and wave-particle duality, the wave function is expanded in a biorthogonal basis of right and left eigenstates of H_eff. The model relies on quantum field ideas of open systems, and the superposition principle guarantees the linearity of the expansion. Numerically, the problem was solved on a uniform grid using the finite difference method followed by diagonalization.

Results

The central result is the proof that the coefficient a2(Ti), responsible for the first excited even decay mode, has a non-monotonic peak at an intermediate initial temperature Ti. This peak is universal: at low Ti, the contribution vanishes due to weak overlap of deeply localized ground states with the delocalized mode φ2; at high Ti, the coefficient vanishes due to equal population of all states. The existence of the maximum is guaranteed by the Sturm–Liouville theorem and does not depend on system size L, which starkly contrasts with the classical counterpart. For observing anomalous crossings in the survival probability S(t,Ti) and trace distance, it is necessary that the over-barrier escape rate Γ2 significantly exceeds the tunneling decay rates of the deep doublets Γ0 and Γ1. Numerical simulations confirmed these predictions, demonstrating clear crossings of S(t,Ti) curves for different Ti, as well as the temperature invariance of the a2(Ti) peak when varying L. Thus, the anomalous acceleration of relaxation for heated quantum states is a consequence of optimal population of delocalized channels that effectively absorb the particle at the boundaries. This mechanism resembles quantum decoherence, causing effective wave function collapse upon measuring survival.

Implications

The results merge the physical picture of the classical Mpemba effect, governed by boundaries, with quantum-mechanical coherence and tunneling. This opens up a new class of problems in quantum information science and quantum measurements, where boundary losses can be designed for accelerated preparation of strongly non-equilibrium states. The universality of the a2(Ti) peak also points to a deep connection between wave function topology and thermodynamic properties of open systems.

Future development

Further research will aim at generalizing to asymmetric potentials, where the parity selection rule is lifted, but the peak persists due to the ordering of the number of nodes in the wave functions. Experimental realization is possible in platforms with ultracold atoms, for instance in Bose-Einstein condensates with optical traps, where the potential shape can be precisely controlled and controllable losses introduced. Also promising is the study of the role of non-Hermitian exceptional points and multiple crossings in the trace distance, which could lead to a multiple quantum Mpemba effect.

Impact

This work will impact the development of quantum thermodynamics, open quantum system theory, and quantum computing, providing a new tool for controlling the relaxation of qubits and quantum simulators.

Next steps

Key steps include experimental verification of the predictions in trapped ions or ultracold atom systems, as well as studying multimode coherent effects, including verifying the existence of multiple crossings in nonlinear state metrics.

Key open problems

The research touches on fundamental problems of the quantum-classical transition and the foundations of statistical mechanics: how the discreteness of the energy spectrum and the topology of wave functions give rise to macroscopic anomalous behavior that is independent of system size. It also sheds light on the nature of irreversibility in quantum systems interacting with an environment.

🎯 Unlike its classical counterpart, which, according to recent work, 'likes to hit the wall', the quantum Mpemba effect turns out to be remarkably robust to system size: increasing the distance to the absorbing boundaries does not change the optimal 'hot' start temperature. This counterintuitive result is a direct consequence of the discrete structure of quantized states and the precise tuning of the number of nodes in the wave function.
