The Fate of a Hot Jupiter: How a Primordial Black Hole Rewrites Planetary Systems ✦

Original: "Three body Simulations of a Primordial Black Hole Encounter with the TOI 2796 System"
· S. V. Vereshchagin, M. I. Kamentsev
arXiv:2607.03724 · 2026-07-04 · CC0 · 3 min · Exoplanets Galaxies
Numerical simulations show that a flyby of a primordial black hole can eject a planet, capture it, or form a triple system.
Abstract

Numerical modeling of the close approach of a primordial black hole (PBH) to the TOI 2796 system, containing a hot Jupiter. Over a 100-day interval, three outcomes were obtained: (i) ejection of the planet from the system; (ii) formation of a bound triple system (semimajor axis ≈ 1 AU, orbital period ≈ 5 days); (iii) capture of the planet by the PBH into a binary system (0.0194 AU, 15 days), which leaves the star. The results underscore the rich dynamical diversity of interactions with PBHs and their potential role in reshaping planetary system architectures.

Context

Why this matters: Primordial black holes (black holes), born in the first instants after the Big Bang, remain one of the most intriguing candidates for dark matter. If they truly roam the cosmos, their gravitational influence on exoplanetary systems might be the only observable signature. The TOI-2796 system with a hot Jupiter on a tight orbit is an ideal testbed for such scenarios. It was Stephen Hawking who showed that lightweight PBHs evaporate, but more massive ones survive to this day, and Vera Rubin revealed the problem of hidden mass.

Methods

How they did it: The dynamics of three bodies — the star, planet, and PBH — were computed by directly integrating Newton's law of universal gravitation. An adaptive LSODA solver from the SciPy library was used, automatically switching between Adams and BDF methods. The three-body problem, known for its chaos since Henri Poincaré, demanded tight error control (rtol=1e-8, atol=1e-8). To find the rare planet-capture by a black hole, a neural network was trained on 475 numerical simulation runs, varying the impact parameter and PBH mass.

Results

What they found: Over 100 days of integration, three qualitatively distinct outcomes emerged. (1) Planet ejection: with a PBH mass of 15 M☉, the exoplanet becomes a free-floating planet and moves beyond 2 AU from the star within 16 days. (2) Triple system formation: a 500 M☉ PBH forms a stable hierarchical configuration with a semi-major axis of about 1 AU and an orbital period of ~5 days; the system remained bound throughout the interval. (3) Planet capture by PBH: at 300 M☉, the neural network found a scenario where the planet transfers to an orbit around the black hole at a distance of 0.0194 AU and period of 15 days, and the resulting pair flies off to 51.5 AU from the star in 100 days. These numbers show just how radically a system's fate can change after a single close encounter.

Implications

What this means for science: The results show that PBHs can act as disruptors or architects of planetary systems. If dark matter is at least partially made of PBHs, then observed orbital anomalies of exoplanets could become their long-sought signature. Moreover, the ejection or capture mechanism naturally explains the existence of free-floating planets and unusual tight binaries.

Future development

Where this could go: Future work will add relativistic corrections and tidal effects to the Newtonian model, and account for the system passing through star clusters. Machine learning methods will enable targeted searches for characteristic “gravitational fingerprints” in gravitational microlensing data from surveys like Rubin and Roman.

Impact

The findings will affect the interpretation of observational data on exoplanetary systems and strategies for indirect detection of dark matter.

Next steps

Next steps include modeling a sample of real systems with different architectures and analyzing the cumulative effect of multiple PBH flybys over cosmological time.

Key open problems

This work is directly tied to the problem of dark matter's nature: if PBHs exist, they could compose part of it. The chaotic dynamics of the three-body problem remain a fundamental challenge in celestial mechanics, and interpreting potential observational signatures requires developing new analysis methods.
