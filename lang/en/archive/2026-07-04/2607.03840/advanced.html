Neutron Stars with a Dark Core: What Does an Agnostic View Reveal? ✦

Original: "Neutron stars with an agnostic Dark sector: Core and Halo configurations from a two-fluid approach"
· Asit karan, Asim Kumar Saha, Tuhin Malik, Constança Providência, Ritam Mallick
arXiv:2607.03840 · 2026-07-04 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · High Energy HEP Phenomenology
How an unbiased approach to dark matter inside neutron stars sets universal constraints and sheds light on the nature of hidden mass.
Abstract

An agnostic model of neutron stars with dark matter admixture is constructed in a two-fluid formalism. The hadronic equation of state relies on chiral effective field theory at low densities and perturbative QCD at high densities, with a speed-of-sound parameterization in the intermediate region. Dark matter as a Fermi gas, its high-density equation of state constrained only by thermodynamic consistency via the speed of sound, without bias toward stiff or soft variants. It is found: light dark matter creates an extended halo and increases tidal deformability; heavy dark matter forms a compact core and decreases it. Observational data switch: for light dark matter, the main constraint comes from gravitational waves; for heavy, from NICER. The dark matter fraction fDM ≲ 0.11 (1σ) for light masses. The approach is weakly dependent on the microphysics of the dark sector, providing conservative bounds. Stars with similar masses but different deformability could become a clear sign of dark matter.

Context

The mystery of dark matter, making up about a quarter of the universe's energy (according to data from the cosmic microwave background and expansion), has long extended beyond the Standard Model. In parallel, neutron stars, born in the gravitational collapse of supernovae and possessing fields second only to black holes, have become unique laboratories for studying matter under extreme conditions. While on the macroscale dark matter only manifests gravitationally, microscopic candidates—from axions to weakly interacting particles—could accumulate in compact objects, altering their properties. Fritz Zwicky, who first suspected the existence of hidden mass, and Karl Schwarzschild, who obtained the first exact solution to Einstein's equations, laid the foundations, and today gravitational waves and X-ray spectroscopy allow us to peer inside neutron stars.

Methods

To avoid the trap of selecting a specific dark matter model, the authors applied an agnostic approach already tested for nuclear matter. The equation of state for ordinary matter is built on chiral effective field theory at low densities and perturbative QCD at high densities, with a flexible speed-of-sound interpolation in between, ensuring only thermodynamic stability and causality (the speed of sound does not exceed the speed of light). For dark matter, the same method is used: at low densities, a degenerate Fermi gas with a single parameter, the particle mass, and at higher densities, randomized speed-of-sound profiles without a high-density 'anchor.' This allowed the generation of about 10^5 configurations of neutron stars with a dark matter fraction from 1% to 15% for seven particle mass values (from 0.2 to 1.1 GeV). Then, the two-fluid Einstein equations (modified Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff equations) were solved jointly with equations for tidal deformability.

Results

The simulations revealed a clear division: light particles (mass ≤ 0.3 GeV) form extended dark halos around the nuclear core, increasing the effective radius and tidal deformability, which is proportional to R^5. Heavy particles (≥ 0.7 GeV), on the other hand, concentrate in a compact core, making the star more compact and reducing deformability. Comparison with radio astronomy data (masses of pulsars up to 2.35 M⊙), X-ray observations from NICER, and the gravitational-wave event GW170817 allowed constraints to be set. For light dark matter, the main filter is tidal deformability: at a fraction above 11%, predictions exceed the range of 70 ≤ Λ ≤ 580 for a 1.4 M⊙ star. For heavy dark matter, the NICER mass-radius measurements are more important. Thus, current data constrain the dark matter fraction to f_DM ≲ 0.11, and this constraint is conservative and independent of the microphysics of the dark sector.

Implications

The work demonstrates for the first time that even without a specific dark matter theory, meaningful information can be extracted from astrophysical observations. The shift of the dominant constraint—from tidal deformability to mass-radius measurements as the particle mass increases—provides a new diagnostic tool. If two neutron stars with similar masses but sharply different deformabilities are found in the future, this will be a 'smoking gun' indicating different dark matter content. Moreover, the approach combines data from LIGO/Virgo and NICER, strengthening the methodology of multimessenger astronomy.

Future development

In the next generation of experiments, more precise mass and radius measurements are expected from NICER and new X-ray observatories, along with an increase in the number of registered neutron star mergers by gravitational-wave detectors (Advanced LIGO, Virgo, KAGRA, and in the future, the Einstein Telescope). This will narrow the allowed parameter space for dark matter and possibly distinguish between fermionic and bosonic particle nature. The agnostic approach can also be extended to the study of quark stars and hybrid configurations involving dark matter.

Impact

The results touch on several areas at once: from elementary particle physics (search for dark matter candidates) to gravitational-wave astronomy and the theory of dense matter. The constraints, obtained without reference to a specific model, serve as a guide for theorists and experimentalists.

Next steps

Next steps include analyzing the dependence of the results on the low-density 'anchor' for dark matter and exploring bosonic models instead of fermionic ones. It is also important to account for rotation and magnetic fields of neutron stars, and for degenerate Fermi gases, to apply more refined quantum statistical methods.

Key open problems

The study is directly linked to two fundamental problems in physics: the nature of dark matter (one of the most important tasks beyond the Standard Model) and the equation of state of superdense matter (a regime where quantum chromodynamics is non-perturbative). It also highlights the need for quantum gravity to describe possible singularities at the center of a neutron star and the role of the dark sector in strong fields, where general relativity is tested.

🎯 If a neutron star contained enough dark matter, its radius could exceed the size of the nuclear component several times over, and then what we see as the star's surface would actually be just the edge of a 'dark cloud'—with the nuclear matter itself remaining hidden deep inside.

🎬 While in science fiction dark matter is often portrayed as a source of incredible energy or a portal to other worlds (for example, in the series 'Dark Matter'), reality offers an equally captivating picture: perhaps millions of neutron stars in our Galaxy carry invisible 'hearts' of dark matter, and we are only learning to find them.
