The Neutrino Chord: How a Supernova Will Unveil the Hidden Mass Order ✦

Original: "Neutrino mass ordering from the next Galactic supernova at DUNE, HK, and JUNO"
· Prantik Sarmah, Sovan Chakraborty, Abinash Medhi, Debanjan Bose, Moon Moon Devi
arXiv:2606.06580v1 · 2026-06-04 · CC BY 4.0 · ⏱ 4 min · HEP Phenomenology High Energy
A neutrino shower from the next massive star explosion in the Milky Way will finally reveal how the masses of the three neutrino types are arranged—normal or inverted.
Abstract

A neutrino signal from a nearby galactic supernova will help determine the neutrino mass hierarchy (which type is the heaviest). Two indicators are considered: the neutronization burst (a sharp peak of electron neutrinos in the first ~20–30 ms) and the rise time of the electron antineutrino flux. Simulations for the DUNE, Hyper-Kamiokande, and JUNO detectors showed that the burst distinguishes normal and inverted hierarchy with >6σ confidence. Adding rise-time data, like using both a lightning flash and a thunderclap, increases certainty. Thus, the next supernova explosion could finally solve the mystery.

Somewhere in the galactic bulge, a massive star lies dormant, ready to trade quiet burning for collapse. Its core will exhaust its fuel, and in a fraction of a second, a neutron star or black hole will be born, releasing 10⁵⁸ neutrinos—almost all the energy of the explosion. This stream of weakly interacting particles will reach Earth after tens of thousands of years, but it will carry a record of processes hidden from telescopes and—most prized of all—reveal the neutrino mass hierarchy.

The puzzle has plagued physicists ever since Wolfgang Pauli predicted these particles: the three known neutrino types have minuscule masses, but which is the lightest and which is the heaviest? The normal hierarchy is like three strings of different tensions: the first two vibrate easily, while the third sounds low and massive. The inverted order flips this—one of the high-pitched voices suddenly becomes the heaviest, like a dissonant chord in a cosmic symphony.

The key to the answer is the neutronization burst: a lightning-fast peak of electron neutrinos occurring in the first milliseconds of collapse, as core protons greedily capture electrons. This 'cry of a newborn neutron star' acts as a litmus test: in the inverted hierarchy it undergoes almost no distortion and is registered by detectors, while in the normal one it fades away, morphing into other flavors due to oscillations. DUNE, with its liquid argon, will record up to 100 events in 20 milliseconds—as many as a whole year of solar neutrino observations. The difference between scenarios exceeds 6σ: a single cosmic explosion delivers an unambiguous verdict. In effect, nature runs an experiment that no earthly accelerator could afford.

At the heart of a collapsing supernova, density reaches nuclear levels, and entropy reaches extreme values. Under these conditions, neutrinos can undergo fast collective oscillations, swapping flavors in sync, like dancers in a single impulse. Yet even with complete mixing, the neutronization peak retains its discriminatory power, opening the way to test the theory of collective effects itself.

A second indicator comes later, during the accretion phase: infalling matter heats the core, and intense fluxes of heavy-lepton neutrinos are born. Their luminosity rises faster than that of electron antineutrinos, and this asymmetry colors the detected signals depending on the hierarchy. The formula for oscillated antineutrinos in the normal hierarchy, F = cos²θ₁₂ F⁰(anti-νₑ) + sin²θ₁₂ F⁰(νₓ), is the quintessence of mixing: the original emissions blend like paints weighted by the angle θ₁₂, changing the 'color' of the signal. The ratio of events accumulated in 20 and 100 milliseconds becomes a reliable indicator: Hyper-Kamiokande will reach nearly 5σ, and a joint analysis with JUNO and DUNE turns an educated guess into firm knowledge.

Capturing this neutrino score in real time would usher in the era of multi-messenger astronomy, where gravitational waves, light, and particles merge into a single narrative. Establishing the mass hierarchy would be a triumph foreseen by John Wheeler, who spoke of extracting information from stellar interiors precisely through neutrinos. Such a result would not only constrain theories beyond the Standard Model but also shed light on the Big Bang and the formation of dark matter—for lightweight neutrinos, a billionth the mass of a proton, influenced the growth of cosmic structures. Just as the cosmic microwave background holds the echo of a hot universe, the neutrino background remembers its first seconds, and the correct mass order is the missing piece of that picture.

The next galactic supernova won't just light up the sky—it will strike the detectors like a neutrino tuning fork, its frequency indicating the correct mass arrangement. Upgrades to observatories and 3D collapse models will reduce errors, and rapid alert protocols will ensure synchronous responses from telescopes. On the horizon lies the equation of state for nuclear matter at extreme densities, the key to neutron star physics and to how the universe forged cosmic dust and heavy elements. An explosion once seen as mere destruction becomes an instrument of knowledge, widening the horizons of an expanding universe.

🎯 In the first 20–30 milliseconds of the neutronization peak, the DUNE detector will register as many neutrinos as it captures from a whole year of observing the Sun. And the total energy carried away by all neutrinos over a few seconds exceeds the optical luminosity of the entire Milky Way over the same period by a factor of a hundred.
