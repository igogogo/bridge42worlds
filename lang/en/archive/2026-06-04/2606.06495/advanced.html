The Trembling Electron and the Mystery of the Universe's Expansion express

Original: "What it takes to solve the Hubble tension through Modifications of Cosmological Recombination II: in light of ACT DR6 and DESI DR2"
· Nanoom Lee, Tianji Zhou
arXiv:2606.06495 · 2026-06-04 · CC BY · 1 min · Cosmology
Tiny fluctuations of the electron's mass in the first moments after the Big Bang almost settle the debate over how fast the universe is expanding.
Abstract

Data-driven solutions are constructed to alleviate the Hubble tension, incorporating new ACT DR6 and DESI DR2 data. A minimal modification of the recombination history via a time-varying electron mass m_e(z) is explored, which raises the H_0 value obtained from CMB data to the SH0ES measurement without degrading the fit to observations. A joint analysis of Planck and ACT data, including lensing, shows that the perturbed correction to m_e(z) completely eliminates the tension and exhibits the same oscillatory structure as in previous Planck-only works, confirming its robustness. Furthermore, the solution mitigates the S_8 tension. However, after adding BAO DESI DR2 data, the perturbed m_e(z) modification cannot fully resolve the Hubble tension. This reflects a fundamental limitation: raising H_0 by altering recombination leads to a lower Ω_m, conflicting with late-time cosmological observations.

380,000 years after the Big Bang, the universe had cooled enough for scattered particles to combine into hydrogen atoms. This stage, recombination, was like a first breath: light detached from matter and raced through space.

Ever since, astronomers have measured the speed of the universe's expansion in two ways. One relies on that ancient light itself (data from the Planck satellite and the ACT telescope). The other tracks the flashes of distant supernovae. But these two 'clocks' stubbornly show different times. This discrepancy is called the Hubble tension, after Edwin Hubble, who discovered the expansion.

In a recent paper, physicists asked: what if the pendulum inside those cosmic clocks had shifted slightly? The electron's mass could play the pendulum's role. If it had quivered by a minuscule amount—less than one ten-thousandth of a percent—the readings from the 'early' clocks would nearly match the 'late' ones. Moreover, this would also ease another contradiction: how dark matter clumps together. But as soon as scientists overlaid a map of present-day galaxies (from the DESI survey), the synchronization broke. Tweaking the expansion rate inadvertently alters the matter density, which no longer aligns with what we observe today.

The electron's mass is one of the constants of the Standard Model of physics. To suggest it varied is to tamper with the immutability of the laws. Yet cosmology demands it more and more insistently.

🎯 The electron's mass determines how strongly atoms bind. If it were slightly different, stars would burn differently, and life might never have emerged.
