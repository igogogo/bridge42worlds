Growth, Bending, and Swirling: How Polymerizing Filaments Generate Motion ✦

Original: "Growing, Buckling, and Swirling: motility from polymerization"
· Naveen Kumar D, Michael J. Shelley, Brato Chakrabarti
arXiv:2607.10446 · 2026-07-11 · CC BY 4.0 · 5 min · Soft Condensed Matter Fluid Dynamics
Theory shows that the growth of biopolymers on a microparticle surface makes it swim, rotate, or move in a spiral—without any molecular motors.
Abstract

A continuum model has been developed for motion at low Reynolds numbers, where active forces emerge self-consistently from nucleation, growth, catastrophes, and hydrodynamic interactions of polymerizing surface filaments. It is analytically shown that polymerization-induced compressive forces trigger a long-wavelength buckling instability, leading to spontaneous symmetry breaking of the filament carpet and large-scale flows. When the model is coupled to a force- and torque-free spheroidal body, diverse regimes appear—spontaneous rotation, directed motion, and chiral swimming—selected by the spatial arrangement of growing filaments. These results establish a general theoretical framework for motility driven by the collective dynamics of polymerizing filaments and suggest new principles for designing synthetic microswimmers.

Context

Movement in the world of low Reynolds numbers (typical for microorganisms) is fundamentally different from our macroscopic experience: here, inertial forces are negligible compared to viscous forces. In this regime, the Stokes equations are linear, and the superposition principle holds: the total velocity field is the sum of fields from each force source. However, this same linearity makes the flow time-reversible—Purcell's famous 'scallop theorem' states that symmetric motions do not produce net translation. Therefore, microorganisms must use irreversible deformations, and filament polymerization is one evolutionarily tested way to circumvent this constraint. Understanding how the collective behavior of many growing filaments generates directed motion is important not only for biology but also for creating autonomous microdevices.

Methods

The authors constructed a continuum model in which active forces arise self-consistently from polymerization kinetics, orientational dynamics, and hydrodynamic interactions. Each filament is treated as a rigid rod growing from an anchor on the surface; its orientation changes under the local fluid flow and an elastic restoring moment. Then an equation for the average orientation field (polarization) of the filaments is derived, closed through a moment approximation based on a kinetic model. The hydrodynamic problem is solved using a boundary integral method that relies on the superposition principle and requires a nontrivial self-consistency procedure, since forces depend on velocity gradients at the surface. For numerical simulations, a boundary integral method with fast multipole acceleration and high-order quadratures was used, along with an implicit scheme to account for feedback.

Results

Linear stability analysis in a half-space showed that a uniformly oriented carpet becomes unstable to long-wavelength bending perturbations when the dimensionless activity exceeds a critical threshold: ¯σ > χ + (¯λ+1)/¯ρ. In the nonlinear regime on a fixed sphere, symmetry spontaneously breaks, and the polarity swirls, forming two antisymmetric vortices with two +1 topological defects at the poles—a structure reminiscent of cytoplasmic flows in Drosophila oocytes. If the sphere is allowed to move freely, this swirling creates a constant torque, and the sphere starts rotating with a steady angular velocity. Since the sphere is free, it must satisfy conditions of zero net force and torque—consequences of the momentum conservation law and the angular momentum conservation law. With nonuniform nucleation distributions, much richer regimes emerge. An equatorial belt produces only rotation without translation, because forces balance each other; a polar patch creates both force and torque, causing the sphere to move along a circular trajectory. A four-point symmetric arrangement cancels the torque, and the sphere swims straight. Finally, a chiral (spiral) nucleation pattern leads to coupled motion: the sphere simultaneously rotates and translates along the spiral axis, with the direction determined by the 'handedness' of the spiral. In an oblate spheroid, the center-of-mass trajectory becomes helical—an effect caused by the anisotropy of hydrodynamic drag. Typical swimming speeds are 0.1–1 μm/s, comparable to speeds of real microorganisms.

Implications

This work provides a unified theoretical framework for describing motility driven by polymer growth and demonstrates that the spatial pattern of nucleation centers acts as a 'program code': its symmetry determines whether the body rotates, swims straight, or moves along a helical path. Unlike the classical squirmmer model, where the surface slip field is prescribed externally, here active forces and filament orientations are computed self-consistently. This approach is also applicable to other systems, such as cortical microtubules driven by molecular motors, allowing a unified description of motor and polymerization activity in cells. For astrobiology, it is important that the polymerization-based motility mechanism broadens the range of possible biosignatures: if microorganisms using filament growth for movement are found on exoplanets, their swimming trajectories could be recognized by Earth-based instruments. This is a direct consequence of Emmy Noether's theorem linking space symmetries to conservation laws. In nonequilibrium systems, the production of entropy determines the direction of processes, and our polymerization carpet is an example of an active system with continuous energy dissipation.

Future development

The next steps will include studying hydrodynamic interactions between several polymerization-driven swimmers, which could lead to collective effects such as schooling behavior or the formation of active crystals. Extending the model to long filaments (like those of Acetobacter xylinum), where the thin-layer approximation breaks down, will require accounting for steric interactions and large bending deformations—here hybrid discrete-continuum methods may be useful. In the long term, such models will allow engineers to design synthetic micro-robots with prescribed motility: by modifying the chemical pattern on a particle's surface, one could program its trajectory. The described phenomena, where reconnection of topological defects governs collective dynamics, find analogies at vastly different scales—from active galactic nuclei to turbulent flows—making this class of models interdisciplinary valuable.

Impact

The results will influence cellular biophysics (understanding motility of Listeria, Acetobacter), materials science (creation of active colloids), and microscale robotics.

Next steps

Experimental verification of predictions on synthetic colloids with tunable actin density and extension of the model to collectives of interacting swimmers.

Key open problems

This work directly addresses the fundamental problem of self-organization in nonequilibrium systems: how do local interaction rules (filament growth and reorientation) generate global motion? This resonates with open questions in the physics of active matter about spontaneous symmetry breaking and the emergence of directed transport in the absence of external fields.
