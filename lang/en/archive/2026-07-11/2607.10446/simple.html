How a Microbe Swims Without Arms or Legs ✦

Original: "Growing, Buckling, and Swirling: motility from polymerization"
· Naveen Kumar D, Michael J. Shelley, Brato Chakrabarti
arXiv:2607.10446 · 2026-07-11 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min · Soft Condensed Matter Fluid Dynamics
By sprouting threads on its surface, a microbe rolls through liquid like a fuzzy ball, propelled by its own growth.
Abstract

Some microbes get around by firing out flagella. But there's another way: they sprout growing filaments that push off surfaces like kayak paddles. Researchers have now developed a theory for how these filaments make micro-particles not just crawl but also spin, tracing out elaborate paths. Could this 'thread motor' become the blueprint for future microrobots?

In a fluid as thick as syrup, a microbe turns into a fuzzy ball. From its surface, ultra-thin threads shoot out—like microscopic harpoons. The push from each newly grown thread propels the cell forward. The pattern of growth points controls movement, like tuning rocket nozzles: by varying their arrangement, you can make the ball spin, swim straight, or spiral. With computer simulations, scientists confirmed this mechanics. Thread growth increases disorder—par for the course in living systems.

The bacterium Acetobacter xylinum, which creates the film in kombucha, grows cellulose fibers longer than its body and rolls like a fuzzy ball.
Slow but steady movement is a potential biosignature in alien oceans.
