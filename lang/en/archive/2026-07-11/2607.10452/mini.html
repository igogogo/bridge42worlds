Local Big Bang: Explosive Outflows at the Birth of Massive Stars ✦

Original: "Explosive Molecular Outflows"
· Luis A. Zapata
arXiv:2607.10452 · 2026-07-11 · CC BY · 1 min
Stellar Galaxies
Molecular streams expanding according to Hubble's law — but not over billions of light-years, just a few. A local Big Bang powered by gravity.
1

Five hundred years ago in the Orion Nebula, two (or more) protostars collided. The impact produced a fan of molecular jets that still fly at 350 km/s — and, like galaxies after the Big Bang, accelerate as they move away from the center. Gravity provided the energy. Such cosmic crashes happen once every 140 years and may explain how the most massive stars ignite.

🎯 The most famous explosive outflow in Orion happened just 500 years ago — literally yesterday on the cosmic clock. Today we observe the 'fingers' of the ejection, racing at 350 km/s, and a gaping void in the center — from which three massive stars were ejected.

E \approx \frac{G M_1 M_2}{R}
The gravitational energy released when two protostars approach to distance R is converted into the kinetic energy of the explosive outflow.
