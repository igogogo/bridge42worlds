Self-Assembly of Dodecagonal Quasicrystals in Dipolar Bose–Einstein Condensates with Optical Feedback ✦

Original: "Self-organized quasicrystals and their excitations in dipolar Bose-Einstein condensates via optical feedback"
· Liang-Jun He, Fabian Maucher, Yong-Chang Zhang
arXiv:2607.10141 · 2026-07-11 · CC BY · 3 min · Quantum Gases
Dipolar Bose–Einstein condensates with optical feedback spontaneously form dodecagonal quasicrystals boasting twelve-fold symmetry.
Abstract

Competing interactions with incommensurate characteristic lengths in a dipolar Bose-Einstein condensate can give rise to quasicrystalline structures lacking translational periodicity. It is shown that combining direct dipole-dipole interaction and photon-mediated interaction, induced by coupling to an excited state and precisely tuned optical feedback, creates two roton minima in the Bogoliubov excitation spectrum. This leads to the emergence of a stable phase of a dodecagonal quasicrystal with rotational symmetry of order 12, achievable under realistic experimental parameters. Additionally, a dynamic protocol for obtaining such states and a universal numerical method for calculating their collective excitations have been developed.

Context

The 1984 discovery of quasicrystals shattered a centuries-old dogma: that crystals must have translational periodicity. These oddball structures, flaunting sharp diffraction peaks and forbidden rotational symmetries, arise in systems with competing length scales. Classic examples are metal alloys with oscillating long-range potentials. Yet big questions linger about how they grow and whether they can exist in equilibrium in quantum systems at near-zero temperatures. Predicted by Bose and Einstein, Bose–Einstein condensates are an ideal sandbox for modeling such states, thanks to jaw-dropping control over atomic interactions. Throwing in electromagnetic forces via quantum optics lets us mimic complex effective potentials—exactly the trick used here.

Methods

The core idea is to use a quasi-2D dipolar condensate of dysprosium atoms placed in front of a mirror. Dipole-dipole forces provide anisotropic long-range interactions, while a laser beam passing through the condensate and bouncing back adds an oscillatory interaction that depends on transverse distance. This interferometric setup with a quarter-wave plate builds a V-type linkage between the ground state and two excited states. Adiabatically eliminating the field yields an effective potential V_opt ∝ cos(k_l r²/(4d)), which together with the dipole-dipole potential gives rise to two roton minima in the Bogoliubov spectrum—an instability rooted in Landau’s work on superfluidity. To find the ground state, we deploy numerical integration of the 2D Gross–Pitaevskii equation in imaginary time with periodic boundary conditions, and for excitations, a projection method maps the dodecagonal quasicrystal (a superposition of plane waves) onto a periodic lattice in four dimensions.

Results

The phase diagram, plotted against average density and scattering length ratio, reveals several phases: a uniform superfluid, a triangular lattice, ladder- and ring-like patterns, and finally, a dodecagonal quasicrystal with twelve-fold symmetry. In the language of quantum thermodynamics, all transitions between these states are first-order phase transitions. The magic ratio of roton momenta, near √(2+√3), is what stabilizes the quasiperiodic order. The excitation spectrum of the dodecagonal quasicrystal packs five gapless modes: two phason modes (from shifts in extra dimensions), two hybrid condensate-phonon modes, and one transverse phonon. Computing the dynamic structure factor, rooted in quantum measurement of density fluctuations, shows that only two modes couple to density and can be spotted experimentally—say, via Bragg spectroscopy. Interestingly, unlike classical quasicrystals, the quantum version boasts a finite superfluid fraction, confirming that superfluidity and quasiperiodic order happily coexist.

Implications

This work blurs the line between classical and quantum quasicrystals, demonstrating that complex order can self-organize in ultracold atomic systems. It paves the way for experimentally probing phasons, superfluid fractions, and other exotic excitations in a controlled playground. The spectrum computation method can be applied to other quasiperiodic structures.

Future development

By tuning the laser detuning, mirror distance, and condensate parameters, one can coax out quasicrystals with ten-fold or other symmetries, and even craft kagome-like patterns. This scheme could be extended to Rydberg condensates, where long-range interactions are also highly controllable, making it a universal platform for simulating quantum phases with competing interactions.

Impact

The findings will ripple through ultracold atom physics, quantum optics, materials science, and perhaps even dynamical systems theory.

Next steps

The next experimental to-dos: create a quasicrystal following this protocol, measure its excitation spectrum using Bragg spectroscopy, and check predictions about superfluid stiffness.

Key open problems

This research directly tackles the fundamental puzzle of how aperiodic order emerges in non-equilibrium and quantum systems, and the broader question of how a dizzying variety of complex structures can arise from simple building blocks.
