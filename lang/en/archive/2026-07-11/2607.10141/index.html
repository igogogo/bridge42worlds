Dance of Dipoles and Light: The Birth of a Dodecagonal Quasicrystal ✦

Original: "Self-organized quasicrystals and their excitations in dipolar Bose-Einstein condensates via optical feedback"
· Liang-Jun He, Fabian Maucher, Yong-Chang Zhang
arXiv:2607.10141 · 2026-07-11 · CC BY · 3 min · Quantum Gases
In a Bose–Einstein condensate with optical feedback, a structure with twelvefold symmetry spontaneously emerges – as if atoms are embroidering a pattern forbidden by nature.
Abstract

In a dipolar Bose condensate (an ultracold gas of atoms with strong dipolar interactions), stable quasicrystals with 12-fold symmetry have been theoretically obtained for the first time. The key idea is to combine two competing interactions: the intrinsic dipole-dipole one and the one induced via photons using laser feedback. This leads to two roton instabilities in the spectrum, giving rise to a rich phase diagram. Interestingly, this mechanism is reminiscent of how frost draws patterns on glass, where different crystallization scales collide. The result opens the door to dynamically creating exotic phases of matter.

In 1984, the world of crystallography was turned upside down: sharp diffraction peaks with fivefold symmetry were discovered in an alloy of aluminum and manganese – as if nature had mocked the strict theorems forbidding repeating patterns from having axes of order 5, 8, 10, or 12. These structures, named quasicrystals, shattered a centuries-old dogma, but their formation in the quantum world at absolute zero remained a mystery. Imagine an ornament that never repeats yet radiates perfect order – that's the aesthetic of aperiodicity. Now physicists have armed themselves with Bose–Einstein condensates and light to recreate this cosmic tapestry in the lab.

The key to self-assembly is dipolar dysprosium atoms, each like a tiny magnet floating in a two-dimensional lake of absolute cold. This 'lake' is a Bose–Einstein condensate, a state of matter predicted by Bose and Einstein. Add a laser beam reflecting off a single mirror, and you get an elegant duet: dipole-dipole electromagnetic interaction and a distance-dependent oscillating optical field – like a frozen ripple of light. This interference trick creates an effective potential resembling two superimposed rhythms: one from the magnets, the other from the photons. In the excitation spectrum of this quantum 'dance', two roton minima appear – two bass notes, a concept tracing back to Landau's work on superfluid helium. Their ratio, close to √(2+√3) – an irrational number inscribed in the geometry of a regular dodecagon – forces the atoms to arrange into a pattern with twelvefold symmetry: an invisible tuning fork setting the quantum liquid to the only possible pitch.

A dodecagonal quasicrystal possesses a 12-fold symmetry axis, which is absolutely forbidden for periodic lattices. Classical crystallography allows only axes of order 1, 2, 3, 4, and 6 – all else was considered a geometric impossibility until 1984.

Using numerical simulations of the Gross-Pitaevskii equation, physicists drew a phase diagram resembling a treasure map: a uniform superfluid, a triangular lattice, ladder and ring structures, and finally – the coveted dodecagonal quasicrystal. From the perspective of quantum thermodynamics, all these transformations are first-order phase transitions, promising characteristic hysteresis when scanning experimental parameters. The quasicrystal's excitation spectrum, calculated through a clever projection method onto four-dimensional space, contains five gapless modes, including phasons – elastic deformations corresponding to shifts in extra dimensions. But the most striking result: unlike its classical cousins, the quantum quasicrystal remains partially superfluid. It's as if the tapestry not only holds its shape but also lets the threads flow freely through the pattern – a coexistence of rigid order and quantum fluidity.

This breakthrough blurs the line between classical and quantum ordering. It hands experimentalists a universal platform: by changing the laser polarization and the mirror distance, you can grow quasicrystals with tenfold symmetry or kagome-like lattices. On the horizon – probing phasons with Bragg spectroscopy and testing predictions about superfluid stiffness. Perhaps we are on the threshold of creating materials with tunable aperiodicity – from super-hard coatings to photonic insulators with programmable band gaps. Ultimately, this dance of atoms reminds us: simple laws can weave complexity beyond any imagination.
