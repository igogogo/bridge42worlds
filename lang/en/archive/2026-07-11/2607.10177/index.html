The Gravitational Conductor: How a Distant Companion Creates Hot Jupiters ✦

Original: "Stellar Multiplicity of M Dwarfs with Short-period Giant Planets, and the Characterization of TOI-5628Ab"

Exoplanets Stellar
Statistical analysis showed that a distant stellar companion raises the odds of finding a hot Jupiter around a red dwarf by six times—a key to unraveling the migration mechanism.
Abstract

Astronomers discovered a hot Jupiter orbiting the M-dwarf TOI-5628 with a period of 4.34 days, and they also found a white dwarf companion at a distance of 2500 AU. They then analyzed all known M-dwarfs with similar giant planets on short orbits and compared them to ordinary M-stars. It turned out that wide companions (100–10,000 AU) appear around 34.2% of stars with hot Jupiters, but only 5.3% of control stars—a sixfold difference. This confirms that the gravity of a distant neighbor can act like a slingshot, triggering high-eccentricity migration: the planet is flung into an elongated orbit, and as it swings close to the star, it brakes and settles into a tight circular path.

A quarter-century ago, Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz discovered the first hot Jupiter—and shattered all the rules of planet formation. A gas giant on a days-long orbit couldn't have formed next to the star: it's far too hot and lacks building material. So, it must have been born far away and then migrated. But what pushed it to dive into the inferno? New evidence fingers an invisible conductor—a distant stellar companion whose gravity, like a baton, sets the rhythm and amplitude of this perilous dance.

The white dwarf companion TOI-5628B has been cooling for just 530 million years, while the system's age is several billion—meaning the planet survived the red giant phase when the star swelled and shed its envelope.

A textbook case is the TOI-5628 system. Using the transit method, the TESS telescope caught the shadow of a planet three-quarters Jupiter's size passing across a red dwarf every 4.34 days. High-precision spectroscopy with SPIRou confirmed a feather-light mass—no more than 0.22 Jupiters. But the real prize lay in archival astrometry: 2500 astronomical units away lurked a white dwarf—the dead core of a former star. This pair forms a ready-made orchestra for the Kozai-Lidov mechanism. The companion's gravity rocks the planet's orbit, making its eccentricity swell like a crescendo in a symphonic poem. When the planet swings close to the star at pericenter, tidal forces quickly damp the wild swings, shrinking the orbit into a tight circle—the hot Jupiter settles in right beside the fire.

Statistics transform this scenario from hypothesis to confidence. Of 38 known M-dwarfs with hot Jupiters, 13 (34%) possess a wide stellar companion. In contrast, among run-of-the-mill M-stars, the same metric is a mere 5%. The odds ratio shines through a simple Bayesian formula:

Why red dwarfs specifically? A low-mass central star gives the gravitational conductor more leverage. In nine of those thirteen systems, the orbital rocking time is under 10 billion years, fitting evolutionary timescales. Added flavor comes from metallicity: giants show a clear preference for stars enriched with heavy elements, hinting at a complex interplay of chemistry and dynamics. But this fraction is only the tip of the iceberg: many planets likely failed to slow down via tidal friction in time and were swallowed by their stars. We observe only the survivors—those that managed to tame their acceleration.

The Roman Space Telescope will directly image dozens of such companions, and PLATO will multiply the sample of hot Jupiters around low-mass stars by hundreds—the conductor will then become visible, and we'll test how universal this musical score really is.

This discovery compels us to rewrite textbooks on stellar and planetary evolution. Hot Jupiters are no longer regarded as loners; their fates are often entwined with a distant star that journeyed from the main sequence to a white dwarf. Going forward, planetary migration models must treat binary systems as the norm, not the exception. When Roman and ultra-high-resolution spectrographs begin their hunt, we may find that many binary sunsets are lit not by a pair of living suns, but by the cold gleam of dead stars, conducting their planetary retinues from the darkness.

🎯 The white dwarf TOI-5628B has a cooling age of just 0.53 billion years, while its progenitor lived 2.5 to 6.9 billion years—meaning the red giant phase and envelope ejection happened after the planet formed, and we're witnessing a system that survived a cataclysm.
