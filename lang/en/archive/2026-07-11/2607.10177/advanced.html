The Role of Wide Stellar Pairs in the Formation of Hot Jupiters around M Dwarfs ✦

Original: "Stellar Multiplicity of M Dwarfs with Short-period Giant Planets, and the Characterization of TOI-5628Ab"
· Tianjun Gan, Alexandrine L'Heureux, Charles Cadieux, Shude Mao, Enric Pallé, Sharon X. Wang, Keivan G. Stassun, Steve B. Howell, Benjamin V. Rackham, Steffani M. arXiv:2607.10177 · 2026-07-11 · CC BY 4.0 · 2 min · Exoplanets Stellar
Statistical analysis shows that having a distant stellar companion increases the odds of finding a hot Jupiter around a red dwarf sixfold, pointing to the key role of high-eccentricity migration.
Abstract

The influence of wide-orbit stellar companions on the formation of hot Jupiters around M-dwarfs remains unclear. We present the discovery of TOI-5628Ab: a giant planet with a period of 4.34 days, a radius of 0.74±0.04 R_J, and an upper mass limit of 0.22 M_J (3σ) orbiting a star of 0.36 M☉, along with a white dwarf companion of 0.59±0.16 M☉ at a projected separation of ~2500 AU. Using Gaia DR3 data, we performed a systematic search for comoving companions (100–10,000 AU) for all M-dwarfs with confirmed short-period giants (P<10 d, R>0.7 R_J) and a matched control sample of field M-stars. The multiplicity fraction among planet hosts was 34.2±9.5%, roughly 6 times higher than among field stars (5.3±3.7%). These results indicate that wide companions promote the formation of short-period giants around M-stars with masses 0.21–0.64 M☉, with high-eccentricity migration being a likely key mechanism.

Context

Hot Jupiters — giant planets with periods under 10 days — were first discovered by Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz in 1995. Since then, the transit method, perfected by David Charbonneau, has unveiled thousands of exoplanets. Yet their migration mechanisms remain debated: the high-eccentricity migration hypothesis predicts that red dwarfs with distant companions should more often host short-period giants. Testing this prediction for low-mass stars has only now become possible thanks to accumulated statistics.

Methods

The discovery of TOI-5628Ab used the transit method (TESS) and high-resolution spectroscopy (SPIRou), with radial velocities derived from the Doppler shift of spectral lines. The star's temperature was calculated using the Stefan–Boltzmann law, and Kepler's third law provided the semi-major axis estimate. For the population analysis, Gaia DR3 astrometry was used: 38 M dwarfs with confirmed hot Jupiters were selected from the catalog, and a search for co-moving companions within 100–10,000 au was performed for each, using kinematic agreement criteria; ground-based photometry was also used to refine parameters. A field sample of 546,681 TIC stars, matched in metallicity, was processed via Monte Carlo.

Results

Out of 38 systems, 13 were found to be multiple (34.2±9.5%), including TOI-5628Ab, where the companion is a white dwarf with a mass of 0.59 M⊙, signaling advanced stellar evolution. For field stars, the multiplicity was 5.3±3.7%, yielding a probability boost of 6.5 times. Estimating the Kozai-Lidov timescale showed that in 9 of the 13 systems it is less than 10 billion years — a clear sign of efficient planetary migration. Planet TOI-5628Ab has a radius of 0.74 RJ and a mass of 0.09 MJ (upper limit 0.22 MJ, 3σ).

Implications

The detected correlation at the 6.5σ level confirms that stellar companions (including white dwarfs) play a key role in shaping the hot Jupiter population around M dwarfs. This strengthens the case for high-eccentricity migration and calls for including binary systems in models of stellar evolution with planets.

Future development

The Roman Space Telescope and next-generation ground-based spectrographs will enable direct imaging of companions and high-precision spectroscopy of their atmospheres, refining masses and orbits. Large-scale surveys with PLATO will expand the sample of hot Jupiters around M dwarfs, allowing tests of migration theories.

Impact

The results will impact planet formation theory, exoplanet survey statistics, and our understanding of binary system evolution with planets.

Next steps

Detailed modeling of sample metallicity is needed, as giants prefer metal-rich M dwarfs. Measuring orbital inclinations is also required to test predictions of migration models.

Key open problems

The study tackles unsolved problems of planet formation and migration in binary systems, as well as the role of stellar evolution in the fate of planetary orbits.
