The Torn Star and the Invisible Beam ✦

Original: "A spectacular multi-wavelength transient associated with an off-axis relativistic jet"
· Delina Levine, Gregg Hallinan, Jean J. Somalwar, Dillon Z. Dong, Ehud Nakar, Kenta Hotokezaka, Vikram Ravi, Assaf Horesh, Jessie M. Miller, Casey Law, Steven T. Myers, Stella K. Ocker, Daniel D. Kelson
arXiv:2607.10047 · 2026-07-11 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min · High Energy
A black hole tore a star apart, and its jet shot past Earth—but astronomers still spotted it.
Abstract

While scanning the sky with the VLA radio telescope, a strange object turned up: a bright optical flash quickly lit up and slowly faded, and a year later powerful radio emission appeared, lasting for years. Looks like a star ripped apart by a black hole, but the jet of matter is pointing sideways — like a flashlight beam grazing past us. How many more hidden cosmic cataclysms does the Universe still conceal?

A powerful flashlight shines sideways, and we see the glow—that's how AT 2019ijn was discovered. Tidal disruption of a star by a black hole launched a jet—a beam of matter traveling at near-light speed. It missed Earth, but a year after the optical flare, radio telescopes detected a long-lasting glow: the jet, slamming into gas, gave itself away. Unlike a supernova, the star wasn't exploded but torn apart.

The jet unleashed energy equal to the Sun's output over a hundred billion years, in just a few days.

In a dwarf galaxy, where star formation is boiling, the distance was calculated from the redshift (Hubble's law). The star's remnants, swirling into a superheated disk, glowed like a perfect heated body—according to Planck's law, its temperature exceeded ten thousand degrees. The main surprise: the black hole turned out to be of intermediate mass, though it was previously thought that only giants launch jets. And the second: had this beam been aimed at Earth, we would have seen one of the brightest gamma-ray bursts—fortunately, it missed. Radio observations will now help search for such hidden threats.
