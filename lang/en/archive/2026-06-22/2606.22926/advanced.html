Ten-Million-Scale Search for Ammonia Catalysts Using Generative AI ✦

Original: "Reaction-Network-Level Discovery of Ammonia Synthesis Catalysts via Ten-Million-Scale Generative Exploration"
· Ruili Li, Rui Qi, Shuoqi Zhang, Qingli Tang, Qingqing Mao, Ritankar Das, Beien Zhu, Yi Gao
arXiv:2606.22926 · 2026-06-22 · CC BY 4.0 · 5 min
Chemical Physics Materials
Scientists used generative artificial intelligence to screen a record 15 million structures and discovered new efficient catalysts for ammonia synthesis.
1

Context

Ammonia synthesis from nitrogen and hydrogen is the cornerstone of modern chemical industry: without it, fertilizer production—and thus global food security—would be impossible. The traditional Haber–Bosch process, which uses iron-based catalysts, operates at high temperatures and pressures, consuming about 2% of the world's energy. To reduce energy consumption and the carbon footprint (today the carbon footprint of ammonia synthesis is enormous due to the use of methane and water), researchers are seeking new materials that can work under mild conditions. But the ammonia reaction isn't a single step; it's a complex network of dissociative and associative pathways, where key intermediates (N*, NH*, NNH*, HNNH*) place different demands on the catalyst surface. A catalyst ideal for one intermediate often fails for others. Thus, discovering a catalyst for ammonia synthesis is a compatibility problem across the entire reaction network, requiring a rethink of traditional screening methods that focus on single descriptors.

Methods

To tackle this complexity, the authors developed a platform based on generative transformers—machine learning architectures originally designed for natural language processing, but adapted to generate atomic structures. The transformer was pre-trained on the OC20 dataset, containing two million surface structures. It was then fine-tuned for the four key intermediates, enabling it to generate structures specific to each one. Large-scale generation on powerful GPU clusters produced roughly 15 million configurations per intermediate—an unprecedented volume for catalytic screening. The vast space was then compressed using hash-based deduplication and clustering to remove repeats and preserve diversity. The selected structures were evaluated using machine learning potentials from EquiformerV2—a neural network that predicts binding energies. This numerical simulation rests on the fundamental laws of quantum mechanics: solving the Schrödinger equation, first formulated by Erwin Schrödinger, and obeying the Pauli exclusion principle (Wolfgang Pauli), density functional theory (DFT) methods can calculate the electronic structure. Meanwhile, the distribution of electrons among states is governed by Enrico Fermi statistics, and the first law of thermodynamics dictates the energy balances on the surface.

Results

The results were stunning. Overlap analysis showed that at typical generation scales (10⁵–10⁶ structures), materials compatible with all four intermediates were virtually undetectable. Only at the ten-million level did 279 catalyst families emerge that satisfied the full spectrum of constraints. Among them, classical catalysts based on iron (including Fe-Pt) and ruthenium were recovered, confirming the screening's validity. But most importantly, previously unexplored compositions were found: Fe-V, Al-Pd-Zr, Al-Ti, Au-Ti, and Cu-Zr. Detailed DFT calculations revealed their distinct preferences for reaction pathways. For instance, Fe-V lowers the activation barrier for N₂ dissociation to 0.72 eV, operating via a dissociative mechanism, while Al-Pd-Zr stabilizes hydrogenated intermediates (NNH* and HNNH*) and follows an associative pathway with a 1.22 eV barrier for the first hydrogenation step. Interestingly, energy balance alone doesn't guarantee effectiveness: Cu-Zr easily splits N₂, but subsequent hydrogenation is energetically unfavorable, underscoring the importance of full network compatibility. In reaction thermodynamics, not only energy but also entropy is crucial, which together with the thermal effect determines the free energy.

Implications

This work shifts the paradigm of catalyst discovery: it shows that considering the entire reaction network is not a luxury, but a necessity. The method not only finds active sites but also maps out entire compatibility landscapes, where catalysts can be chosen for specific mechanisms. The recovery of known industrial catalysts confirms the approach's reliability, while the discovery of new families demonstrates its predictive power. For the chemical industry, this means the ability to purposefully design catalysts for complex, multi-step processes like ammonia synthesis through computational experiments that complement lab work.

Future development

In the future, this approach can be extended to other industrially relevant reactions—from CO₂ reduction to methanol oxidation—where similarly branched intermediate networks are at play. Improving the accuracy of ML potentials and incorporating kinetic factors (rate constants, solvent effects) will enable a shift from energy screening to full prediction of catalytic activity. Moreover, generative models will be able to account not just for composition, but also nanoparticle morphology, defects, and reaction conditions, bringing virtual screening closer to reality. Integration with self-driving labs could close the loop from hypothesis generation to experimental validation.

Impact

The results will impact several fields: in chemical engineering, by accelerating the development of catalysts for 'green' ammonia; in materials science, by advancing multiscale modeling methods; in artificial intelligence, by fostering generative architectures that can handle 3D structures and physical constraints.

Next steps

Next steps include experimental verification of the predicted materials (primarily Fe-V and Al-Pd-Zr) in lab reactors, and expanding the generative space to binary and ternary alloys, accounting for stability under reaction conditions. In parallel, the ML potential itself will be improved to enhance the accuracy of energy predictions.

Key open problems

This article is directly connected to an unsolved problem in chemical physics: how to rationally design catalysts for complex reaction networks, bypassing empirical approaches. It also touches on a fundamental question about scaling relations in catalysis: can we break the linear dependencies between binding energies of different intermediates and thus overcome the activity 'ceiling' predicted by volcano-shaped curves? The ten-million-scale computational study suggests the existence of rare but achievable materials that circumvent these limitations.

🎯 Interestingly, ammonia is one of the most produced chemicals in the world—its annual synthesis exceeds 150 million tons, comparable to the mass of all humans on Earth. Meanwhile, nature's nitrogen fixation process by bacteria uses nitrogenase enzymes, whose active sites contain molybdenum or vanadium—elements also found in the new catalysts predicted by AI.

🎬 In Andy Weir's sci-fi novel The Martian, the astronaut synthesizes water from hydrogen and oxygen, and fertilizes plants with his own waste. But for a full-fledged Martian farm, ammonia is needed—and thus ultra-efficient catalysts for its synthesis from local resources. It's not far-fetched that such generative AI methods could help design catalysts for colonizing the Red Planet.
