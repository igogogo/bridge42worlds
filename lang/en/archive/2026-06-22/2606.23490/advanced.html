Anomalous charge density wave in a two-dimensional superatomic superconductor ✦

Original: "Anomalous charge density wave in a two-dimensional superatomic superconductor"
· Boqin Song, Shuaishuai Sun, Zhongxu Wei, Xinbo Wang, Xiaoping Ma, Kaifa Luo, Lei Wang, Jun Deng, Xu Chen, Tian Qian, Shuya Xing, Zhihai Cheng, Jiangang Guo, Tianping Ying, Xiaolong Chen
arXiv:2606.23490 · 2026-06-22 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min
Superconductivity
An anomalous charge density wave driven by transverse phonons has been discovered, with giant displacement and unusual thickness dependence in the superatomic material Au₆Te₁₂Se₈.
1

Context

Understanding quantum states in reduced-dimensionality materials is one of the central challenges of modern condensed matter physics. The transition from individual atoms to superatoms as building blocks adds new degrees of freedom and can lead to emergent phenomena inaccessible in conventional atomic crystals. A charge density wave is a collective quantum state with a periodic modulation of electronic density, often coexisting with superconductivity and magnetism, and its study in superatomic systems is just beginning. The investigation of CDW in Au₆Te₁₂Se₈ is important for elucidating the nature of phase transitions in such exotic materials, where fermion statistics of carriers and quantum-mechanical description of electronic states play a significant role.

Methods

Using a combination of methods including low-temperature transmission electron microscopy, scanning tunneling microscopy (STM), and ultrafast pump-probe spectroscopy, the authors studied structural and electronic transitions in Au₆Te₁₂Se₈. Samples of varying thickness were prepared by exfoliation. Density functional theory (DFT) was used to analyze the band structure. Thermodynamic and transport measurements allowed construction of the phase diagram, and coherent phonon analysis revealed decoherence of vibrations at the phase transition. A key tool was also the superposition of states in pump-probe to track gap dynamics.

Results

It was found that in thin samples a CDW forms with wave vector 1/3 b* and period a×3b, as confirmed by the appearance of superlattice reflections in electron diffraction. Using STM, the transverse displacement of superatoms of ~4 Å was directly visualized—1–2 orders of magnitude larger than in conventional CDWs, indicating the quantum nature of the transition. Ultrafast spectroscopy revealed the opening of a CDW gap of 14 meV and softening of phonon modes, pointing to the key role of electron-phonon coupling described by the Planck–Einstein relation. Most striking is the size effect: the CDW transition temperature TCDW increases from undetectable in the bulk to 110 K at nanometer-scale thickness, opposite to the usual suppression by thermal fluctuations. Competition between CDW and superconductivity was also observed, with the superconducting critical temperature being suppressed as TCDW rises. These results were enabled by precise measurement of structural and electronic parameters.

Implications

The results broaden the understanding of CDW physics, demonstrating that transverse phonon modes can be no less important than longitudinal ones, and that in superatomic systems CDW stabilization can occur at unexpectedly large thicknesses due to weak electronic anisotropy. The work establishes for the first time direct competition between CDW and superconductivity in a superatomic material, opening new avenues for studying Fermi-liquid instabilities and unconventional pairing mechanisms. Moreover, the discovered quantum state represents a realization of a transverse Peierls transition, which may stimulate theoretical research in this area.

Future development

Future research could focus on detailed mapping of phonon spectra using inelastic X-ray scattering to confirm the role of transverse modes, and on searching for similar phenomena in other superatomic systems. The development of numerical methods for modeling superatomic phonons and their interaction with electrons will help predict new materials with desired thermodynamic properties. Also promising is the creation of devices that control quantum decoherence via CDW, which may find applications in superposition-based quantum circuits.

Impact

The discovery will impact the physics of quantum phase transitions, quantum field theory in low-dimensional systems, and the design of new superconductors, as well as methods of optical spectroscopy for probing collective excitations.

Next steps

The immediate steps will be measurements of low-energy phonon dispersion and studies of the influence of pressure and magnetic field on the competition between CDW and superconductivity using precision measurements.

Key open problems

This research addresses the fundamental problem of understanding collective quantum states in materials with unconventional coupling and may shed light on the mechanisms of high-temperature superconductivity, where CDW is often considered a competing or cooperating phase. In particular, it touches on the question of the role of energy quanta in forming superconducting pairing.
