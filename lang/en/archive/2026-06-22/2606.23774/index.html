The Universe's Recipe: A Sub-Saturn Around a Cool Star Reveals a Standard Method for Making Giants ✦

Original: "C, N, O, S, and photochemistry in a temperate giant planet orbiting a late M dwarf"
· Michael Zhang, Qiao Xue, Jeehyun Yang, Vighnesh Nagpal, Michael R. Line, Guangwei Fu, Matthew C. Nixon, Jacob L. Bean, Peter Gao, Eliza M. -R. Kempton, Luis Welbanks, Edward M. Bryant, Daniel Bayliss, Madison Brady, Jean-Michel Désert, Vincent Van Eylen, Jonathan J. Fortney, Andrés Jordán, Vivien Parmentier, Caroline Piaulet-Ghorayeb, Elyar Sedaghati, Kevin B. Stevenson, Amaury H. M. J. Triaud
arXiv:2606.23774 · 2026-06-22 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Exoplanets
The spectrum of exoplanet TOI-6894 b, like a molecular fingerprint, showed: even around a small star, gas giants form according to the standard 'solar' recipe.
Abstract

The James Webb Space Telescope just got a remarkably clean spectrum of exoplanet TOI-6894b's atmosphere. This sub-Saturn, at a toasty ~420 K, circles a cool M-dwarf. The spectrum is rich with methane and carbon disulfide, and—for the first time ever in a transit—ammonia has been spotted unequivocally. Models of its photochemistry suggest its metallicity is 3 to 10 times solar, while the ratios of C/O, N/O, and S/O are surprisingly sun-like. Even more intriguing: its makeup is a dead ringer for Jupiter and Saturn, even though its star is much smaller and cooler than the Sun.

Some 158 light-years away, around the dim star TOI-6894, a red dwarf, a planet moves leisurely. Its year lasts less than two Earth days, yet it is a cool world with an equilibrium temperature around 420 K, just a bit warmer than a sauna. This exoplanetary giant, TOI-6894 b, almost exactly mirrors Saturn's size, though it weighs noticeably less. Its star is one of the calmest in its class, free of violent flares and dark starspots that usually blind telescopes. This very calmness allowed JWST to capture, for the first time, a clean, almost laboratory-grade spectrum of the atmosphere of a planet of this type.

Imagine a cosmic kitchen: the star is the stove where planets are cooked; the protoplanetary disk is the pantry with ingredients; and the planet itself is the finished dish. Astronomers suspected that the recipes for gas giants change depending on how hot the 'stove' is. But TOI-6894 b upends this notion. Its atmosphere is the finished dish, and spectroscopy let us taste it.

For the first time in the history of transit observations, ammonia (NH₃) appeared so clearly that its absorption bands are visible to the naked eye on the plot—three sharp peaks at 1.5, 1.95, and 2.95 µm.

The spectrum, taken by the NIRSpec instrument in PRISM mode, revealed a full suite of volatile molecules. Water filled the plateau between 2.5 and 3.0 µm, methane etched characteristic dips at 1.6–1.7 and 2.2–2.4 µm, and carbon dioxide produced a strong peak near 4.3 µm. But the biggest surprise was carbon disulfide (CS₂)—a molecule born of photochemistry: the star's ultraviolet light, albeit weak, triggers reactions that turn some carbon and sulfur into this pungent substance (on Earth, it smells like rotting apples). Its peak at 4.65 µm dominates the long-wavelength end of the spectrum.

Models indicate a metallicity 3–10 times higher than solar. But here's the key: the C/O, N/O, and S/O ratios turned out to be practically the same as those of our Sun, and consequently, of Jupiter and Saturn. The ingredients are the same, the proportions are the same, even though the 'stove'—a low-mass red dwarf—is five times lighter than the Sun and cannot heat the surroundings as vigorously. This is a strong argument that core accretion is a universal mechanism, operating even in sparse protoplanetary disks. The planet managed to gather about 12 Earth masses of heavy elements, then rapidly accrete a gaseous envelope. Gravitational instability, another possible scenario, could not so neatly reproduce solar proportions. It seems the universe's cookbook is uniform, and our Jupiter and Saturn are not unique masterpieces but typical dishes from the galactic menu.

This result opens the door to systematic mapping of planets' chemical evolution. In the future, by refining photochemical models and accumulating observations with JWST, we will be able to measure volatile element abundances for dozens of cool giants. Missions like ARIEL and the Roman Space Telescope will make this routine. Then we'll understand whether the universe's recipe encyclopedia is truly universal for all stars, or if exotic dishes, with flavors yet unknown, await on the outskirts of the Galaxy.

🎯 Carbon disulfide (CS₂) had only been found on a couple of hot Jupiters before TOI-6894 b, where temperatures soar past a thousand degrees. Here, it arose thanks to the soft ultraviolet light of a cool star—and became the brightest signature in the spectrum.
