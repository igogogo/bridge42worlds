Self-poling films: How ultrathin ferroelectrics retain switchable polarization ✦

Original: "Atomic-scale theory of robust out-of-plane ferroelectricity in ultrathin films"
· Fengbo Yuan, Yujia Teng, Karin M. Rabe, Yubo Qi
arXiv:2606.23508 · 2026-06-22 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Materials
A new theoretical model explains why some ultrathin films, such as HfO₂-based ones, retain switchable polarization despite the depolarizing field, thanks to self-poling effects and the switchable role of surface layers.
Links in the knowledge graph 1

Context

Why is this important? Shrinking electronic devices demand ferroelectric films just a few nanometers thick. Yet classical electrodynamics predicts that the depolarizing field from surface charges should suppress spontaneous polarization. This contradiction called for a fresh theoretical approach. Even Landau laid the foundations for the thermodynamic description of ferroelectrics, but the stability problem in ultrathin layers remained unsolved.

Methods

How was it done? The researchers used first-principles calculations within density functional theory to model ultrathin BaTiO₃ and HfO₂ films with various adsorbates such as hydrogen and hydroxyl groups. They analyzed macroscopic electrostatic potentials and atomic displacements to understand the contribution of the work function of surface layers. The Quantum ESPRESSO software with the PBEsol exchange-correlation functional was employed, and electron spectroscopy methods were used for band structure analysis.

Results

What was found? It was established that polarization stability in HfO₂ layers stems from two effects: 'self-poling' and 'switchable surface layer role.' In so-called 'characteristic structures,' the same atomic layer at the surface can act as a low-work-function layer for one polarization direction and a high-work-function layer for the opposite, much like how, according to Coulomb, Coulomb's law governs charge distribution. Adsorption of oxygen on the surface can either stabilize or destroy the polar state depending on concentration. Calculations showed that films 1.7 nm and 3.8 nm thick have a constant potential difference in vacuum, determined only by the type of adsorbate, and the internal electric field is stronger the thinner the film. For HfO₂ with 6 hafnium layers, the polar phase is stable at certain oxygen coverages m/n, and increasing the thickness to 10 layers broadens the stability range. These findings are supported by analysis of quantum mechanical band bending effects.

Implications

What does it mean for science? The theory generalizes the understanding of ferroelectricity in ultrathin films, linking it to atomic structure and work function. It explains why classical perovskites like BaTiO₃ lose polarization as thickness decreases, whereas HfO₂ and the recently discovered bismuth oxide retain it. This paves the way for the rational design of new ferroelectric materials for nanoelectronics, based on the 'characteristic structure' concept.

Future development

How might the topic evolve? The proposed model encourages the search for other materials with suitable crystal architecture, including layered oxides and two-dimensional materials. Further research may incorporate quantum tunneling effects and the influence of defects on the work function. Additionally, dynamic modeling of polarization switching with electrodes could be explored.

Impact

Which areas will it impact? The design of non-volatile memory, nanosensors, and ferroelectric field-effect transistors will gain clear scaling principles, and computational materials science methods will be enriched with new descriptors of polarization stability.

Next steps

Next steps in research. Experimental verification of the theory's predictions on epitaxial HfO₂ films with controlled surface coverage, as well as high-throughput screening of materials with 'characteristic structures' using machine learning methods.

Key open problems

Connection to unsolved physics problems. The work addresses the fundamental problem of scaling functional materials: how to preserve collective properties (ferroelectricity) down to a few atomic layers? This is part of the broader challenge of understanding phase transitions in low-dimensional systems and the role of surface states.

🎯 Fun fact. Hafnium oxide (HfO₂), used to make ferroelectric memory, also serves as an insulating layer in transistors—its high dielectric constant helps reduce leakage currents. So one material can both store charge and insulate it!

Key numbers

  • BaTiO₃ film thickness: 1.7 nm and 3.8 nm
  • number of HfO₂ layers: 6 and 10
  • potential difference in vacuum: constant, independent of thickness
  • oxygen coverage m/n: from 0 to 2.0
  • critical thickness for polarization loss in perovskites: less than 2–3 nm
