Train Station Delta: Three Types of Passengers express

Original: "How do activity-end trip characteristics affect the choice for shared micromobility? A latent class choice modelling approach for train station egress trips in the Netherlands"
· Nejc Geržinič, Mark van Hagen, Hussein Al-Tamimi, Niels van Oort, Dorine Duives
arXiv:2505.03297 · 2025-05-06 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min · Physics and Society
A study in the Netherlands reveals three scenarios for how people leave a train station — and it's changing urban planning approaches.
Abstract

Leveraging stated preference surveys and latent class modeling, researchers analyzed how Dutch residents decide to travel to and from the station. Three distinct groups emerged: 'multimodal sharing enthusiasts' (58%)—open to various micromobility modes based on trip specifics; 'cautious cyclists averse to sharing' (16%)—prefer personal bicycles, perceiving rentals as complex and hazardous; 'sharing opponents opting for public transport' (27%)—shun micromobility in favor of buses and trams. For short distances, pedestrian accessibility remains paramount, reinforcing transit-oriented development policies. Over longer spans in high-demand zones, dense and frequent public transport networks are warranted, while low-demand stations are better served by diverse micromobility options—though too many choices might merely compete for the same riders.

After the train arrives, passengers flow from the station like water through a delta: each chooses their own channel. Dutch scientists surveyed thousands of people and identified three types. 'All-Rounders' (58%) easily switch between buses, rental scooters, and walking. 'Homebodies on Wheels' (16%) stay loyal to their own bicycle but fear rental devices — though this fear is often exaggerated: accidents with them are far fewer than perceived. 'Tried-and-True' fans (27%) prefer buses or trams, steering clear of any scooters. This mess of preferences isn't just statistics. It shapes urban planning: if the destination is a stone's throw away, almost everyone walks, so wide sidewalks are needed. But when the trip is longer, it's crucial that buses don't compete with rental options. The spectrum of possibilities expands, and the challenge is to make flows merge into a single seamless network rather than collide.

🎯 A short walk is the queen of convenience: almost everyone chooses to walk if it takes no more than a few minutes.
