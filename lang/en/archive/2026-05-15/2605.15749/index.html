Scientists tuned into the cosmic gravitational hum, hunting for primordial black holes, and heard not a single new note.
Abstract

Using data from the first months of LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA's fourth observing run, scientists have set the tightest limits yet on the abundance of primordial black holes (PBHs) in the mass range 0.6–100 solar masses. The strongest constraints come from direct merger detections; the faint gravitational-wave background adds extra, but looser, bounds. If we assume some detected bursts were caused by PBHs, the limits weaken slightly, but a joint fit with astrophysical black holes shows no statistically significant contribution from primordial ones. In short, dark matter likely isn't made of these—it's something else.

The universe hums like an orchestra: its notes are gravitational waves—ripples in spacetime predicted by Albert Einstein. The LIGO observatories, made exquisitely sensitive thanks to Rainer Weiss, strive to catch these whispers. Most often, we hear thunderous crashes—the mergers of ordinary black holes born from stars. But astrophysicists tried to pick out quiet, barely-there notes—signals from primordial black holes that emerged right after the Big Bang.

These tiny specks (some lighter than an asteroid) might actually be dark matter—the invisible glue holding galaxies together. The idea of such ghost holes was fleshed out by Stephen Hawking. Scientists choreographed computer dances of pairs of such holes and compared them with real data. The result was stunning: the orchestra played not a single new note. Almost all the chords we heard belong to ordinary black holes.

Primordial black holes haven't been dismissed as myth, but their potential contribution to dark matter has been squeezed to a negligible fraction. The study set the tightest constraints yet on such objects with masses from 0.6 to 100 solar masses, and for the first time probed the range from one ten-thousandth to ten thousand solar masses.

🎯 If you squeezed Earth down to the size of a grape, it would become a black hole. Primordial black holes can be trillions of times lighter, yet their gravitational whisper is still detectable.
