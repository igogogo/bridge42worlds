Solid-State Gigahertz Gravitational Wave Detector: Hunting for Relic Stochastic Background and Primordial Black Holes ✦

Original: "Solid-state gravitational-wave detectors at GHz frequencies: the search for the primordial stochastic GW background and light primordial black hole binaries"
· Juan Garcia-Bellido
arXiv:2607.13741 · 2026-07-15 · CC BY 4.0 · 5 min · General Relativity Cosmology
How to turn a sapphire single crystal into a quantum antenna to catch gravitational waves from the Big Bang and mergers of Planck-scale black holes.
Abstract

Reheating after inflation generates a stochastic gravitational-wave background with a non-thermal spectrum, peaking in the gigahertz range at strains around 10⁻³⁰. Experiments based on the inverse Gertsenshtein effect only reach megahertz, so a solid-state detector accumulating signal over years is proposed. The design involves a modular array of ~10³ single crystals of ultra-pure sapphire (10 cm)³, read out by cryogenic single-phonon sensors; segmentation provides thermal isolation and background suppression via coincidences. Material analysis shows diamond is more efficient per unit volume, but large crystals are unavailable. The strategy is twofold: a narrowband resonant search for the background and a broadband approach with coincident tagging for transient mergers of light primordial black holes of planetary and subplanetary masses, whose signals would hint at dark matter.

Context

The direct detection of gravitational waves by LIGO/Virgo opened a new era in astronomy. Yet the frequency range above tens of kilohertz remains largely unexplored: known astrophysical objects cannot emit at 1–10 GHz with detectable amplitude, so a detection there would point to new physics — primarily processes in the early Universe. The strongest source of such a background is the nonlinear reheating phase after inflation, generating a stochastic gravitational-wave background (GWB) peaking in the few-GHz range. A second motivation comes from mergers of lightweight primordial black holes (PBHs), which cannot form from stars and are a leading candidate for dark matter. Their chirp signal ends at gigahertz frequencies if the masses are planetary or smaller. Thus, detection in this band would shed light on the birth epoch of the Universe and the nature of hidden mass.

Methods

The proposed detector uses monocrystalline sapphire as a macroscopic quantum medium, where a passing GW excites lattice vibrations — phonons. Unlike the classical analysis of Weber bars Weber, the authors perform a full quantum-mechanical calculation that accounts for the crystal’s band structure. A key role is played by Bragg’s law (W. L. Bragg): the phonon wavevector k is related to the GW wavevector k_g by k = k_g + b_n, with b_n a reciprocal lattice vector. This makes the interaction effectively one-dimensional, so every extremum point of a phonon band gives a Van Hove singularity in the density of final states. The phonon creation probability is computed using Fermi’s golden rule. Calculations were performed for crystals of various dimensionalities — graphene (2D) and 3D salts, sapphire, diamond — showing that for macroscopic sizes, 3D lattices win thanks to the factor (L/a)⁴. To suppress thermal background, the detector must operate at millikelvin temperatures, with the main noise limit being quantum shot noise from the discreteness of phonon events, which requires treating quantum decoherence. A modular design of ~10³ cubes, each 10×10×10 cm³, equipped with cryogenic single-phonon sensors based on superconducting quantum interference devices (SQUIDs) or transition-edge sensors (TES) — an example of quantum sensing — provides thermal isolation, favorable Poisson statistics, and background rejection by coincidence.

Results

The baseline phonon production rate for h ~ 10⁻³⁰ is minuscule: for NaCl, one event in 110 years. But the resonance at a Van Hove singularity boosts the rate to Γ ~ 10⁻⁸ s⁻¹, meaning one event every few years. The enhancement comes from a longer effective coherence time and operation at a frequency where the density of states diverges as (ω − ω_c)⁻¹/². The key coherent cross-section expression: σ ∝ (2πG/c³) m̄ c_s (L/a)^(d+1). For a sapphire cube with L = 0.2 m, parameters m̄ ≈ 3.4×10⁻²⁶ kg, c_s ≈ 10 km/s, a ≈ 5 Å yield σ ≈ 0.68 millibarns — a macroscopic value by microphysical standards. With a modular assembly of 10³ such cubes, the total rate is the sum of individual rates, and the statistical significance grows with the square root of integration time. Material comparison revealed that diamond has triple the cross-section per unit volume and better calorimetric properties, but cannot be grown as large (> several cm) defect-free single crystals, so sapphire remains the practical choice. It’s also shown that the detector can operate in two modes: narrow-band resonant for accumulating the stochastic background, and broadband for tracking PBH chirps. The Bose–Einstein condensate concept opens an alternative route to quantum enhancement.

Implications

Successfully building this detector would open a window into high-energy gravitational-wave astronomy, inaccessible to electromagnetic telescopes. Detecting the stochastic background would confirm the inflationary reheating scenario and constrain the energy scales of grand unification. Registering a PBH chirp would be direct proof of sub-solar-mass black holes and point to their contribution to dark matter. The technology of quantum phonon measurement itself finds applications in quantum sensing and can be adapted to search for other light dark matter particles.

Future development

Further development follows several threads: materials science — growing large defect-free diamond crystals or finding other compounds with high sound speed and a high density of singularities; bandwidth expansion — detuning cells by orientation, material, and size to cover the 0.1–10 GHz range; integration with other methods — using a Bose–Einstein condensate as an active medium where GWs create phonons via the dynamical Casimir effect; and readout improvements — employing squeezed states to beat the shot-noise limit. In addition, developing robust modulation analysis algorithms will enable searches for annual signal variations caused by Earth’s motion around the Sun.

Impact

The technology will impact gravitational-wave astronomy, early-Universe cosmology, particle physics (axion experiments), and quantum metrology. It will also spur advances in cryogenic electronics and ultra-pure crystal growth.

Next steps

Next steps include building a prototype from one or a few sapphire cubes with TES sensors to demonstrate the quantum sensitivity threshold, and measuring background count rates in an underground lab. In parallel, detailed band-structure modeling of promising materials should be done to maximize the Van Hove effect.

Key open problems

The detector is directly linked to three grand problems: the nature of dark matter (are PBHs a key component?), the details of cosmological inflation and reheating, and testing quantum gravity effects at frequencies near the Planck scale. A detection would provide the first experimental data on the Universe at temperatures above 10¹⁰ GeV.
