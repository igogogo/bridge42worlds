Sapphire Tuning Fork: How to Hear the Echo of the Big Bang ✦

Original: "Solid-state gravitational-wave detectors at GHz frequencies: the search for the primordial stochastic GW background and light primordial black hole binaries"
· Juan Garcia-Bellido
arXiv:2607.13741 · 2026-07-15 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min · General Relativity Cosmology
Sapphire single crystals are quantum antennas that capture gigahertz gravitational waves born in the Universe's first moments.
Links in the knowledge graph 1

Right now, gigahertz gravitational waves from the newborn Universe are passing right through us. To eavesdrop on this whisper, physicists are building a sapphire tuning fork, where the wave excites sound quanta—phonons. One such phonon might come from the inflationary epoch, when space expanded faster than light. And if we catch the chirp of merging primordial black holes, we'll grab dark matter by the hand. Perhaps in a few years, the first sapphire bell will ring in an underground lab.

🎯 The sapphire detector "feels" gravity with the same cross-section as a neutron crashing into an atomic nucleus. And the born phonon lives only 10 microseconds—shorter than a flash of realization.

\sigma \simeq \frac{2\pi G}{c^3} \bar{m} c_s \left(\frac{L}{a}\right)^{d+1}
The cross-section is a 'catching net' for gravitons: it tells how transparent or opaque the crystal is to the wave. The larger and denser the crystal, the higher the chance of catching a signal.
\Gamma \sim \frac{1}{8} \frac{\bar{m} a c_s}{\hbar} \left(\frac{L}{a}\right)^{d+1} h^2 \omega_g
This formula predicts how often the crystal will 'ring': the higher the gravitational wave's frequency and the more it distorts space, the more often phonons are born. Doubling the crystal's size can increase the event rate a hundredfold.
