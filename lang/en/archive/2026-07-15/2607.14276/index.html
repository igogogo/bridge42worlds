Photon Fog Forging Antimatter ✦

Original: "Cosmological $$\gamma$$-$$\gamma$$ Pair-Production Background"
· Mika A. Gelowicz, Thomas Siegert, Saurabh Mittal, Laura Eisenberger, Dimitris Tsatsis, Niklas C. Bauer, Rudi Reinhardt, Patrik Ehrmann, Manja L. Zimmerer, Hiroki Yoneda, Tomohiko Oka, Tristan Bouchet, Manuel R. H. W. Skalka
arXiv:2607.14276 · 2026-07-15 · CC BY · 2 min · Cosmology High Energy
Collisions of cosmic background photons spawn pairs, and their secondary glow unravels the mystery of the gamma-ray sky.
Abstract

The Cosmic Photon Background (CPB) is diffuse radiation filling the Universe at all wavelengths. Scientists have calculated how collisions of its photons with each other produce electron-positron pairs up to redshift z=10. The peak of pair production is at z≈2.7, and subsequent scattering of these particles on the background photons (inverse Compton effect) makes a noticeable contribution to the cosmic gamma-ray background. In the energy range 1 MeV – 1 GeV, this process explains up to 20% of the observed radiation, helping to reconcile theory with observations.

The mystery of antimatter in space has long puzzled astrophysicists: where do positrons come from, settling like snowflakes into our detectors? The answer might lurk in the most ubiquitous gamma-ray background. The Universe is soaked in diffuse light—the cosmic photon background (CPB). It’s woven from the echo of the Big Bang, picked up by Penzias and Wilson as the cosmic microwave background, and the glow from quasars, supernovae, and active galactic nuclei. This photon fog seems ghostly, but its particles are capable of a miracle: colliding, they give birth to matter from pure energy.

The photon density of the CPB is minuscule—trillions of times sparser than in a room lightbulb. But the expansion of the Universe, discovered by Hubble, stretches that rarefied volume across billions of light-years, turning the rarest photon encounters into an unavoidable cosmological process.

The threshold for creation is simple but profound: the product of two photon energies must exceed the electron's rest-mass energy squared (E₁E₂ ≥ mₑ²c⁴). When the condition is met, an electron and a positron pop out of the vacuum—like droplets condensing from a weightless fog. Astrophysicists traced how their birth rate ramped up with redshift z. The peak came around z≈2.7, when the Universe was bubbling with star formation—back then, the fog was densest and hottest. Today, in the local Universe, the process has nearly ground to a halt: just 2×10⁻³⁶ pairs per cubic centimeter per second.

The newborn pairs don't vanish without a trace. Slicing through the intergalactic medium, they offload energy onto the ever-present cosmic microwave background—inverse Compton scattering begets secondary gamma rays. It's these photons, according to calculations, that chip in 10–20% of the observed cosmic gamma-ray background at energies of 1–100 MeV. Without invoking dark matter or other exotica, this mechanism sews up a decades-old mismatch. A striking detail: each positron born in that era is a messenger from a time when the Universe was half its current age, and from its gamma-ray tracks we can read the history of the stellar high noon.

Altogether, up to z=10, the Universe churns out about 10⁵⁴ positrons per second. The energy of these particles stretches up to teraelectronvolts, and their trails are like neon dust on the black velvet of space.

The way forward lies with the next generation of gamma-ray telescopes and more refined CPB maps. This will not only clarify the background's nature but also pin down models of the early Universe. In essence, we're watching light embroider its own past, turning into the very matter we're made of.

🎯 Even though each galaxy produces only ~10³⁵ positrons per second, the collective contribution of the cosmic photon fog to the gamma-ray background reaches 10–20%.
