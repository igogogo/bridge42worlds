How Polarization of Light Reveals the Spin of Black Holes ✦

Original: "Signatures of Black Hole Spin in Horizon-Scale Polarimetry"
· George N. Wong, Daniel C. M. Palumbo, Zachary Gelles, Andrew Chael
arXiv:2607.14218 · 2026-07-15 · CC BY · 4 min
High Energy
Polarimetric observations of the event horizon using VLBI allow us to reconstruct the magnetic field structure and constrain the spin of supermassive black holes.
Abstract

The spin of a black hole, described by the dimensionless spin parameter, gives rise to the Kerr metric and acts as an energy reservoir exchanging energy with plasma. Using Very Long Baseline Interferometry (VLBI), we have begun to resolve polarized emission on the scale of the event horizon. We distinguish between polarimetric signatures tied to photon propagation in curved spacetime and effects mediated by magnetic fields threading the horizon. So far, Event Horizon Telescope (EHT) observations more reliably constrain magnetic field geometry, variability, source orientation, magnetic flux state, and the disk-jet connection than the value or direction of spin. Future programs will need to combine complementary polarimetric diagnostics, testing whether a consistent spin-dependent interpretation holds up across independent data and realistic models.

1

Context

The spin of a black hole is a dimensionless parameter that, along with mass, fully describes an isolated stationary black hole within the framework of general relativity. In astrophysical systems, spin affects how matter moves near the event horizon, the efficiency of energy extraction through the accretion disk, and the formation of powerful relativistic jets. Because angular momentum accumulates during accretion and mergers, the distribution of spins carries information about galaxy evolution. However, spin cannot be measured directly — it manifests indirectly through its influence on magnetized plasma and radiation in a strong gravitational field.

Methods

To identify polarization signatures of spin, the authors used numerical simulations of general relativistic magnetohydrodynamics (GRMHD) and polarized radiative transfer, comparing the results with data from radio interferometry with very long baseline (VLBI) by the Event Horizon Telescope (EHT). A key tool was the decomposition of complex linear polarization into azimuthal harmonics β_m, introduced in the context of studying energy extraction by R. Penrose. Spin influences the observed polarization through three channels: photon propagation in the Kerr metric, boundary conditions at the horizon, and the dynamics of magnetized plasma. The simulations made it possible to separate the effects of gravitational lensing from those of the accretion flow.

Results

Analysis of polarization images of M87* and Sagittarius A* obtained by the EHT in 2017–2021 showed that both sources have ordered magnetic fields and are in a magnetically arrested disk (MAD) state, where magnetic pressure dominates. For M87*, the resolved linear polarization reaches 15%, and the electric vector position angle (EVPA) is close to azimuthal, corresponding to a β_2 parameter with a phase of about –160°. However, the spin information is complex: different models predict different a* values for the same data. For example, the β_2 phase in the force-free approximation is related to the ratio of toroidal to radial magnetic field components, but accounting for plasma inertia and Faraday rotation shifts the estimates. For Sgr A*, polarization loops on the Q–U diagram indicate clockwise rotation, and rapid variability requires careful treatment of Faraday effects. Overall, polarimetry most reliably constrains the magnetic flux and orbital inclination, rather than the magnitude or sign of the spin.

Implications

These results deepen the understanding of the physics of accretion and jets in strong gravitational fields. While directly measuring spin remains challenging, polarimetry enables mapping of magnetic fields near the horizon, which is crucial for testing energy extraction mechanisms like the Blandford–Znajek process and for building a unified picture of jet formation. The consistency of data from horizon to jet scales in M87* supports a model with an ordered magnetic field connecting the black hole to the large-scale outflow.

Future development

In the future, space-based VLBI missions such as the Black Hole Explorer (BHEX) will play a key role, providing ultra-long baselines to resolve the photon ring and sub-rings. This will allow direct testing of general relativity in the strong gravity regime. Multi-wavelength polarimetry at 86, 230, and 345 GHz will help separate the source contributions from propagation effects, and dynamic imaging and variability analysis will distinguish turbulent fluctuations from persistent geometric signatures.

Impact

The results will impact black hole astrophysics, accretion physics, and gravitational-wave astronomy, since black hole spin is a key parameter for interpreting mergers detected by LIGO and Virgo. Moreover, polarimetric diagnostic methods can be applied to other classes of objects, such as blazars and quasars, to study their jet structure.

Next steps

Next steps include expanding the libraries of numerical simulations with variation of all parameters, including inclination, electron thermodynamics, and positron content, as well as performing joint Bayesian analysis of polarization data from the EHT, ALMA, and future instruments. A key step will be testing consistency between diagnostics sensitive to different aspects of the system.

Key open problems

The article is directly connected to unsolved problems in physics: the Blandford–Znajek mechanism and rotational energy extraction from black holes, the nature of magnetically arrested accretion, the formation and collimation of relativistic jets, and the origin of magnetic fields in accretion flows. Polarimetric constraints on spin will help advance our understanding of these issues.

🎯 Interestingly, Sagittarius A*, the supermassive black hole at the center of our Milky Way, evolves about 1500 times faster than M87* because its mass is 1500 times smaller. Therefore, its polarization pattern changes over minutes, while M87* requires hours and days.

Key numbers

  • EHT angular resolution at 230 GHz: ~20 microarcseconds
  • M87* ring diameter: 43.3 microarcseconds
  • Sgr A* ring diameter: 51.8 microarcseconds
  • Peak linear polarization of M87* in 2017: ~15%
  • Dimensionless spin parameter a*: from –1 to 1
