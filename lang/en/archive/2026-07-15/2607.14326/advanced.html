The Helium Breath of a Rocky Planet: Atmospheric Escape from LHS 1140b ✦

Original: "Helium escaping from the atmosphere of a nearby rocky exoplanet orbiting in a habitable zone"
· Collin Cherubim, Shreyas Vissapragada, Tim Cunningham, Annabella G. Meech, David Charbonneau, Robin Wordsworth, Aaron Householder, Johanna Teske, Leonardo A. Dos Santos, Nicole L. Wallack, William Misener, Zifan Lin, Andrew McWilliam, Michael Zhang, Jason A. Dittmann, Mercedes López-Morales
arXiv:2607.14326 · 2026-07-15 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Exoplanets
High-resolution spectroscopy has revealed variable helium escape from the atmosphere of a rocky exoplanet in the habitable zone, changing our understanding of atmospheric retention.
Abstract

Spectroscopic observations of the rocky exoplanet LHS 1140b in the habitable zone of a low-mass star revealed helium absorption in its atmosphere during transit in the near-infrared range. The signal was recorded in 2024 but absent in 2025, indicating a variable atmospheric outflow. The data are interpreted as evidence of an upper atmosphere dominated by helium and depleted in hydrogen; other volatiles remain at lower altitudes, consistent with atmospheric fractionation models. No helium was detected on the smaller, more irradiated planet LHS 1140c. These results shed light on the processes of atmospheric retention and loss for rocky worlds around red dwarfs.

Context

Studying exoplanet atmospheres of Earth-like worlds is key to understanding their habitability. Of particular interest are rocky planets in the habitable zones of red dwarfs, where intense stellar radiation can completely strip away the gaseous envelope, leaving a lifeless desert. The 'cosmic shoreline' concept divides worlds into those that have kept and those that have lost their atmospheres, and observations of such systems are crucial for testing it.

Methods

The team led by David Charbonneau used high-resolution spectroscopy with the WINERED spectrograph (Magellan Clay) in transit mode to observe the LHS 1140 system. A key element was applying the Doppler effect to isolate the planetary signal and construct transmission spectra. The analysis involved modeling the signal with Gaussian profiles and Gaussian processes to account for noise, as well as hydrodynamic modeling with the p-winds code.

Results

In 2024, an absorption line of helium was detected at a depth of 1.24%, along with a preceding tail indicating outflow. Models with the p-winds code yielded a mass loss rate of about 2·10^8 g/s, an outflow temperature of ~5160 K, and an H:He ratio of only ~10^{-3}. This points to a helium-dominated upper atmosphere, where water and other volatiles may be trapped at lower levels due to a cold trap. For the neighboring planet LHS 1140c, no signal was detected, consistent with its lack of an extended atmosphere. In 2025, the signal at LHS 1140b also did not appear, indicating variability in the escape, likely linked to stellar activity.

Implications

The results confirm that rocky planets around quiet red dwarfs can retain atmospheres, even if heavily fractionated. This shifts the proposed 'cosmic shoreline' in favor of the possible existence of secondary atmospheres. However, the variability of the escape shows that even old stars can periodically increase their impact on a planet, which must be accounted for in evolutionary models.

Future development

Further observations with high-resolution spectrographs, as well as with JWST, will make it possible to search for similar helium leaks on a larger number of exoplanets, statistically delineating the 'shoreline'. Three-dimensional hydrodynamic modeling that includes magnetic fields and X-ray emission will provide a more accurate picture of atmospheric evolution.

Impact

The discovery impacts our understanding of atmospheric evolution of rocky worlds, habitability criteria, and the interpretation of transit spectroscopy data from future missions.

Next steps

Long-term monitoring of LHS 1140b is planned to identify the connection between leak variability and stellar cycles, as well as searches for helium in other candidates using high-resolution spectroscopy.

Key open problems

The work is directly connected to the problem of the 'cosmic shoreline' — how and which planets retain atmospheres. It also touches on issues of fractionation and the role of helium in the evolution of planetary envelopes, which is important for interpreting data from future telescopes.
