Original: "Radio Galaxy Zoo EMU: Harnessing Citizen Science and AI to Advance Open Science Catalogues"
Humans and AI team up to build the sharpest radio map of the heavens.
Abstract

Radio telescopes see millions of objects, but telling which one is an active galaxy and which is just noise is very hard—like distinguishing a cat from a dog in a blurry photo. The Radio Galaxy Zoo EMU project has enlisted volunteers to help scientists, and smart algorithms double-check their answers. Will we manage to map all the hidden galaxies of the southern sky?

The ASKAP radio telescope sifts through cosmic sand, scooping up millions of radio sources—glowing 'seashells'. These are distant galaxies where supermassive black holes spew out energy. Analyzing radio waves reveals their chemical makeup. But sorting through this mountain of finds by hand is beyond scientists' reach. Volunteers from the Radio Galaxy Zoo project lend a hand. On the website, anyone can sort images like shells on a beach: is it a lone galaxy or a complex structure? The human eye catches what algorithms miss. Then these labels are fed to neural networks, and the machine learns to sort on its own.

Humans still outpace computers at spotting giant structures: some radio galaxies stretch for millions of light-years, remaining invisible to algorithms.

Thanks to thousands of participants, the EMUCAT catalog has become more accurate, and millions more discoveries lie ahead. This is how order emerges from chaos, and science advances through shared effort.
