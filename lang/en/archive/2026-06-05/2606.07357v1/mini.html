Original: "Origin of monolithic high-z galaxies and UV luminosity of mergering high-z galaxies in the cosmological model with non-standard spectrum of density perturbations"
A tiny 'hump' in the primordial density ripples explains why the James Webb sees so many mature galaxies in the cradle of time.

The James Webb Space Telescope sees too many mature galaxies in the early Universe—as if the orchestra played the finale before the conductor appeared. The solution: a microscopic 'hump' in the spectrum of primordial ripples: nature played one note slightly louder, and giant galaxies were born ahead of schedule. This is not just a patch for the theory, but the first clue linking quantum fluctuations from inflation to the birth of colossal stellar systems. Future sky surveys promise to turn the hypothesis into a new reality.

🎯 The term 'Population IV stars' was coined by the authors for primordial giants in massive halos—in contrast to classical Population III stars, born in mini-halos. These short-lived beacons could have seeded the cosmos with metals, shaping the chemical face of the entire universe.

P_{\text{bump}}(k) = P_{\Lambda\text{CDM}}(k) \left[1 + A \exp\left(-\frac{(\log k - \log k_0)^2}{\sigma_k^2}\right)\right]
Parameterization of a Gaussian peak in the density spectrum at wavenumber k0 with amplitude A and width σk.
P_{\text{fs}}(k) = P_{\text{bump}}(k) \exp(-2k^2/k_{\text{fs}}^2)
Exponential suppression on scales smaller than k_fs, analogous to the free-streaming effect of warm dark matter particles.
