Freo Doctor: How Wind Governs the Fate of Meteorites ✦

Original: "Freo Doctor: Atmospheric Modelling for Meteorite Falls and Spacecraft Re-Entries"
· Hadrien Devillepoix, Martin Cupák
arXiv:2606.07144v1 · 2026-06-05 · CC BY 4.0 · ⏱ 3 min · Exoplanets Atmospheric and Oceanic Physics
High-resolution atmospheric modeling reveals the critical uncertainty that wind introduces into predicting meteorite fall locations.
Links in the knowledge graph 1

Perth residents know: when the daytime heat becomes unbearable, relief arrives from the ocean—a fresh breeze affectionately called the Freo Doctor. The same wind, only on a global scale, decides the fate of cosmic visitors plunging into the atmosphere. A meteorite fall is a two-act spectacle. First, a fiery trail from a comet or asteroid streaks across the sky as a bolide, then comes the quiet, almost invisible phase—dark flight. It's here, in the final tens of kilometers, that air currents become both director and capricious actor. The Devillepoix and Cupák team peered into the atmosphere's invisible dance—the main uncertainty in meteorite hunting.

The simulation included 1107 possible scenarios at 1-kilometer resolution—like trying to guess every gust’s path over a small town, factoring in heating from the Sun and the whims of humidity.

The tool was WRF—an open-source weather model that recreates the dynamics of the lower 30 kilometers of the atmosphere. The scientists ran an ensemble of 1107 scenarios. The tiniest input error cascades like an avalanche: the entropy of nonlinear equations kicks in. The result is staggering. For a kilogram-sized meteorite, the median shift is 143 meters; for a ten-gram fragment, it’s already 307 meters. The spread is kilometers, especially in extreme weather. The relationship turned out to be a power law: Δx ∝ m^{-0.16}—the lighter the traveler, the more freely it flutters in the wind’s hands. So for a few moments, a meteorite becomes part of the atmosphere, obeying the same laws that scatter pollen or ash.

The result: 12 meteorites recovered thanks to these calculations. Each one is a time capsule from the depths of the Solar System, where we could never peer otherwise.

For search teams, this means one thing: the era of simply drawing ellipses of dispersion is over. Now you must weave probabilistic wind-drift maps into the forecast, or else precious cosmic material will lie untouched in the bushes. The authors suggest launching radiosondes promptly when a bright bolide is spotted—so the computer gets a real-time snapshot of the actual atmospheric state, instead of feeding on outdated global data. Photometry and Spectroscopy give us speed and trajectory, but only a partnership with meteorology can close the chain from the sky to the museum showcase.

And so each recovered stone will tell its story—not only about itself but also about how the wind-doctor delivered it a verdict of acquittal or conviction. Along the way, the sharpened methods will also be useful for catching return capsules and tracking space debris—wind governs both natural and human-made objects alike. Thus, the Freo Doctor transforms from a local legend into a full participant in the grand astrophysical game, where the stake is the very history of the Solar System.

🎯 The name 'Freo Doctor' is borrowed from the cool breeze that brings daily relief to the residents of Perth, Australia. So it is with meteorite hunting: correctly accounting for wind can be a lifeline, while ignoring it is a sure path to fruitless searches.
