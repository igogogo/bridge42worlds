The Magnetic Heart of Red Giants express

Original: "Extending asteroseismic magnetometry across the diverse landscape of magnetic structures"
· Nicholas Z. Rui, J. M. Joel Ong, Armand Leclerc, Daniel Lecoanet, Lisa Bugnet, Janosz W. Dewberry, Bastien Liagre, Stéphane Mathis
arXiv:2606.07787 · 2026-06-05 · CC BY · 1 min · Stellar
Magnetic fields deep inside red giants drive their pulsations – and now scientists can calculate that.
Abstract

Using asteroseismology (the study of 'starquakes'), scientists measure magnetic fields in the cores of red giants. A new study extends a method that previously worked only for fields aligned with the rotation axis to arbitrary configurations. It shows that non-axisymmetric fields can cause avoided frequency crossings (where modes approach without merging and exchange properties) and conversion of waves from one polarization to another, especially in weak fields. It’s like two coupled pendulums passing oscillations back and forth. This finding refines the limits of applicability for simplified theories.

Red giants are swollen, aging stars. Deep inside, an ocean of plasma churns: heat forces its way from the core to the surface, generating waves like a mighty surf. The star's magnetic field acts like an invisible seabed – it steers these waves, changing their height and speed. Previously, astronomers could only account for a smooth, symmetrical 'seafloor,' but the real picture is much more tangled.

A new calculation method now lets us recreate waves in magnetic fields of any shape. It turns out a wave can change its nature on the fly: upon hitting a strong field, it doesn't disappear but morphs into a different type of wave. Where the field weakens, waves even leap over 'magnetic ridges,' preserving their energy.

But if the field is too powerful, the star 'goes silent' at certain frequencies – its pulse becomes irregular.

These findings make spectral diagnostics of stars more accurate. After all, a similar fate awaits our own Sun: in billions of years, it will become a red giant with a restless magnetic heart.

🎯 Red giants are our Sun’s future. When the [tag:hydrogen]hydrogen[/tag] in its core runs out, it will swell up, and its magnetic field may become as chaotic as those of the stars we’ve studied.
