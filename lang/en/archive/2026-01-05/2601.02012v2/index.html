Betelgeuse Is Not Alone: A Stellar Companion Found ⚡ экспресс

Original: "The Betelgeuse Enigma: The Betelbuddy Hypothesis"
· Priya Hasan
arXiv:2601.02012v2 · 2026-01-05 · CC BY 4.0 · ⏱ 1 min · Stellar
Astronomers have captured the first image of Betelgeuse’s dim companion, lost in its glare.
Abstract

The dimming of Betelgeuse in 2019–2020 (the star became three times fainter) was chalked up to a dust eruption, not an imminent supernova. But the mystery lingered: observations hinted at an unseen companion. On July 21, 2025, it was directly imaged for the first time with the Gemini North telescope and the 'Alopeke instrument. The companion is 6 magnitudes dimmer than Betelgeuse and orbits right inside its vast atmosphere at an angular separation of 52 mas (milliarcseconds). This discovery, though of modest significance (1.5σ), lines up beautifully with predictions and could shed light on the red supergiant's behavior. The next good window to catch it is November 2027.

In 2019, Betelgeuse suddenly dimmed, sparking rumors of an imminent explosion. But clouds of cosmic dust turned out to be the culprit. Now, the Gemini North telescope has discerned a minuscule companion star in its dazzling glare—just as calculations had predicted.

Betelgeuse is like a stadium floodlight, and its companion is a firefly flickering right in the beam. It’s 250 times fainter, but astronomers managed to measure its position and the difference in brightness. The match with theory was impressive.

The proximity explains long-standing oddities of the giant. Crucially, the companion orbits Betelgeuse in just a few years. This swift dance not only pushes back a possible supernova explosion, but also gives us a chance to keep tracking the pair. The next observation is set for November 2027.

🎯 Unofficial names for the companion are Betelbuddy or Siwarha. Its full orbit around Betelgeuse takes just a few years—by cosmic standards, a very brisk dance.
