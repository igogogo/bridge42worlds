Supermassive Black Holes Spin Independently of Their Galaxies ✦

Original: "The direction of rotation of supermassive black holes is unrelated to the direction of rotation of the host galaxy"
· Loren Gigi, C. Martin Gaskell
arXiv:2607.06902 · 2026-07-08 · CC BY · 4 min · Galaxies
Spectropolarimetry of active galactic nuclei reveals that their spin orientation is random relative to their host disks.
Abstract

Comparing the apparent spin directions of active galactic nuclei (AGN) and their host disk galaxies was done using spectropolarimetry: AGN spin was determined via the change in polarization position angle across broad lines, caused by presumed equatorial scattering. The number of co-rotating and counter-rotating systems is equal. This rules out the 'avoidance zone' hypothesis for radio jets due to small black hole axis tilts; biconical outflows from the narrow-line region also avoid the galaxy plane. The results point to random spin axis orientations in the studied black hole mass range. Deviations of the polarization position angle from the equatorial scattering model are explained by dust clumpiness. Cases of position angle variation over years are noted, possibly due to moving dust clumps or variable continuum anisotropy.

Context

The spin orientation of a supermassive black hole at the center of a galaxy dictates the efficiency of relativistic jet launching and the power of feedback, which in turn regulates star formation and bulge growth. It was long thought that gas from the galactic disk, falling into the accretion disk, gradually aligns the hole’s spin axis with the system’s overall angular momentum via the Lense–Thirring effect (frame-dragging of inertial frames). However, radio observations using radio astronomy revealed a “zone of avoidance”: jets shy away from the galactic disk plane, which could be explained either by closely aligned axes (small tilts) or by the impact of the dense interstellar medium on jet propagation. Directly measuring the spin direction of the active nucleus can resolve this dilemma.

Methods

For a sample of 54 type-1 active galactic nuclei, the spin direction of the broad-line region was derived via spectroscopy and spectropolarimetry. The approach relies on an equatorial scattering model: photons from the approaching and receding sides of the rotating gas disk strike dust clumps at different position angles, imprinting a distinctive S-shaped swing in the position angle of polarization across the profile of Balmer lines (Hα, Hβ). If the red wing has, on average, a larger position angle than the blue wing, the rotation is clockwise, and vice versa. For the host galaxies, the spin direction was recovered from the winding of spiral arms (assuming they trail the rotation) or from the velocity field obtained via spatially resolved spectroscopy.

Results

Of the 54 objects studied, 27 displayed clockwise rotation and 27 counterclockwise. Among those for which the galaxy spin direction could be determined, 11 systems turned out to be aligned and 13 counter-aligned, consistent with a random orientation within statistical uncertainties. This result contradicts the idea that the radio-jet “zone of avoidance” is due to small angles between the black hole axis and the galaxy axis; additionally, biconical outflows from the narrow-line region were found to avoid the disk plane with a statistical significance of p ~ 0.03. Deviations from the ideal S-shape of the position-angle curves match the clumpy nature of the scattering dust and its motion, corroborated by a couple of cases of long-term polarization variability.

Implications

Equal fractions of co- and counter-rotating pairs imply that on scales of black holes with masses from 10^6 to 10^9 M⊙, accretion is predominantly chaotic, with frequent flips of angular-momentum orientation. This rules out the classic scenario of steady, coherent feeding of gas from the disk and demands a dominant role for random accretion episodes and galaxy mergers in shaping the central object’s spin. Consequently, jet-launching efficiency and feedback must be time-variable and depend less on the global galaxy structure than on local conditions within a kiloparsec of the nucleus.

Future development

The immediate next step is to enlarge the sample by including spiral galaxies with low black-hole masses, where theoretical models predict better preservation of the initial angular momentum. New spectropolarimetric observations on 8–10 meter-class telescopes are needed, as well as systematic monitoring of known objects to test the predictions of the moving dust-clump model. Incorporating spin evolution into cosmological simulations that include feedback will clarify how random orientation affects the growth of supermassive black holes and the quenching of star formation.

Impact

The findings impact theories of galaxy evolution, the physics of accretion and relativistic jet launching, and models of gravitational-wave sources, where the spin of merging black holes determines the recoil amplitude.

Next steps

High-quality spectropolarimetry is needed for nearby spiral galaxies with dynamically measured black-hole masses, along with multi-year monitoring of objects with known polarization variability.

Key open problems

This work directly addresses the unresolved question of supermassive black hole growth: does coherent or chaotic accretion dominate at different stages of cosmic evolution? It also touches on the nature of active-nucleus feedback, the jet collimation mechanism, and how black hole spins encode the merger history of their host galaxies.

🎯 Spectropolarimetry lets us “see” the rotation direction of gas around a black hole, even when we’re staring straight into its center: polarized light reaches us after scattering off a dusty torus that acts like a giant mirror, reflecting the blue and red wings of emission lines in distinct ways.
