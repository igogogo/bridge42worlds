Black Holes Spin Every Which Way, Ignoring Their Galaxies ✦

Original: "The direction of rotation of supermassive black holes is unrelated to the direction of rotation of the host galaxy"
· Loren Gigi, C. Martin Gaskell
arXiv:2607.06902 · 2026-07-08 · CC BY · 3 min
Galaxies
Spectropolarimetry of 54 active galactic nuclei reveals their spins are randomly scattered, like spinning tops after a kick, paying no heed to their parent disks.
Abstract

Astronomers compared how supermassive black holes spin on their axes and how their host galaxies rotate. It turns out that the number of cases with aligned and opposite rotation is equal. This refutes the hypothesis that jets (narrow plasma streams) avoid the galactic plane because black hole axes are rarely strongly tilted. In fact, it seems the axis orientations are completely random — at least for the studied black hole masses. Like a die: thrown billions of years ago, and now each black hole 'looks' in its own direction.

1

For decades astrophysicists painted a neat picture: a galaxy, like a giant carousel, spins in a unified rhythm, and the supermassive black hole at its center inherits that dance. Gas from the disk, falling into the accretion disk, supposedly drags the hole’s axis along via the Lense-Thirring effect, aligning the spin with the overall angular momentum. But new work on 54 active galactic nuclei shatters this idyll. It turns out the central black hole is not a docile cog in the carousel but a restless spinning top: chaotic accretion kicks its axis in random directions, like a wayward hand setting the toy askew.

The galaxy is a carousel, the central black hole a spinning top whose axis is kicked every which way by random nudges, heedless of the overall rhythm.

To 'see' the elusive axis, scientists used spectroscopy and spectropolarimetry. The method is like interrogating a hall of mirrors: dust clumps near the hole scatter light, acting as a warped mirror. For the red and blue wings of Balmer lines, the polarization position angle behaves differently — a characteristic S-shaped swing appears. If the red wing has a larger angle, the spin is clockwise, and vice versa. In parallel, the twist of spiral arms or the velocity field determined the rotation direction of the galaxy itself.

The result is stunning: among 54 objects, exactly 27 spin clockwise, 27 counterclockwise. And for those with reliably determined galaxy direction, 11 were aligned, 13 counter-aligned. Within uncertainties — pure chance, like flipping a coin 24 times. This pattern rules out the classic coherent feeding scenario and asserts the reign of chaos — frequent galaxy mergers and random episodes of material infall. Consequently, jet power and feedback that regulates star formation must be time-variable and depend not on global structure but on local weather around the nucleus — jazz, not a symphony.

Equal fractions of co- and counter-spin mean the mysterious 'avoidance zone' of radio jets (they rarely lie in the galaxy disk) is not due to precise alignment but likely to shielding by dense interstellar medium.

The outlook is thrilling. Now cosmological simulations tracking spin evolution must include random spin reorientations. This will allow better predictions of when a galaxy will flare with an active nucleus and when its center will fall into dormancy. The crucial next step is spectropolarimetry of nearby spiral systems with small black holes, where hope still lingers to catch traces of past synchronization. At stake is not just a curious fact but a whole layer of feedback physics — the lever by which black holes steer the fate of their galaxies. And here’s food for thought: spin randomness means that even twin galaxies can live radically different lives — one may fall asleep forever, the other burst into a blaze of new stars. In the cosmos, chaos isn’t a bug; it’s an evolutionary feature.

The dimensionless spin a describes how fast a black hole rotates: it can range from -1 to 1. It was its orientation, not just its magnitude, that became the bone of contention — like a spinning top, not only how fast but which way it spins matters.
