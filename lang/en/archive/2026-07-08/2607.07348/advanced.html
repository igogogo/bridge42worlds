Cosmic Acceleration in Question: Why DESI Sees Deceleration but SDSS Doesn't? ✦

Original: "Present Day Cosmic Acceleration from SDSS and DESI BAO: A Call for Finer Tomography of the DESI Bright Galaxy Survey"
· Anna Chiara Ferri, Ruchika, Alessandro Melchiorri
arXiv:2607.07348 · 2026-07-08 · CC BY 4.0 · 5 min · Cosmology
Different surveys of baryon acoustic oscillations paint a contradictory picture of the current expansion of the universe, and the problem lies in the redshift depth.
Abstract

Analysis of baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO) from DESI DR2 data (over 14 million galaxies and quasars) combined with CMB radiation and type Ia supernovae reveals a preference for time-varying dark energy. Comparison with SDSS data shows: DESI+Planck yields $$w_0 = -0.41^{+0.21}_{-0.22}$$ and $$q_0 = 0.10^{+0.21}_{-0.23}$$, while SDSS+Planck gives $$w_0 = -0.71^{+0.19}_{-0.18}$$ and $$q_0 = -0.22^{+0.20}_{-0.21}$$. The reason lies in the different minimum effective redshifts: $$z_{\rm eff}\approx0.295$$ for DESI and $$\approx0.15$$ for SDSS. Present-day parameters $$w_0$$ and $$q_0$$ are sensitive to data at $$z\approx0$$; high $$z$$ require CPL parameterization extrapolation. DESI, lacking data at $$z<0.295$$, is forced to extrapolate, shifting $$q_0$$ into positive territory and $$w_0$$ above $$-1$$. Pantheon+ restores low-redshift data and returns $$q_0$$ to negative values, relieving the tension with ΛCDM. Thus, the anomaly is a sample artifact, not new physics; split tomography is proposed for further testing.

Context

The question of whether the universe is accelerating now is one of the key issues in cosmology. The foundations of modern cosmology were laid by Edwin Hubble, who discovered the expansion, and Georges Lemaître, who predicted it theoretically. The acceleration was discovered through observations of distant type Ia supernovae by Saul Perlmutter and colleagues. After that discovery, the model with a cosmological constant (ΛCDM) became the standard for dark energy. However, new data from spectroscopic surveys DESI and SDSS, measuring baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO) in the distribution of galaxies and quasars, combined with cosmic microwave background (CMB) and gravitational lensing of the CMB, allow precise constraints on the properties of dark energy. Recent DESI DR2 results hint at a possible evolution of the equation of state over time, and the most intriguing aspect is that the median deceleration parameter q0 turns out to be positive, contradicting the established picture of an accelerating universe. This discrepancy with SDSS conclusions requires an explanation: either there is hidden systematics in the data, or we are misinterpreting the parameterization of dark energy. The authors hypothesize that it all comes down to the different redshift depths of the surveys.

Methods

The analysis used public MCMC chains from the Planck, SDSS DR16, and DESI DR2 collaborations. Within a unified CPL (w0-wa) parameterization, where w(a)=w0+wa(1-a), data combinations were compared: Planck 2018 CMB (temperature and polarization spectra plus lensing), BAO measurements from DESI (lowest effective zeff≈0.295 from the Bright Galaxy Sample BGS) and SDSS (including the MGS sample with zeff≈0.15), as well as the Pantheon+ supernova compilation. Models with constant w (wCDM) and massive neutrinos were also considered. The key tool was the deceleration parameter q0, which in a flat universe is expressed through Friedmann equations and Hubble's law as q0 = 1/2 Ωm + 1/2 (1+3w0) ΩDE. A robustness test was performed: the low-redshift anchor was removed from the SDSS analysis to mimic the DESI situation.

Results

In the CPL model, the Planck+SDSS combination yields an accelerating universe: w0 = -0.71 (+0.19/-0.18), q0 = -0.22 (+0.20/-0.21), and the transition from deceleration to acceleration occurred at zcrit ≈ 0.76. A completely different picture emerges from Planck+DESI: the median q0 = +0.10 (+0.21/-0.23) formally lies in the deceleration regime (though compatible with zero within 1σ), w0 = -0.41 (+0.21/-0.22), and the q(z) function crosses zero twice — at z∼0.08 and ∼0.86. This indicates that without additional low-redshift data it is impossible to claim that present-day expansion is accelerating. Adding Pantheon+ to Planck+DESI radically changes the situation: q0 = -0.37±0.06, w0 = -0.84±0.06, and acceleration is robustly recovered. The key experiment — removing the nearby MGS sample from SDSS — raises the effective redshift to ~0.38 and shifts q0 to -0.10 (+0.33/-0.35), bringing it closer to the DESI value. This proves that the discrepancy is indeed due to the difference in the minimum z eff.

Implications

The main conclusion: the apparent preference for a non-accelerating universe in the DESI BAO+CMB combination is not necessarily a hint of new physics. It naturally arises from the need to extrapolate the CPL parameterization into the z < 0.3 range, where DESI lacks direct distance measurements. This does not negate the stronger evidence for dynamical dark energy (non-zero wa) obtained when adding supernovae, but it emphasizes that interpreting current data requires caution. To reliably answer the question “is the universe accelerating now”, a robust anchor at z → 0 is necessary.

Future development

The most direct development is the implementation of finer “tomographic” binning in the Bright Galaxy Sample (BGS) of the DESI survey. Currently, BGS covers the range 0.1 < z < 0.4 as a single bin; splitting into two (e.g., 0.1–0.25 and 0.25–0.4) would yield a point with z eff ≈ 0.18, practically equivalent to the best SDSS measurement. This would allow testing whether the conclusions of the two surveys converge at the same redshift “depth”. Further prospects are tied to future spectroscopic surveys (Euclid, Nancy Grace Roman), which will provide an even more detailed map of expansion in the local universe.

Impact

The results will impact the interpretation of DESI data and the planning of observational strategies, as well as cross-validation between different surveys. They are important for the whole of cosmology, testing the expansion of the universe ΛCDM model, and searching for deviations from standard physics.

Next steps

The next step is a detailed forecast of what minimum z eff and what accuracy in determining q0 can be achieved with fine BGS binning, taking into account cosmic variance. A cross-analysis with fully unified versions of CMB data is also needed to isolate the BAO contribution.

Key open problems

The study is directly connected to the fundamental problem of the nature of dark energy. Is it a cosmological constant, or a dynamical field whose equation of state changed over time? The uncertainty about present-day acceleration is part of a broader crisis of the standard model, including the “Hubble tension”. Additionally, the work touches upon systematic errors in BAO measurements and the reliability of parameterizations used to describe dark energy.
