Nebula Orchestra: Why Lines Hear Different Densities ✦

Original: "There is no single density: star-forming regions and galaxies hold more dense ionized gas than long assumed"
· J. Eduardo Méndez-Delgado, Christophe Morisset, William J. Henney, Niv Drory, Oleg V. Egorov, Sebastián F. Sánchez, Juna A. Kollmeier, Kathryn Kreckel, Guillermo Blanc, Grażyna Stasińska, Evelyn J. Lugo-Aranda, Aida Wofford, Simon C. O. Glover, Evgeniya Egorova, Rodolfo de J. Zermeño, Lesly C. Castañeda-Carlos, Fu-Heng Liang, Natascha Sattler, Irene Cruz-González, Joel R. arXiv:2607.07973 · 2026-07-08 · CC BY · 3 min · Galaxies
What was thought to be instrument error turned out to be a symphony: each forbidden line sings the density it can hear in a multi-voiced distribution from whisper to roar.
Abstract

Emission lines—the glow of ionized gas—are the primary tool for studying nebulae. Lines sensitive to density gave values differing by a factor of a hundred. It was thought that inaccurate data or stratification was to blame. Now it's clear: each line is tuned to its own density, like a sieve, and real nebulae have a wide range of densities. Therefore, the lines show different parts of this range, not an average value. This means there is much more dense gas in nebulae than previously assumed, and all calculations (masses, pressures, composition) will have to be revised.

The Orion Nebula is a showcase of astrophysics, a laboratory for studying star formation right in our backyard. For over a century, its light has been split into spectra, and each forbidden line whispers its own version of how many electrons per cubic centimeter. But the versions don't match: sulfur insists on one thing, oxygen another, nitrogen whispers the unimaginable. It's as if a symphony orchestra is playing, but each listener hears only their own instrument—the violinist thinks it's quiet, while the timpanist is deafened.

But the nebula is not a featureless soup, but a boiling ocean of plasma, where foam of rarefied gas coexists with droplets of super-dense matter. Each emission line is like a tuning fork, set to a specific density 'note.' [S II] sings when there are tens of particles around, while [Cl III] and [Fe III] respond only at thousands. This is not a defect of atomic physics, but the richness of the medium itself—density at a single point in the nebula varies by 4–5 orders of magnitude.

Only 10% of Orion's emission comes from ultra-dense droplets (over 10,000 particles/cm³). Imagine: the whisper of a dozen people in a stadium outshouts the roar of thousands—that's how these dense cores dominate the forbidden lines.

A team of astronomers used the SDSS-V/LVM survey, which for the first time enabled spatially resolved spectroscopy of the entire nebula simultaneously using 16 different indicators in the same volumes. It turned out that all indicators form a strict hierarchy: the higher the indicator's maximum sensitivity (n_M), the greater the density it shows. The correlation is described by the line log n_e = 0.29 log n_M + 1.52 (r=0.90). Direct numerical simulation showed that such behavior is natural if the gas follows a power-law distribution H_n ∝ n_e^{β} with β≈-1.3—density spans from tens to hundreds of thousands of particles per cubic centimeter. And when scientists isolated a narrow density layer using Doppler shift in shock waves, the hierarchy vanished—the lines sang in unison, like an orchestra that suddenly got a conductor.

This discovery is not a correction to textbooks, but a rewriting of entire chapters. All quantities that astrophysicists derived from emission lines, assuming a single density value—mass of ionized gas, pressure, chemical composition (metallicity), feedback energy—now require reassessment. The broad density distribution appears to be not an exception, but the rule for the interstellar medium, from local star-forming regions to distant galaxies, a legacy of gravito-turbulent fragmentation of parent clouds. A polyphonic fugue, where each voice tells its own story, and only together they reveal the true structure.

Imagine: a mercury thermometer shows Arctic cold, while a laser one shows tropical heat. You'd call that a malfunction. But for Orion, it's reality: in the same spot, some indicators see hundreds of particles, others tens of thousands, each in its own density 'well.' The error was not in the lines, but in our desire to average everything out.

In perspective—a revision of standard methods. Instead of a single number, we'll need entire distribution functions, self-consistent photoionization models, and grids covering the range of power-law parameters. JWST and large-scale surveys like SDSS-V will allow us to trace how the density hierarchy changes with redshift—during the epoch of vigorous star formation in the Universe. And then, perhaps, we'll begin to hear not individual notes, but the whole symphony of interstellar gas, from the quietest pianissimo to the grandest fortissimo.

🎯 In the exact same spot of the Orion Nebula, sulfur lines 'see' a hundred electrons per cubic centimeter, while iron lines see a hundred thousand. It's like a room thermometer showing 0°C, while a non-contact one shows 100°C. Now we know: both were right.

\log_{10} n_{\mathrm{e,obs}} = (0.29 \pm 0.05) \log_{10} n_{\mathrm{M}} + (1.52 \pm 0.27)
This equation shows that the observed density increases with the indicator's maximum sensitivity: the higher-density regions it 'sees,' the larger the value it reports. The slope of 0.29 indicates that the density reported by the indicator lies somewhere between the low and high values of the distribution.
n_{\mathrm{M}} = \arg \max \left| \frac{\mathrm{d} \log R}{\mathrm{d} \log n_{\mathrm{e}}} \right|
Here n_M is the density at which the change in the emission line ratio R is maximal—the point of best sensitivity. It's like an ear that best distinguishes loudness at a certain level.
