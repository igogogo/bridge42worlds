Ionized Nebulae Hide a Wide Range of Gas Densities ✦

Original: "There is no single density: star-forming regions and galaxies hold more dense ionized gas than long assumed"
· J. Eduardo Méndez-Delgado, Christophe Morisset, William J. Henney, Niv Drory, Oleg V. Egorov, Sebastián F. Sánchez, Juna A. Kollmeier, Kathryn Kreckel, Guillermo Blanc, Grażyna Stasińska, Evelyn J. Johnston, Héctor J. Ibarra-Medel, Alfredo J. Mejía-Narváez, César Esteban, Jorge García-Rojas, Amrita Singh, Ivan Yu. Katkov, Evan D. Skillman, Rogelio Orozco-Duarte, Igor A. Zinchenko, Alejandra Z. Lugo-Aranda, Aida Wofford, Simon C. O. Glover, Evgeniya Egorova, Rodolfo de J. Zermeño, Lesly C. Castañeda-Carlos, Fu-Heng Liang, Natascha Sattler, Irene Cruz-González, Joel R. Brownstein, Thomas Hilder, Donald P. Schneider
arXiv:2607.07973 · 2026-07-08 · CC BY · 3 min · Galaxies
Different spectral density indicators in ionized nebulae yield varying values because each is sensitive to its own part of a broad density distribution, not to a single value.
Abstract

Discrepancies in determining electron density in ionized gas via emission lines, reaching two orders of magnitude, were usually attributed to errors in atomic data or ionization stratification. It has been shown that the reason is more fundamental: each diagnostic line is sensitive to its own density range, and in nebulae with a broad density distribution, the lines register only the regions they are tuned to. A simple relationship was discovered between the density returned by a line and the density of its maximum sensitivity; this relationship is confirmed from HII regions to entire galaxies. Therefore, there is no single value for electron density—nebulae have a broad distribution containing far more dense gas than any single indicator showed. Estimates of masses, pressures, composition, and energetics, based on the assumption of a single density, require revision for star-forming regions and galaxies throughout the Universe.

Context

Electron density is a key parameter governing the cooling, pressure, and emission of ionized gas in astrophysics. Traditionally, it's measured using ratios of forbidden lines, but different line pairs yield contradictory results, calling into question the accuracy of physical characteristics derived from spectroscopy. Resolving this paradox opens the door to more reliable models and interpretations, from local star formation regions to distant galaxies.

Methods

To test this hypothesis, the authors used data from the SDSS-V/LVM survey, which provided spatially resolved spectroscopy of the entire Orion Nebula with simultaneous coverage over a broad wavelength range. This enabled them to construct density maps using 16 different indicators based on forbidden lines in the same volume elements. They also incorporated the DESIRED database, containing spectra of nebulae and galaxies with direct temperature measurements. For interpretation, they built a minimal forward model of three-dimensional plasma with a power-law distribution of emission measure over density, ignoring ionization stratification. Line emission was calculated using the PyNeb code, and the resulting ratios were inverted to density in the same manner as in observational studies.

Results

The results showed that the median densities obtained by different indicators fall along a clear linear sequence as a function of their maximum sensitivity (n_M). In the Orion Nebula, the correlation is described by log n_e = 0.29 log n_M + 1.52 with a correlation coefficient r=0.90. This hierarchy holds for different ions and wavelengths, as well as in a sample of extragalactic star formation regions and galaxies with varying metallicity. Direct numerical simulation reproduced this pattern using a broad power-law density distribution with an exponent β≈-1.3 and a range spanning nearly five orders of magnitude. Estimates indicate that (10±5)% of the emission measure comes from gas with density above 10^4 cm⁻³, with the upper limit of the distribution extending to at least 10^5.2 cm⁻³. High-resolution observations of shocked regions in Orion, using Doppler shift to separate components, confirmed that when a narrow density range is isolated, the hierarchy vanishes.

Implications

The discovery of systematic bias in density indicators means that all quantities derived from forbidden lines under the assumption of a single density — ionized gas masses, pressure, filling factor, chemical composition, energy fluxes — must be revised. The broad density distribution indicates that in the ionized interstellar medium, structures coexist ranging from diffuse gas to dense filaments and condensations. This also explains the origin of temperature inhomogeneities, previously attributed to separate effects but now directly linked to density structure.

Future development

Future research should aim to replace the single-density model with distributions consistent with the data. Self-consistent photoionization models that incorporate the observed density hierarchy and methods for recovering the distribution function from spectra will be needed. With new data from JWST and surveys like SDSS-V, it will become possible to trace the evolution of density structure to high redshifts and study its connection to turbulence and gravitational fragmentation of parent clouds.

Impact

The results will influence the interpretation of spectroscopic observations in astrophysics from local star-forming regions to distant galaxies, as well as estimates of key parameters such as mass, metallicity, and feedback energetics in galaxies.

Next steps

It is necessary to develop and implement models with realistic density distributions into standard emission-line analysis methods, as well as to create libraries of photoionization model grids covering a wide range of density distribution parameters.

Key open problems

This work touches on the fundamental problem of our incomplete knowledge of ionized gas structure in the Universe. The broad density distribution may be a relic of gravoturbulent fragmentation of molecular clouds, linking the physics of star formation with observable signatures in the optical range. The exact form of the distribution function and its connection to ionization processes and dynamics remain open questions.
