Stellar J-Harvesting: A New Path to Extraterrestrial Technosignatures ✦

Original: "Stellar J-Harvesting: a novel angular momentum technosignature and first search in the Kepler field"
· Sahin Torlakcik
arXiv:2607.07781 · 2026-07-08 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · Stellar Galaxies Instrumentation
Star spin as a clue: a pilot search for anomalously slow rotators among 6725 Kepler stars opens a new window for SETI, free from infrared noise.
Abstract

The concept of Stellar J-Harvesting (SJH) is introduced — a technosignature where an artificial system extracts rotational angular momentum from a star. Unlike a classic Dyson sphere, such a system does not necessarily produce an excess in the mid-infrared; its main indicator is anomalously slow rotation compared to stars of the same type. The energy dependence on period is derived, possible interaction channels are outlined, and then a search for relatively slow rotators among Kepler stars is performed using color–surface gravity bins. After eight false-positive filters, the clean sample contains 6,725 main-sequence stars of spectral classes F, G, K. Two slow rotators with >4σ deviation were found, but Gaia DR3 and WISE data point to more mundane causes: unresolved binary systems and low metallicity. Therefore, no discovery claim is made; a conservative pilot upper limit on the frequency of strong SJH-like signals is set at f_SJH < 4.5×10⁻⁴.

Context

Searches for extraterrestrial intelligence have traditionally looked for stellar megastructures that re-radiate light in the infrared. However, no candidate has been confirmed, prompting a turn to alternative energy sources. A star's rotational angular momentum is a colossal reservoir: for instance, the Sun's rotational kinetic energy is ~2.3×10³⁵ J. Harvesting it with an engineered structure would require relatively small facilities, emit negligible heat invisible to telescopes, but would alter the rotation period—in accord with the law of conservation of angular momentum—something measurable thanks to Kepler's photometric mission and spectroscopic data from Gaia. The idea traces back to pioneering work by William Borucki, who discovered thousands of exoplanets and created rotation catalogs.

Methods

The sample of 6725 main-sequence FGK stars (4000–7000 K, log g ≥ 4.2) with known rotation periods was cleaned with eight filters: stars with low metallicity ([Fe/H] < -0.5), astrometric and spectroscopic binaries, subgiants, and variables were excluded. For each star, the deviation of the logarithm of the period from the median value in a color–gravity cell (Δlog P) was computed. The statistical significance z was determined considering the spread in the cell. This population-relative test does not depend on unreliable age estimates from gyrochronology; instead, it relies on the fundamental second law of thermodynamics to estimate the inevitable heat emission. Additionally, WISE images in four infrared bands, interpreted within the Stefan–Boltzmann law, were used to check the stellar environments.

Results

After filtering, only two stars (KIC 6606183 and KIC 9834255) showed >4σ slowdown, with periods of 61 and 65 days, whereas typical periods for their peers are 5–10 days. If interpreted as an SJH signal, over 95% of the rotational energy has been extracted, implying a radical violation of the conservation of angular momentum. However, Gaia DR3 data revealed low metallicity ([M/H] = -0.60) and a 9.4-σ astrometric anomaly for the first candidate, hinting at an unseen companion, while the second showed signs of flux contamination from a neighbor. WISE infrared images, sensitive to emission according to the Stefan–Boltzmann law, did not show a clear excess, but the resolution (~6″ in W3) is insufficient to exclude a physical connection between close sources. Thus, both candidates have plausible astrophysical explanations, and we do not claim a detection. The conservative upper limit on the prevalence of strong SJH signals is f_SJH < 4.5×10⁻⁴ (95% confidence level).

Implications

The key result is demonstrating that the technosignature space is not limited to infrared heat. Angular momentum provides an independent and testable search channel that, unlike a Dyson sphere, does not require shading the star. The proposed method can be applied to any stellar catalog with measured rotation periods, including future TESS and Gaia DR4 data. Discovering even a single confirmed SJH candidate would be direct evidence of technological activity by an alien civilization, comparable to Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar's breakthroughs in stellar evolution.

Future development

Development of this topic will proceed along three lines. First, spectroscopic monitoring of radial velocities and v sin i measurements for the candidates will clarify their nature. Second, an analysis of stars with low chromospheric activity will be conducted—it is expected that an SJH star experiences braking without strengthening its magnetic field, unlike natural magnetic braking. Third, a targeted radio search for synchrotron radiation from braking arrays (SSA) with VLA or LOFAR could yield a secondary technosignature. Finally, high-resolution interferometric observations will reliably resolve multiple systems.

Impact

This work impacts several fields: astrobiology and SETI, by expanding the set of search features; stellar physics, by stimulating detailed study of the causes of anomalously slow rotation, touching on star formation processes; and engineering thought, by setting new constraints on possible megastructures.

Next steps

Immediate next steps include obtaining high-resolution spectra for the two candidates to measure metallicity and radial velocities, as well as observations with high-angular-resolution interferometers to check for binarity. In parallel, the algorithm should be applied to an expanded TESS sample.

Key open problems

SJH is directly linked to the Fermi paradox and the question of the prevalence of technological civilizations. It also touches on fundamental problems of stellar rotation physics, such as the nature of magnetic braking in Sun-like stars and the cause of weakened braking after a critical Rossby number, which may result from a violation of conservation of angular momentum in the outer layers.
