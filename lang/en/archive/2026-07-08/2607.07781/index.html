Cosmic Brake: How to Catch Aliens by the Spin of Stars ✦

Original: "Stellar J-Harvesting: a novel angular momentum technosignature and first search in the Kepler field"
· Sahin Torlakcik
arXiv:2607.07781 · 2026-07-08 · CC BY 4.0 · 2 min · Stellar Galaxies Instrumentation
Analysis of 6725 Kepler stars shows: we can search for aliens by anomalously slow rotation — without a single infrared photon.
Abstract

Astronomers have proposed a new type of technosignature — 'stellar jet harvesting' (SJH), where a hypothetical system extracts rotational energy from a star, slowing it down. Unlike a Dyson sphere, such an object doesn't necessarily shine brightly in infrared, and the key feature is anomalously slow rotation. Analysis of 6,725 sun-like stars from the Kepler catalog yielded only two suspicious cases, but these are explained by binary systems or low metallicity. The upper limit for strong SJH signals is less than 0.045%. The main outcome is the search method itself: without a discovery, it sets targets for future telescopes, and 'slowpoke' stars can already be searched for in open data.

For decades, SETI has hunted for Dyson spheres—hypothetical shells that capture a star's light stars and re-radiate it in the infrared. But the cosmos remains silent. Perhaps an alien intelligence operates more elegantly: instead of collecting light, it tames the rotation of a sun, like a giant flywheel. Locked in that axial spin is 2.3×10³⁵ J of energy—enough to power a civilization for billions of years. Imagine an invisible magnetic grip: it siphons off angular momentum, turning the star into a cosmic dynamo, and barely heats up space. Only the slow fading of the vortex betrays the interference.

The core of the method is comparing rotation periods with the population norm. The Kepler mission, brainchild of William Borucki, gifted us photometric portraits of thousands of stars and their exoplanets. The idea is simple: if among a cohort of twins in color and activity, one star spins anomalously slowly—it’s suspect. The scale is set by rotational energy: E_rot = ½ k M R² Ω². For the Sun, k~0.059; to make the slowing statistically significant, you’d need to extract over 94% of that energy.

Extracting 94% of the rotational energy is like nearly stopping a mountain-sized spinning top in a cosmic blink: a billion years.

The pilot search sifted through 6725 stars, discarding binaries, variables, and those with metallicity below [Fe/H] = –0.5. Two candidates—KIC 6606183 and KIC 9834255—spun with periods of 61 and 65 days, while the norm is 5–10. Alas, spectroscopy and Gaia astrometry exposed the ruse: the first turned out to be metal-poor ([M/H] = –0.60) with an invisible companion, the second contaminated by a neighbor's light. No aliens, but we got a hard upper limit: f_SJH < 4.5×10⁻⁴.

The formula Δlog P = –½ log₁₀(1–f) turns the fraction of extracted energy f into an observable slowdown—a mathematical detective story without clues.

This work isn’t a failure; it’s a compass in the fog of the Fermi paradox. It proves that technosignatures aren’t limited to thermal noise. Angular momentum is a clean, independent channel, now ready to be applied to TESS data and the future Gaia DR4. On the horizon: radio searches for synchrotron emission from magnetic brakes using VLA and LOFAR, plus interferometric hunts for binarity. We stand on the brink of discoveries comparable to the work of Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar on stellar evolution. And finally, ponder this: could the astrophysical debates over magnetic braking and slow rotators already be a glimpse of alien handiwork in star formation? A flywheel slowing down in a silent galaxy might be the first whisper of another intelligence.
