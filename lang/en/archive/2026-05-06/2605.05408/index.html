Cosmic Silence: 70,000 Stars Without a Signal ⚡ экспресс

Original: "Results of ten years of UCLA SETI searches with the Green Bank Telescope"
· Jean-Luc Margot
arXiv:2605.05408 · 2026-05-06 · CC BY · ⏱ 1 min · Instrumentation
The telescope listened to 70,000 stars, but all suspicious signals turned out to be earthly interference.
Abstract

Radio astronomers scanned 70,000 stars with the 100-meter Green Bank Telescope, hunting for narrowband signals (possible technosignatures) at frequencies between 1.15 and 1.73 GHz. Out of 100 million candidates, all turned out to be earthly interference — no nearby powerful alien transmitters were found. The estimate shows: fewer than 0.0063% of stars within 20,000 light-years could have a transmitter with EIRP > 5×10¹⁶ W that we would notice. It’s like looking for a single lit candle in a galactic metropolis. 40,000 volunteers helped analyze the signals, and artificial intelligence speeds up the weeding out of interference.

Searching for extraterrestrial signals is like trying to hear a whisper at a roaring stadium. The Green Bank Telescope listened to 70,000 stars, but 100 million bursts turned out to be earthly noise — from phones, satellites, microwave ovens. Automation filtered out almost everything, and the rest was checked by 40,000 volunteers. There's no alien whisper.

Silence itself is important. The transmitter we were looking for is tens of times more powerful than all the energy humanity has ever emitted. We'd have noticed it with ease. But there are fewer than six such 'horns' per 100,000 stars within 20,000 light-years. The stadium is empty.

We searched at 1.42 GHz — the natural 'radio pulse' of hydrogen, the most abundant element in the universe. Many scientists believe that this is the logical frequency to expect messages.

🎯 The total energy of all human radio broadcasts ever made is less than the power of a single strong transmitter sought by this telescope.

🎬 In the movie 'Contact,' radio telescopes pick up an extraterrestrial signal. The real search, so far, is more like an attempt to hear silence.
