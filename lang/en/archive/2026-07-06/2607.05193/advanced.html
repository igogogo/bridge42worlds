Discovery of a Planet-Scale Moon around a Brown Dwarf ✦

Original: "Planetary-Mass Exosatellite Detected Around the Substellar Companion of a Star"
· Kevin Hoy, Alice Zurlo, Pablo A. Peña R., Jana Köhler, Silvano Desidera, Raffaele Gratton, Cecilia Lazzoni, Simon Petrus, Florian Rodler, Jonathan Smoker, Valentina D'Orazi, Ilaria Carleo, Ilaria Giovannini
arXiv:2607.05193 · 2026-07-06 · CC BY · 4 min · Exoplanets Stellar
For the first time, the radial velocity method has spotted a massive moon orbiting a substellar companion of a star.
Abstract

High-resolution spectroscopy (VLT/CRIRES+) of the brown dwarf CD-35 2722 B, obtained via direct imaging, revealed periodic radial velocity variations indicating the presence of satellites. At least one candidate exists with a minimum mass of 0.743 Jupiter masses and an orbital period of 169 days; the best-fit model also includes a second satellite with a mass of 0.277 M_J and a period of 87 days, though its parameters are less certain. The orbits are near a 2:1 mean motion resonance, similar to the Galilean moons. This is the first successful application of the radial velocity method to detect exomoons, opening prospects for studying planet formation, dynamical evolution of systems, and the search for habitable environments beyond the Solar System.

Context

Since the first exoplanet around a Sun-like star was discovered by Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz in 1995, the search for moons around exoplanets—exomoons—has been a top priority in observational astronomy. These objects can hold key information about planet formation mechanisms and dynamical evolution, and could potentially expand habitable zones. Despite a few candidates, no confirmed detection had been made—until now. CD-35 2722 B is a young brown dwarf on a well-separated orbit around its host M-dwarf star, making it an ideal target: the large angular separation and favorable contrast avoid contamination from stellar light. Applying the radial velocity method, based on the Doppler effect, to the brown dwarf's own spectrum led to the first detection of a periodic signal caused by the orbital motion of a moon.

Methods

Observations were carried out in 2023–2025 using the CRIRES+ spectrograph (spectral resolution ~100,000) on the VLT in AB-nodding mode for sky subtraction. The spectrograph slit was oriented perpendicular to the star's direction, effectively preventing stray light. Radial velocities were extracted with the viper code, which builds an empirical spectral template of the object without relying on theoretical models, and were averaged over nod pairs to improve precision (typical error ~31 m/s). Orbital analysis was performed with the EMPEROR code, which uses Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) numerical modeling of orbits. Keplerian models were fitted iteratively, starting with a single moon and adding components until they significantly improved the Bayesian evidence. Additionally, the Roche limit and Hill radius were computed, accounting for the variable distance to the star, to confirm the dynamical stability of the orbits within the law of universal gravitation.

Results

Lomb–Scargle periodogram analysis revealed a highly significant peak at a period of ~170 days (false alarm probability <0.1%). The best-fit model includes two moons on nearly circular orbits. The first has a period of 169.5 days, a minimum mass of 0.74 Jupiter masses, and a semi-major axis of 0.20 AU. The second, with a period of 87.5 days and a minimum mass of 0.28 Jupiter masses (semi-major axis 0.13 AU), is near a 2:1 resonance with the first. Both periods are determined with high precision, but the second moon has alternative solutions (periods around 70 and 115 days) due to an incomplete phase curve. A single-moon model requires a high eccentricity (0.29), which is mathematically degenerate with two resonant bodies. Comparison with the expected rotation period of the brown dwarf (<0.65 days) rules out rotational modulation as the signal source. The calculated Roche limit (8.4 brown dwarf radii) and Hill radius (>1 AU) confirm that the orbits lie well within the stability zone.

Implications

This discovery provides the first direct evidence of planet-scale exomoons and has profound implications for planet formation theories. The system likely formed through gravitational instability in the disk around the brown dwarf, explaining the anomalously high moon-to-host mass ratio (up to 2%). This contrasts with core accretion, which dominates in our solar system and produces much smaller moons. The presence of a 2:1 orbital resonance points to significant migration early in the system's evolution. It also calls into question the definitions of planets and moons in hierarchical systems, where planetary-mass objects orbit substellar bodies.

Future development

Full characterization of the system requires additional spectroscopic epochs to unambiguously determine the second moon's period and measure eccentricities. In the future, direct imaging of these moons with next-generation instruments (e.g., METIS on the ELT) will provide their radii and temperatures. New hydrodynamical models of disks around brown dwarfs will help understand the conditions that birth such massive moons. Advancements in high-resolution spectroscopy of exomoons will pave the way for searching for smaller, potentially habitable bodies.

Impact

These findings will impact the physics of planet formation, dynamics of gravitationally bound systems, astrobiology (by expanding the concept of habitable zones), and observational astronomy (boosting moon searches around wide-orbit companions).

Next steps

Immediate steps include scheduling dedicated observations of CD-35 2722 B to fill in the phase curve and mining archival data from other instruments. On the theoretical side, incorporating moon systems into population synthesis models is a priority.

Key open problems

The discovery sharpens the problem of massive moon formation: why does our solar system lack anything above 0.1 Jupiter masses, even though gravitational instability models predict such objects should be common? It also forces a reexamination of the boundary between planets and moons in light of recent findings of free-floating planets and binary systems with extreme mass ratios.
