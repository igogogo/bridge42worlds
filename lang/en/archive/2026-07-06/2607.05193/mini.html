Gravitational Duet: Brown Dwarf Reveals Its Massive Moons ✦

Original: "Planetary-Mass Exosatellite Detected Around the Substellar Companion of a Star"
· Kevin Hoy, Alice Zurlo, Pablo A. Peña R., Jana Köhler, Silvano Desidera, Raffaele Gratton, Cecilia Lazzoni, Simon Petrus, Florian Rodler, Jonathan Smoker, Valentina D'Orazi, Ilaria Carleo, Ilaria Giovannini
arXiv:2607.05193 · 2026-07-06 · CC BY · 1 min · Exoplanets Stellar
For the first time, high-resolution spectroscopy has allowed us to hear the orbital rhythm of two giant planets around a substellar object.
Links in the knowledge graph 1

Astronomers eavesdropped on a brown dwarf and caught a duet: two giant planets circling in resonance, like notes in a chord. Their masses are almost like Jupiter’s, and the host is a smoldering ember, a failed star. Now it’s clear: even such objects have their own massive moons. This brings us closer to worlds like in “Avatar” and forces us to rethink where to look for life.

🎯 The mass of the largest satellite is 0.74 MJup, and its mass ratio to its host is about 2%. This is almost twice the value for the Earth–Moon system (1.2%), and two orders of magnitude higher than Ganymede with Jupiter (0.04%).

🎬 In science fiction, moons of gas giants often serve as settings — for example, Pandora in “Avatar.” The discovery of real massive moons around brown dwarfs makes such scenarios far less hypothetical.
