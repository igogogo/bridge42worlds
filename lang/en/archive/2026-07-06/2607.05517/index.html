The Logarithmic Revolt of Monopoles ✦

Original: "Constant Scaling Fails for Global Monopole Networks"
· Wakutaka Nakano, Wen Yin
arXiv:2607.05517 · 2026-07-06 · CC BY · 3 min
HEP Phenomenology Cosmology
Large-scale simulations show that the density of global monopoles in the expanding universe does not plateau but slowly creeps upward—like in a growing metropolis, the number of street violinists keeps rising even after the neighborhoods have stopped expanding.
Abstract

Global monopoles are hypothetical objects that emerged during phase transitions in the early universe. It was thought that their network evolves according to a scaling law with a constant density. However, numerical simulations revealed that the density parameter ξ behaves differently: it grows logarithmically rather than staying constant. This is like a foam where the number of bubbles slowly changes, even though each bubble itself expands faster. The deviation was characterized by an exponent γ ∼ 0.5. This finding is important for theories of dark matter, axions, and gravitational waves generated by monopole dynamics.

1

When the cooling cosmos, in the aftermath of the Big Bang, lost its primordial symmetry, tensions arose in the fields—like microcracks forming in solidifying glass. Global monopoles, predicted by Gerard 't Hooft and Polyakov, became such “scars” on the fabric of the universe. Each monopole is a tiny but incredibly massive knot of field. Goldstone modes around it create a tension that grows with distance: the energy E(R) ≈ 4π v² R tells us that pulling apart a monopole-antimonopole pair is as hard as stretching an invisible string to infinity.

If the monopole network held a constant number per Hubble bubble, today’s visible universe would host just a few—a cosmic rarity. The logarithmic drift means there could be hundreds, even thousands of times more. Enough to leave an imprint in gravitational lensing or the cosmic microwave background polarization.

Imagine a growing metropolis. At first, each neighborhood has about the same number of street violinists. The city expands, and you expect the density of musicians to stabilize. But no—their numbers grow, as if the cultural memory of the districts attracts new performers from the future. A similar logarithmic drift was found by the authors in large-scale numerical experiments with monopoles. The parameter ξ—the average number of defects in a Hubble cell—didn’t freeze onto a plateau but continued to grow following ξ = A + B log η, where η is conformal time. In the matter-dominated era, B was 0.087 ± 0.029, and in the radiation era, a hefty 0.584 ± 0.148.

The key innovation was introducing a fractional response γ = d log ξ / d log(mᵣ/H), measuring how sensitive the abundance is to the hierarchy between the radial mode mass mᵣ and the Hubble parameter H. For most initial fluctuation spectra, γ ≈ 0.4–0.5—meaning ξ grows as the square root of the mass ratio. This behavior can’t be squeezed into a simple picture of instantaneous annihilation. The pair lifetime τ_ann = D (c₀ + c₁ ln(D/r_core)) directly indicates that the separation is delayed by a logarithmic memory of the distance to the core. Perhaps the culprit is radiation of Goldstone waves or angular momentum transport—the microscopic mechanism remains to be unraveled. Curiously, the logarithmic growth of defect density links monopoles to glassy systems and spin glasses: there, aging also follows a log t law—as if time loses count of its steps.

The “fat monopole” technique, which artificially altered the coupling constant as λ/a², achieved a dynamic range of 10⁶ in time. This trick is akin to scaling a model airplane in a wind tunnel: to see the wingtip vortices, an engineer enlarges the wing tenfold, sacrificing exact similarity for resolution.

This discovered logarithmic growth forces a reassessment of cosmological predictions. If there are more monopoles than we thought, all related signals get a boost: the production of dark matter (including axions and dark photons) becomes more efficient, primordial black holes form more often, and the stochastic gravitational wave background may acquire a blue tilt detectable by pulsar timing arrays. Constraints from cosmic microwave background anisotropy also need recalculation to account for the slowly growing defect population. Early-universe physics gains an extra tuning knob, and we get a chance to peek behind the curtain of the Standard Model through the behavior of these topological “scars.”

🎯 If the monopole network froze, only a few such objects would remain in the visible universe—a drop in the cosmic ocean. But the logarithmic growth turns them into a horde capable of bending light from distant quasars and mixing their pattern into the cosmic microwave background polarization. Thus, a gentle logarithmic enhancement opens a window to new physics.
