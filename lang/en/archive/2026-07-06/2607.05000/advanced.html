Canonical Quantization of Neurons: How to Apply Quantum Mechanics Laws to Machine Learning ✦

Original: "Canonical quantization of neurons"
· Alexander He, Nana Liu, Mark M. Wilde
arXiv:2607.05000 · 2026-07-06 · CC BY · 4 min · Quantum Physics Statistical Mech Machine Learning
Scientists turned classical neurons into quantum observables, paving the way for new machine learning algorithms on quantum data.
Abstract

Canonical quantization has been applied to the artificial neuron: the energy function is replaced by a quantum Hamiltonian, and activation is implemented via matrix functional calculus, resulting in an activation observable measured on the input state. Quantized neurons were investigated for function approximation with the goal of reconstructing an unknown observable from labeled quantum data. Hybrid algorithms were developed: for measuring the activation observable, quantum algorithms 'degree of one cumulant' and 'Schrödingerization' are used, while for estimating the gradients of the quadratic error — classical random sampling, the Hadamard test, and Hamiltonian simulation. Experiments show increased expressiveness of quantized neurons compared to classical ones on representative tasks. This approach systematizes the construction of quantum machine learning primitives and lays the foundation for neural networks optimized for quantum data.

Context

The modern quantum information community actively seeks ways to harness intrinsic quantum effects to accelerate machine learning. Quantum states, thanks to superposition, can carry more information than classical bits. However, until now, architectures of quantum neurons were built ad hoc, without relying on the fundamental principle of canonical quantization that works so well in physics. This new work fills that gap by applying the time-tested procedure of elevating classical variables to operators obeying the uncertainty relation to the basic element of artificial intelligence—the neuron.

Methods

The approach is based on replacing the classical energy function of a neuron w^T z + b with a parameterized quantum Hamiltonian H(θ) = Σ_j θ_j H_j, where H_j are local operators acting on qubits. Canonical quantization, going back to the work of Paul Dirac, prescribes replacing real variables with Hermitian operators subject to commutation relations. Then a nonlinear activation φ is applied to this Hamiltonian via functional calculus, yielding an activation observable φ(H(θ)). For the hyperbolic tangent with a temperature parameter T that determines the quantum-statistical regime of the neuron, the authors derive an integral representation that allows evaluating loss function gradients using hybrid quantum-classical algorithms: a combination of classical random sampling, the Hadamard test, and quantum simulation of time evolution governed by the Schrödinger equation (discovered by Erwin Schrödinger), the idea for which was proposed by Seth Lloyd. An alternative method for measuring the activation observable uses a continuous variable—a qumode—and the Schrödingerization technique, reducing the problem to evolution under e^{i x̂ ⊗ H(θ)/T₂}. These algorithms rely on superposition and entanglement, unavailable in classical computation, as well as the superposition principle that underpins quantum mechanics.

Results

Numerical experiments on a seven-qubit system compared a quantum neuron with a transverse-field Ising Hamiltonian (non-commuting) and a classical model with a commuting Hamiltonian. The task was to approximate an unknown observable generated by activation from another transverse-field Ising model. After 2000 training epochs, the quantum model achieved a mean squared error of about 0.01, significantly outperforming the classical one, which plateaued above 0.1. The reason is that non-commuting terms (σ_X) generate new directions in operator space, including σ_Y-type terms arising from multiplication of non-commuting Paulis, expanding expressivity. Even with the same number of parameters (14), the quantum neuron more effectively captured complex correlations in the training states, which included entangled Bell states and mixed states.

Implications

The work demonstrates that canonical quantization is not just a physical formalism but a powerful tool for building quantum primitives for machine learning. It provides a strict recipe: take a classical model, replace its energy with a Hamiltonian, and the activation with a matrix function. This paves the way to systematically construct quantum neural networks that naturally process quantum data, bypassing the inefficient mediation of classical representations. By analogy with quantum Boltzmann machines, quantized neurons could become a standard building block for learning on quantum states.

Future development

In the future, the research could develop in several directions. First, analyzing the barren plateau problem—the vanishing of gradients during training—is critical, requiring adaptation of existing methods for this new class of parameterized models. Second, scaling to a larger number of qubits and using real quantum datasets (e.g., from quantum chemistry or condensed matter physics) will allow assessing practical utility. Finally, hybrid architectures where the first layer consists of quantized neurons and subsequent layers are classical could combine the advantages of quantum processing with the power of deep learning.

Impact

The method will impact quantum computing by providing a new way to create trainable models for quantum data, as well as quantum machine learning, where theoretically grounded building blocks have been lacking.

Next steps

Immediate next steps include investigating gradient behavior in quantized neurons to rule out barren plateaus and expanding the library of activation functions, as already done in a companion paper for ReLU, sigmoid linear unit, and others.

Key open problems

The work directly addresses the unsolved problem of expressivity of quantum models and their trainability. The emergence of barren plateaus in variational quantum circuits is one of the main challenges in modern quantum machine learning, and quantized neurons offer a new testbed for studying it. Moreover, the connection to quantum thermodynamics via the temperature parameter T hints at possible analogies with thermal states and phase transitions in learning.
