Collisionless damping of gravitational instability in fuzzy dark matter ✦

Original: "Collisionless damping of the gravitational instability in fuzzy dark matter: spectral shape and quantum-to-thermal crossover"
· Yosuke Matsumoto, Kohji Yoshikawa, Naoki Yoshida
arXiv:2607.04893 · 2026-07-06 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · Cosmology Plasma Physics
Scientists have developed a quantum kinetic theory explaining how initial particle velocities and quantum pressure shape the spectrum of inhomogeneities in ultralight dark matter.
Abstract

A quantum-kinetic linear theory of gravitational instability is presented for a universe with 'fuzzy' dark matter. Starting from the Wigner transport equation and using Landau's approach to the linearized Wigner-Poisson system, a kinetic dispersion relation is derived that fully accounts for quantum effects via the plasma dispersion function. The growth rate of inhomogeneities is characterized by the dimensionless parameter α = k_qJ/k_J, where k_qJ and k_J are the quantum and thermal Jeans wavenumbers. An analytic expression is obtained for the spectral slope at the cutoff wavenumber; the spectrum undergoes a sharp transition at α ~ 0.5, separating regimes dominated by thermal Landau damping and quantum pressure. Application to fuzzy dark matter shows that the cutoff scale and spectral shape are sensitive to the particle mass and initial velocity dispersion, providing a method to simultaneously constrain these parameters from the observed matter power spectrum. The theory lays the groundwork for studying the transition from early thermal states to the formation of Bose-Einstein condensates in galactic structures.

Context

Understanding how the smallest structures form in dark matter is crucial for solving the 'cusp' problem in dwarf galaxies and interpreting data from cosmological surveys. The fuzzy dark matter (FDM) model with ultralight bosons can replace cold dark matter on small scales, but there was a discrepancy between particle mass estimates from soliton core properties and from the Lyman-alpha forest. The proposed quantum kinetic approach for the first time consistently accounts for quantum pressure and thermal motion effects, which is necessary for adequately describing the initial stages of perturbation growth and subsequent condensation into a Bose–Einstein state.

Methods

The authors start from the density matrix and the Wigner transform to obtain the Wigner transport equation — a quantum analogue of the collisionless Boltzmann equation. The equation includes the de Broglie wavelength, comparable to galactic scales. Then, linearizing the Wigner–Poisson system and applying the Landau method for plasma, they derive a kinetic dispersion relation where quantum corrections are accounted for exactly via the plasma dispersion function. By solving this equation numerically using numerical simulations, they introduce a dimensionless parameter α — the ratio of quantum to thermal Jeans wavenumbers, first introduced by James Jeans, and analyze the growth and damping of modes.

Results

It is found that the cutoff wavenumber kc, separating growing and damped modes, depends non-monotonically on α: for α ≲ 0.76 it is close to the quantum scale, and for α ≳ 0.76 to the classical one; the maximum value kc/kqJ ≈ 1.06 is reached at α ≈ 0.5. However, the most striking result is the abrupt change in the spectrum's slope (the growth rate derivative) near the cutoff: for α > 1 the slope is finite and corresponds to the classical limit, while for α < 0.5 it rapidly increases in absolute value, reflecting the suppression of collisionless damping due to the depletion of free-streaming particles. This crossover is directly related to the uncertainty principle dictating quantum pressure. In physical units, for a particle mass of 10⁻²² eV and velocity dispersion up to 30 km/s, the same cutoff scale can arise in regimes ranging from quantum-dominated to thermal, implying a strong dependence of the observed power spectrum on the thermal state. Application to the epoch of redshift z=99 demonstrates the spectrum shape's sensitivity to initial conditions.

Implications

The theory provides a microscopic justification for the transition from the collisionless kinetic regime to the hydrodynamic description of a Bose–Einstein condensate in fuzzy dark matter. Thus, gravitational instability in the quantum regime exhibits features not characteristic of classical systems. The observed power spectrum slope on small scales becomes a sensitive indicator not only of particle mass but also of their initial velocity dispersion, which may resolve existing discrepancies between different astrophysical estimates.

Future development

In the future, the developed formalism can be generalized to cosmological expansion and used to predict the transfer function of linear perturbation theory incorporating kinetic and quantum effects. Additionally, numerical simulations solving the Wigner transport equation via the Vlasov method will allow tracing the nonlinear evolution and the birth of soliton cores from an incoherent initial state in galaxies.

Impact

The results will directly impact the analysis of high-resolution spectroscopy data (Lyman-α forest) and the setup of hydrodynamical simulations including baryonic feedback. More accurate transfer functions for fuzzy dark matter will enable the interpretation of deep sky surveys revealing structure suppression on kiloparsec scales.

Next steps

The immediate next step is calculating the full linear power spectrum including cosmological expansion and the obtained quantum-kinetic corrections. Subsequently, three-dimensional simulations of perturbation collapse with a kinetic code will demonstrate the formation of soliton profiles at the halo center.

Key open problems

The article addresses fundamental problems of dark matter: particle nature (mass, statistics), formation of dwarf galaxy cores, and resolution of the 'too-big-to-fail' dilemma. The proposed quantum-thermal crossover mechanism could be part of a broader explanation for the diversity of density profiles in galaxies without invoking complex baryonic processes.

🎯 The plasma dispersion function used in this work was originally developed for analyzing nuclear fusion in hot plasma, but turned out to be key for understanding gravitational instability in a quantum dark matter gas, too.
