How a Polyglot Neural Network Uncovered an Atomic Secret: Why Protective Coatings Destroy Solar Cells ✦

Original: "Data-driven atomistic modelling of hybrid halide perovskite passivation"
· Laura-Bianca Paşca, Henry J. Snaith, Volker L. Deringer
arXiv:2607.05321 · 2026-07-06 · CC BY · 3 min
Materials Computational Physics
Two-stage training of neural network potentials revealed how an excess of passivating molecules forces them to stand upright, deforming the perovskite crystal lattice and undermining solar cell efficiency.
Abstract

Scientists used machine learning to model the protective coating of perovskite solar cells. The approach resembles training language models: first, the model is trained on a vast database of hyP-26 structures, then fine-tuned on a small dataset for a specific molecule. For the first time, this allowed a detailed study of how amino-silane molecules interact with each other on the surface as coating density changes—from chaotic distribution to an ordered layer. Interestingly, the same principle of continual fine-tuning is used to adapt AI to new tasks.

1

Hybrid perovskites are the rising stars of photovoltaics: their efficiency is growing so fast it feels like we're on the brink of a solar revolution. But these materials, like intricate atomic mosaics, are extremely vulnerable—moisture, heat, and light quickly turn them to dust. Applying organic passivating molecules initially seemed like a panacea—an ultra-thin protective varnish that shields the surface. However, a paradox soon emerged: too much protection doesn't save, it destroys. The varnish starts dissolving what it was meant to strengthen. Why? The answer was hidden in the atomic ballet at the crystal's edge, and to glimpse it, a special interpreter was needed—a neural network potential trained like a polyglot.

Machine learning here works like a linguist mastering an unfamiliar language. First, the model absorbs the universal "grammar" of interatomic interactions—a vast corpus of structures from the `hyP-26` dataset, featuring carbon, oxygen, hydrogen, and silicon. Then comes fine-tuning on a specific "dialect": the coordination of the aminosilane molecule AEAPTMS on the perovskite surface. This two-stage approach, borrowed from large language models, cut the force prediction error in half—from 240 to 90 meV/Å—and avoided catastrophic forgetting, where accuracy plummets when encountering unfamiliar chemical contexts.

To simulate the behavior of a single molecule on the surface, a system of over 3000 atoms had to be calculated—the scale of an entire nanocrystal.

When the neural network "read" the coating dynamics, a drama of self-sabotage unfolded. Up to a concentration of 0.8 molecules per nm², the passivator lies flat, hugging the surface with multiple anchors and delicately filling defects. But crossing this Rubicon changes everything. Crowding forces the molecules to stand on end—like passengers in an overcrowded train car, compelled to squeeze vertically. Their long hydrocarbon tails, like elbows in a crowd, push the surroundings aside, penetrate the surface layer, and shatter the octahedral "bricks" of the crystal. Octahedral tilt angles increase, lead–halogen bonds lengthen—the structure strains at the seams. At maximum coverage, the fraction of distorted octahedra reaches 7.1%—enough to trigger a destructive chain reaction.

The apparent accuracy of models without intermediate fine-tuning was often an illusion of mutual error compensation—like a bad translator who speaks fluently from context but misses the essence.
Just one extra molecule per dozen octahedra—and the crystal goes from protected to doomed. The nanoworld reveals a fragile balance where a microscopic excess triggers a cascade, much like a single mutation in a genome can alter a cell's fate.

This atomic insight flips the search strategy. Now virtual screening of thousands of passivators is possible even before lab tests: we seek molecules with a perfect balance of steric effects—short and flexible enough not to crowd each other. In the future, integration with generative models will speed up the creation of self-adapting coatings, while accounting for oxygen-containing groups and hydrogen bonds will make numerical experiments more realistic. It's a step toward cracking one of the toughest nuts: predicting the long-term stability of functional materials under real-world conditions—from solar cells to catalysts and sensors on silicon substrates.
