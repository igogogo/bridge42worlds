Dark energy with a sign change: How the transition from negative to positive density smooths out the Hubble tension ✦

Original: "Alleviating the Hubble Tension with Smooth Sign-Switching Dark Energy: Full CMB Constraints with DESI and PantheonPlus"
· Mariam Bouhmadi-López, Hsu-Wen Chiang, Beñat Ibarra-Uriondo
arXiv:2607.05044 · 2026-07-06 · CC BY · 4 min · Cosmology General Relativity HEP Phenomenology HEP Theory
The ECDM model with a smooth change in the sign of dark energy density resolves the contradiction between early and late measurements of the Hubble constant.
Abstract

A sign-changing dark energy scenario (ECDM model) is considered, where the dark energy density gradually transitions from negative to positive. A consistent formulation of perturbation equations is constructed, ensuring stability even when the equation of state parameter diverges at the transition moment. Comparison is made with various cosmological data: cosmic microwave background measurements (Planck 2018, ACT DR6, SPT-3G), baryon acoustic oscillations (DESI DR2), Type Ia supernovae (Pantheon+), and the local Hubble constant value (SH0ES). Accounting for perturbations allowed assessment of the impact on structure growth and CMB anisotropy. The results confirm full compatibility of the model with precision observations, the easing of Hubble tension, and indicate a change in the late-time dynamics of the Universe.

Context

The standard cosmological model ΛCDM is remarkably successful, but faces a serious challenge — the Hubble tension. According to Hubble's law, discovered by Edwin Hubble and predicted by Georges Lemaître within the expanding Universe after the Big Bang, the recession velocity of galaxies is proportional to distance, and the proportionality factor — the Hubble constant — determines the rate of expansion of the Universe. However, modern local measurements give a value around 73 km/s/Mpc, while cosmic microwave background from the early Universe points to 67 km/s/Mpc. The discrepancy exceeds 5σ, hinting at incompleteness in our understanding of dark energy. The traditional cosmological constant suffers from theoretical problems, so dynamical models where dark energy evolves are being explored. One intriguing possibility is sign-change models of dark energy density, where it transitions from negative values in the past to positive today.

Methods

To test the ECDM model, the researchers used Monte Carlo Markov chains (MCMC) with a modified CAMB code, based on Friedmann's equations for background dynamics and linearized perturbation theory. The analysis included high-resolution cosmic microwave background data from Planck, ACT, and SPT, photometric light curves of Type Ia supernovae from PantheonPlus (with local calibration by SH0ES), and spectroscopic surveys of galaxies from DESI, revealing redshifts of baryon acoustic oscillations — sort of sound waves of the early Universe. The main technical difficulty: when the density of dark energy crosses zero, its equation of state diverges, making standard perturbation equations unsuitable. The authors avoided this by redefining variables to maintain regularity over the entire range of redshifts. This allowed, for the first time, a consistent treatment of dark energy perturbations.

Results

The analysis revealed two branches: a slow and a fast transition of dark energy. However, supernova and BAO data confidently rule out the slow branch, leaving a fast transition at a characteristic redshift z† ≈ 2.9–3.1. Notably, extremely fast transitions (with logarithmic rate log₁₀η > 0.5) are also inconsistent with observations: they cause a sharp spike in structure growth, conflicting with data on gravitational lensing of the cosmic microwave background and redshift measurements. The resulting Hubble constant in ECDM is ~69.3 km/s/Mpc (combination of CMB+DESI+PantheonPlus), which is noticeably closer to local measurements and reduces the expansion tension to ~2σ. Most statistical criteria (AIC, DIC, WAIC) prefer ECDM over the standard ΛCDM, especially with the SH0ES calibration. The model predicts a slight enhancement of large-scale structure growth and a characteristic signal in the integrated Sachs–Wolfe effect — additional anisotropy in the cosmic microwave background on large angular scales, caused by the evolution of gravitational potentials. The CMB lensing power spectrum is also slightly enhanced compared to ΛCDM.

Implications

The results strengthen the case for sign-change models of dark energy as a viable alternative to ΛCDM. They indicate that dark energy is not a cosmological constant but a dynamical field going through a phase of negative pressure. This could explain why dark energy dominates precisely in the present era, alleviating the coincidence problem. The exclusion of instantaneous transitions suggests a smooth nature of this phenomenon, possibly linked to a phase transition in the dark sector.

Future development

Future research should go beyond phenomenological parameterization and build a theoretical model, for example, based on a scalar field with an appropriate potential. This will allow studying the microphysics of the transition and its impact on nonlinear structure growth. Upcoming missions like Euclid and the Roman Space Telescope, with their data on weak lensing and galaxy clusters, will provide stringent tests. More precise measurements of the cosmic microwave background from future experiments are also important.

Impact

Sign-change models of dark energy directly impact late-Universe cosmology, the interpretation of cosmic microwave background, supernova, and large-scale structure data. They also challenge theoretical physics by requiring an explanation for negative energy density.

Next steps

The first priority is to build a realistic model of dark energy based on quantum field theory, free from instabilities. It is also necessary to conduct a joint analysis with full power spectrum data from future surveys and to account for nonlinear effects.

Key open problems

The work addresses one of the major unsolved problems — the Hubble tension in the expansion rate. It is also connected to the cosmological constant problem, offering a dynamical alternative, and indirectly touches on the nature of dark matter, as the modification of expansion affects constraints on its density.
