Quantum Enhancement of Information-Mediated Energy Transfer ✦

Original: "Quantum enhancement of information-mediated energy transfer"
· Shotaro Oki, Yuki Kadono, Kaito Tojo, Takahiro Sagawa, Ken Funo
arXiv:2606.27868 · 2026-06-26 · CC BY · 3 min · Statistical Mech Quantum Physics
Collective quantum effects make it possible to amplify information flow in open quantum systems, creating a powerful and efficient Maxwell’s demon and enabling ultrafast charging of quantum batteries.
Abstract

For interacting open quantum systems, a thermodynamic uncertainty relation is derived, linking information flow to entropy production in nonequilibrium steady states. A quantum engine is constructed where N-fold degenerate ground and excited states are collectively coupled to thermal reservoirs. Collective transitions linearly enhance heat currents and information flow with N, while entropy production remains constant—realizing an autonomous quantum Maxwell demon that harnesses collective amplification of information flow to pump heat against a temperature gradient. Away from the steady state, collective interactions amplify the unitary component of quantum information flow, leading to a quadratic increase in the charging power of free-energy quantum batteries. The results demonstrate scalable advantages of quantum coherence and collective effects in quantum engines.

Context

In the thermodynamics of information, information flow is seen as a resource that allows us to bypass the limitations of the second law of thermodynamics. The classic Maxwell’s demon can create a heat flow from cold to hot, and its quantum counterparts have already been demonstrated in the lab. However, until now it remained unclear whether quantum coherence and collective interactions could boost information flow without a proportional increase in entropy production. This problem lies at the heart of quantum thermodynamics and open quantum systems.

Methods

The authors derived a short-time thermodynamic uncertainty relation linking information flow to partial entropy production and activity (the average frequency of quantum jumps). Building on this, they constructed a model of two N-fold degenerate subsystems collectively coupled to thermal reservoirs. Thanks to quantum coherence among collective states, activity scales as N² while entropy production remains of order one. This circumvents the usual current–dissipation trade-off. For the steady-state regime they used dissipative control, and for the non-steady case unitary evolution with an interaction Hamiltonian that does not commute with local energies. Two scenarios were explored: an autonomous Maxwell demon with two baths attached to one subsystem, and a quantum battery. Numerical simulations confirmed the analytical estimates.

Results

In the steady state, the Maxwell demon generated a reverse heat current that grew linearly with N, while the total entropy production stayed constant—the second law was never violated thanks to information redistribution between subsystems. Information flow reached O(N), while partial entropies remained O(1). In the non-steady case, collective entanglement sped up quantum battery charging: the charging time dropped to 1/N² and the average power grew as N². At the same time, the free-energy increase stayed finite. The effect arises from a quadratic boost of the unitary contribution to information flow, made possible by the spectral width of the collective interaction.

Implications

This work for the first time quantitatively connects collective enhancement of activity with a scalable advantage in information-thermodynamic devices. It sets new design principles for quantum information machines, where coherence and degenerate states serve as resources. It reshapes our view of the Maxwell demon paradox in the quantum world and opens the path to powerful, energy-efficient processors based on quantum thermodynamics.

Future development

The model can be generalized to many-body systems and finite times. An exciting prospect is experimental realization on platforms like superconducting qubits, ion traps, or quantum dots. Further study of non-Markovian effects and optimal entropy control protocols will bring the concept closer to practical quantum technologies. Autonomous heat engines that outperform their classical counterparts may become possible.

Impact

The findings will influence the design of quantum heat engines, energy-harvesting systems, and low-heat-dissipation quantum computers. They are also relevant for understanding non-equilibrium statistical physics and information processing in biological systems.

Next steps

Experimental verification of the predicted scalings in controllable quantum systems. Investigating the role of imperfections and decoherence to assess the practical reachability of the enhancement.

Key open problems

The article ties into fundamental questions about the nature of information as thermodynamic fuel and the limits of the second law of thermodynamics. It also touches on the scalability of quantum advantage in non-equilibrium systems and the link between entanglement and thermodynamic fluxes.
