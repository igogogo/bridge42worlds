Multi-view Self-distillation of Light Curves with Astrophysical Informedness ✦

Original: "Domain-Informed Multi-View Self-Distillation for Astronomical Light-Curve Representation Learning with JEPA"
· Yicheng Rui
arXiv:2606.28446 · 2026-06-26 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min
Instrumentation Stellar Artificial Intelligence
A method for representing irregular astronomical time series is proposed, combining C-RoPE time encoding, measurement uncertainty handling, and LeJEPA self-distillation across three domain projections.
Abstract

An approach is proposed for learning representations of irregular astronomical time series (light curves) based on JEPA architecture with semantic preservation, uncertainty-aware tokenization, and multi-view self-distillation. The model is trained with LeJEPA regularization on the LEAVES dataset and tested on the StarEmbed benchmark. On StarEmbed, it outperforms handcrafted features on 15 of 16 metrics. Linear probing with few examples yields macro-F1 of 42.56 ± 7.21 (1 sample/class) and 63.58 ± 1.20 (100 samples). Beyond variable star classification, the representations are useful for finding similar objects, parameter estimation, and detecting photometric zero-point drift. In cross-domain adaptation on 12 PYRREGULAR datasets, the method matches or exceeds SOTA on 5 datasets (vs no more than 3 for previous leaders). The results confirm that multi-view self-distillation with domain-specific considerations is effective, and for time series, inductive biases are critical rather than a universal architecture.

1

Context

Modern astronomy is entering the era of petabyte-scale data streams: photometric surveys such as the Legacy Survey of Space and Time at the Vera C. Rubin Observatory will record billions of light curves nightly from variable stars, transients, and supernovae. Classifying these objects is fundamental for studying stellar evolution and cosmological measurements via redshift. Hand-crafted features, created since the time of Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin, remained the standard for decades, but their extraction requires expert knowledge and doesn't scale. Existing deep learning models for time series struggle with extremely irregular sampling, complex noise, and the vast range of timescales inherent in astronomical observations. New approaches are needed that incorporate astrophysical inductive biases directly into the architecture.

Methods

The authors proposed a domain-informed architecture based on machine learning, combining three complementary representations of each light curve: 1) the original irregular series; 2) the generalized Lomb–Scargle periodogram spectrum, revealing periodicities; 3) the phase-folded curve, stacked according to the best period. Each representation uses a separate transformer encoder with two key innovations. Continuous Rotary Position Embedding (C-RoPE) sets the rotation phase of attention vectors proportional to the actual timestamps t_i rather than the ordinal position, encoding the temporal translational invariance of astronomical signals. Error-Aware Numeric Embedding (EANE) treats the measured brightness not as a point but as a Gaussian distribution N(m_{t_i}, e_{t_i}^2) and averages embeddings over neighboring quantized bins with weights proportional to probability, naturally accounting for heteroscedastic noise. The model is trained unsupervised using the LeJEPA loss function, which simultaneously aligns latent representations of different projections (invariant part) and regularizes their distribution to a standard normal (sigma regularization), optimizing the space for linear probing. Pre-training was conducted on the LEAVES dataset (~980k variable stars from ASAS-SN, Gaia, and ZTF), and testing on the StarEmbed benchmark (~40k curves from Catalina Surveys).

Results

The full model outperformed hand-extracted features on 15 of 16 StarEmbed classification metrics using various classifiers (MLP, k-NN, logistic regression, random forest). The gap is especially pronounced in few-shot mode: with one labeled example per class, macro-averaged F1 reaches 42.56 ± 7.21% vs. 30.28% for hand-crafted features; with 100 examples—63.58 ± 1.20% vs. 62.39%. Ablation experiments confirmed the importance of each component: removing C-RoPE or EANE reduces performance; excluding any representation (especially the periodogram) also degrades results. Interestingly, a model with a symmetric CLIP loss underperforms LeJEPA regularization. UMAP visualization of the latent space reveals compact clusters corresponding to variable star classes without using labels. In detecting photometric zero-point drift, the model reliably detects brightness jumps even at low amplitudes of 0.05 magnitudes, with EANE contributing significantly at small drifts. When adapted to 12 irregular PYRREGULAR datasets from other domains, the model achieves parity or superiority on 5 of them, whereas the best predecessor led on only 3.

Implications

The work shows for the first time that a machine learning model without a teacher can outperform expert features in classifying astronomical light curves. This marks a shift from artisanal feature engineering to automated extraction of physically meaningful representations, crucial for handling mega-survey data. The proposed C-RoPE and EANE components can be reused in other tasks with irregular time series and heteroscedastic errors, and the three astrophysical projection strategy highlights the universal need for domain-specific inductive biases instead of a silver-bullet general architecture. This shifts the paradigm from general-purpose transformers to purpose-built encoders for specific domains.

Future development

The method can be extended to multimodal data, e.g., simultaneous photometry and spectroscopy (future missions like JWST or upcoming telescopes), as well as alert streams requiring incremental learning. Replacing hard-coded transformations (periodogram, phase folding) with differentiable learnable modules remains an open problem, promising to eliminate manual feature crafting completely. Integration with physical models predicting light curves (e.g., for exoplanets or supernovae) will enable interpretable models and solve inverse problems of numerical simulation at big data scales.

Impact

The technology will directly impact variable star science, accelerating classification and discovery of rare classes; cosmology, by improving distance estimation via Cepheids and RR Lyrae; and stellar physics, providing a tool for statistical studies of stellar evolution and star formation. Outside astronomy, the adapted architecture is promising for financial time series, medical signals, and geophysical data.

Next steps

Needed are: research on differentiable learnable analogs of periodogram and phase folding to remove manual steps; scaling pre-training to full LSST data; and creating a generalized benchmark for irregular time series spanning multiple scientific domains.

Key open problems
