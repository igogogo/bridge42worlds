Non-Newtonian two-dimensional electron fluid: the role of local Joule heating in nonlinear magnetoresistance ✦

Original: "Non-Newtonian two-dimensional electron fluid in magnetic field"
· P. S. Alekseev, M. A. Semina
arXiv:2606.27788 · 2026-06-26 · CC BY · 3 min · Mesoscale
Local heating turns a two-dimensional electron gas into a non-Newtonian fluid whose viscosity depends on the velocity gradient, explaining anomalous differential magnetoresistance in ultraclean GaAs quantum wells.
Abstract

A mechanism for non-Newtonian behavior of a two-dimensional electron fluid, driven by local Joule heating, is proposed. The shear viscosity of electrons depends non-monotonically on the velocity gradient: depending on the flow regime, the fluid becomes dilatant or pseudoplastic. Hydrodynamic equations for velocity and temperature profiles are solved in a geometry close to Poiseuille flow. It is shown that the nonlinearity arising from local heating explains the nontrivial differential magnetoresistance in ultrapure GaAs quantum wells at high currents. Therefore, a two-dimensional non-Newtonian electron fluid is realized in such systems.

Context

Electron fluids in ultraclean conductors at low temperatures exhibit hydrodynamic properties reminiscent of classical fluids but with quantum features due to Fermi statistics and degeneracy. Back in his Fermi liquid theory, Landau showed that collective effects in such systems can be described by macroscopic parameters. However, recent experiments on two-dimensional electron gas in a magnetic field have revealed anomalous nonlinear magnetoresistance that falls outside the standard Newtonian paradigm established by Newton. Local Joule heating, discussed in this work, dramatically alters the thermal properties of the electron fluid, leading to a dependence of viscosity on shear rate.

Methods

To describe the nonlinear transport, the authors developed a hydrodynamic model based on momentum and energy conservation equations. Viscous electron flow in a magnetic field generates Joule heat, which is removed by acoustic phonons. The balance of heat generation and dissipation leads to local heating: the electron temperature becomes dependent on the velocity gradient: α (Te — Tph) = m ηxx (dV/dy)^2. The shear viscosity, in turn, is sensitive to temperature, closing the self-consistent problem. The equations were solved analytically for a simple Poiseuille flow geometry in a long channel, with numerical analysis for critical parameters. The influence of magnetic field was also accounted for via the dimensionless parameter β = 2 ω_c τ_2,ee.

Results

The key result is a nonmonotonic dependence of the nonlinear viscosity η_nl on the velocity gradient G=dV/dy. In weak fields (β<1), the system behaves as a pseudoplastic fluid—viscosity decreases with increasing G. In stronger fields (β>1), it becomes dilatant, i.e., viscosity increases with G, and at even larger G it drops again, reminiscent of the behavior of degenerate astrophysical media under extreme conditions. The velocity profile turned out to be close to parabolic but with a blunter top. The calculated differential magnetoresistance r(B) qualitatively reproduces experimental data for GaAs quantum wells: in the zero-current limit, r(B) is a Lorentzian, and as the current increases, a nonmonotonic shape emerges—a local minimum at B=0 and maxima at small fields. The nonlinearity parameter ν is estimated as ~0.3 at maximal current, confirming the model’s applicability. Importantly, the model predicts the existence of critical values ν_cr(β), above which the steady-state solution loses stability, pointing to possible nonequilibrium phase transitions in the electron fluid, fully consistent with Boltzmann’s concept of entropy growth.

Implications

This work lays the groundwork for understanding nonlinear hydrodynamic regimes in quantum electron systems. It not only explains the puzzling magnetoresistance behavior but also introduces the concept of a non-Newtonian electron fluid, extending analogies with classical rheology. This may stimulate the search for new collective phenomena, such as turbulence or shear banding, in ultraclean semiconductor heterostructures.

Future development

Further research may include thermal conductivity, thermoelectric effects, and other nonlinearity mechanisms, such as those related to memory of interparticle correlations. Of particular interest is the study of complex two-dimensional geometries, where hydrodynamic nonlinearities could manifest even more vividly. The method’s development will also allow prediction of new types of instabilities and unsteady regimes, potentially important for applications in quantum electronics.

Impact

The results will impact the physics of mesoscopic systems, the development of sensitive magnetic field sensors, and devices based on the control of spin and charge currents.

Next steps

It is necessary to experimentally verify the predicted dependence of viscosity on velocity gradient, for instance using local probes, and to investigate the transition to unstable regimes at high currents.

Key open problems

The study is directly related to fundamental problems of nonequilibrium quantum statistical mechanics and turbulence in strongly interacting Fermi systems. It also raises the question of the microscopic origin of viscosity and the limits of applicability of hydrodynamic description at the nanoscale.
