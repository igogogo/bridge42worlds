Entropic Signature of Complexity: Non-extensive Analysis of Protein Lengths from Archaea to Humans ✦

Original: "Nonextensive Statistical Signatures of the Bilaterian Transition in Proteome Length Distributions"
· Sertac Eroglu
arXiv:2606.27985 · 2026-06-26 · CC BY · 3 min · Statistical Mech Biological Physics
Tsallis statistical mechanics reveals a continuous rise in proteome organizational complexity along the evolutionary ladder, from compact bacterial to heavy-tailed human distributions.
Links in the knowledge graph 1

Context

Why it matters: Classical statistical mechanics, laid down by Boltzmann, assumes independence of elements and leads to exponential distributions. But real proteomes – linear polymers made of carbon, oxygen, and hydrogen atoms – show heavy tails or compact support, pointing to long-range correlations that can't be described without generalizing the concept of entropy. Tsallis non-extensive statistical mechanics, an extension of quantum thermodynamics for complex systems, introduces the index q, which characterizes the degree of deviation from additivity. This index acts as a biosignature of proteome organizational complexity, letting us put a number on the invisible 'architecture' of a protein ensemble.

Methods

How it was done: Complementary cumulative distributions of protein lengths were built for 22 reference proteomes from UniProtKB. Using numerical methods for maximum likelihood estimation (MLE), a truncated discrete q-exponential distribution was fitted. Parameters q and scale l0 were estimated via Nelder–Mead optimization from 36 starting points; uncertainty was assessed through non-parametric bootstrap (500 replicates). Akaike information criterion was used for model comparison. All processing was done in Python, with some help from machine learning techniques for optimization.

Results

What they found: The index q separated into three statistically independent regimes. For prokaryotes, unicellular organisms, and non-animal multicellulars, q<1 (super-extensive regime, compact support): the lowest q was 0.745 for Haemophilus influenzae. For two cnidarians and the basal bilaterian Capitella teleta, the 95% confidence interval of q crossed one (boundary regime). All higher bilaterians fell into the sub-extensive regime (q>1, heavy tails), with q increasing monotonically in deuterostomes: from 1.033 (sea urchin) to 1.147 (human). The q-exponential outperformed the ordinary exponential in all 22 cases (ΔAIC from 19 to 5133), and its rank among five competing distributions rose with organism complexity. The characteristic scale l0 showed no phylogenetic structure, confirming that the biological signal is encoded precisely in the entropic index.

Implications

What it means for science: The gradient in q proves that proteins aren’t randomly scattered, but form a statistical ensemble whose complexity mirrors the organism’s evolutionary advancement. The thermodynamic interpretation links sub-extensivity (q>1) to proteome cooperativity: adding new proteins doesn’t just increase the system’s size, it qualitatively rewires interaction networks – a direct consequence of the generalized second law of thermodynamics for non-additive systems.

Future development

Where this could go: The next step is breaking q down by functional protein categories – for example, separately for intracellular and secreted proteins, or for those with intrinsically disordered regions. If q truly reflects the strength of network interactions, it should be higher for proteins involved in dense regulatory networks. Mixed q-exponential models are also promising for proteomes with marked functional heterogeneity, like Bacillus subtilis.

Impact

Which fields will feel the impact: This work offers a new quantitative tool for comparative genomics and evolutionary biology, enabling proteome complexity to be rated on a single scale. The method can be extended to other biological distributions – gene lengths, degrees of protein–protein interactions, expression levels – paving the way to building 'entropic portraits' of organisms.

Next steps

Next steps for research: Perform a denser phylogenetic sampling within bilaterians to precisely locate the q=1 transition point, and check whether the monotonic rise in q holds for all chordates, not just the four studied here.

Key open problems

Connection to unsolved problems in physics: The article directly tackles the fundamental question of a quantitative measure for complexity in living systems, echoing the lack of a generally accepted definition of 'biological complexity' and the search for thermodynamic foundations of evolution.

Key numbers

  • range of q: from 0.745 (H. influenzae) to 1.147 (Homo sapiens)
  • number of proteomes studied: 22
  • maximum advantage of q-exponential over exponential (ΔAIC): 5133 (for humans)
  • minimum half-width of confidence interval for q: 0.003 (for humans)
  • increase in q for deuterostomes: from 1.033 (sea urchin) to 1.147 (human)
