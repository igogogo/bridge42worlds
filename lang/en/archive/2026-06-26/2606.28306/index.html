Symphony of the Quantum Drum: Collective Modes of Helium-3 After a Heat Shock ✦

Original: "Excitation of Collective Modes in a Chiral Superfluid by Thermal Quench"
· Noble Gluscevich, J. A. Sauls
arXiv:2606.28306 · 2026-06-26 · CC BY · 3 min · Superconductivity Statistical Mech
Rapid cooling of a superfluid ³He-A film generates a complex spectrum of collective excitations, including the Higgs mode and the Nambu–Goldstone mode, revealing the dynamics of symmetry breaking and the role of topological defects.
Abstract

When superfluid helium-3 is ultra-rapidly cooled into the A-phase, in addition to defects like vortices and domain walls (predicted by the Kibble-Zurek mechanism), collective oscillations of chiral domains are excited. Simulations showed how thermal noise shapes the spectrum of these modes, and how after cooling, defect coalescence begins. Unexpectedly: the dynamic exponent smoothly changes from 1 to 2 as damping increases, reminiscent of a transition from elastic to viscous dynamics. This links laboratory quantum liquids with cosmic phase transitions.

Imagine: superfluid helium-3 in a thin film receives a sudden heat shock. In that instant, a whole orchestra of collective oscillations awakens within it — like striking an invisible drum produces a multi-voiced chord. The Kibble–Zurek mechanism, familiar from the cracking of ice during rapid freezing, here acquires a quantum elegance. In the chiral A-phase, where the order parameter rotates like a compass needle in abstract space, cooling triggers a cascade of bosonic modes. Each has its own inertia, its own effective mass. Thus, spontaneous symmetry breaking gifts us not only the Higgs mode (named after Peter Higgs), but also a doublet of chiral modes, and the massless breath of Nambu–Goldstone.

The sharper the blow to the quantum "drum," the richer the spectrum. A rule familiar to musicians applies: the strength of a strike on a string generates overtones. In a numerical experiment based on stochastic Ginzburg–Landau theory, the quantum field description turned this soundtrack into a musical score. The spectral density of the numerical simulation revealed clear thresholds: the Higgs mode with mass 2Δ (Δ is the superfluid gap) and the chiral E± with mass around 1.41Δ. Beyond the thresholds, the power decays as 1/ω, like an echo fading after a gong. The massless mode sounds different — its spectrum is 1/ω with no threshold at all, a direct consequence of Noether’s symmetries. It resembles the acoustics of a Bose condensate, where each mode is a string, and the mass threshold sets its fundamental tone.

Astonishingly: the mass of the Higgs mode in helium-3 equals the energy needed to break a Cooper pair — 2Δ. The same mechanism endows particles with mass in the Standard Model, but here it is played out by a macroscopic ensemble of atoms.

The true magic, however, lies in universality. The smooth variation of the dynamical exponent z from 1 to 2, manifested in the Kibble–Zurek freeze-out time scaling (ˆt ∝ τ_Q^δ, where δKZM changes from 0.313 to 0.488), connects laboratory superconducting order with cataclysms on cosmological scales. The same process that strings vortex lines in helium might have shaped the structure after the Big Bang or governed the rotational glitches of neutron stars. If the inflationary expansion of the Universe is a grand “heat shock,” then experiments with ³He become an act of quantum archaeology, reproducing events of 13.8 billion years ago.

The practical outlook is no less thrilling: by detecting these modes via NMR or acoustics, we create quantum sensors sensitive to topology. Nonlinear harmonics lead straight into the heart of quantum turbulence — a terra incognita full of blank spots. Further modeling in three-dimensional nanochannels, accounting for dissipation and energy cascades, promises to reveal the final secrets: how coherent structures emerge from chaos and whether they can be controlled. And for now, if you listen closely, each note carries the echo of the Universe’s birth — as if the quantum drum preserves the score of time itself.

🎯 The Higgs mode in superfluid ³He was predicted decades before the discovery of the Higgs boson in particle physics, and its mass equals the doubled energy gap 2Δ — exactly the energy required to break a Cooper pair. A remarkable coincidence of scales in the world of quantum fluids.
