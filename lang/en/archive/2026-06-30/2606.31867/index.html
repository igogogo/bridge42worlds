A Two-Color Chord That Cracks Magnetic Symmetry

Original: "Giant perpendicular Edelstein polarization in 2D compensated magnets via bichromatic Floquet driving"
· Mohsen Yarmohammadi, Daegeun Jo, Marco Berritta, Libor Šmejkal, James K. Freericks, Peter M. Oppeneer
arXiv:2606.31867 · 2026-06-30 · CC BY · 3 min
Materials Mesoscale Strongly Correlated Quantum Physics
Combining Rashba spin-orbit coupling and two-color Floquet pumping, physicists have for the first time achieved giant perpendicular spin and orbital polarization in symmetry-forbidden d-wave altermagnets.
Abstract

Unlike ordinary magnets, d-wave altermagnets are forbidden from generating spin polarization when a current is applied (Edelstein effect). However, combining Rashba spin-orbit coupling with simultaneous illumination by two laser frequencies (bichromatic pumping) allows this prohibition to be bypassed. A huge perpendicular Edelstein effect emerges (up to 1.5 Bohr magnetons), with orbital moments making the dominant contribution. It's like a second light frequency, acting as an extra dimension, 'breaks' the symmetry and creates a contactless magnetic field. Such giant polarizations are a direct path to ultrafast recording in perpendicular magnetic memory.

1

Traditional electronics operates on charge, but spintronics and the nascent orbitronics dream of incorporating the particles' own rotation. At the heart of this lies the Edelstein effect: a current flowing in materials with Rashba spin-orbit coupling generates spin polarization. Electrodynamics as taught by Maxwell long ago showed us how to control fields, while quantum mechanics of Dirac handles spins. Yet in d-wave altermagnets, nature sets a cunning trap: C₂z symmetry imposes an ironclad ban on any perpendicular polarization component. Imagine a symphony orchestra: the conductor (the crystal lattice) ensures that no musician (electron) plays a forbidden note—current may flow, but spins must remain horizontal. This spells disaster for MRAM magnetic memory, where bit switching with perpendicular anisotropy specifically requires transverse magnetization.

A quantum ballet: the emergent orbital polarization arises not from atomic orbital momentum, but from the self-rotation of an electron wave packet in momentum space—a purely geometric effect akin to the Berry phase, first described by Dirac and Landau.

Physicists found a loophole, turning a ban into an invitation. They harnessed Floquet engineering—a technique where rapid periodic driving with a high-frequency laser effectively rewrites a material's properties. The key trick is two-color pumping: two laser beams with frequencies ω and 2ω, like two musical tones, interfere and create beats in the crystal. Thanks to the nonlinear Rashba interaction, their interference chord generates a planar Zeeman field B∥ that explicitly breaks the accursed C₂z symmetry. The mathematical conductor's desk is described by the van Vleck expansion and the effective Hamiltonian: \( H_{\text{eff}} \approx H_0 + \frac{[H_{-1}, H_{+1}]}{\hbar \omega} + ... \) . Here the commutator of the Floquet harmonics H_{-1} and H_{+1} is the very “crescendo” that temporarily revokes the orchestra's rules.

The effective magnetic field spawned by this chord is estimated at 10–30 meV—in solid-state physics terms, that equates to a colossal 85–250 T, far outstripping even the strongest steady-state magnets.

The results of numerical simulations for a d-wave altermagnet (akin to real RuO₂) are mind-boggling. The perpendicular spin and orbital polarization reaches 0.5–1.5 Bohr magnetons, with the orbital contribution dominating everywhere. Moreover, the sign of the polarization flips when tuning the relative amplitude of the second harmonic S—at S=2, destructive interference kicks in and polarization reverses. It's like fine-tuning the volume of instruments to change the emotional hue of a symphony. The chirality of the laser beams and the orientation of the altermagnetic order parameter (d_x²−y² vs. d_xy) provide extra degrees of freedom for orchestration.

Such controllable giant polarization offers a direct path to field-free, ultrafast writing in spintronic and orbitronic devices. No bulky electromagnets are needed—information will be written and erased by modulated light. Experimentalists will now need to test the predictions using time-resolved spectroscopy and magneto-optical polarimetry. Beyond that, the work adds fuel to fundamental questions: how can we stabilize nonequilibrium topological phases born of light? Here quantum thermodynamics enters the stage, probing the limits of equilibrium laws. Essentially, we are learning to juggle prohibitions, turning them into resources—and who knows which other symmetry shackles will fall under the onslaught of light chords.
