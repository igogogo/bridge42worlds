Running Under Tension: Primordial Black Holes, Spectral Running, and the Hubble Tension ✦

Original: "Running into tension: primordial black holes from ultra-slow-roll inflation, spectral running, and the Hubble tension"
· Miguel A. Sabogal, Antonio J. Iovino, Sunny Vagnozzi
arXiv:2606.31362 · 2026-06-30 · CC BY · 5 min · Cosmology General Relativity HEP Phenomenology HEP Theory
Data from Planck, ACT, and SPT show that accounting for the Hubble tension only worsens the problem of ultra-slow-roll inflation models for producing primordial black holes.
Abstract

Single-field ultra-slow-roll (USR) inflation models — a mechanism for forming primordial black holes — predict a negative running of the spectral index (alpha_s<0), which grows with the black hole mass. This conflicts with hints of a positive running from ACT CMB data. Parameters extracted from the CMB are sensitive to the pre-recombination expansion history, where new physics motivated by the Hubble tension may act. Using axion-like early dark energy (EDE) as an example, we investigate the impact of such physics on alpha_s and the viability of USR models with data from Planck, ACT, SPT, and DESI DR2. Updated constraints on alpha_s and the running of running beta_s are obtained. In most data combinations, adding EDE increases alpha_s: fixing the acoustic scale theta_s, EDE raises the ratio of diffusion to acoustic scales theta_d/theta_s, and the shift in alpha_s compensates for the extra damping by boosting power on small scales. Thus, acknowledging the Hubble tension as a real problem sharpens the challenges for USR models. Overall, selecting inflation models based on n_s and alpha_s parameters is premature without understanding the pre-recombination history.

Context

Primordial black holes (PBHs) are one of the most intriguing candidates for the role of dark matter. They could have formed from overdense clumps in the early universe, which arise from a sharp enhancement of primordial curvature perturbations during inflation. One popular mechanism for such enhancement is an ultra-slow-roll (USR) phase in single-field inflation, first proposed by Alan Guth. However, data on the cosmic microwave background, particularly from the ACT telescope, point to a positive spectral running α_s, while USR models predict α_s < 0. This conflict calls into question the very idea of PBH formation this way. Adding to the intrigue is the Hubble tension—a discrepancy in measurements of the Hubble constant, which may indicate unknown physics in the epoch before recombination. The authors investigate how such new physics (exemplified by early dark energy) affects α_s and, consequently, this debate.

Methods

To study the influence of pre-recombination physics on α_s, the scientists used the Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC) method and Bayesian analysis. They generated 32 model variants, combining data on the cosmic microwave background from three experiments—Planck, ACT, and SPT—along with baryon acoustic oscillation (BAO) data from the DESI DR2 instrument. The baseline models were ΛCDM and an extension with axion-like early dark energy (EDE), where the Friedmann equations are solved together with the Klein–Gordon equation. In some models, the running of the running of the spectral index, β_s, was also varied.

Results

The main result: for most data combinations, the transition from ΛCDM to EDE increases the central value of α_s. For example, when combining all three cosmic microwave background experiments (CMB-SPA) within ΛCDM, α_s = 0.0054±0.0050, while in EDE α_s = 0.0063±0.0057. Adding DESI data hardly changes the picture. This shift, though small (~0.1–0.5σ), is systematic. The physical reason is that EDE, in order to resolve the Hubble tension, reduces the sound horizon by ~7%, which forces an increase in the ratio of the diffusion angular scale to the acoustic scale (θ_d/θ_s). To compensate for the additional damping in the tail of the cosmic microwave background spectrum, more power is required on small scales, which is achieved by increasing α_s (as well as n_s). Importantly, this effect directly follows from Hubble's law and the Friedmann equations, linking the expansion of the universe to angular scales. When the authors translated the α_s constraints onto the PBH mass plane, it turned out that using the most complete dataset (CMB-SPA+DESI) in the EDE model, even the asteroid-mass window (~10^{-17}–10^{-12} M_⊙) is closed at the 2σ level. In other words, accounting for the Hubble tension does not rescue USR models; rather, it amplifies their conflict with observations. Interestingly, the sensitivity to the choice of CMB experiment is very high: data from ACT alone exclude almost the entire range of PBH masses, while SPT currently lacks sufficient precision.

Implications

These results underscore a crucial methodological lesson: the inflationary parameters (n_s and α_s) extracted from cosmic microwave background data are not direct measurements of the power spectrum of primordial inflationary perturbations. They depend on the assumed cosmological model, in particular on the physics before recombination. As long as the nature of the Hubble tension remains unclear, any selection of inflation models based on such parameters is premature. The work also shows that if the Hubble tension is real and requires new physics, then USR inflation as a mechanism for producing PBHs faces even greater difficulties. In particular, solar and sub-solar PBHs, important for gravitational wave detectors, are under even greater pressure from the data.

Future development

In the future, data from new cosmic microwave background experiments, such as the Simons Observatory and CMB-S4, will significantly refine constraints on α_s and test the robustness of the conclusions. It is also important to study other early dark energy models and more general pre-recombination physics scenarios to understand how universal the discovered effect is. Developing non-inflationary mechanisms for generating PBHs or exploring multi-field inflation models may point the way to resolving the tensions.

Impact

The results directly impact the cosmology of the early universe, inflationary models, and black hole physics, as well as the interpretation of data from gravitational-wave detectors LIGO, Virgo, KAGRA, and future observatories such as the Einstein Telescope and LISA, which search for mergers of PBHs.

Next steps

It is necessary to repeat the analysis for other early dark energy models (e.g., new EDE, AdS EDE) and to study how the choice of a specific USR inflation implementation affects the relationship between α_s and PBH mass. It is also important to improve the consistency of data from different CMB experiments to rule out instrumental systematics.

Key open problems

The article directly addresses several open problems in modern physics: the Hubble tension, the nature of dark matter (if PBHs are a component), the problem of initial conditions for inflation, and the question of how to correctly extract information about the physics of the early universe from cosmological observations.
