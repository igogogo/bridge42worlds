Genomic Acoustics: How DNA Multifractality Arises from a Simple Power-Law Echo

Original: "Multifractal Scaling in Hi-C Maps"
· Seong-Gyu Yang, Lucas Hedström, Jan Smrek, Ludvig Lizana
arXiv:2606.31997 · 2026-06-30 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min
Statistical Mech Biological Physics
The mass exponent τ(q) in Hi-C maps is not a mysterious cascade, but a direct echo of the contact probability obeying a power law.
Abstract

DNA packaging in the nucleus exhibits multifractal scaling — a complex structure repeating at different scales. The authors mathematically showed that this behavior directly follows from the power-law contact probability P(s) between genome regions (an empirically measured quantity). The key result: for large values of the order parameter q, the slope of the mass exponent τ(q) is given by either 2−γ or 1, depending on the exponent γ. This is a universal property linking chromosome architecture to polymer physics — like a landscape shaped by a single parameter, working from yeast to humans.

1

The crumpled DNA thread in the cell nucleus is not a chaotic knot, but an intricate melody played on a polymer string. Modern experiments Hi-C, like highly sensitive microphones, capture the echo of contacts between distant genome regions, turning spatial organization into a two-dimensional probability matrix. For a long time, mathematical analysis of these maps revealed multifractal richness — a multitude of scaling exponents hinting at a complex hierarchy of folds. However, new research has shown that all this polyphony is merely the overtones of a single note.

The central theme of our story is the remarkable kinship between genomic fractality and musical harmony. Just as the overtone spectrum of a vibrating string is uniquely determined by the fundamental tone, the multifractal mass exponent τ(q) is fully derived from the power law P(s)∼s^{−γ} — a fundamental property of the polymer globule. In the standard model, the contact probability between loci decays as s^{-γ}, and this simple formula, like a tuning fork, sets the entire scale. Multifractal analysis, based on the concept of Rényi entropy, decomposes this melody into a spectrum of generalized dimensions. The analytical calculation, supported by numerical simulations of synthetic maps, reveals the mechanics: diagonal elements (intra-segment contacts) give a universal slope of 2 for small q, while off-diagonal ones for large q lead to τ(q)=q−1 if γ≥1, or another linear form if γ<1. In this regime, the genome behaves like a one-dimensional thread — higher harmonics are cut off, and the multifractality degenerates.

At γ=0.7, observed in fine-scale windows, the slope of τ(q) is 1.3, which is exactly 2−γ. Such arithmetic simplicity testifies that multifractality here requires no cascade mechanisms, but is a direct reflection of the two-point statistics.

This regularity is striking in its universality. Analysis of experimental data for eight species — from the classic human and mouse to the exotic New Zealand fur seal and black swan — consistently reproduces the same piecewise linear form of τ(q). Nature seems to sign off on the formula, making the multifractal spectrum a kind of unique biometric fingerprint of polymer physics. The practical value of the discovery is enormous: the slope of τ(q) at large q provides a new way to measure the exponent γ, more robust to noise than direct fitting of P(s). It is like determining the pitch of a sound from the arrangement of overtones, without listening to the fundamental tone itself — the spectroscopy method becomes especially reliable in noisy records.

The multifractal formalism, born in the depths of turbulence theory to describe cascade phenomena, encounters a paradox: unlike turbulent systems, genomic fractality turned out to be false complexity. There are no hierarchical cascades here — only a strict function of two-point correlations.

The method's prospects extend far beyond a single biological problem. Chromatin packing dynamics in the cell cycle, integration with data from spatial microscopy to reconstruct 3D architecture — these directions promise a breakthrough if we look at the problem through the lens of a simple multifractal index. Moreover, the universal approach is suitable for any networks with power-law correlations: from cosmological webs of galaxies to social graphs. Knowing just the exponent γ, one can predict the multifractal 'signature' of a system and distinguish true complexity from illusory.
