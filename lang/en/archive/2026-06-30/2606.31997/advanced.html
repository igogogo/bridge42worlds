Multifractal Scaling of Hi-C Maps: From Contact Probability to the Mass Exponent

Original: "Multifractal Scaling in Hi-C Maps"
· Seong-Gyu Yang, Lucas Hedström, Jan Smrek, Ludvig Lizana
arXiv:2606.31997 · 2026-06-30 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Statistical Mech Biological Physics
The mass exponent τ(q) follows directly from the power law of contact probability P(s)∼s^{−γ}, linking multifractality to polymer physics.
Abstract

Genome organization displays multifractal scaling, yet its origin lacked a direct physical explanation. This work derives that the multifractality of Hi-C maps follows directly from the power-law contact probability P(s) ∝ s^{-γ} — an empirically measured quantity. From P(s) with a single exponent γ, the mass exponent τ(q) is analytically obtained, describing the scaling of the q-th moment of contact density with window size l. This reflects a geometric competition between intra- and inter-segment contacts. The slope of τ(q) at large q equals 2−γ (for γ<1) or 1 (for γ≥1). The behavior is robust to noise and observed across organisms, suggesting universality. The analysis is also extended to a double power-law P(s). These results establish a direct link between multifractality and polymer contact statistics, with the large-q slope of τ(q) mapping onto the known polymer contact exponent.

Context

The spatial organization of chromatin is one of the key unsolved problems in molecular biology. Hi-C experiments Hi-C, akin to a kind of interaction spectroscopy, measure contact frequencies between loci. The observed power-law decay P(s)∼s^{−γ} with γ≈1 indicates a 'crumpled globule' conformation. Simultaneously, multifractal scaling was discovered in the same maps, but its origin remained a mystery. Our study fills this gap, showing that multifractality naturally emerges from the geometry of the contact map and the form of P(s), without invoking cascade processes.

Methods

The authors used numerical simulations of synthetic Hi-C maps based on a standard model with one or two power-law exponents, and analyzed experimental data from human, mouse, and six Australian species. Multifractal analysis followed standard procedure: the map was covered with squares of size l, the mass μ_l in each square was computed, and the generalized partition sum Z(q,l)=∑μ_l^q. The exponent τ(q) was determined from the scaling Z(q,l)∼l^{τ(q)} using a finite-difference derivative. The method also yields generalized dimensions D_q, related to Rényi entropy. Analytical contributions from diagonal (intra-segment) and off-diagonal (inter-segment) contacts were derived, leading to a piecewise linear τ(q) and linking its slopes to γ. Robustness was tested by adding white noise.

Results

Experimental τ(q) for human chromosome 1 (γ≈1.09) showed two linear regimes: a slope of 2 for small q and 1 for large q, exactly matching the theoretical prediction τ(q)=q−1 for γ≥1. For a smaller window l=200 kb, where effective γ≈0.7, the slope was ∼1.3 (=2−γ), confirming the formula τ(q)=q(2−γ)−1. Analysis of maps from mouse and six other species, including the New Zealand fur seal, black swan, and subterranean clover, showed the same pattern, underscoring the universality of multifractal scaling. Noise did not disrupt the behavior as long as the power-law P(s) held.

Implications

For the first time, a direct link is established between multifractal analysis of Hi-C maps and fundamental contact exponents of polymer physics. The slope of τ(q) at large q directly yields the bulk contact exponent β_b, previously extracted only from fits to P(s). This provides a new, noise-robust method for estimating polymer exponents that does not require structural models. Moreover, it is shown that multifractality here is reducible to two-point statistics, unlike in turbulent systems, and is not a signature of hidden cascades.

Future development

The proposed approach can be extended to chromatin dynamics, for example, during the cell cycle, and to joint analysis with spatial microscopy data. An intriguing direction remains extracting the surface contact exponent β_s from τ(q), currently obscured by the map geometry.

Impact

These results impact chromatin biophysics, offering a new tool for interpreting Hi-C data. The method can be adapted for analyzing other network or matrix data with power-law correlations, including social and cosmological networks.

Next steps

The analysis should be applied to higher-resolution Hi-C maps and under various biological conditions, such as knockout of genes involved in loop extrusion.

Key open problems

This study connects the problem of extracting three-dimensional chromatin organization from two-point contacts with universal scaling laws. It also raises the question of the exact relationship between γ and the spatial fractal dimension d_f, especially in the regime γ<1, where d_f>3.
