Global Storm AI: How STRATA Rewrites Atmospheric Modeling Rules ✦

Original: "Scaling Storm-Resolving Atmospheric AI Simulation to the Entire Planet"
· Zeyuan Hu, Akshay Subramaniam, Noel Keen, Tao Ge, Jaideep Pathak, Mohammad Shoaib Abbas, Suman Ravuri, Karthik Kashinath, Naser Mahfouz, Peter Caldwell, Mike Pritchard, Noah Brenowitz
arXiv:2606.31248 · 2026-06-30 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Atmospheric and Oceanic Physics cs.CE Machine Learning
The new AI model STRATA, for the first time, emulates atmospheric convection across the entire planet at 4.9 km resolution, achieving 50× energy efficiency.
Abstract

Global models with explicit convection-resolving, like SCREAM, require ~1 MWh per day, limiting long-term simulations. STRATA is an autoregressive neural-network emulator trained on 17 days of SCREAM data with 4.9 km grid spacing (~25 million cells) and a 10-minute time step. The method relies on the locality of the atmosphere over short intervals: the model trains on spatial tiles, and the global forecast is built by overlapping stitching. The architecture includes 3D attention, stereographic RoPE for grid invariance, and an artifact-suppressing decoder. Kilometer-scale emulation requires an order of magnitude more FLOPs per grid point than coarse AI weather models, in line with the higher information density of convection. STRATA produces stable 24-hour global forecasts with realistic dynamics, though large-scale errors grow. Energy efficiency: 48 model days per MWh (50× better than SCREAM); throughput: 741 model days per day on 512 H100 GPUs. Code and data are open-source.

Context

Atmospheric processes that drive extreme precipitation and cloud feedbacks remain the main source of uncertainty in climate predictions. Current numerical models of the global atmosphere, forced to parameterize convection due to coarse resolution (25–100 km), suffer from systematic errors. Global storm-resolving models (GSRMs) explicitly capture deep convection on kilometer-scale grids, but they guzzle enormous amounts of energy—about 1 MWh per model day. As John von Neumann, who laid the foundations of numerical weather prediction, pointed out, the key to accuracy lies in resolution—and today we stand on the brink of achieving that with AI.

Methods

The STRATA approach is built on a tile strategy: it trains on small spatial patches (128×128 cells, ~576 km) with 10-minute steps, amassing a wealth of local examples. This locality makes physical sense—in 10 minutes, acoustic waves travel only about 200 km. The architecture features 3D patch embeddings, local 3D attention (neighborhood attention), and a novel positional encoding method called StereoRoPE, based on stereographic projection. This allows it to incorporate geopotential and solar radiation as forcing. To suppress artifacts linked to the high information entropy of convective fields, a pixel-wise anti-aliasing decoder is used. In essence, the model learns to approximate the Navier-Stokes equations and moist air thermodynamics (including the ideal gas law) purely from data.

Results

STRATA produces stable 24-hour global forecasts with realistic kilometer-scale dynamics: tropical cyclones, orographic precipitation, island convection, and frontal systems. Quantitative metrics back up the quality—the Fractions Skill Score for precipitation handily beats persistence, and precipitation power spectra closely match the SCREAM benchmark. Energy efficiency reaches 48 model days per MWh, roughly 50 times better than SCREAM, while throughput on 512 H100 GPUs hits 741 model days per wall-clock day. Scaling analysis reveals that emulating convection demands about 10× more FLOPs per grid cell than coarse-resolution weather models, a consequence of the higher entropy in convective data.

Implications

This work marks a breakthrough in numerical modeling of the atmosphere, making global storm-resolving modeling practically accessible. It opens the door to century-scale ensemble calculations for assessing shifts in extreme precipitation frequency and the role of cloud feedbacks. Moreover, the drastic drop in energy consumption (equivalent to cutting CO₂ emissions) aligns with sustainable computing goals.

Future development

Next steps include hybridizing with a coarse global model to improve large-scale circulation, and gradually pushing resolution toward 100–200 m—the threshold needed to directly resolve low clouds and feedbacks tied to humidity and solar radiation. Such systems will offer fresh insights into climate sensitivity.

Impact

These findings will directly influence climatology, meteorology, and energy-efficient computing, spurring a shift from classic models to hybrid AI systems.

Next steps

Immediate plans involve training on longer, more diverse data samples and devising methods to correct large-scale biases, perhaps via conditional generative approaches.

Key open problems

This research is intimately tied to a central problem in climate physics—quantifying cloud feedbacks, the largest source of uncertainty in warming forecasts. It also touches on the fundamental question of extreme weather predictability in a changing climate.
