The Inevitable Fate of the de Sitter Universe: From Scale Invariance to Dark Energy ✦

Original: "The unavoidable de Sitter fate of a scale-invariant Universe"
· Chiara Cecchini, Massimiliano Rinaldi
arXiv:2606.31472 · 2026-06-30 · CC BY · 3 min
General Relativity Cosmology HEP Phenomenology HEP Theory
Any stable scale-invariant theory of gravity predicts a non-zero cosmological constant, unless the quartic coupling is eliminated.
Abstract

Flat cosmological solutions are studied within a general scale-invariant scalar-tensor theory of gravity. It is proven that any stable configuration with non-degenerate gravitational dynamics possesses a non-zero cosmological constant. The exception occurs when the quartic self-interaction of the scalar field vanishes. Since this condition is not protected from radiative corrections, a residual cosmological constant emerges as a robust prediction of such theories. The result points to the possibility that dark energy is a natural consequence of an early scale-invariant phase in the universe's evolution.

1

Context

The cosmological constant problem is one of the most glaring contradictions between theory and observation. A naive estimate of the vacuum energy density from quantum field theory exceeds the value measured from supernovae (about 10⁻⁴⁷ GeV⁴) by 120 orders of magnitude. This suggests that the classical theory should already suppress a large contribution, yet even in the absence of dimensionful parameters—in scale-invariant theories—the residual dark energy does not vanish. The work of Cecchini and Rinaldi uncovers this mechanism, relying on a powerful tool—Noether's theorem, and shows that accelerated expansion is not a bug but a feature of such models.

Methods

The authors considered the most general action for a single scalar field φ, nonlinearly coupled to curvature: J(R/φ²), where R is the Ricci scalar. To avoid arbitrariness in interpreting gravity, they moved from the original Jordan frame to the Einstein frame via a conformal transformation—a technique tracing back to the work of Dicke. In this frame, gravity obeys Einstein's equations in minimal form, and cosmological evolution for the FLRW metric follows Friedmann's equations. The key was applying Noether's theorem: the conservation of the Noether current, discovered by Emmy Noether, constrains the field's evolution to an elliptical trajectory and forbids the point φ=0, thus leading to a non-zero vacuum.

Results

The analysis showed that any stable fixed point with non-degenerate gravity (i.e., with a non-zero Planck mass) obeys the condition J'(X*)X* = 2J(X*). Substituting into the potential yields V_EF ∝ J(X*)/J'(X*)², which vanishes only if J(0)=0, corresponding to λ=0—the absence of a quartic self-coupling. However, even if one sets λ=0 in the classical Lagrangian, quantum corrections inevitably revive this term—a situation familiar from the Standard Model, where the quartic coupling of the Higgs field vanishes only with fine-tuning of parameters. Thus, dark energy in the form of a cosmological constant turns out not to be ineradicable but natural: born in the early inflationary phase, the Universe inherits it as an inevitable outcome.

Implications

This result changes the perspective on the cosmological constant. Instead of wondering why dark energy is so small, one should ask about the values of dimensionless constants in the fundamental theory. Acceleration no longer looks like an alien add-on but emerges from the same symmetries that govern gravity at high energies. Thus, scale invariance does not solve the problem but recasts it in the spirit of "naturalness": small numbers must be protected by symmetry.

Future development

Future development may involve including additional scalar fields (as in two-field models where λ=0 is achieved along a flat direction) or supersymmetry. It is also interesting to study the renormalization group flow of the quartic coupling near the Planck scale: perhaps dark energy will turn out to be connected to the behavior of the Higgs potential. Another path is to look for observational imprints of this inevitability, for example, in the spectrum of primordial fluctuations.

Impact

This work will impact models of inflation and dark energy, as well as attempts to unify them into a single picture, such as in "quasi-de Sitter" scenarios. It stimulates a re-examination of the role of classical symmetries in quantum gravity.

Next steps

The next steps are to verify the robustness of the conclusion when adding matter and to study quantum corrections near the fixed point. It is also important to build a realistic model where the quartic coupling is naturally suppressed, possibly via a mechanism analogous to the Higgs in the Standard Model.

Key open problems

This paper touches on the cosmological constant problem, the hierarchy of scales, and the nature of dark energy. It emphasizes that even "prohibitive" symmetries do not guarantee a zero vacuum and connects high-energy inflationary physics with the observed acceleration.
