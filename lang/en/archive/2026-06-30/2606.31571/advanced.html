Nonlinear growth of gravitational waves from cosmological phase transitions under the influence of the Universe's expansion

Original: "Nonlinear growth and amplification of phase-transition gravitational waves induced by cosmic expansion"
· Xiao Wang, Chi Tian, Csaba Balázs
arXiv:2606.31571 · 2026-06-30 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · HEP Phenomenology Cosmology
Taking cosmic expansion into account in three-dimensional hydrodynamic simulations of early Universe phase transitions revealed a nonlinear, almost exponential growth of gravitational wave energy, leading to an amplification of their spectrum by tens to hundreds of times.
Abstract

The first three-dimensional hydrodynamic simulation of cosmological first-order phase transitions in an expanding background has been performed. The simulations consistently account for the changing transition intensity throughout the nucleation process of slow transitions. It was found that beyond reducing the average distance between bubbles by effectively boosting the nucleation rate, cosmic expansion causes a nonlinear increase in the gravitational wave energy fraction. This leads to amplification of gravitational wave spectra by a factor of 10–100. The amplification is more pronounced for initially weak transitions than for moderately strong ones. The results demonstrate the complexity and importance of accurately modeling slow phase transitions with the expansion of the Universe taken into account.

Context

Why it matters: Gravitational waves from first-order cosmological phase transitions are one of the most promising windows into physics beyond the Standard Model. Such transitions might have occurred in the early Universe, when the cooling cosmic plasma spontaneously rearranged, giving rise to bubbles of a new phase that collided and produced shock sound waves. Until now, all serious calculations neglected the expansion of space, considering it a minor effect. However, for slow transitions lasting a fraction of the Hubble time, this approximation is not justified—and our work shows for the first time the dramatic consequences of including the background cosmological dynamics.

Methods

How it was done: We conducted a series of three-dimensional hydrodynamic simulations within the 'Higgsless' approach, which replaces the microscopic dynamics of the Higgs field with bubble walls moving at constant speed. Unlike all predecessors, Einstein's field equations were directly solved in the Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker (FLRW) metric, taking into account cosmic expansion governed by Hubble's law and Friedmann's equations (the link between Hubble and expansion). The secret to success lies in simultaneously accounting for two effects: the decrease in average radiation density, causing the transition strength parameter α(t) to grow as a^4, and the increase in bubble number due to the accelerating expansion of physical volume. The simulations reproduced a Poisson nucleation process in an expanding cosmology; the gravitational waves radiated by sound waves were computed from the transverse-traceless part of the stress-energy tensor.

Results

What we got: The main finding is a violation of the linear growth of gravitational wave energy after percolation. In a static Universe, GW energy increases proportionally to the lifetime of sound waves. Under expansion, however, a nonlinear feedback kicks in: growing α(t) amplifies the kinetic energy of the plasma, which forces the generation of metric perturbations—and in the first few β^−1 units, the growth becomes nearly exponential. As a result, the amplitude of the Ω_GW spectrum increases by tens of times (for initially intermediate-strength transitions, α0 = 0.05) and even hundreds of times (for weak transitions, α0 = 0.005). For example, with a wall velocity v_w = 0.4 and β/H_n = 20, the amplification reaches O(50). The peak frequency shifts upward due to the smaller average distance between bubbles. The spectral slopes at low and high frequencies remain virtually unchanged.

Implications

Significance: The results overturn previous ideas about the role of cosmic expansion in generating gravitational waves. Semi-analytical sound shell models predicted signal suppression; we instead see a giant amplification. This means that slow transitions in the early Universe, previously considered unpromising for observation, may in fact dominate the stochastic gravitational-wave background. The interpretation of existing radio astronomical observations from pulsar timing arrays and future observations with laser interferometers will need to reassess the connection between transition parameters and the signal.

Future development

Future development: The work opens several directions. First, the variable bubble wall velocity must be taken into account—in reality, it depends on the transition strength and could further enhance the effect. Second, the sound wave lifetime is crucial: we used a simple estimate based on the eddy turnover time, whereas in an expanding medium turbulence decays differently. Third, transitions with strong supercooling, where bubble collisions dominate over sound, may have their own surprises. Finally, incorporating fully microphysical simulations (with the Higgs field dynamics) will eliminate artifacts of the 'Higgsless' approximation.

Impact

Impact on: The results directly affect the interpretation of data from pulsar timing arrays (NANOGrav, EPTA, PPTA, CPTA) and the search strategy for gravitational waves by future space-based interferometers (LISA, Taiji, TianQin), as well as ground-based LIGO in the high-frequency band. They are also important for indirectly constraining models beyond the Standard Model, including various extensions of the Higgs sector and grand unified theories.

Next steps

Next steps: The immediate tasks are to conduct simulations over a wider parameter range (especially for strong and ultra-relativistic transitions) and to study in detail the decay time of acoustic waves by including physical viscosity and turbulence. It is also necessary to develop efficient spectrum emulators suitable for multidimensional data analysis from radio telescopes and gravitational-wave observatories.

Key open problems

Connection to unsolved problems: The generation of a stochastic background of gravitational waves is directly linked to three big questions: what is the nature of dark matter (a phase transition in the early Universe could have produced dark matter particles); how did baryon asymmetry arise (electroweak baryogenesis in extensions of the Standard Model); and is there a 'desert' between the electroweak scale and the Planck energy, or is physics rich with intermediate transitions that we can hear.

🎯 If we lived in a static Universe, gravitational waves from a phase transition would continue to grow linearly forever—as long as sound waves exist. But in an expanding Universe, the growth itself becomes explosive, then naturally fades due to the decreasing plasma density.

🎬 In Greg Egan's novel 'Schild's Ladder', vacuum phase transitions are explored that can reshape spacetime. Our work shows that even in 'gentle', non-catastrophic transitions, the Universe's expansion can generate unexpectedly powerful gravitational waves—a kind of echo of cosmic restructuring that may be louder than we thought.

Key numbers

  • amplitude amplification of the spectrum: up to 100 times (for weak transitions)
  • transition strength parameter α0: 0.005 (weak) and 0.05 (intermediate)
  • ratio β/H_n: from 15 to 25 (transition duration)
  • bubble wall velocity v_w: 0.4–0.8 in fractions of the speed of light
  • characteristic peak frequency: ~10^-6 Hz for T_n ~ 100 GeV
