Quantum Biology: Life as a Coherent State of a Chromosome ✦

Original: "Fock-Space Formulation of the Lifetime of a Unicellular Organism"
· Yehuda Roth
arXiv:2606.31185 · 2026-06-30 · CC BY 4.0 · 5 min · Biological Physics
A new physical approach defines the lifetime of a bacterium through the quantum coherence of its genetic code.
Abstract

Life is defined as a dynamic tendency to preserve identity. For a single-celled organism, identity is contained in chromosomal DNA, so a bacterium is alive as long as it actively maintains its chromosomal configuration and is capable of replication. In the study, the biological identity of a bacterium is equated with the coherence of its DNA code in an abstract state space. Within the Fock space formalism, bacteria with given codes are interpreted as occupied fermionic modes; replication, repair, and death are modeled by creation and annihilation operators. Lifespan is defined as the integral coherence time of the autocorrelation function of code occupancy. In a minimal Markov model, it turns out to be the inverse of the decay rate of the corresponding identity mode.

Context

What is life? This question, posed by Erwin Schrödinger in his famous book 'What is Life?' (1944), still remains open. Traditional biology describes life through a set of properties — metabolism, replication, homeostasis — but does not provide a single physical criterion. In recent years, approaches have emerged that define living things through the ability to maintain a coherent state in some asymptotic space, resisting decoherence (the decay of coherence due to interaction with the environment). For multicellular organisms, this coherence is classical in nature, as quantum effects are suppressed at the macroscopic level. However, for a single bacterium, there is a fundamental possibility to rely on a quantum field description of its chromosome as a unified quantum system. Such an approach allows us to rigorously introduce the concept of 'life' at the microscopic level and connect it with the unitary evolution of a quantum state.

Methods

The authors construct a Fock space — the mathematical apparatus of quantum field theory, where admissible DNA genetic codes are considered as basis states |γ⟩. For each code and each 'cell' (in an abstract sense — a lattice site), fermionic creation c† and annihilation c operators are introduced, which satisfy anticommutation relations: two bacteria with the same code cannot occupy the same cell. This reflects the idea that each cell can contain at most one bacterium of a given genetic type. Using these operators, effective operators for replication (transfer of the code to another cell), repair (correction of mutations — transition from an erroneous code to the correct one), and death (removal of the bacterium) are constructed. The dynamics of the whole system are described in the Heisenberg picture, where the time evolution of operators is given by some unitary (or generally non-unitary) evolution operator Û(t), obeying the Schrödinger equation with an effective Hamiltonian that includes the energetic 'cost' of various codes. The coherence of identity is quantitatively assessed through the autocorrelation function of the total number of bacteria with a given code: Cγ(t) = ⟨ ˆNγ(t) ˆNγ(0)⟩ / ⟨ ˆNγ(0)²⟩. The lifespan τγ is defined as the integral of this function over time: τγ = ∫₀∞ Cγ(t) dt. This integral measures how long the correlation in time between the presence of the code persists, i.e., how long the system 'remembers' its identity. In the simplest, Markovian approximation (without memory of past events), identity loss occurs with a constant probability λ per unit time, which gives an exponential decay of the correlation: Cγ(t) = e^{-λ t}, and then τ = 1/λ.

Results

The central result of the work is a closed formula linking the lifespan of a single-celled organism to a single parameter λ, which describes the rate of identity degradation due to decoherence. In a more general, non-Markovian case, the lifespan captures the entire time profile of the autocorrelation function and can be expressed through the spectrum of the dynamics generator. Numerical values of λ can be obtained from experimental data on mutation rates and repair efficiency. For example, for a typical bacterium with a genome size of about 4.6 million base pairs and an error probability of the order of 10⁻⁹ per base pair per replication, the estimated rate of damage accumulation gives λ ∼ 10⁻⁶–10⁻⁵ per generation. The model predicts that an organism is alive as long as its chromosomal code retains a high degree of state purity, and death is a phase transition of decoherence, after which the identity is irretrievably lost and information about the original code is scattered among many erroneous configurations. Interestingly, repair acts as an active process that counteracts the natural tendency toward an increase in entropy and restores coherence. This agrees with the concept of living systems as dissipative structures that maintain low entropy through an energy flux.

Implications

The proposed formalism provides a quantitative, subject-independent measure of biological individuality and death, based on quantum information. This opens the way to a rigorous physical classification of living systems: from viruses (which perhaps do not possess sufficient coherence) to multicellular organisms, where identity is maintained collectively. The model can also be applied to analyze the effectiveness of antibiotics and immune responses, which can be viewed as processes that induce decoherence or block repair. On a fundamental level, the work links biological mortality to the irreversibility of quantum measurement, building bridges between quantum field theory and the theory of complex systems.

Future development

In the future, the proposed approach could be extended to multicellular organisms, where identity emerges as a macroscopic coherent state protected by correlations among many cells. A hierarchical model could be built: bacterium → colony → tissue → organism, each level with its own type of coherence. It is also promising to include explicit mechanisms of mutagenesis and selection, which would enable modeling of evolutionary processes. Integration with quantum sensors and genome editing technologies could lead to practical applications in combating pathogens. Moreover, the question remains open about the role of quantum entanglement between different parts of the chromosome, which could further stabilize identity.

Impact

The results will impact astrobiology (search for life criteria beyond Earth), quantum biology, and medical physics (quantitative models of aging and carcinogenesis).

Next steps

The next step will be to estimate the parameter λ for model organisms based on existing data on mutagenesis and repair, as well as experimental verification of predictions about the behavior of autocorrelation functions in bacterial populations, for example, using time-resolved fluorescence microscopy.

Key open problems

The work addresses the fundamental problem of defining life (the phenomenon of 'life' in terms of physics) and connects it with the quantum-classical transition — one of the central problems in the interpretation of quantum mechanics. It also hints at the possibility of a universal criterion for life, analogous to the second law of thermodynamics for entropy, but for biological organization.

🎯 Interestingly, the use of fermionic statistics to describe bacteria in an abstract Fock space resembles the Pauli exclusion principle, which forbids two electrons from occupying the same quantum state. Here, the analog is a 'cell' that cannot be occupied by two bacteria with identical codes — this guarantees the uniqueness of the carrier of given genetic information at each point in the model's space-time. This approach echoes Erwin Schrödinger's ideas about an aperiodic crystal as the bearer of life.

C_{\gamma}(t) = \frac{\langle \hat{N}_{\gamma}(t) \hat{N}_{\gamma}(0) \rangle}{\langle \hat{N}_{\gamma}(0)^2 \rangle}
Shows how strongly the state of the system at time t correlates with the initial state; for a living organism it is close to one, for a dead one it tends to zero.
\tau_{\gamma} = \int_{0}^{\infty} C_{\gamma}(t) \, dt
The lifespan equals the area under the autocorrelation curve; it is larger the longer the system retains memory of its identity.
\tau_{\gamma} = \frac{1}{\lambda_{\gamma}}
If identity loss occurs at a constant rate λ, then the lifespan is inversely proportional to this rate — similar to the half-life of a radioactive nucleus.

Key numbers

  • Lifespan of E. coli bacterium: approximately 20–30 minutes in the logarithmic phase
  • Size of bacterial chromosome: about 4.6 × 10⁶ base pairs
  • Mutation rate: ~10⁻⁹ errors per base pair per replication
  • Quantum coherence time in DNA: up to a few hundred femtoseconds for electronic excitations
  • Estimated λ parameter for a typical bacterium: 10⁻⁶–10⁻⁵ per generation
