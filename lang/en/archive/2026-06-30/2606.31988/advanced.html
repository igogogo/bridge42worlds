A Unified View of Dark Matter and Cosmology: Diffusion Models in Gravitational Lensing ✦

Original: "Joint inference of weak lensing convergence map and cosmology with diffusion models"
· Benjamin Remy, Chihway Chang, Rebecca Willett
arXiv:2606.31988 · 2026-06-30 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Cosmology Instrumentation
The new JADE method combines reconstruction of gravitational lensing maps with Bayesian inference of cosmological parameters without a differentiable simulator.
Abstract

A method is proposed for joint inference of cosmological parameters and convergence maps from weak lensing data using implicit inference based on diffusion models. Training on simulations does not require a differentiable forward model for MCMC sampling. The transformer neural network processes pixel data and cosmological parameters as a single token sequence, enabling a single network to generate posterior samples of maps and parameters conditioned on a noisy shear field. On simulated log-normal fields in wCDM cosmology, the model recovers maps and parameters consistent with traditional MCMC, with MIRA calibration 0.635±0.017 (optimum 0.667). The recovered fields correctly reproduce two-point statistics and non-Gaussian distribution. This method paves the way for field-level analysis on more realistic, non-differentiable simulators.

Context

Modern sky surveys like LSST collect vast amounts of data on the shapes of billions of galaxies. The weak gravitational lensing effect, predicted by Einstein within general relativity, manifests as coherent distortions of these shapes due to the gravity of dark matter. Extracting cosmological information from these data traditionally relies on compressing them into correlation functions, but this loses much of the information encoded in non-Gaussian features of the matter distribution. Moving to field-level analysis promises a radical improvement in precision, but requires simultaneously solving two tasks: reconstructing the hidden mass maps and estimating cosmological parameters—which is exactly what this work accomplishes.

Methods

The authors proposed JADE, based on diffusion models—generative neural networks that learn to turn noise into realistic data samples. Unlike traditional approaches, here the model is trained directly on simulations of log-normal convergence fields that reproduce the non-linear clumping of matter and depend on six cosmological parameters of the wCDM model: densities of dark matter, dark energy, fluctuation amplitude, and others. The architecture uses a transformer that processes patches of the map flattened into tokens together with cosmological parameters as a single sequence, allowing mutual interactions to be captured. At inference time, the trained model, given an observed shear field, generates joint posterior samples of convergence maps and parameters by solving a stochastic differential equation.

Results

The JADE model, tested on simulated LSST Y10 data (5×5 degree field, 128×128 pixels, 5 tomographic bins with redshift), showed excellent agreement with classical MCMC sampling. The cosmological posterior distributions for parameters Ωc, σ8, w0, and others matched NUTS chains, and the calibration metric MIRA reached 0.635±0.017 (ideal 2/3). The recovered convergence maps not only reproduce two-point correlations (power spectrum with relative error of 6.7%), but also correctly capture non-Gaussian statistics, including one-point distributions with relative error of 5.2%. Importantly, all checks were performed frame-by-frame: for each sample, an independent simulation was generated at the corresponding parameters, confirming the internal consistency of the joint posterior.

Implications

The work demonstrates that deep learning can unify field-level and parameter inference in a single Bayesian scheme, eliminating the need for a differentiable forward model—which has long been a bottleneck for such analyses. This means that arbitrarily complex simulators, including full N-body and hydrodynamical codes, can now be used without sacrificing the ability to perform faithful Bayesian inference. The method lays the foundation for data analysis that retains all non-Gaussian information, which is especially valuable at small scales where non-linear structure formation generates a rich higher-order correlation structure.

Future development

Future development of the method includes transferring it to more realistic N-body simulations and extending it to curved celestial spheres rather than the flat-sky approximation. An important direction will be incorporating gradients of the forward model into training, if available, to improve the efficiency of using expensive simulations. Moreover, the architecture could be adapted for joint reconstruction of the initial conditions of the Universe, enabling tests of cosmological models through prediction of unobserved fields.

Impact

The proposed approach will impact extragalactic astronomy and cosmology by providing a tool to extract maximum information from upcoming surveys. It can also be used in other tasks requiring joint reconstruction of fields and global parameters, such as high-energy astrophysics or the physics of the expansion of the Universe.

Next steps

The immediate next steps include applying JADE to more realistic simulations, accounting for masking and systematic effects, and exploring the possibility of multi-probe analysis combining lensing with other cosmological observables.

Key open problems

The method is directly connected to the problems of dark matter and dark energy, allowing more precise measurement of the parameters governing the evolution of the Universe. It also brings us closer to fully utilizing the information contained in the large-scale structure, which is one of the key challenges of modern cosmology.
