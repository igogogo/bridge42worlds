Polaron with Oscillating Coupling: How Quantum Fluctuations Awaken Active Motion ✦

Original: "Self-propulsion of a polaron with an oscillating coupling to its quantum bath"
· Jacopo Romano, Andrea Gambassi
arXiv:2606.31555 · 2026-06-30 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min
Statistical Mech Quantum Gases
Periodic switching of the sign of the impurity's interaction with a quantum liquid turns it into a self-propelled 'acton'.
Abstract

The dynamics of a polaron (impurity particle) in a quantum gas with a periodically time-varying sign of coupling to the medium is studied. By eliminating the degrees of freedom of the environment, an effective drag force proportional to velocity is derived. It is found that above a critical modulation frequency, the friction coefficient becomes negative at low velocities, leading to self-sustained motion. In the classical limit, the transition regime is described as a function of modulation frequency and the chemical potential of the environment. First-order quantum corrections indicate the stability of the effect, though it can be suppressed by sufficiently precise measurements of the impurity's coordinate.

1

Context

Active matter — from flocks of birds to nanomotors — breaks equilibrium, converting energy into directed motion. However, at the quantum level, no one has yet observed analogs of swimming bacteria or self-propelled colloids. Landau introduced the concept of polarons — particles surrounded by a cloud of excitations in a quantum liquid — which seemed passive. But theorists at SISSA discovered that rhythmically switching their coupling to the environment can endow them with a will of their own.

Methods

Using quantum field theory in the Keldysh formalism, the authors performed a numerical simulation of an impurity in a Bose gas above the condensation temperature (i.e., without superfluidity). The interaction V(x,t) was described by a short-range potential with amplitude g(t) = g0 cos(ωt), which periodically switched the coupling from attractive to repulsive. By integrating out the bath degrees of freedom and going to the semiclassical limit, they derived an effective Langevin equation for the impurity with a velocity-dependent friction force F(v,t) and noise. The key was the analysis of the period-averaged viscosity γ(v).

Results

The calculations showed that at low modulation frequencies, the viscosity γ(v) is always positive — the impurity is slowed down by the medium. But when the frequency ω exceeds a critical threshold ω_c (depending on the bath particle energy E_K and chemical potential μ), a region with γ(v)<0 appears at low velocities. This means that the medium accelerates the impurity instead of slowing it down! An acton is born — an active polaron that spontaneously picks a direction and accelerates to a velocity v*, determined by the condition γ(v*)=0. At high velocities, the viscosity becomes positive again, so v* is a stable attractor. Numerically, for typical parameters (μ=-1, E_K=1), ω_c≈3.5, and the self-propulsion velocity v* reaches ~9 (in dimensionless units). Analysis of the wake in the quantum bath revealed that for the acton, the density depletion region shifts forward and helps push the particle — unlike a passive polaron, whose wake drags it back. Quantum corrections due to the uncertainty principle blur the initial momentum and can suppress the transition if the initial state is too localized (large σ_p). However, frequent precision position measurements, by 'freezing' the system and causing wave function collapse, restore the active behavior. In doing so, the fluctuation-dissipation theorem is violated, and the growth of entropy indicates entropy production, which is characteristic of active systems.

Implications

The discovery of self-propelled polarons blurs the boundary between classical and quantum active matter. It proves that directed motion can arise without artificial constructs — simply by periodically 'shaking' the quantum liquid. This result is a fundamental step in the quantum thermodynamics of non-equilibrium systems and the design of nanomotors operating at ultralow temperatures, where the second law of thermodynamics is crucial.

Future development

Future research will focus on accounting for stronger quantum decoherences in the acton's dynamics, especially beyond the Markovian approximation, and on studying a superfluid bath — there, self-propulsion could conflict with the Landau critical velocity. Equally enticing is the prospect of collective behavior of actons: their wake patterns can generate non-reciprocal interactions, giving rise to flocks of quantum particles.

Impact

This work will impact the physics of ultracold atoms, quantum thermodynamics, and the engineering of open quantum systems, where ways to achieve directed transport at the microscopic level have long been sought.

Next steps

Experimentalists will need to implement interaction modulation in Bose-Einstein condensates or degenerate Fermi gases using optical traps and Feshbach resonances, and verify whether the impurity indeed starts moving on its own. Theorists should rigorously examine the collective dynamics of many actons.

Key open problems

The study directly tackles the problem of quantum active matter — how non-equilibrium energy injection alters quantum correlations and generates long-range order. It also resonates with the question of the role of measurements in quantum dynamics (the Zeno effect in active systems) and the search for quantum analogs of motors.

🎯 The authors dubbed the active polaron an 'acton,' emphasizing that the particle becomes an 'agent,' akin to a biological entity. Even zero-point quantum fluctuations do not prevent it from finding a direction.
