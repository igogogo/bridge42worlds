Topology of the Universe: From Circles in the Sky to Quantum Corrections ✦

Original: "The Topology of the Universe"
· Craig J. Copi, Deyan P. Mihaylov, Anna Negro, Amirhossein Samandar, Glenn D. Starkman, Yashar Akrami, George Alestas, Stefano Anselmi, Javier Carrón Duque, Fernando Cornet-Gomez, Linn Htat Lu, Andrew H. Jaffe, Arthur Kosowsky, Mikel Martin Barandiaran, Thiago S. Pereira, Catherine Petretti, Andrius Tamosiunas
arXiv:2606.24886 · 2026-06-23 · CC BY · 4 min · Cosmology General Relativity HEP Theory
An overview of methods, data, and prospects for detecting nontrivial spatial topology.
Links in the knowledge graph 1

Context

Cosmology relies on the Friedmann equations, which describe the expansion of a homogeneous and isotropic Universe. However, the local Einstein equations say nothing about global topology—whether it is possible to travel far enough to return to the starting point, closing a loop. Such a loop would mean that space is multiply connected and its volume is finite. The question of topology intrigued Einstein and Lemaître even then, and today it can be answered by observing the large-scale structure of the cosmic microwave background (CMB). Because if the Universe has a nontrivial topology, for example, a three-torus, then gravitational waves and matter density will violate statistical isotropy—the key principle that allows distinguishing the microwave background from random noise.

Methods

Experimentally, topology is searched for using the CMB by analyzing maps from the WMAP and Planck satellites. The primary method is searching for matched circles on the surface of last scattering: if our fundamental domain is smaller than the diameter of this sphere, light from a single distant object can reach us from two opposite directions, creating matching temperature patterns. A more sensitive approach is the Bayesian analysis of the sky's covariance matrices, where off-diagonal elements signal a violation of isotropy. This requires numerical simulations of the CMB in multiply connected spaces—for example, the COMPACT collaboration computed full eigenmode spectra for all 18 orientable Euclidean topologies. Additional information comes from polarization maps, as topology also breaks parity, mixing E- and B-modes. Furthermore, spectroscopic data from distant galaxies are used for three-dimensional mapping of inhomogeneities, which obey the Hubble law.

Results

Direct searches for circles in WMAP and Planck data revealed no statistically significant matches, setting a lower limit on the distance to the nearest clone of at least 98.5% of the LSS diameter. Bayesian analysis extended this limit to 1.03 LSS diameters for some topologies, though only a small fraction of the parameter space has been explored. Calculations of the Kullback–Leibler divergence show that even when the shortest closed loop is 1.3 times larger than the LSS, the signal can be detected. Moreover, due to the violation of homogeneity, the shortest loop in the Universe can be up to six times shorter than the one passing through us—meaning topology can reveal itself far beyond the range of direct circle tests.

Implications

Detecting a nontrivial topology would imply that the inflationary model did not erase all traces of the initial conditions, and the duration of inflation was limited. It would open a window into pre-inflationary physics, possibly connected to quantum gravity. Topology violates the cosmological principle: homogeneity and isotropy cease to be global, requiring a revision of the interpretation of lensing and other secondary effects. Quantum corrections akin to the Casimir effect could also appear, influencing the spectrum of gravitational waves and CMB anisotropies.

Future development

With the launch of the polarization-focused LiteBIRD satellite and the stratospheric Taurus telescope, sensitivity to low multipoles (ℓ<200) is expected to improve severalfold. This will allow detection of predicted T-E-B correlations even if the topological scale exceeds the Hubble radius. Large-scale galaxy surveys such as Euclid and SPHEREx will provide a three-dimensional map of matter distribution, where topological information is spread over many more modes. Numerical methods will be improved to account for non-Gaussianity and non-orientable manifolds.

Impact

The discovery of topology will affect the interpretation of CMB data and large-scale structure, forcing a re-evaluation of constraints on dark matter and dark energy. It will also spur the development of quantum cosmology and string theory, where topology plays a key role in the compactification of extra dimensions.

Next steps

Immediate next steps include completing a full Bayesian analysis of Planck data for all orientable Euclidean topologies and incorporating polarization maps. In parallel, methods are being developed to process upcoming experiments, accounting for systematic effects and masking of the Galactic plane.

Key open problems

The topology problem is directly connected to the question of initial conditions of the Big Bang and the nature of inflation. It also touches on the cosmological constant and constraints on curvature. More fundamentally, it is part of an old question: whether one can recover the geometry of the 'drum' of the Universe from its vibration spectrum—a question that goes back to the famous query by mathematician Mark Kac.

🎯 Mathematician Mark Kac asked in 1966: 'Can one hear the shape of a drum?' It turned out not always, but most shapes are distinguishable. The search for the Universe's topology is a giant experiment in 'listening' to the cosmic drum through the cosmic microwave background.
