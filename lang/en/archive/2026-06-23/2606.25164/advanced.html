Shock Acceleration of Outflows in NGC 1068: Dust as a Tracer ✦

Original: "Dust destruction signals shock-accelerated outflows in the nearby active galaxy NGC 1068"
· Luke R. Holden, Clive N. Tadhunter, Daniel J. B. Smith, Martin A. Bourne, Marina I. Arnaudova, Isaac M. Mutie
arXiv:2606.25164 · 2026-06-23 · CC BY 4.0 · 2 min
Galaxies High Energy
Emission line analysis revealed that gas outflows in the active galaxy NGC 1068 are accelerated by shock waves that destroy dust.
Abstract

Based on deep spectroscopic observations of the nearby active galaxy NGC 1068, the acceleration mechanism of large-scale gas outflows driven by the active nucleus was investigated. Flux ratios of high-ionization coronal forbidden lines [NeV]λ3425 and [FeVII]λ6087 indicate that the undisturbed gas in the disk is depleted in refractory elements because they are locked up in dust grains, whereas the outflow directly above the disk is almost dust-free. Similar evidence was obtained from lines of less ionized elements. Diagnostic ratios show that the density of the outflowing gas is 19–110 times greater than that of the disk gas. These results suggest that the outflows are accelerated by fast shock waves that simultaneously compress the gas and destroy dust. The study confirms the crucial role of coronal emission lines as tracers of the disruptive impact of nuclear activity on the interstellar medium.

1

Context

Massive gas outflows observed in active galactic nuclei play a key role in galaxy evolution, regulating star formation. However, it is still unclear exactly how they are accelerated. The nearby Seyfert galaxy NGC 1068, with its kiloparsec-scale radio jet and multiphase outflows, is an ideal laboratory for addressing this question.

Methods

Using the Xshooter spectrograph on the Very Large Telescope (VLT), deep spectroscopy was obtained for a region 230 pc from the nucleus, at the base of the northeastern radio lobe. Analysis of Doppler shifts of emission lines allowed separation of radiation from the disk gas (narrow components) and the outflow (broad, shifted by ~250 km/s). To measure density, standard [S II] ratios and a new method of transauroral lines were used, and dust content was assessed from lines of refractory elements, such as iron and nickel, compared to neon.

Results

It turned out that the density of the outflowing gas is 19–110 times higher than that of the disk, ruling out acceleration by radiation pressure and requiring compression in shock waves. Crucially, the ratio of coronal lines [Ne V] to [Fe VII] in the outflow is 14.4 times lower than in the disk, directly indicating the destruction of dust in shock waves (via sputtering). Additional diagnostics involving [Fe II], [Ni II], and [Ca II] confirm that the outflow is virtually dust-free, whereas in the disk the dust content is typical for the interstellar medium, altering the relative element abundances.

Implications

These results for the first time directly link the acceleration of outflows in NGC 1068 to fast shock waves, which compress and heat the gas, destroying dust. Consequently, coronal iron lines become a reliable tracer of shock processes in active nuclei. This settles a long-standing debate about the acceleration mechanism and provides important constraints for feedback models.

Future development

Future observations with James Webb (JWST) will allow similar diagnostics to be applied to large samples of AGN to determine how universal the shock mechanism is. Analyzing other refractory elements, such as silicon and calcium, will help reconstruct the structure of dust grains and the dynamics of their destruction in shock waves.

Impact

The work will impact the theory of galaxy evolution, the physics of cosmic dust, and the interpretation of spectroscopic data of active nuclei.

Next steps

Detailed numerical modeling of shock waves including dust destruction, and comparison with JWST data in the mid-IR range.

Key open problems

The results are directly connected to the fundamental problem of feedback: how supermassive black holes regulate galaxy growth. Understanding the outflow acceleration mechanism is essential for building self-consistent models of cosmic structure formation.

🎯 Calcium, a super-refractory element, possibly survives in shock waves, remaining in dust grain cores, which explains the absence of its emission line.

Key numbers

  • Outflow density compared to disk: 19–110 times higher
  • Outflow velocity: ~250 km/s
  • Distance from nucleus: 230 pc
  • Outflow density ([S II]): 5,370 cm⁻³
  • Outflow density (transauroral method): 14,125 cm⁻³
