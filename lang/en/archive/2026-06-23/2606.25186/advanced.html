Causality as a Universal Limiter on Neutron Star Radii ✦

Original: "Causality alone bounds the maximum radius difference between different-mass neutron stars"
· Aleksi Kurkela, Tuhin Malik
arXiv:2606.25186 · 2026-06-23 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min
High Energy
The condition of causality (sound speed less than light speed) imposes a strict upper limit on the radii of neutron stars of different masses, independent of the equation of state model.
Abstract

We investigate how the assumption of a unified causal equation of state (EoS) correlates the radii of neutron stars of different masses and reduces observational uncertainties. Based on causality and chiral effective field theory (χEFT) near saturation density, an analytical upper bound is derived: R(2.0 M⊙) ≤ 1.16 R(1.4 M⊙) − 1.1 km. This bound is exactly saturated by a one-parameter family of analytical EoS. Applying this constraint to independent NICER posterior distributions for pulsars PSR J0437-4715, PSR J0614-3329, and PSR J0740+6620 leaves only 7.5% of their joint volume and rules out the large-radius tail for PSR J0740+6620. The approach isolates universal physical implications of a causal EoS from effects of a specific prior model, providing a transparent benchmark for interpreting neutron star observations.

1

Context

Neutron stars are the densest objects in the Universe, where matter is compressed to nuclear and supranuclear densities. Their structure is described by a unified equation of state (EoS) linking pressure and energy density. However, until now, observation-based radius estimates have strongly depended on the choice of a specific EoS model. The breakthrough of the new work is that it for the first time derives radius constraints based solely on the fundamental principle of the constancy of the speed of light (sound cannot outrun light) and the assumption that all neutron stars obey the same EoS. This allows separation of universal physical consequences from artifacts of model assumptions. Predicted by Fritz Zwicky and discovered as radio pulsars by Jocelyn Bell Burnell, these objects are now becoming even more precise cosmic laboratories.

Methods

The authors constructed two types of equation-of-state ensembles: a maximally agnostic one using a fractal bridge covering the entire space of thermodynamically stable and causal EoS, and a commonly used parameterization of the sound speed c_s^2(ε). As the low-density baseline, chiral effective field theory (χEFT) calculations were used, and at high densities, perturbative QCD. For each EoS, the hydrostatic equilibrium equation Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff (TOV) was solved numerically within general relativity, and mass and radius were computed. Only EoS that allow a neutron star with a maximum mass of at least 1.97 M⊙, corresponding to observations of the most massive pulsars, were selected. This approach allowed isolating the pure effect of causality without additional model assumptions.

Results

The central result is the upper bound on the radius of a neutron star of mass 2.0 M⊙, expressed via the radius of a canonical 1.4 M⊙ star: R_2.0 ≤ 1.16 R_1.4 − 1.1 km. This limit is saturated by a special family of EoS in which, immediately after the end of the χEFT region, a phase transition occurs, and then the sound speed is maximal (c_s^2 = 1). The bound does not depend on the choice of prior distribution and is a direct consequence of the causality principle. When this bound is imposed on independent posterior radius distributions of three pulsars obtained by the [instrument:NICER]NICER[/instrument] observatory (PSR J0437−4715, PSR J0614−3329, and PSR J0740+6620), it turns out that the joint probability shrinks to 7.5%. This mainly happens due to the removal of an unphysical large-radius tail for the most massive pulsar J0740+6620 (initially its median radius was about 12.5 km, and after the constraint it dropped to ~11.4 km). Moreover, a comparison with a hypothetical EoS family with a conformal sound speed (c_s^2 = 1/3) shows that such a soft equation of state is incompatible with the data: it lies below the observational contours. Hence, in the interiors of neutron stars, the sound speed must necessarily exceed the conformal limit, and in the most likely scenarios, it approaches the speed of light.

Implications

The obtained bound for the first time provides a model-independent tool to test the consistency of neutron star radius measurements. It allows, without resorting to full Bayesian inference using EoS priors, to sharply narrow the allowed parameter region and cut off unphysical values. This is especially important for identifying possible deviations caused by exotic physics, such as the presence of dark matter in interiors. The method also demonstrates that even minimal and indisputable physical principles can yield strong constraints.

Future development

As data accumulate from [instrument:NICER]NICER[/instrument] and future missions such as STROBE-X or eXTP, the constraint will become ever tighter. Moreover, the approach can be directly generalized to tidal deformabilities measured in gravitational-wave events involving neutron stars. In the future, it will become a standard test for any new EoS model claiming to describe superdense matter.

Impact

The results will have a direct impact on the interpretation of data from X-ray observatories like [instrument:NICER]NICER[/instrument] and future telescopes, on the analysis of gravitational waves from neutron star mergers (LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA), and on the theory of nuclear matter at high densities, including searches for phase transitions.

Next steps

Immediate next steps include applying the method to other known pulsars (e.g., PSR J0030+0451) and refining the low-density input using improved χEFT calculations, which will slightly tighten the constraint.

Key open problems

The work is directly connected to the unsolved problem of the equation of state of cold superdense matter and to the question of the existence of phase transitions in neutron star cores, which remains one of the key issues in astrophysics and nuclear physics.
