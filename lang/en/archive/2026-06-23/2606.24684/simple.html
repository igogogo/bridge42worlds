Galaxy mergers ignite only the brightest and dustiest cores ✦

Original: "Only obscured yet luminous active galactic nuclei are closely associated with galaxy mergers: Direct observational evidence from type 2 active galactic nuclei"
· Yongmin Yoon, Yongjung Kim, Dohyeong Kim, Jaejun Cho, Woowon Byun
arXiv:2606.24684 · 2026-06-23 · CC BY · 1 min
Galaxies
Only bright and dusty active galactic nuclei owe their birth to mergers.
Abstract

Picture a hidden campfire: bright flames are only visible when they are wrapped in thick smoke. Astronomers have found that supermassive black holes at the centers of galaxies show signs of recent collisions only if they are both powerful and heavily dust-obscured. It's like catching the moment right after a merger, before the dust settles. Random coincidence or a key to galaxy evolution?

1

In the center of every galaxy slumbers a supermassive black hole. When gas and dust fall onto it, an active nucleus ignites — a blinding torch. Often the trigger is a galaxy merger, but not all nuclei retain its traces. Compare it to a campfire: a fresh log gives a bright flame and thick smoke. That's what the most powerful and dusty nuclei are like. Astronomers examined 748 dusty nuclei, searching for tidal tails — stellar streams betrayed by the glow of oxygen and hydrogen in spectra.

Nearly 70% of nuclei that are both bright and obscured by dust (dimming the light by more than half) have tails. In dim or lightly dusty nuclei, mergers are rare. So, only the "smokiest" torches ignite after collisions. The burst of star formation lasts tens of millions of years, but the tails last billions — like charred branches of a dead campfire. This fits into galactic evolution.

Tidal tails, like charred logs, live hundreds of times longer than a nucleus's active phase: it lasts tens of millions of years, while tails last billions.
The discovery builds on the work of Maarten Schmidt, who spotted quasars, and Johann Balmer, whose formulas helped "weigh" the dust.
