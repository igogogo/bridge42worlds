The Quantum Recipe for Active Matter: How Spins Speed Up Atoms ✦

Original: "Quantum-enabled active matter at the atomic scale"
· Sabrina Burgardt, Julian Feß, Alexander Guthmann, Silvia Hiebel, Aritra K. Mukhopadhyay, Sangyun Lee, Michael te Vrugt, Benno Liebchen, Hartmut Löwen, Raphael Wittkowski, Artur Widera
arXiv:2606.24615 · 2026-06-23 · CC BY-SA 4.0 · 4 min
Quantum Physics Mesoscale Quantum Gases Statistical Mech
An experiment with individual cesium atoms demonstrates for the first time active motion powered by quantum spin-exchange collisions.
1

Context

The push to miniaturize active particles down to atomic scales confronts the dominance of quantum effects. Just as Einstein explained Brownian motion, modern active matter research aims to understand particles that draw energy from their surroundings. At the atomic level, however, classical mechanisms give way to quantum ones: Schrödinger evolution of the wavefunction and wave–particle duality become decisive. This work for the first time realizes activity at the scale of individual atoms, using quantum spin exchange to create directed motion.

Methods

The experiment was carried out with an ensemble of about 68 cesium-133 atoms, trapped in an optical dipole trap and immersed in an ultracold cloud of 7700 rubidium-87 atoms at a temperature of about 460 nanokelvin, close to the conditions for Bose–Einstein condensation. Activation was provided by inelastic s-wave spin-exchange collisions, with cross sections calculated from first principles using the Schrödinger equation within a multichannel approach. In each such collision, the Zeeman energy difference of two hyperfine states (Zeeman effect) is released as kinetic energy, while the reverse (endothermic) processes are suppressed due to the ultralow collision energies, leading to a violation of the second law of thermodynamics at the microlevel—effectively a breakdown of detailed balance. The quantum nature of scattering further enhances activation: high-energy atoms have a lower collision probability, prolonging their free path. For modeling, two complementary approaches were used: numerical simulation with an event-based Monte Carlo method and a coarse-grained active Langevin model derived from kinetic theory.

Results

The spin-exchange collisions cause a significant transient broadening of the spatial distribution of cesium atoms: its width can exceed the passive case by a factor of 4 at a magnetic field of 3 G, corresponding to an activation energy Q ≈ 51 μK—two orders of magnitude larger than the bath's thermal energy. The broadening is temporary, since the spin-state spectrum of cesium is limited (maximum 6 exothermic collisions), after which the atom thermalizes via elastic collisions. The dynamics agree excellently, without free parameters, with predictions from both quantum-mechanical cross-section calculations in Monte Carlo simulations and the active Langevin model, confirming that cesium atoms behave as classical active Brownian particles but with a quantum energy source. Increasing the temperature of the rubidium bath weakens the effect because the relative contribution of the activation energy decreases.

Implications

This work turns ultracold atomic mixtures into a controllable platform for quantum thermodynamics of active matter. It enables experimental exploration of fundamental questions, such as entropy production in nonequilibrium quantum systems and the limits of applicability of fluctuation–dissipation theorems. Moreover, the system can serve as a testbed for studying quantum decoherence in active media, since the interaction with the environment (the rubidium bath) is well described by the Markov approximation.

Future development

Further cooling of the system will enhance quantum superpositions and matter-wave effects, allowing observation of competition between activity and localization in changing potentials. Restoring the spin state of cesium via radio-frequency radiation could extend the active period, and increasing the number of active atoms will open the door to collective quantum phenomena such as flocking or mobility-induced phase separation. In the future, such systems may find applications in quantum information technologies, for example in reservoir computing.

Impact

Beyond the physics of active media and statistical mechanics, the technique will impact ultracold quantum chemistry and quantum-state engineering. The ability to precisely control energy exchange between internal and external degrees of freedom of individual particles is valuable for quantum simulators and sensors.

Next steps

Near-term steps include experiments with a larger number of cesium atoms to observe collective effects, as well as a transition to a degenerate rubidium gas, where anomalous diffusion and long-lived correlations may radically alter the active dynamics.

Key open problems

The realized system touches on key unsolved problems in physics: the microscopic origin of the arrow of time through entropy production in the quantum limit, the role of the observer in the quantum dynamics of open systems, and the problem of quantum thermalization. Boltzmann sought to explain irreversibility through statistics, and today quantum active matter offers a new angle on this fundamental puzzle.
