Quantum Ratchet: Spins Kickstart Motion in Icy Matter ✦

Original: "Quantum-enabled active matter at the atomic scale"
· Sabrina Burgardt, Julian Feß, Alexander Guthmann, Silvia Hiebel, Aritra K. Mukhopadhyay, Sangyun Lee, Michael te Vrugt, Benno Liebchen, Hartmut Löwen, Raphael Wittkowski, Artur Widera
arXiv:2606.24615 · 2026-06-23 · CC BY-SA 4.0 · 3 min
Quantum Physics Mesoscale Quantum Gases Statistical Mech
Physicists turned cesium atoms into active quantum particles: spin-exchange collisions work as a one-way motor—a ratchet powered by magnetic energy.
1

In 1905, Einstein showed that the chaotic motion of pollen in water results from the jostling of invisible molecules. A century later, physicists have learned not only to observe but also to control motion at the quantum level. New work demonstrates active matter in its most miniature embodiment: individual cesium atoms, immersed in an ultracold rubidium mist, become microscopic motors. But this is no perpetual motion machine—it runs only as long as the quantum fuel lasts.

The heart of the mechanism is spin-exchange collisions. When wave-particles of cesium and rubidium meet, their hyperfine states can flip the spin projection, like partners in a quantum tango swapping roles. The Zeeman energy difference is released as a kinetic kick. This kick reaches 51 microkelvin—two orders of magnitude hotter than the surrounding near-condensate. But the main trick: the reverse process, which absorbs energy, is almost forbidden at 460 nanokelvin. Thus emerges a quantum ratchet—the spin pawl clicks only one way, inevitably accelerating cesium until the supply of seven spin states runs dry. That, mind you, is a six-“tooth” battery. Astonishingly, nature pulls a similar trick in living cells: the rotor of ATP synthase, the main molecular motor of life, spins in one direction thanks to a proton gradient. Here the gradient is magnetic, and the cogs are spins.

The actual asymmetry is so strong that for every inelastic “green” collision, there are up to 52 elastic ones—but the latter merely scatter, without taking away the gained speed.

Numerical simulation via Monte Carlo and a coarse-grained Langevin equation with no fitting parameters reproduced the atoms’ dynamics to within a pixel’s jitter. Measurements showed that the cesium atoms behave like classical active Brownian particles. Only, their energy source is quantum and exhaustible. The temporary quadrupling of the cloud’s width is direct proof of directed motion emerging from a microscopic violation of detailed balance.

This playground for quantum thermodynamics opens a door to experimental investigation of the arrow of time at the atomic scale. Here we can see how entropy is born in nonequilibrium systems, how decoherence interacts with activity, and where the limits of the fluctuation-dissipation theorem lie in the quantum world. Moreover, replenishing the spin fuel with radiofrequency radiation and scaling up the number of active atoms promises observation of collective quantum choreography: flocking, phase separation, and perhaps a new type of quantum density waves.

In an ultracold bath millions of times colder than the cosmic microwave background, tiny bursts of spin-exchange energy ignite motion hotter than room warmth.

In the long run, such systems could become elements of quantum computing in the style of reservoir computers or precision sensors. But above all, they remind us that even near absolute zero, nature knows how to build ratchets, and Boltzmann, who sought irreversibility in statistics, would have been fascinated by this quantum extension of his debate with Schrödinger on life and order.
