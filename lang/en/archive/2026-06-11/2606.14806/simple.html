Classical gravity would give a single particle the power of a supercomputer, but physics forbids it—so gravity must be quantum.
Abstract

Imagine that gravity doesn't obey quantum laws but simply bends space for quantum particles. In such a hypothetical universe, just one massive quantum bit could solve the most complex computational problem in seconds—something that would take ordinary computers an eternity. It's like having a universal key that opens any lock, prompting the question: maybe gravity is quantum after all?

Gravity seems simple: it keeps us on the ground and makes planets orbit. But if it were classical—described by Einstein's equations as the curvature of spacetime, while all matter follows the quantum laws of the Standard Model—then an ordinary quantum bit (qubit) falling freely would solve unsolvable problems. It turns out gravity influences the qubit so that its trajectory becomes a computation. It's like a marble rolling through a bumpy labyrinth and finding the shortest path—and that path turns out to be the answer. Problems like perfectly packing a suitcase are beyond even supercomputers.

NP-complete puzzles, like creating a conflict-free schedule, would take a regular computer billions of years to solve.

But there's a fundamental principle: nothing physical can compute faster than an ordinary computer. If gravity were classical, a falling qubit would break that rule. Since we don't witness such magic in reality, gravity must be quantum. So it's not physics but computation theory that demands quantum gravity—an unexpected twist. Hawking, Penrose, and Wheeler grappled with this: without quantum gravity, we can't understand black holes or the birth of the Universe.

🎯 A qubit is a quantum bit that can be 0 and 1 at the same time. However, in this scenario, quantum speedup alone isn't enough—you need gravity to be tricky.
