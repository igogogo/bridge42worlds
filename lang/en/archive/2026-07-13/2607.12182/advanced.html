Quantum Entanglement in PET: A New Era of Medical Imaging ✦

Original: "Roadmap Towards Quantum Entanglement Positron Emission Tomography (QE-PET)"
· Paweł Moskal, Shiva Abbaszadeh, Harmanjeet Singh Bilkhu, Peter Caradonna, Pragya Das, Praveen Gurunath Bharathi, Ana Marija Kožuljević, Zdenka Kuncic, Deepak Kumar, Mihael Makek, Marek Nowakowski, Siddharth Parashari, Kaustav Prasad, Gregory R. Romanchek, Sushil Sharma, Kenji Shimazoe, Ewa Stępień, Sodai Takyu, Miwako Takahashi, Mizuki Uenomachi, Ritesh Verma, Taiga Yamaya
arXiv:2607.12182 · 2026-07-13 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Medical Physics Quantum Physics
Harnessing the quantum entanglement of annihilation photons paves the way for improved image quality and new diagnostic biomarkers.
Abstract

Annihilation photons are quantum-entangled in polarization, a property unused in today’s clinical PET. We review the current state of research on harnessing this entanglement for medical diagnostics. The physics fundamentals and detector system development for future Quantum-Entangled PET scanners are explored—devices designed to extract entanglement via Compton scattering of photons off electrons. Experimental data on entanglement and decoherence are presented alongside theoretical insights. These methods aim to suppress random coincidences for cleaner PET images, employ entanglement degree as a biomarker for pathology and tissue oxygenation, and establish a pH-imaging technique. Whether this can reach clinical practice remains an open challenge, inviting the scientific community to drive this field forward.

Context

Modern positron emission tomography (PET) is based on detecting two 511 keV gamma rays from positron annihilation. Until recently, however, the quantum properties of these photons, such as the superposition of polarization states and their decoherence, were ignored. Fundamental research by Einstein, Bell, and Bohm showed that Bell's theorem makes it possible to experimentally distinguish quantum entanglement from classical correlations. In the context of PET, this opens the opportunity not only to improve image quality but also to obtain new physiological information.

Methods

To measure the degree of entanglement, polarimetric methods based on Compton scattering of photons are used. Detectors made of plastic scintillators or semiconductors (CZT) register both the initial hit of annihilation photons and subsequent scattering. The azimuthal distribution of the angle φ between the scattering planes of the two photons is analyzed, described by the function F(φ) = F₀[1 - CQE·A(θ₁)A(θ₂)cos(2φ)]. This allows calculating the parameter CQE — the degree of quantum entanglement, independent of scattering angles. Experiments are conducted on prototypes such as the modular J-PET with plastic strips, GAGG and LYSO crystal matrices, and hybrid Compton-PET systems.

Results

Key results were obtained in 2023–2025: (i) entanglement is not destroyed by Compton scattering of one photon at angles θS < 36°, as confirmed by decoherence measurements; (ii) the degree of entanglement is not maximal for annihilation in a porous polymer (CQE < 1), indicating dependence on tissue type; (iii) the measured asymmetry factor R at scattering angles of 81.7° was 2.00 ± 0.03 (for a maximally entangled state, R = 2.84 is predicted, and for a separable one, 1.63). This opens the possibility of using CQE as a biomarker of hypoxia: for adipose tissue CQE ≈ 0.89, for water 0.82, and when the partial pressure of oxygen changes from 50 to 10 mmHg, CQE changes by 0.0019.

Implications

These results mean that quantum entanglement could become a new dimension in PET, allowing not only improved signal-to-noise ratio through suppression of random coincidences but also visualization of tissue microenvironment. In particular, entanglement degree maps can reflect oxygenation level, which has direct relevance to cancer diagnosis. This also stimulates the development of multi-channel detector systems and machine learning algorithms for processing multiple scatterings.

Future development

Further development of QE-PET involves building full total-body systems with high sensitivity, such as the planned TB J-PET with an axial field of view of 2.43 m. Theoretical models of multiple Compton scattering (3-Compton) will be integrated into Geant4 simulations. Work is underway to adapt iterative reconstruction methods for simultaneous recovery of activity distribution and CQE parameter. Three-photon entanglement from ortho-positronium decay is also being investigated, potentially leading to new imaging types.

Impact

QE-PET technology will impact nuclear medicine by enabling multiparametric imaging within a single scan, as well as quantum informatics by providing a platform for studying decoherence in the high-energy regime. Moreover, the development of hybrid Compton-PET systems will contribute to the advancement of radiotheragnostics.

Next steps

Next steps include clinical validation of CQE as a biomarker on large patient cohorts, improving the timing and energy resolution of detectors to levels < 100 ps and < 1%, and developing standardized imaging and reconstruction protocols for QE-PET.

Key open problems

QE-PET touches upon the fundamental problem of quantum decoherence in macroscopic systems and the question of the limits of quantum mechanics at the mesoscale. Measuring the degree of entanglement of annihilation photons may shed light on the nature of wave function collapse and testing Bell inequalities in a new energy range.

🎯 Historically, the first experimental confirmation of polarization correlation of annihilation photons was obtained by Wu and Shaknov in 1950, but only in 2025 did the J-PET group discover that entanglement is not maximal and depends on the material. And the survival of entanglement after scattering, contrary to expectations, came as a surprise, explained only in 2024.

🎬 The idea of quantum entanglement as 'spooky action at a distance' is depicted in many science fiction works, such as 'The Quantum Thief' by Hannu Rajaniemi, where entanglement is used for instantaneous communication. QE-PET, by detecting real entangled photon pairs within a patient's body, resembles a medical 'quantum communicator'.

  • R ratio for separable photons: 1.63
  • Scattering angle for maximum correlation: 81.7°
  • Threshold scattering angle in tissue for entanglement preservation: 36°
  • QE-PET sensitivity for fourfold coincidences (TB J-PET): ~3 cps/kBq
