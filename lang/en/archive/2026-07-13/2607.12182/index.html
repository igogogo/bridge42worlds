The Quantum Waltz of Annihilation: What Photons Reveal About Tissues ✦

Original: "Roadmap Towards Quantum Entanglement Positron Emission Tomography (QE-PET)"
· Paweł Moskal, Shiva Abbaszadeh, Harmanjeet Singh Bilkhu, Peter Caradonna, Pragya Das, Praveen Gurunath Bharathi, Ana Marija Kožuljević, Zdenka Kuncic, Deepak Kumar, Mihael Makek, Marek Nowakowski, Siddharth Parashari, Kaustav Prasad, Gregory R. Romanchek, Sushil Sharma, Kenji Shimazoe, Ewa Stępień, Sodai Takyu, Miwako Takahashi, Mizuki Uenomachi, Ritesh Verma, Taiga Yamaya
arXiv:2607.12182 · 2026-07-13 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min
Medical Physics Quantum Physics
By measuring the polarization entanglement of annihilation photons, scientists turn a PET scanner into a tool capable of distinguishing tissue types and oxygen levels.
Abstract

Photons born from positron-electron annihilation are quantum-entangled in polarization: measuring one instantly determines the state of the other. Today’s PET scanners ignore this link, but a fresh concept—quantum-entangled PET—aims to exploit it by measuring Compton scattering. This promises a triple breakthrough: cleaning up images by rejecting false signals, using entanglement strength as a biomarker for disease, and creating a pH-mapping method. Surprisingly, the decay of entanglement (decoherence) can itself reveal secrets about the tissue environment, much like a fading echo tells you about a room’s acoustics.

1

Each positron annihilation is like the birth of a pair of dancers, connected by an invisible thread of polarization. Two gamma quanta fly apart, but their states remain entangled: measuring one instantly determines the other — as if one partner senses every movement of the other, no matter the distance. Einstein himself called it 'spooky action at a distance,' and today it opens a window into diagnostics.

Standard PET simply registers coincidence — two photons, therefore an annihilation. QE-PET, however, listens in on their polarization duet. The photons pass through the body, sometimes experiencing Compton scattering — as if a dancer brushes against a branch. But here's the surprise: if the scattering angle is less than 36°, decoherence does not occur, and the pair continues to move in sync. This effect, confirmed in recent measurements, is what makes the technology feasible.

Entanglement is not destroyed by small-angle scattering — much like a light touch doesn't throw an experienced dancer off the beat, allowing them to finish the figure.

To decode the message, they use a polarimetric method. Scintillation detectors register the azimuthal scattering angles of each photon, and then a function is constructed: F(φ) = F₀[1 – CQE·A(θ₁)A(θ₂)cos(2φ)]. Here, CQE is the degree of quantum entanglement, and the modulation factors A(θ) are known from Compton theory. The depth of modulation directly indicates the strength of correlation: for a fully entangled state, the cosine is maximally pronounced; for a separable state, it fades. Experiments from 2023–2025 at the J-PET facilities showed something surprising: CQE differs in different materials. For fat tissue, CQE ≈ 0.89; for water, 0.82; and a change in oxygen partial pressure shifts this value by fractions of a percent — enough to become a biomarker for hypoxia.

At a scattering angle of 81.7°, the measured asymmetry factor R was 2.00 ± 0.03, whereas for maximal entanglement one expects 2.84, and for separable photons 1.63. This proves: annihilation in matter does not produce perfectly entangled pairs.

It seems the environment where the positron annihilates leaves its imprint on the quantum connection — like the dance pattern depends on the floor surface. This yields not just an anatomical image, but a spectral-polarization portrait of the tissue, where QE-PET combines polarimetry with Compton scattering spectroscopy. The TB J-PET project — a total-body scanner with a 2.43-meter axial field — will be able to reconstruct a three-dimensional map of entanglement distribution throughout the body. Such multiparameter imaging will allow simultaneous visualization of tumors, oxygenation levels, and perhaps therapy efficacy. This is a step toward turning superposition and quantum measurement into routine clinical tools. Physicists will gain a playground for studying decoherence in a high-energy regime, while doctors get a way to peer into the world of antimatter inside us.

🎯 The polarization correlation of annihilation photons was first observed by Wu and Shaknov in 1950. But it wasn't until 2025 that the J-PET team discovered that entanglement is not maximal and depends on the material, and survival after scattering was only explained in 2024. It took 75 years to discern the nuances of this quantum dance.

🎬 Quantum entanglement as a communication channel appears in Hannu Rajaniemi's novel 'The Quantum Thief.' QE-PET, capturing entangled pairs inside the body, resembles a medical quantum communicator deciphering the language of molecular processes.
