Searching for Biosignatures on Transiting Habitable Planets with ELT/ANDES ✦

Original: "Biosignature detectability on transiting habitable worlds with ELT/ANDES"
· E. Kurzawa-Ferrandez, A. Bello-Arufe, R. Hu
arXiv:2607.12017 · 2026-07-13 · CC BY · 3 min
Exoplanets Instrumentation
Simulations show that water vapor and carbon dioxide are the easiest molecules to detect in the atmospheres of Earth-like planets around red dwarfs.
Abstract

A simulation pipeline is presented for evaluating the detectability of CO₂, H₂O, O₂, and CH₄ via high-resolution transmission spectroscopy with the future ANDES spectrograph on the ELT. The modeling was performed for cloud-free Earth-like atmospheres with noise characteristics calculated for the seeing-limited regime using the ANDES exposure time calculator. A new Bayesian cross-correlation function (CCF) with molecule-specific kernels and an autoregressive model for accounting correlations was developed. 18 known transiting exoplanets in the potentially habitable zone were analyzed; the number of transits required for confident detection (log Bayesian factor ≥2) was determined. Water vapor is the easiest target: for TRAPPIST-1 planets, 10–19 transits are needed, for LHS 1140 b — 30. For CO₂, CH₄, and O₂, approximately 1.5, 3, and 4 times more are needed, respectively. These estimates serve as a lower limit assuming ideal observing conditions, absence of systematic errors, and perfect trend correction.

Context

Searching for biosignatures like oxygen and methane in the atmospheres of terrestrial exoplanets is one of modern astrophysics' grand quests. With the deployment of the 39-meter ELT and its high-resolution ANDES spectrograph (R~100,000), we now have a window into the atmospheres of potentially habitable worlds around red dwarfs. Transit method observations combined with high-resolution spectroscopy let us pick out faint molecular lines from the stellar glare, using the Doppler effect.

Methods

We ran numerical simulations of transmission spectra for 18 rocky exoplanets from the Habitable Worlds Catalog. For each, we built cloud-free, Earth-like atmospheres with a two-layer temperature profile and prescribed amounts of H2O, CO2, CH4, and O2. Equilibrium temperature was estimated from the Stefan–Boltzmann law under a blackbody approximation. Using the radiative transfer code petitRADTRANS, we produced high-resolution synthetic spectra and injected realistic noise based on the ANDES exposure time calculator. To assess significance, we performed a Bayesian analysis of the cross-correlation function with an AR(1) autoregressive model that accounts for correlations between adjacent Doppler shifts.

Results

Our analysis shows that water vapor is the easiest to detect: on TRAPPIST-1 planets, it can be firmly spotted in 10–19 transits, and on LHS 1140 b in 30. Carbon dioxide needs about 1.5 times more transits; methane, 3 times; and oxygen, 4 times compared to water. Out of the 18 planets studied, confident (log B ≥ 2) detection of at least one molecule in under 100 transits is possible for eight, with TRAPPIST-1 d leading the pack—all four gases can be detected there in fewer than 36 transits. Targets like TOI-700 d and LP 890-9 c would require hundreds of transits, making them practically out of reach for sensible observing programs.

Implications

These results paint a realistic, albeit optimistic, picture of hunting for biosignatures with next-generation ground-based telescopes. They highlight that even under ideal conditions, detecting oxygen remains a monumental challenge requiring years-long campaigns. Meanwhile, lighter molecules like water and carbon dioxide could be spotted within reasonable timeframes, opening the door to statistical studies of climate and potential habitability on planets around red dwarfs.

Future development

Advances in suppressing telluric contamination and stellar activity, along with adaptive optics and coronagraphy (HDC mode), will let us expand the target sample to include nearby non-transiting planets. Moreover, future space missions like the Habitable Worlds Observatory could complement ground-based efforts, offering unprecedented sensitivity in the search for biosignatures.

Impact

This study will influence the planning of ELT observing programs and the development of high-resolution spectroscopy methods and Bayesian analysis of astronomical data. The findings are pivotal for astrobiology and our grasp of life's prevalence in the universe.

Next steps

Detailed modeling is needed to assess how clouds, aerosols, and stellar activity affect molecular detectability, along with validation of telluric-line removal techniques on real data from existing high-resolution spectrographs.

Key open problems

This work directly tackles the fundamental challenge of searching for extraterrestrial life and interpreting biosignatures, including the issue of false positives (e.g., abiotic oxygen or methane). It also delves into the atmospheric evolution of Earth-like planets under the intense activity of red dwarfs.
