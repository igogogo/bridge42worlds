Molecular Melodies: How ELT/ANDES Will Tune Into the Breath of Exoplanets ✦

Original: "Biosignature detectability on transiting habitable worlds with ELT/ANDES"
· E. Kurzawa-Ferrandez, A. Bello-Arufe, R. Hu
arXiv:2607.12017 · 2026-07-13 · CC BY · 2 min
Exoplanets Instrumentation
Water and carbon dioxide turned out to be the easiest prey for ANDES, while oxygen hides four times deeper—that's the lay of the land in the hunt for exoplanet breath.
Abstract

Scientists have modeled whether the future ANDES spectrograph on the Extremely Large Telescope (ELT) can find water, carbon dioxide, oxygen, and methane in the atmospheres of Earth-like planets around other stars. Using a new analysis method, they estimated how many transit observations are needed. Water vapor is easiest — from 10 to 30 transits for the nearest candidates. Oxygen and methane, possible signs of life, require 3–4 times more, meaning years of observations even under ideal conditions. It's like listening for a faint signal: you need many sessions to separate the whisper from the noise.

1

The light of a distant star carries within it the silent hum of the atmospheres of planets orbiting it. For a long time, capturing it was like trying to make out a whisper over a roaring waterfall. But with the advent of the 39-meter Extremely Large Telescope (ELT) and the ANDES spectrograph with a resolution of 100,000, this task is no longer hopeless. Now each planet is like a string in a vast cosmic orchestra. The molecules in its gaseous envelope leave a unique spectral fingerprint—a musical staff on which water, carbon dioxide, methane, and oxygen are written in the finest absorption lines. Astronomers are learning to tune into these strings, silencing the deafening solo of the parent star.

The ANDES spectrograph can resolve lines separated by just 0.03 nanometers—it's like noticing a new violin appear in an orchestra of a thousand instruments.

The method of high-resolution spectroscopy combined with the transit method resembles the work of a conductor: it picks out the oboe part from the symphonic chaos. Researchers built synthetic atmospheres for 18 selected rocky worlds, modeled their transmission spectra, and applied Bayesian cross-correlation analysis. This mathematical trick compares the real signal with a template—a synthetic 'portrait' of the molecule, shifted to every possible orbital velocity. The result of the numerical experiment was soberingly clear: water vapor is detected fastest—in just a dozen transits for the TRAPPIST-1 planets. Oxygen, however, is the most capricious target. It eludes us like a mirage and requires four times the signal accumulation.

The best target is TRAPPIST-1 d: all four gases are detected in fewer than 36 transits. If the planet were a note, its atmosphere would sound as a pure chord after just a year of observations.

By capturing water and carbon dioxide, we obtain not just a point on a diagram but a climatic passport of the world. Next comes methane, a possible hint of biological activity. And only then, accumulating transits over years, comes oxygen, the great iconoclast of astrobiology: its abundance in the atmosphere is hard to explain without life. But we shouldn't forget—even this mighty note can sound off-key: abiotic processes like the photodissociation of water can create an oxygen illusion. Therefore, every chord requires an ensemble check. This sequence dictates the strategy for hunting biosignatures: confirmed lower limits on the number of observations turn a dream into a telescope schedule. Looking ahead, improvements in suppressing terrestrial and stellar noise, along with the future space observatory Habitable Worlds Observatory, will expand the sample and allow us to hear the breath of even non-transiting neighbors.

🎯 The TRAPPIST-1 system is a red dwarf the size of Jupiter, with seven planets crammed into orbits that would all fit inside Mercury's orbit; three of them receive about as much stellar heat as our Earth.
