Neural Network Extracts Spin and Orbital Moments from XMCD X-ray Spectra ✦

Original: "Neural-network-based reconstruction of spin and orbital angular momentum from X-ray magnetic circular dichroism spectra"
· Tetsuro Ueno
arXiv:2607.11058 · 2026-07-13 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min
Materials
Using deep learning, physicists have learned to directly recover spin and orbital angular moments from the full line shapes in X-ray spectroscopy.
1

Context

The magnetism of transition metals underpins modern technologies, from magnetic recording to spintronics. X-ray magnetic circular dichroism (XMCD) is a powerful spectroscopy tool that separately determines the spin and orbital contributions to the magnetic moment by using polarized radiation. However, the standard sum-rule analysis only operates on integral intensities and requires careful background handling, introducing systematic errors. The rapid advancement of machine learning methods opens up new possibilities for extracting hidden information from complete spectral lines.

Methods

The authors framed the task as an inverse problem: recover physical quantities from spectral shapes. To build a labeled dataset, XAS and XMCD spectra at the L₂,₃ edges of Fe, Co, and Ni were simulated using the Quanty package, which implements multiconfigurational calculations, with systematic variation of parameters: crystal field splitting (10Dq from 0.8 to 1.6 eV), spin-orbit coupling (scaling factors α₃ₔ and α₂ₚ), and exchange field (Hex up to 0.01 eV). A total of 3240 spectra were generated. A neural network with two hidden layers (256 and 128 neurons) was trained to map intensity vectors to the expectation values ⟨Sz⟩ and ⟨Lz⟩ obtained from ground-state wave functions. Numerical simulation and quantum sensing techniques were used to ensure accuracy.

Results

The trained model showed high accuracy on the test set: the coefficient of determination R² exceeded 0.99, and the root-mean-square error was below 0.02 ℏ for both moments. As revealed by the dependencies, variations in 10Dq shift spectral peaks, while Hex modulates the dichroism amplitude. The network learned to separate these contributions, effectively solving the quantum-mechanical inverse problem without relying on integral rules. Notably, the accuracy for ⟨Lz⟩ was systematically slightly higher—likely because the orbital moment has a more pronounced effect on the fine spectral structure through electromagnetic transitions.

Implications

The proposed approach does not replace sum rules but complements them by enabling information extraction from the full line profile. This is especially valuable for weak signals (e.g., in dilute systems) and in automated experiments where manual analysis becomes a bottleneck. The work bridges theoretical calculations and real measurements, paving the way for reliable determination of local magnetic moments.

Future development

The method can be extended to other 3d metals and light elements, and to experimental data accounting for instrumental broadening and noise. In the future, a universal tool for real-time XMCD spectrum analysis integrated with synchrotron radiation sources could be developed.

Impact

The work will impact condensed matter physics, spintronics, and materials science by accelerating the characterization of magnetic materials.

Next steps

Next steps include testing robustness against noise and applying to experimental spectra, as well as generalizing to non-collinear magnetic structures.

Key open problems

The research is directly connected to the long-standing problem of accurately determining spin and orbital moments from XMCD data, where traditional sum rules face limitations due to the need to account for the magnetic dipole term and background ambiguities.
