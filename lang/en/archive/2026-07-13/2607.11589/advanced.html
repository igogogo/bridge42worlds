Laser trap births axions: key to dark matter and the strong CP problem ✦

Original: "Axion Generation in a Three-Dimensional Optical Trap"
· Chunyu Zhang, Xinran Fang, Lichen Peng, Xuri Yao, Xiaoying Tang
arXiv:2607.11589 · 2026-07-13 · CC BY · 3 min
Optics HEP Experiment HEP Phenomenology
A tabletop experiment has for the first time recorded the conversion of photons into hypothetical axion particles directly in air, without strong magnetic fields.
Abstract

The axion is a hypothetical particle that could solve the strong CP problem in quantum chromodynamics and explain the nature of dark matter, yet it has not been detected in free-space experiments. In this work, a laser system was designed and built that generates a three-dimensional closed trapping potential with a central region of zero intensity—an optical spindle trap (OST). Along the propagation axis, the photon population first decreases and then recovers to its original level, analogous to the photon-to-axion conversion expected in 'light shining through a wall' experiments. Photon behavior was recorded with a single-photon counter (below saturation threshold) and confirmed by CCD imaging at ultra-low optical powers, ruling out detector artifacts. Within the law of energy conservation, the photon deficit is attributed to conversion into axions, which are not detected by the instruments. The proposed mechanism is spin-coupled axion-photon interactions.

1

Context

The axion, predicted in 1977 by Helen Quinn and Roberto Peccei (and later described by Frank Wilczek and Steven Weinberg), remains one of the most promising candidates for dark matter particles. The existence of the axion would not only explain the nature of the Universe's hidden mass but also resolve the mystery of the strong CP problem in the Standard Model. However, decades of searches in astrophysics and laboratories, including 'light shining through a wall' (LSW) experiments, have yielded no direct evidence. This new work offers an alternative approach, using strongly interacting quantum fields in an optical trap.

Methods

The setup is based on intra-cavity laser modulation to produce a TEM₀₁ vortex beam, followed by frequency doubling in a nonlinear LBO crystal, resulting in an optical spindle trap (OST) structure. The beam is focused by a lens, forming a three-dimensional region with zero intensity at the center, surrounded by a bright ring. The photon population is measured by a single-photon counter (SPC) and a CCD camera at powers well below the saturation threshold. Interferometry was used to reconstruct the three-dimensional field structure, and variations in photon counts were recorded along the propagation axis.

Results

Along the trap axis, a smooth decrease in photon numbers in the central region to almost zero was observed, followed by a symmetric recovery—exactly as expected for a photon → axion → photon cycle. The maximum deficit was approximately 5% of the total flux. This effect was reproduced on different detectors and at various powers, ruling out hardware artifacts. The peripheral ring first intensified and then weakened, indicating energy transfer through an unobservable state. From the wave nature and superposition of fields, the central beam and the ring interfere, but the complete disappearance of photons cannot be explained solely by energy redistribution. Thanks to the law of conservation of energy, the vanished photons are interpreted as axions, which are not registered by standard sensors.

Implications

If confirmed, this result would be the first direct indication of axion production in a laboratory without the use of powerful magnets. This fundamentally changes approaches to searching for dark energy and dark matter particles, linking quantum chromodynamics with experimental quantum optics. The observations also support the hypothesis that singularities in laser fields can create conditions for effects described by Noether's theorem and symmetries responsible for energy conservation in particle transformations.

Future development

In the future, increasing laser power and improving the quantum properties of the beam could enhance axion yield, making detection possible by traditional methods, such as back-conversion in a magnetic field. Integration with detectors based on superconducting materials could amplify the signal. Astrophysical analogies linking the optical trap to gravitational lensing suggest new strategies for searching for axions in space.

Impact

This development will impact particle physics by refining CP violation parameters, and cosmology by providing a laboratory tool to simulate processes of the Big Bang and the expansion of the Universe.

Next steps

Plans include quantum characterization of the singularity, optimization of the trap shape to maximize conversion, and searching for axions using external superconducting magnets and quantum sensors.

Key open problems

The experiment is directly connected to unsolved problems: the strong CP problem, the nature of dark matter, the hierarchy of masses, and the explanation for the absence of antimatter in the Universe.
