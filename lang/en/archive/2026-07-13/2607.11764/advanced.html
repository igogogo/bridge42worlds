Weighing the Graviton: Stellar Orbits vs. Quantum Gravity Mass ✦

Original: "A way to constrain a graviton mass from astronomical observations"
· Alexander F. Zakharov, Predrag Jovanovic, Dusko Borka, Vesna Borka Jovanovic
arXiv:2607.11764 · 2026-07-13 · CC0 · 4 min · General Relativity Cosmology Galaxies
Analysis of bright stars' motion near the supermassive black hole at the Galactic center has placed new constraints on the mass of the graviton, a key candidate in quantum gravity theory.
Abstract

Theorists have developed versions of massive gravity free from the flaws of earlier models. Astrophysicists discuss constraints on the graviton mass from astronomical data. From analyzing the shape of the gravitational-wave signal from binary black hole mergers detected by LIGO, an upper limit has been set. Constraints also come from tracking the motion of the bright star S2 near the Milky Way’s center using data from GRAVITY and Keck. Other astronomical methods for constraining it are briefly described. These observations impose ever tighter limits on gravity theories.

Context

Understanding gravity's nature remains a central task of physics. The classical general relativity of Einstein has triumphed in every test, yet provides no answer to the puzzles of dark matter and dark energy. Moreover, it fundamentally resists quantization, creating a rift with the rest of physics. Massive gravity theories, where the field quantum—the graviton—has a non-zero mass, offer an elegant bypass by modifying gravity's law on large scales. The most sensitive tests of such theories are possible in extreme conditions—near the event horizons of supermassive black holes or when analyzing gravitational waves.

Methods

Two complementary approaches were used to constrain the graviton mass, based on archival astrometric measurements of guide stars S2 and S38 in the Galactic center, obtained with Keck telescopes and the VLTI/GRAVITY interferometer. In the first method, a Yukawa addition exp(-r/λ) was introduced into the Newtonian potential, where λ is the graviton's Compton wavelength (according to the law of universal gravitation in the weak-field limit). Taking into account Kepler's third law, which links the period and semi-major axis for nearly Keplerian orbits, the orbital precession, parameterized by the f_SP factor from GRAVITY collaboration data, was compared with a modified expression that includes contributions from the Yukawa potential and GR corrections at the first post-Newtonian order. In the second, numerical modeling of orbits was performed by minimizing χ² over all orbital parameters, including the central black hole mass, distance, and eccentricity, using an evolutionary algorithm.

Results

Both methods yielded consistent upper limits on the graviton mass: for star S2, mg < (124.9 ± 120.2) × 10⁻²⁴ eV/c² from the precession analysis and mg ≈ (142.9 ± 24.1) × 10⁻²⁴ eV/c² from direct fitting; for S38, mg < (76.9 ± 73.3) × 10⁻²⁴ eV/c². These values are roughly an order of magnitude weaker than the best constraints from gravitational-wave events by the LIGO-Virgo collaboration (mg < 2.4 × 10⁻²³ eV/c²), but they are obtained by a completely independent method in the strong-field regime. This complements earlier observations of gravitational redshift in the spectrum of S2, which confirmed GR. Remarkably, the graviton's Compton wavelength for the best fit to S2 reaches 5.8 × 10⁴ AU, confirming the colossal sensitivity of astrometry. The deviation from Schwarzschild precession (first described by Karl Schwarzschild) is proportional to the square of the ratio of the semi-major axis to λ, making the method particularly effective for stars with large orbits. Interestingly, the Yukawa correction always increases precession, so constraints only arise when an excess pericenter shift relative to GR is observed.

Implications

The results reinforce the status of stellar dynamics near supermassive black holes as a precision laboratory for testing fundamental physics. They leave an imprint on the interpretation of hidden mass—if the graviton has mass, the need for some dark matter vanishes. Agreement with LIGO limits underscores the complementarity of electromagnetic and gravitational-wave astronomy. Moreover, lower bounds on the gravity interaction length rule out a whole class of theories with small Compton wavelengths, such as some f(R) variants, and support dRGT-type models where the graviton could be ultralight.

Future development

Key improvements are expected from the GRAVITY+ interferometer, whose recent activation promises to boost astrometric precision to tens of microarcseconds. This will allow more stars, including tight orbits, to be incorporated into the analysis, possibly uncovering systematic deviations hinting at new physics. In parallel, theory must advance in accounting for nonlinear Vainshtein screening effects that could mask massive graviton signatures in local tests. Merging data with future gravitational-wave catalogs (ET, LISA) will create a multichannel test.

Impact

The methodology will directly influence data analysis from next-generation missions like THEIA and MICADO@ELT, aimed at mapping the Galactic center. It also stimulates dialogue between experts in black hole mergers and stellar dynamics, uniting efforts in the search for GR deviations.

Next steps

In the near term—refinement of the f_SP factor using new GRAVITY Collaboration data (2024 update) for four S-stars simultaneously. Combining with planetary ephemerides, where record constraints have already been achieved, also looks promising.

Key open problems

The question of the graviton mass is inextricably linked to dark energy—perhaps the cosmological constant is a direct consequence of the gravitational field's massiveness. Moreover, it is key to the problem of quantizing gravity: a non-zero mass would make the theory renormalizable, bringing us closer to a unified picture. And, of course, it is a central element in solving the dark matter mystery, first formulated by Fritz Zwicky in the 1930s.
