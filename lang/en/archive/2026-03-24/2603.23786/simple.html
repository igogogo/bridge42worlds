The Vera C. Rubin telescope has released a trial dataset — millions of objects and dozens of new asteroids.
Abstract

The first test dataset from the new Vera Rubin telescope is like a trial run of a machine that will soon start mapping the entire cosmos. It contains 2.3 million objects, including 93 new asteroids. This is just a drop in the ocean of future discoveries, but it already demonstrates the instrument's power. What will we see when the telescope operates at full capacity?

Links in the knowledge graph 1

Astronomers have tested the new Vera C. Rubin telescope in Chile, much like adjusting the focus and aperture on a brand-new camera. The first images covered a patch of sky the size of 60 full Moons—and immediately revealed millions of galaxies and stars. The imaging was done in various colors, from invisible ultraviolet to thermal infrared. This technique—photometry—lets you determine the composition of distant galaxies by their color, just as you can tell what a metal is by its hue. Among the stationary stars, the camera caught moving specks—93 unknown asteroids. But the real discovery lies ahead. Starting in 2026, the telescope will survey the entire sky every three nights, turning a static picture into a movie. Then we will see supernovae ignite and how invisible dark matter, like an invisible hand, bends the light.

🎯 The total volume of data from this test run is 3.5 terabytes, roughly the equivalent of 700 full-length HD movies.

🎬 In the movie 'Armageddon,' an asteroid is spotted too late. Surveys like this will help detect dangerous objects well in advance.
