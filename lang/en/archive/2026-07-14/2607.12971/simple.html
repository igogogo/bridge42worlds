Light and the Gravitational Whisper of Binary Stars ✦

Original: "A new model for the continuum spectra of AM CVn binaries and multi-messenger inference with normalizing flows"
· Nathan Steinle, Samar Safi-Harb, Austin MacMaster, Liliana Rivera Sandoval, Katelyn Breivik, Tyrone E. Woods, Tom Maccarone, Tom Wagg
arXiv:2607.12971 · 2026-07-14 · CC BY · 1 min · High Energy General Relativity
Scientists built a model that uses light and gravitational waves to reveal the structure of tight white dwarf pairs.
Abstract

Two stars whirl so tightly that one pulls matter from the other, as if into a cosmic whirlpool. Future telescopes and gravitational wave detectors will simultaneously see the light and 'hear' the space tremors from such pairs. The model predicts: only one in 7,000 systems will be noticeable to both instruments. What secrets will these cosmic 'fireflies' unveil?

In weightlessness, a scorching-hot skillet and a pat of butter whirl around. This is a massive white dwarf (a star whose maximum mass was calculated by Chandrasekhar) and its lightweight companion, rich in helium. Gravity makes the butter drip onto the skillet, and along the way it forms a glowing ring — an accretion disk. This disk is heated to millions of degrees and shines brightly in X-rays and ultraviolet. By splitting the light into colors (spectroscopy), astronomers learn the temperature and composition. The pair's rapid rotation generates gravitational waves — ripples in space itself.

Only one in seven thousand such pairs will fall into the sights of three future telescopes simultaneously — a true cosmic lottery.

Scientists built a detailed numerical model based on the work of Kepler and Planck. It calculates the binary system's emission, accounting for stellar aging (evolution), chemical composition (metallicity), and cosmic dust in the interstellar medium. To quickly decode signals, they used machine learning — a neural network trained on millions of examples. Now it accurately predicts masses and orbits even from incomplete data.

🎯 The prototype of the AM CVn class, the star AM Canum Venaticorum, rotates with a period of just about 17 minutes, and its lightweight companion is almost entirely made of helium.
