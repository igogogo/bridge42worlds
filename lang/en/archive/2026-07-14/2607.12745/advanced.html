Genetic algorithms test the Hu–Sawicki f(R) gravity model against DESI and Pantheon+ data ✦

Original: "Exploring Hu-Sawicki-like modified gravity with Genetic Algorithms"
· Chiara De Leo, Elisa Fazzari, Matteo Martinelli, Savvas Nesseris
arXiv:2607.12745 · 2026-07-14 · CC BY · 3 min
Cosmology General Relativity
Current cosmological data allow only minimal deviations from ΛCDM within f(R) models reconstructed by genetic algorithms.
Abstract

Using genetic algorithms, the possibility of deviations from ΛCDM was explored within viable Hu-Sawicki type f(R) models. A pipeline was developed that combines the GA code GATO with the cosmological code CANDI: each analytic f(R) function was tested for stability, recovery of the matter-dominated era, general relativistic limit, and the 'chameleon' screening mechanism. The ansätze that met the conditions were then compared with DESI DR2 data on baryon acoustic oscillations and the Pantheon+ catalog of type Ia supernovae. The largest deviations were found at late times when curvature is small, and they quickly fade with increasing redshift. The Om(z) diagnostic shows only a slight departure from the constant behavior of ΛCDM, and the effective equation of state of dark energy weakly evolves from a quintessence-like to a phantom-like phase. Overall, current background data allow only limited deviations from the standard ΛCDM model within the class studied.

1

Context

Recent data from supernovae and baryon acoustic oscillations from the DESI collaboration hint at a possible evolution of dark energy, casting doubt on the standard ΛCDM model. Testing alternative theories of gravity, such as f(R) models, becomes critically important. These theories generalize Einstein's general relativity, and their cosmological solutions are considered in the Friedmann–Lemaître metric. In them, the accelerated expansion of the Universe is explained not by a cosmological constant but by a modification of spacetime geometry, which effectively describes dynamic dark energy. However, many such models face stability problems and fail local gravity tests.

Methods

To find viable F(R) functions, the study used machine learning—genetic algorithms that, through symbolic regression, reconstruct analytic perturbations around the Hu–Sawicki model. The survival of functions was assessed by their ability to satisfy theoretical stability conditions (including absence of tachyonic instabilities and the chameleon screening mechanism) while also fitting supernova data from Pantheon+ and BAO from DESI DR2 at various redshifts. For each candidate, a system of modified Friedmann equations, derived from the Einstein equations for the f(R) metric, was solved numerically, and the cosmological evolution was found via numerical simulation.

Results

The best reconstructed F(R) function shows only negligible deviations from ΛCDM: the amplitude of variation in the effective equation of state of dark energy w_eff is on the order of 10⁻⁷, and the Om(z) diagnostic deviates from a constant by no more than 0.1% at low redshifts. The model confidently recovers the standard gravity limit at high energies, and chameleon screening suppresses the fifth force in dense environments. A slight decrease in χ² compared to ΛCDM does not allow statistically significant superiority of modified gravity to be claimed.

Implications

The results mean that current observations of the expansion of the Universe impose tight constraints on modifications of gravity: the allowed class of f(R) models must practically coincide with ΛCDM at all redshifts except the very smallest. This aligns with the chameleon mechanism, which effectively hides deviations from general relativity in laboratory and astrophysical systems. Thus, if dynamic dark energy exists, its manifestation in background data is minimal.

Future development

Future studies should include perturbation analyses (growth rate of structures, weak lensing) where gravity modifications manifest more prominently. Expanding the machine search to more general functional forms of f(R), as well as using data from upcoming surveys (Euclid, Roman), will either detect deviations or push the model even closer to ΛCDM.

Impact

The work impacts cosmology and gravity theories, encouraging a shift from phenomenological parameterizations of dark energy to physically motivated models, and demonstrates the power of machine learning in solving inverse problems of fundamental physics.

Next steps

Direct comparison of reconstructed f(R) functions with the CPL parameterization under a unified statistical analysis, and inclusion of cosmic microwave background data for tighter constraints.

Key open problems

The paper addresses one of the key problems in physics—the nature of dark energy and the cosmological constant problem—proposing a method to search for microscopic deviations from ΛCDM within viable modifications of gravity.

🎯 Genetic algorithms work like biological evolution: random mutations and crossovers generate generations of functions, and only those best adapted to the data and physical constraints survive. In this study, the surviving functions turned out to be almost indistinguishable from the cosmological constant.

F(R) = -\frac{2\Lambda}{1+\left(\frac{b\Lambda}{R}\right)^n}
Parameterization of deviation from GR; as b→0 reproduces ΛCDM.
w_{\rm eff} = -1 - \frac{1}{3}\frac{y_H'}{y_H}
Diagnostic of expansion dynamics; w_eff=-1 corresponds to cosmological constant.
Om(z) = \frac{[H(z)/H_0]^2 - 1}{(1+z)^3 - 1}
In ΛCDM Om(z)=Ω_m is constant; evolution would indicate modified gravity or dynamic dark energy.
