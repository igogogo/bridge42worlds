Fractional Holographic Dark Energy: How Nonlocality Drives the Universe’s Acceleration ✦

Original: "An Interplay Between Fractional Calculus and Holographic Dark Energy"
· Ayush Bidlan
arXiv:2606.22431 · 2026-06-21 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min
General Relativity Cosmology HEP Theory
A new dark energy model combines the holographic principle with fractional calculus, explaining accelerated expansion without a cosmological constant.
1

Context

A key problem in modern cosmology is the nature of the accelerated expansion of the Universe, discovered through observations of type Ia supernovae. The ΛCDM model with a cosmological constant faces fine-tuning and coincidence problems, motivating the search for dynamic alternatives. The holographic principle, linking information in a volume to its boundary, offers an elegant path: dark energy could be determined by an infrared scale. However, the standard holographic model with the Hubble cutoff L = H⁻¹ does not produce acceleration. This work introduces fractional quantization of black holes through quantum geometrodynamics, modifying the entropy and, consequently, the dark energy density. This opens the possibility of observable dynamics with minimal assumptions.

Methods

The starting point is the fractional Wheeler–DeWitt equation for a Schwarzschild black hole, where the spatial derivative is replaced by a Riesz fractional derivative of order α (1 < α ≤ 2). This generalization, motivated by fractional quantum mechanics and Lévy path integrals, leads to a modified Bekenstein–Hawking entropy: S ∝ A^{(2+α)/(2α)}. Then, using Cohen’s inequality, one derives the density of fractional holographic dark energy (FHDE): ρ = 3c² M_Pl² L^{(2-3α)/α}. By choosing the Hubble cutoff L = H⁻¹ and using Friedmann equations for a spatially flat Universe, evolution equations are derived for the density parameter Ω_DE, the equation-of-state parameter w, and the deceleration parameter q. The analysis is supplemented by a reconstruction of FHDE in effective field models (quintessence, k-essence, dilaton, DBI, tachyon, Yang–Mills condensate) and extensions to modified gravity theories (Brans–Dicke, Horndeski, Proca–Nuevo, DGP).

Results

Numerical integration shows that for Lévy index α values close to 1.1, FHDE reproduces the observed picture: dark energy dominates at low redshifts (Ω_DE → 0.69 today), the equation-of-state parameter w stays in the quintessence regime (-1 < w < -1/3) and approaches -1 in the future, and the transition from deceleration to acceleration occurs at z ≈ 0.38, consistent with cosmic microwave background and supernova data. On the w–w′ plane, the model falls in the “freezing” quintessence region. However, the square of the sound speed becomes negative in the far future, indicating a classical instability. Reconstructions in scalar and gauge fields confirm that smaller α lead to smoother behavior, and string-inspired candidates (dilaton, DBI) favor different regimes. Future singularity analysis within the Granda–Oliveros cutoff reveals the possibility of a Big Rip only with nonlinear interaction of dark sectors, while Little Rip and Pseudo Rip occur with nonlinear interaction in the Hubble cutoff.

Implications

FHDE establishes a direct link between nonlocal effects of quantum gravity and large-scale cosmic dynamics. Unlike many phenomenological models, here the modification has a microscopic grounding through fractional quantum mechanics and the Bekenstein–Hawking relation. This makes the α parameter a potentially measurable indicator of fundamental physics beyond standard cosmology. The success of the Hubble cutoff, impossible in standard HDE, highlights that nonlocal memory can naturally generate acceleration. Moreover, the model offers a way to resolve Hubble tension and the cosmological constant problem.

Future development

Near-term prospects include detailed comparison with data from DESI, Euclid, and future surveys, which will allow constraints on α and distinguish FHDE from other entropic models (Tsallis, Barrow). The integrated Sachs–Wolfe effect, growth of structure analysis, and gravitational-wave background could provide additional signatures. On the theoretical front, a quantum-cosmological treatment is important, especially to resolve singularities such as the Big Rip, and extend to the inflationary era.

Impact

The model impacts late-Universe cosmology and fundamental physics, offering a bridge between fractional calculus, information theory, and observational cosmology.

Next steps

Construct joint constraints on α using DESI DR2, Pantheon+, and Planck data via Markov chain Monte Carlo; investigate the stability of fractal structures in the nonlinear regime.

Key open problems

FHDE directly addresses the cosmological constant problem, softening the 120-order discrepancy, and offers a dynamic explanation for Hubble tension via α-dependence. The nonlocal nature opens a window into quantum gravity and holographic duality, potentially linking cosmology to AdS/CFT.

S \propto A^{\frac{2+\alpha}{2\alpha}}
Horizon area A is raised to a power depending on the Lévy index α; at α=2, the standard relation S ∝ A is recovered.
\rho_{\mathrm{DE}} = 3c^2 H^{\frac{3\alpha-2}{\alpha}}
The dark energy density is expressed through the Hubble parameter H and the parameter α. In the standard limit α→2, one obtains a constant density.
w_{\mathrm{DE}} = -1 + \frac{(3\alpha-2)(1-\Omega_{\mathrm{DE}})}{2\alpha - \Omega_{\mathrm{DE}}(3\alpha-2)}
w depends on the dark energy fraction Ω_DE and α; a negative value for α<2 provides acceleration.
