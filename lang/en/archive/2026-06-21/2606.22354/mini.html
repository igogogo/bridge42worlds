Thunder and Nimbus: Birth of a Bow-Shock Nebula from a Neutron Star ✦

Original: "EMU discovery of Thunder: a bow-shock PWN powered by PSR J1631-4722 escaping Nimbus SNR (G336.7+0.5)"
· S. Lazarević, C. Maitra, A. Ahmad, R. Z. E. Alsaberi, S. Dai, D. Leahy, M. D. Filipović, P. G. Edwards, O. Kargaltsev, M. Abdelmaguid, J. D. Gelfand, J. West, S. Hutschenreuter, R. Brose, R. Kothes, V. Velović, C. Burger-Scheidlin, B. D. Ball, G. Graham, A. M. Hopkins, G. P. Rowell, S. F. Rahman, Z. J. Smeaton, S. Taziaux
arXiv:2606.22354 · 2026-06-21 · CC BY · 1 min · High Energy
Radio telescopes captured the cometary nebula 'Thunder' — the bow-shock trail of a pulsar piercing the supernova remnant 'Nimbus', revealing the final act of neutron stars.
Links in the knowledge graph 1

After the explosion, a neutron star sometimes gets a kick and flies out of its cradle like a cannonball. Plowing into the expanding remnant, it paints a cometary nebula — a lightning bolt in a cloud of hot gas. The magnetic field here is strained to a hundred microgauss and stretched over light-years, as if traced by a giant invisible pen. Such systems are living chronicles of neutron star motion and particle acceleration. Deciphering them, we will learn to predict the mergers that shake spacetime itself.

🎯 In the images, the supernova remnant is a genuine cosmic cloud, and the pulsar’s bow shock piercing it is lightning. Hence 'Nimbus' and 'Thunder': poetry helps us not drown in a sea of faceless coordinates.

Flux density as a function of frequency: the index α speaks to the efficiency of particle acceleration — a flat spectrum (α ≈ –0.27) in the pulsar wind nebula reveals a powerful source of relativistic electrons.
