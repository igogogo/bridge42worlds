Neutrinos from the Fermi Bubbles: IceCube Confirms Cosmic-Ray Acceleration to PeV Energies ✦

Original: "Fermi bubbles detected in $$\sim$$100 TeV neutrinos"
· Uri Keshet, Ilya Gurwich
arXiv:2606.22387 · 2026-06-21 · CC BY · 3 min
High Energy
Analysis of IceCube neutrino observatory data has for the first time detected an excess of high-energy neutrinos from the edges of the Fermi bubbles, indicating proton acceleration to PeV-scale energies in shock waves.
1

Context

The center of our Galaxy occasionally produces powerful bipolar jets of plasma, as evidenced by the giant Fermi and eROSITA bubbles. These structures, observed in X-rays and gamma rays, expand into the interstellar medium, creating shock waves where particles are accelerated to extreme energies via the Fermi acceleration mechanism. However, direct evidence for the acceleration of protons—the main cosmic rays—was missing. Neutrinos, born from collisions of accelerated nuclei with gas, could clarify the picture, but previous searches, including radio astronomy methods, were unsuccessful. The key turned out to be new data from the neutrino telescope IceCube and the active galactic nucleus.

Methods

Researchers compared the HESE event map from the 12-year IceCube dataset with the gamma-ray distribution from the Fermi-LAT telescope and X-ray spectral images from the eROSITA observatory, led by Rashid Sunyaev. To isolate the signal, they used spatial correlation methods and brightness profiles built by stacking along the bubbles' edges. The background was estimated by randomly shuffling the right ascensions of events. The analysis focused on neutrino energies above 100 TeV, where the atmospheric background contribution is minimal.

Results

The correlation of the neutrino map with the gamma-ray map outside the Galactic plane (|b|>30°) reached 3.7σ. In the northern Fermi bubble, an excess of 7 events above 100 TeV (with ~1 expected) was found, corresponding to a global significance of >4σ, and >5σ for track-like events. Analysis of shells localized via X-ray data revealed an excess of >7σ in the northern shell. Modeling of Fermi acceleration in shock waves showed that the energy of cosmic ions in each bubble is about 3×10^{54} erg (with a factor 3 uncertainty), which is ~10% of the total outburst energy. The ratio of electron to ion energy η ~ 7×10^{-4} is consistent with radio and gamma-ray observations.

Implications

The detection of neutrinos from the Fermi bubbles is the first direct evidence of proton acceleration to PeV energies in our Galaxy beyond supernova remnants. This confirms that such large-scale outflows from active galactic centers can be factories of high-energy cosmic rays. The derived acceleration efficiency (around 10%) constrains shock-wave models. Moreover, the predominantly leptonic nature of the bubbles' gamma-ray emission receives independent confirmation, which is important for interpreting observations of other Galactic structures.

Future development

With the accumulation of data from IceCube-Gen2 and other next-generation neutrino telescopes (e.g., KM3NeT), sensitivity will increase, enabling detailed study of the neutrino spectrum from the bubbles. This will help refine the acceleration mechanism and the composition of cosmic rays. It also remains to be determined whether the bubbles are linked to periods of activity of the supermassive black hole Sgr A* or to bursts of star formation.

Impact

The results open a new window into the physics of shock waves and particle acceleration mechanisms. They stimulate the development of multi-messenger astronomy, combining neutrino, radio, and X-ray observations to probe hidden processes in the Milky Way's center.

Next steps

Independent confirmation of the signal by other instruments is needed, for example, by the global network of neutrino telescopes. It is also important to study the contribution of lower-latitude parts of the bubbles and possible time variability.

Key open problems

The discovery is directly connected to the problem of the origin of high-energy Galactic cosmic rays: the Fermi bubbles may be one of the main sources of PeV particles. In addition, it sheds light on the nature of Galactic center activity and the feedback mechanisms between the supermassive black hole and the galaxy.

🎯 Although the Fermi bubbles were discovered in 2010, their exact origin remains debated: some scientists link them to black hole jets, others to a powerful burst of star formation. The neutrino signal for the first time gives a 'taste' of the accelerated particle physics, helping to solve this mystery.

  • neutrino energy: >100 TeV
  • global significance (northern bubble): >4σ
  • significance including tracks: >5σ
  • local excess in the shell: >7σ
  • ion energy in one bubble: ~10^{54.5} erg
