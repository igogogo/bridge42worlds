Dark matter barely interacts with neutrinos ✦

Original: "High resolution Lyman-{\alpha} forest constraints on dark matter-neutrino scattering"
· Markus R. Mosbech, Olga Garcia-Gallego, Vid Iršič, Matteo Viel, Julien Lesgourgues
arXiv:2607.15020 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY · 1 min · Cosmology HEP Phenomenology
Light from distant quasars shows that dark matter and neutrinos interact negligibly.
Abstract

Scientists searched for 'traces' of dark matter interacting with neutrinos by analyzing light from distant quasars, much like reading history from tree rings. It turned out that even if such an interaction exists, it is very weak—the tightest constraint to date. So, is dark matter even more invisible than we thought?

Light from distant quasars—the ultra-bright cores of galaxies—pushes through a ghostly forest of hydrogen clouds. Each atom leaves a dark line in the spectrum, like a personal autograph. By collecting hundreds of these signatures, astronomers reconstruct a map of invisible gas.

Still, it's unclear whether dark matter interacts with the almost intangible neutrinos. The growth of entire galaxies depended on this. To find out, scientists created forty-eight universes inside a computer—numerical simulations—and used neural networks to compare them with actual observations. Redshift—the stretching of light by the expanding Universe—let them peer billions of years into the past.

It turns out the possible interaction is a hundred times weaker than hinted by cosmic microwave background data. Dark matter and neutrinos barely notice each other.

Dark matter remains remarkably lonely. This strengthens our picture of the cosmos, where galaxies evolve just as we observe them. Astonishingly, over the entire age of the Universe, a dark matter particle may never have collided with a neutrino—so indifferent are they to each other.

🎯 The Lyman-alpha forest is no actual forest but hundreds of dark tick marks in a quasar’s spectrum. Each mark is like a hydrogen atom’s personal autograph along the light’s journey.
