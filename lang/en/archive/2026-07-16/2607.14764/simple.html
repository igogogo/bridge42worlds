The Unheard Knock of Cement Reveals Its Nanostructure

Original: "Boson peak and medium-range elastic heterogeneity in calcium silicate hydrate probed by terahertz spectroscopy and low-temperature calorimetry"
· Xiangyu Li, Ying Chen, Jipeng Luo, Ya Chen, Linhao Wang, Lidan Tian, Gan Ding, Zeyu Lu, Zhangli Hu, Biqin Dong, Yue Li, Zongjin Li
arXiv:2607.14764 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY · 2 min
Materials Disordered Systems
Scientists have for the first time registered unique vibrations in cement gel, revealing just how unevenly it’s assembled at the nanoscale.
Abstract

Hardened concrete isn't exactly solid: at the microscale, it resembles a frozen liquid. Such materials have a special 'vibrational signature' — the boson peak. For the first time, scientists have studied it in detail in cement stone and linked it to concrete durability. Imagine: from the barely perceptible trembling of nanometer-sized regions, you can predict how a bridge will withstand decades of loads.

1

Everyone has at some point tapped a watermelon to gauge its ripeness by the sound. Physicists used a similar trick, but instead of fingers, they used flashes of invisible light, and instead of a watermelon, cement gel—the main binding agent in concrete. By directing short electromagnetic pulses and catching the response with ultrasensitive interferometry, they performed a kind of spectroscopy—analysis of how the material interacts with radiation. And they heard a clear 'ring' at a frequency of about one terahertz (a trillion oscillations per second). This signal is called the boson peak, after Satyendra Nath Bose and Albert Einstein, who described the collective behavior of particles. It appears in all disordered solids, from glass to ice cream, and carries information about how motley their insides are at the nanoscale.

The boson peak exists even in window glass and amorphous ice, but each has its own frequency—like different little bells.

Cement gel is not just mud, but a layered material made of chains of silicon and oxygen atoms, interspersed with calcium ions. By changing the calcium-to-silicon ratio (Ca/Si), you can control how stiff individual nanoregions become. To prove this, scientists not only 'listened' to the sample with light but also measured its heat capacity at ultralow temperatures, tracking entropy—a measure of disorder in the system. Where classical physics no longer works, the rules of quantum thermodynamics come into play. The researchers applied a model similar to the law discovered by Max Planck for light, and thereby precisely determined the peak frequency. Both methods independently revealed the same boson peak, confirming predictions of numerical simulations at the molecular level. From the peak shape, they extracted the number γ—a parameter of elastic heterogeneity. The smaller γ is, the more uniform the material, and thus the less it will creep under load over time.

At Ca/Si ≈ 1.0, vibrations decay the least—cement seems to find its most transparent 'acoustic' point.

Why does this matter? The boson peak provides a non-destructive rapid test: you don't need to break a sample to understand how it will behave in years to come. You can quickly assess industrial waste (fly ash, slag) that is now added to low-carbon cements, and select mixes with the best durability. Moreover, the same peak defines concrete's extremely low thermal conductivity—its ability to retain heat. In the future, such a 'nano-knock' will help design warmer homes and longer-lasting bridges, simply by tweaking the recipe in the molecular kitchen.

🎯 The boson peak can be found in window glass, amorphous ice, and even caramel, but each material rings at its own frequency, like a distinct musical note.

🎬 In Andy Weir’s novel The Martian, concrete modules on Mars are battered by extreme temperature swings. Understanding the boson peak would help future engineers choose the ideal cement for those conditions.
