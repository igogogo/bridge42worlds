Boson Peak Reveals Nano-Secrets of Cement Gel

Original: "Boson peak and medium-range elastic heterogeneity in calcium silicate hydrate probed by terahertz spectroscopy and low-temperature calorimetry"
· Xiangyu Li, Ying Chen, Jipeng Luo, Ya Chen, Linhao Wang, Lidan Tian, Gan Ding, Zeyu Lu, Zhangli Hu, Biqin Dong, Yue Li, Zongjin Li
arXiv:2607.14764 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY · 5 min · Materials Disordered Systems
Terahertz spectroscopy and low-temperature calorimetry have for the first time detected the boson peak in concrete's main component, linking it to elastic heterogeneity at the nanoscale.
Abstract

The boson peak (BP), a universal vibrational anomaly of disordered media, has been systematically characterized in calcium silicate hydrate (C-S-H), the binding phase of hardened cement, for five Ca/Si ratios (0.5–1.7). Using time-domain terahertz spectroscopy and low-temperature calorimetry after Bruggeman correction for impurities, the BP is localized near 1 THz. The integrated spectral weight and the peak in Cp/T³ decrease monotonically with increasing Ca/Si, while the terahertz peak amplitude is maximum at Ca/Si = 1.0, reflecting a structural crossover between depolymerization of silicate chains and filling of interlayer space by calcium. From BP analysis, a dynamic correlation length of ~1 nm (0.3–2 nm) and an elastic heterogeneity parameter γ are obtained, decreasing from 0.98 to 0.48. The BP frequency, normalized to the Debye frequency (ν_BP/ν_D = 0.15–0.17), falls within the range of silicate glasses. The parameter γ, governing the distribution of energy barriers for local rearrangements, becomes a quantitative composition-dependent descriptor of intrinsic creep and heat transport in C-S-H, linking nanoscale dynamics to macroscopic concrete durability.

Context

The durability of concrete—the planet's most abundant construction material—is determined by the properties of calcium silicate hydrate (C-S-H), formed during cement hydration. However, C-S-H is an X-ray amorphous substance whose structure is chaotic at nanometer scales, and it is this disorder that controls its mechanical behavior. The crucial 'middle' range (0.5–5 nm) remains inaccessible to ordinary methods: spectroscopy like nuclear magnetic resonance and infrared spectroscopy see only short-range order, while X-ray scattering reveals mesoscale packing. In glass physics, this niche is filled by the boson peak—an excess of low-frequency vibrational states over the predictions of Debye's model, appearing in the 0.1–3 THz range. Named after Satyendra Nath Bose and Albert Einstein, whose work on Bose–Einstein statistics describes collective excitations, this peak carries information about elastic heterogeneity. For C-S-H, its existence was predicted by molecular dynamics, but had not been confirmed experimentally. Detecting the boson peak promises direct access to the distribution of nanoscale stiffness fluctuations, which govern long-term creep and thermal conductivity of the material.

Methods

Five C-S-H samples with Ca/Si ratios from 0.5 to 1.7 were synthesized using silica and calcium oxide. Phase purity was monitored by X-ray diffraction and infrared spectroscopy. For terahertz measurements, powders were mixed with a polymer diluent, and the composition was optimized through orthogonal design to broaden the transmission band. Absorption and dielectric loss spectra were recorded by terahertz time-domain spectroscopy (THz-TDS) with interferometric detection, allowing simultaneous measurement of amplitude and phase of electromagnetic radiation in the 0.1–5 THz range. Impurities of crystalline Ca(OH)₂ and CaCO₃ were removed using Bruggeman's effective medium theory. Heat capacity was measured in a relaxation-method calorimeter from 2 to 300 K, analyzing the quantity Cp/T³, which should be constant for a Debye crystal; any hump indicates a boson peak and is tied to the system's entropy. The boson peak in THz spectra was isolated by fitting a damped harmonic oscillator model, and the correlation length was estimated from ξ = v_t/(2πν₀) using transverse sound velocity from molecular dynamics simulations.

Results

After impurity correction, all samples exhibited a broad peak in normalized dielectric losses ε″(ν)/ν near 1 THz—a hallmark of the boson peak. Fitting gave a central frequency ν₀ from 0.94 to 1.05 THz, weakly increasing with Ca/Si. Much more strongly changing are the oscillator strength S and damping Γ. The integrated spectral mass S and the peak in Cp/T³ (the entropy contribution is proportional to Cp/T³) decrease monotonically with increasing Ca/Si, while the apparent peak height S·ν₀/Γ reaches a maximum at Ca/Si ≈ 1.0. This composition is the point of minimum damping, where vibrations are least suppressed: silicate chains made of silicon and oxygen are already fragmented, but interlayer calcium has not yet filled the space. Calorimetry confirms the excess of low-frequency states with a Cp/T³ peak at 7–12 K. The peak frequency, recalculated from temperature via k_B T/h with a correction for systematic calorimetric shift, agrees with terahertz data. Applying the soft potential model with a density of states following a distribution analogous to Planck's law for radiation allowed extracting the peak frequency from heat capacity at 1.13–1.34 THz. Both methods independently point to the same energy scale, proving the nature of the observed peak.

Implications

The extracted elastic heterogeneity parameter γ (from 0.98 to 0.48) quantitatively links nanoscale stiffness fluctuations to macroscopic properties. The decrease of γ with Ca/Si predicts a reduction in the intrinsic creep of hardened cement paste, confirming the hypothesis about the role of nanostructural heterogeneity in long-term deformation. The boson peak also sets a minimum lattice thermal conductivity (~0.8–1.0 W/m·K), practically independent of composition, which is explained by the dominance of diffuse heat transport over propagons and aligns with the Landauer principle for quantum thermodynamics of disordered media. The detected correlation length of about 1 nm bridges the gap between short-range order and mesostructure, offering a new dynamic probe for amorphous materials.

Future development

The proposed two-probe approach—combining terahertz spectroscopy and low-temperature calorimetry—is applicable to any amorphous material. In cement science, it can be extended to supplementary cementitious materials (fly ash, slags) to non-contact assess their reactivity in low-carbon binders. Further development of interferometry methods in the terahertz range will improve resolution and enable transition from powders to bulk samples, while combination with inelastic neutron scattering will reveal the anisotropy of elastic heterogeneity.

Impact

The results directly impact cement physicochemistry, construction materials science, and glass physics. They offer a new indicator for optimizing concrete compositions with respect to durability and thermal protection.

Next steps

Measurements on hydrated cement pastes under controlled humidity are planned, along with comparing the boson peak to direct creep tests on the same samples.

Key open problems

The boson peak remains one of the unsolved problems in condensed matter physics: its microscopic origin is still debated. This work shows that in C-S-H it is closely linked to nanoscale elastic heterogeneity, supporting the hypothesis of quasi-localized vibrations as the source of excess states. Moreover, it touches on the nature of the glass transition and the role of 'soft modes' in the dynamics of disordered systems.

🎯 The same boson peak is observed in window glass and even in amorphous ice, but its frequency varies from material to material. In cement, it turned out to be surprisingly close to the peak in silicate glasses, even though C-S-H's structure is layered and contains water.

🎬 In Andy Weir's novel 'The Martian,' concrete modules on Mars are subjected to extreme temperature swings. Understanding heat transport and creep through the boson peak could help future engineers choose the optimal cement composition for extraterrestrial buildings.
