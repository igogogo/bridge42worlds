Cement's Boson Hum: Terahertz Waves Eavesdrop on Nano-Inhomogeneities

Original: "Boson peak and medium-range elastic heterogeneity in calcium silicate hydrate probed by terahertz spectroscopy and low-temperature calorimetry"
· Xiangyu Li, Ying Chen, Jipeng Luo, Ya Chen, Linhao Wang, Lidan Tian, Gan Ding, Zeyu Lu, Zhangli Hu, Biqin Dong, Yue Li, Zongjin Li
arXiv:2607.14764 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY · 1 min · Materials Disordered Systems
For the first time, a boson peak detected in cement gel sheds light on the vibrational life of the nanoworld, linking elastic fluctuations to concrete durability.
Links in the knowledge graph 1

Imagine a drum with uneven tension: its sound reveals the stress distribution. The boson peak in cement is the same 'voice' of the nanoworld: the ~1 THz frequency unveils stiffness fluctuations at the nanoscale. By tuning this hum, we can create concrete that doesn’t creep for centuries—and might one day head to Mars.

🎯 The same boson peak can be heard in window glass and amorphous ice, but in cement its frequency is surprisingly close to that of silicate glasses, even though C-S-H's structure is layered and water-rich.

🎬 In Andy Weir’s novel 'The Martian', concrete modules on Mars suffer from wild temperature swings. Understanding heat transport and creep through the boson peak could help future engineers choose the optimal cement mix for extraterrestrial structures.
