Invisible Gravity: The Dance of Double Stars Challenges Newton ✦

Original: "Revisiting Data Quality Control and Multiple-star Modeling in Wide Binary Gravity Tests: Confirmation of MOND-type Gravitational Anomaly at Low Acceleration"
· Kyu-Hyun Chae, Youngsub Yoon
arXiv:2607.14450 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min
Galaxies High Energy Stellar General Relativity HEP Theory
Even after the strictest data cleansing, gravity in wide binary systems stubbornly exceeds Newton's prediction, chipping away at the foundations of standard physics.
1

Two figure skaters on an endless ice sheet. Their dance is barely noticeable — but something beneath the ice speeds them up faster than it should. That’s exactly how gravity behaves in wide binary stars: Gaia data have stubbornly confirmed the anomaly three times over. If the effect is real, dark matter might turn out to be a mirage, and physics textbooks will start to crack at the seams.

🎯 The acceleration at which a star pulls a distant companion in a typical wide binary is so minuscule that if you were to start falling with such acceleration from a height of 1 meter, it would take longer than the age of our universe to reach the floor.

v_c(r) = \sqrt{\frac{G_N M_{\text{tot}}}{r}}
Newtonian circular speed: the speed at which a star would orbit the center of mass if gravity were standard
\tilde{v} = \frac{v_p}{v_c(r_p)}
dimensionless velocity: the observed value divided by the Newtonian prediction — if it exceeds one, gravity is stronger than expected
