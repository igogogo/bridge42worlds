Gravitational Anomaly in Wide Binary Stars Persists Despite Strict Data Control ✦

Original: "Revisiting Data Quality Control and Multiple-star Modeling in Wide Binary Gravity Tests: Confirmation of MOND-type Gravitational Anomaly at Low Acceleration"
· Kyu-Hyun Chae, Youngsub Yoon
arXiv:2607.14450 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY 4.0 · 5 min · Galaxies High Energy Stellar General Relativity HEP Theory
New research shows that even with the strictest data selection and accounting for hidden stellar companions, the anomalous boost in gravity at low accelerations remains robust.
Abstract

Objective: Re-examine gravitational tests in wide binaries using 2D velocities. Method: Analysis of the acceleration plane, the distribution of normalized relative velocity (ratio of measured speed to Newtonian circular speed), and its median profile. Result: Rigorous data control and realistic modeling of multiple systems do not eliminate the low-acceleration gravitational anomaly but confirm it—the boost factor γ ≈ 1.3–1.6 agrees with MOND predictions and numerical orbit calculations. Studies that denied the anomaly were erroneous due to calibration of the multiple-system fraction assuming Newtonian dynamics, biased quality cuts, or insufficient statistics. These findings strengthen modified gravity as an alternative to dark matter.

Context

The question of how exactly gravity works on large scales remains open. Observations of galaxies and clusters point to a lack of visible mass if Newton's law of universal gravitation Isaac Newton and Einstein's general relativity are correct. The traditional solution is the dark matter hypothesis, first proposed by Vera Rubin to explain galaxy rotation curves. However, an alternative approach—Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND)—suggests that gravity strengthens at accelerations below a threshold of ~1.2×10^{-10} m/s². Wide binary stars, with large separations between their components (thousands of astronomical units), provide an ideal testing ground for this hypothesis, because hypothetical dark matter is practically absent inside their orbits. Recent debates have flared up over how to correctly select data and account for invisible third stars that could mimic a gravitational anomaly.

Methods

The researchers analyzed three public samples of binary stars from the Gaia mission, each with different quality criteria: the Chae (2023) sample, which filters by random alignment probability; the Banik et al. (2024) sample, which emphasizes the normalized velocity error; and the Pittordis et al. (2025) sample, which imposes the constraint ruwe<1.2, effectively removing tight binaries with periods of about 3 years. To model the contribution of hierarchical systems (triple stars), they used a 'realistic approach' called PSS, in which the inner orbit of a hidden companion is bounded from below depending on the visible separation of the outer pair. A crucial innovation was the mandatory use of the Newtonian regime (small separations, high accelerations) to calibrate the triple fraction f_trip—only in this way can false conclusions be avoided. Then they performed an acceleration plane test and analyzed the distributions of the dimensionless velocity \tilde{v} = v_p / v_c(r_p). Comparisons were made both with purely Newtonian predictions and with state-of-the-art numerical simulations of orbits within QUMOND, which fully account for the external field effect of the Galaxy. All tests accounted for individual constraints on orbital eccentricities, uncertainties in photometric masses, and a correction for the perspective effect based on the Doppler effect.

Results

The analysis showed: no combination of strict quality criteria, including the much-discussed 'Banik cut' on the \tilde{v} error, eliminates the statistically significant velocity excess in the low-acceleration regime. For the sample with the most stringent restrictions (distance <150 pc, ruwe<1.2, filtering via color-magnitude diagram to exclude unresolved red dwarf binaries), the median normalized velocity in the Newtonian regime perfectly matches the theoretical value of 0.527±0.004, confirming the data's purity. However, in the MOND regime (separation > r_M), the median rises to 0.61–0.63, corresponding to a gravitational boost factor γ = g/g_N = 1.4–1.5. The statistical significance of the deviation from Newton exceeds 5σ when samples are combined. The study that claimed no anomaly (Cookson et al. 2026) turns out to have used a sample of only 61 systems in the MOND regime—8 times fewer than needed for a reliable conclusion. Moreover, it applied an incorrect mass–luminosity relation that underestimated the Newtonian baseline. Simulations with a larger number of objects and correct masses recover a clear increase in \tilde{v} with separation. Importantly, the anomaly does not vanish even with an artificially inflated triple fraction: trying to force the data to fit Newton in the low-acceleration regime immediately creates a contradiction in the Newtonian regime.

Implications

These findings seriously undermine the standard gravity picture on the scale of binary stars and provide a strong argument in favor of modified theories. They imply that the dynamics of sparse astrophysical systems, such as wide binaries or the outer parts of galaxies, may be governed not only by dark matter but also by gravity's own boost. This calls into question the need to introduce vast amounts of invisible mass in the Universe. For astrophysicists, this means recalibrating methods for estimating masses and the dynamical state of objects, while for theorists, it demands building a consistent relativistic MOND theory compatible with cosmological data.

Future development

The immediate future of gravity tests is tied to the enormous data volume of Gaia DR4 (release in December 2026), which will contain improved proper motions and radial velocities. This will enable full-scale 3D modeling of the orbits of thousands of individual binaries, minimizing statistical uncertainties. Meanwhile, numerical codes for two-body problems in a MOND potential are being refined and already show superiority over test-particle approximations. By the end of the decade, wide binary stars are expected either to definitively confirm the breakdown of the Newton–Einstein paradigm at low accelerations or to reveal previously unknown systematic effects in Gaia astrometry.

Impact

The results will affect observational cosmology, theories of galaxy formation, the interpretation of rotation curves, and direct detection experiments for dark matter. They will also spur the development of ultra-precise astrometry missions and laboratory tests of gravity at low accelerations.

Next steps

It is necessary to expand samples of 3D radial velocities using ground-based spectrographs such as GALAH and WEAVE, and to refine models of hierarchical systems depending on metallicity and stellar evolutionary stage. Blind tests using different MOND transition functions are also planned to evaluate their compatibility with the data.

Key open problems

The problem of the nature of hidden mass in the Universe, the question of the limits of general relativity's applicability, and the mystery of accelerated expansion (dark energy) are all indirectly touched by this research. Abandoning standard gravity in the weak-field limit may require revising many chapters of astrophysics and cosmology.
