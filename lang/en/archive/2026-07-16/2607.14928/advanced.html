Coloring Black Holes: Choices in Creating the First Image of the Event Horizon ✦

Original: "Coloring Black Holes: Epistemic and Aesthetic Choices in Astronomical Imaging"
· Rodrigo Ochigame, Emilie Skulberg, Jeroen van Dongen
arXiv:2607.14928 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY · 4 min · History and Philosophy of Physics High Energy Instrumentation General Relativity
One-sentence essence: An analysis of how astrophysicists, using algorithms and data from eight radio telescopes, transformed sparse signals into a single image of a black hole's shadow, and why this image is the result of not only observations but also numerous conscious choices.
Abstract

In 2019, the Event Horizon Telescope (EHT) collaboration released the first image of a black hole's shadow, obtained from observations. However, the final picture was just one of many plausible visualizations of extremely noisy and sparse data. At every processing step — from calibration to image synthesis — a choice between competing approaches or averaging of their results was required. This paper analyzes the epistemic and aesthetic decisions made by the EHT and shows how a different choice would have changed the final image. The key conclusion: the main scientific value lies not in a single canonical snapshot, but in demonstrating a constrained variability that preserves the key shadow structure intact.

Context

Direct observations of black holes were long considered an almost hopeless task. Although their existence followed from general relativity by Einstein, the event horizon does not radiate, and the angular size of even the nearest supermassive objects is vanishingly small—roughly like an orange on the Moon. The breakthrough became possible thanks to radio astronomy and VLBI interferometry, which combines signals from telescopes across the planet into a single virtual instrument the size of Earth. In 2017, the Event Horizon Telescope collaboration used eight such stations to capture emission at a wavelength of 1.3 mm from the plasma around the black hole M87*. However, between raw data and the final image lay a chasm: measurements were catastrophically sparse, and each pixel corresponded to an infinite number of possibilities consistent with the noise.

Methods

Our study is based on a historical-epistemological analysis of the visualization process. We meticulously examined EHT publications, technical reports, and the open-source code of the algorithms. Moreover, we independently varied the parameters of the programs to reconstruct alternative images, demonstrating how the snapshots might have looked under different but equally justified choices. This allowed us to trace the role of numerical simulations in validating algorithms and to assess the influence of machine learning on the final image. Parallels with classic scientific practices—from Anton Pannekoek's 'mean subjective image' of the Milky Way to 'blind' analyses in particle physics—helped clarify how scientists cope with the underdetermination of data.

Results

It turned out that even under strict constraints, the final image was not strictly dictated by the data. At every stage—from calibration to 'fiducial' construction—there were several equally valid paths. For example, the classic CLEAN algorithm broke the sky into point sources, while regularized maximum likelihood (RML) methods implicitly introduced expectations, such as a gravitationally lensed ring structure around the accretion disk. The final publication averaged three images from different pipelines, and the choice of color scheme (orange-black) was just one of 196 options. Numerically, key parameters showed robustness: out of 37,500 combinations for one algorithm, about 1,500 gave similar morphology. Yet fine details varied greatly, and it was this limited variability that testified to the reality of the black hole in the active galactic nucleus of M87 with its powerful jet.

Implications

For the philosophy of science, the EHT work demonstrates a new type of evidence: not a single snapshot, but a 'demonstration of limited variability' under different assumptions. Instead of hiding ambiguity, one could present the whole spectrum of plausible images, which would strengthen confidence in the conclusions. On a practical level, the case highlighted how blurred the line is between observation and simulation: algorithms were tested on synthetic data, and machine learning (the PRIMO project) transferred patterns from simulations onto real measurements, sparking debates about circular reasoning when testing Einstein's equations. Historically, Karl Schwarzschild had already found the first exact solution for a non-rotating black hole, and Roger Penrose proved the inevitability of singularities.

Future development

The future Next Generation EHT project will add new telescopes, including space-based ones, which will dramatically improve UV-plane coverage and bring reconstructions closer to the true appearance of black holes. In parallel, Bayesian ensembling methods that honestly report uncertainties will be developed. Improved simulations are expected to allow more accurate modeling of plasma around accretion disks, and new VLBI networks will enable testing of gravitational lensing in strong fields.

Impact

The results impact not only the astrophysics of active galactic nuclei and relativistic jets, but also the methodology for handling highly noisy data in geophysics and neuroscience.

Next steps

The immediate task is to rethink the status of 'raw data': the collaboration must resolve the debate over what level of preprocessing is sufficient for independent verification. It is also necessary to conduct blind tests with a wider set of synthetic images to exclude confirmation bias.

Key open problems

The work directly touches on the problem of underdetermination of theory by data and the search for objectivity when classical methods of independent verification are inapplicable. It also resonates with the question of how simulations can serve as an empirical foundation when their validation in the regime of interest is impossible—a central challenge for early-Universe physics and quantum gravity.
