How a Bent Crystal Turns Sound into Rotation

Original: "Curvature Converts Phonon Hall Viscosity into Phonon Angular Momentum"
· Pablo A. Morales
arXiv:2607.14868 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY · 2 min · Mesoscale HEP Theory
Scientists have theoretically shown that in curved crystalline membranes, sound vibrations generate a weak but measurable torque due to a special quantum viscosity.
Abstract

In a flat membrane, bending waves don't produce any special friction—the Hall viscosity. But if the membrane is curved, bending causes twisting, and a tiny torque appears. For a few layers of α-RuCl₃, it's estimated at 10⁻²² N·m—already detectable with instruments. Could curvature be the secret to measuring hidden quantum forces?

In everyday life, we’re used to a flexible sheet, like foil, simply bobbing up and down when bent. But in the world of quantum crystals, things are different. Some materials have a remarkable property — the Berry phase, which manifests as Hall viscosity. This means that if you shift layers inside such a crystal, a sideways motion emerges, similar to a car skidding on a slippery road, but without any friction. In a flat membrane, bending sound waves don’t create shear, so Hall viscosity remains ‘invisible’. However, just slightly curving the membrane — giving it the shape of a tiny bowl — makes each vertical deflection imperceptibly twist the material in-plane, like a figure skater pulling their arms in while spinning. This approach essentially turns the curved membrane into a miniature tool for mechanical spectroscopy, allowing the quantum properties of the material to be explored.

Hall viscosity doesn’t waste energy on heating. It doesn’t increase entropy, it merely redirects motion — like an ideal gearbox that turns wobbling into steady rotation.

Building on the equations of elasticity developed by Lev Landau and the quantum Berry phase, physicists calculated this effect for the magnetic material α-RuCl3. Even an extremely faint sound at a frequency of 10 billion oscillations per second generates a torque of roughly 2×10⁻²² Newton-meters. On an everyday scale, that’s vanishingly small — it would take tens of thousands of years for that torque to rotate a postage stamp by one degree. But for modern interferometric sensors, originally built to hunt for gravitational waves, this signal is already detectable. Moreover, flipping the magnetic field direction changes the rotation sign, allowing it to be reliably picked out from background noise.

The effect is reminiscent of acoustic polarimetry: just as crystals can rotate the plane of light polarization, the curved membrane makes the ‘polarization’ of sound waves precess.

This phononic analog of the Einstein–de Haas effect opens the door to ultra-sensitive quantum sensors and microscopic gyroscopes. The work deepens understanding of quantum thermodynamics and paves the way for precise quantum measurements of mechanical angular momentum in two-dimensional materials.

🎯 If this tiny torque were continuously applied to a screw the size of a postage stamp, it would take about a hundred thousand years to turn just one degree. Yet modern sensors, cooled to near absolute zero, can detect it in a fraction of a second.

\chi_{E_{xy},\eta} = \frac{i\vartheta_H \omega q^2}{4D_T} \left[ \Delta K - \frac{q^2(\lambda K + 2\mu K_1)}{D_L} \right]
This expression shows how strongly a sound wave shifts atoms sideways when the membrane is curved. Hall viscosity ϑ_H turns vertical jitter into an asymmetric shear that depends on curvature.
\langle\langle\ell_z\rangle\rangle = \frac{\rho\vartheta_H \omega^2 q^2 |\eta|^2 \sigma_K^2}{4 D_L D_T} \frac{(\lambda+\mu)(2\lambda+\mu)}{\lambda+2\mu}
The main result: a static bend in the membrane generates a steady average rotation proportional to the square of the wave amplitude and the magnitude of Hall viscosity. Without curvature, everything vanishes.
