Spin from Bend: How Curvature Twists Sound

Original: "Curvature Converts Phonon Hall Viscosity into Phonon Angular Momentum"
· Pablo A. Morales
arXiv:2607.14868 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY · 3 min · Mesoscale HEP Theory
Curvature makes massless phonons acquire angular momentum, turning Hall viscosity into a measurable mechanical torque.
Abstract

In flat crystalline membranes, bending modes typically don't feel the phonon Hall viscosity—a quantum coefficient of frictionless transport. But the authors showed that static curvature couples bending to in-plane strain, creating a Hall channel. For shallow isotropic ripples, a stationary torque emerges, proportional to the mean curvature. For a few atomic layers of α-RuCl₃, the estimate yields ~10⁻²² N·m—already within reach of torsion sensors. Just as a curved sheet resists twisting differently than a flat one, nanoscale curvature unlocks access to hidden quantum viscosity.

In 1915, Albert Einstein and Wander Johannes de Haas demonstrated that when an iron rod is magnetized, it twists slightly—the angular momentum of electrons is transferred to the crystal lattice. A century later, physicists discovered that a similar dance can be performed by phonons—quanta of sound—if the material possesses phonon Hall viscosity. This strange viscosity doesn’t slow things down but skews motion, much like the Coriolis force in the ocean, and only shows up in media with broken time-reversal symmetry. Recent experiments with bulk α-RuCl₃ using ultrasonic spectroscopy have revealed its traces, yet in the two-dimensional limit, the flexural phonon remained silent—until curvature was added to the theory.

Curvature acts as a translator from the language of bending to the language of shear. Picture a cymbal: strike a flat plate, and it will vibrate up and down, barely creating lateral stresses. But bend it—and every flexural ripple automatically engages in-plane stretching and compression, like invisible springs. In a crystalline membrane, this interaction is described by the second fundamental form of the surface. The key ingredient is a gyroscopic term, born from the adiabatic Berry phase discovered by Michael Berry in 1984. This term, proportional to the Hall viscosity ϑ_H, generates no entropy and resembles a spinning top that rotates forever unless disturbed.

The acoustic Faraday effect: just as a magnetic field rotates the plane of polarization of light, Hall viscosity 'twists' the plane of sound deformation. This has already been observed in bulk materials using polarimetry, but in curved membranes it finds new life.

When a flexural wave travels across a curved membrane, geometric transduction produces a shear response that is odd under time reversal. Analytically, it splits into a direct contribution from the traceless curvature and an indirect one dictated by longitudinal deformation. Averaging over an ensemble of isotropic gentle ripples yields a stationary angular momentum ⟨ℓ_z⟩ pointing along the normal to the mean plane, which flips sign upon magnetic field reversal. Mathematically, this is captured by a concise relation where the moment density is quadratic in the ripple amplitude and linear in Hall viscosity: ⟨⟨ℓ_z⟩⟩ = (ρϑ_H ω² q² |η|² σ_K²)/(4 D_L D_T) · (λ+μ)(2λ+μ)/(λ+2μ). Behind these compact symbols lies a fundamental mechanism: the Lamé elastic moduli λ and μ define the stiffness of the medium, while D_L and D_T govern the longitudinal and transverse dynamics, but it is Hall viscosity that conducts the rotation.

The predicted torque for an α-RuCl₃ membrane just 5×5 microns across is about 2×10⁻²² N·m—as if a grain of sand on the tip of a propeller would cause it to turn by one degree in a hundred thousand years. But ultrasensitive torsional 'balances', cooled to millikelvin temperatures, can detect such movements in a fraction of a second.

Experimental verification of this 'geometro-phononic' spintronics lies in the vein of technologies originally honed for gravitational-wave observatories. Torsional sensors with capacitive or interferometric readout can register the predicted torque, and quantum sensing will allow calibrating the response down to single-phonon levels. The unveiled mechanism promises new protocols for quantum measurement of mechanical angular momentum and could become the basis of acoustic polarimetry for characterizing topological phases. In the longer term, it opens the possibility of building acoustic gyroscopes on two-dimensional materials—devices that convert thermal vibrations into stable rotation without moving parts. This challenges our understanding of quantum thermodynamics of non-dissipative processes, because the system continuously generates angular momentum without producing entropy or violating the second law. Curvature here is not a perturbation but an architect, without whom the stunning effect would remain hidden in equations. From the elasticity theory of Landau to the quantum geometry of Berry phases—the story comes full circle, granting us a new tuning knob over sound in flatland.

🎯 The predicted torque of 10⁻²² N·m is so tiny that even on microscopic scales it seems like a toy. If such a torque acted continuously on a screw the size of a postage stamp, it would take over 100,000 years to rotate by one degree. Yet torsional sensors cooled almost to absolute zero, developed for gravitational-wave observatories, can spot this motion in fractions of a second.

\chi_{E_{xy},\eta} = \frac{i\vartheta_H \omega q^2}{4D_T} \left[ \Delta K - \frac{q^2(\lambda K + 2\mu K_1)}{D_L} \right]
This expression shows how strongly a sound wave shifts atoms sideways when the membrane is curved. Hall viscosity ϑ_H turns vertical jitter into an asymmetric shear that depends on curvature.
\langle\langle\ell_z\rangle\rangle = \frac{\rho\vartheta_H \omega^2 q^2 |\eta|^2 \sigma_K^2}{4 D_L D_T} \frac{(\lambda+\mu)(2\lambda+\mu)}{\lambda+2\mu}
The main result: a static bend in the membrane generates a steady average rotation proportional to the square of the wave amplitude and the magnitude of Hall viscosity. Without curvature, everything vanishes.
