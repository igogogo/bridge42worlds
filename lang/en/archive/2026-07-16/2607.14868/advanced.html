How Curvature Turns Phonon Hall Viscosity into Angular Momentum

Original: "Curvature Converts Phonon Hall Viscosity into Phonon Angular Momentum"
· Pablo A. Morales
arXiv:2607.14868 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY · 4 min · Mesoscale HEP Theory
Theoretically demonstrated: in curved crystal membranes, flexural vibrations generate directional phonon angular momentum thanks to Hall viscosity.
Abstract

In a flat crystalline membrane, the low-energy spectrum is dominated by bending modes that don't couple to the phonon Hall viscosity. Static curvature converts normal displacements into in-plane strain, opening a Hall channel. The traceless curvature directly couples to the Hall shear, while the mean curvature couples indirectly through ordinary elastic shear. Together they generate an in-plane phonon angular momentum. For a statistically isotropic gentle ripple, the time average yields a stationary odd-in-field torque proportional to the mean-square curvature, with sign governed by the Hall viscosity. An estimate based on the bulk Hall viscosity of α-RuCl₃ predicts a torque of ~10⁻²² N·m for a membrane a few atomic layers thick—within reach of existing torsion sensors. This bending-to-shear response is a direct route to measuring phonon Hall viscosity in atomically thin crystals.

Context

Crystals that break time-reversal symmetry can host phonon Hall viscosity—a non-dissipative transport coefficient that manifests in the acoustic Faraday effect and the phonon Hall effect. Recent ultrasonic measurements in bulk α-RuCl3 have allowed estimation of this viscosity, but its existence in ultimately thin, suspended membranes remains an open question. In flat geometry, the membrane's flexural mode, which dominates the low-energy spectrum, carries no polarization and does not couple to the Hall sector. Since real membranes always have static curvature—whether controlled bending or natural ripples—it is crucial to understand how curvature activates this link. The effect is further motivated by the analogy with the Einstein–de Haas effect, where angular momentum is carried by phonons.

Methods

We formulate the Hall-viscous dynamics of a curved crystalline membrane using a Lagrangian with a quadratic term arising from the adiabatic Berry phase, introduced by Michael Berry. This term, proportional to the phonon Hall viscosity ϑ_H, represents an area in shear strain space, breaks T-symmetry, and does not generate entropy, being purely gyroscopic. The key is geometric transduction: on a curved surface, the normal displacement enters the strain tensor linearly through the second fundamental form, mixing with in-plane phonons. By separating the deviatoric and mean curvature contributions, we derive coupled equations of motion based on the principle of least action and Hooke's law for isotropic elasticity, developed by Landau and Lifshitz. Solutions are sought in local principal coordinates, followed by ensemble averaging over statistically isotropic shallow ripples to obtain the global torque.

Results

We obtained an analytical expression for the curvature-induced shear response, odd under time reversal. It consists of a direct contribution from deviatoric curvature and an indirect one mediated by longitudinal strain. For a cylindrical geometry with a radius of 1 µm and a flexural mode amplitude of 1 nm at 10 GHz, the shear strain is about 10^{-8} far from resonance—an acoustic analog of polarization rotation. The derived phonon angular momentum ⟨ℓ_z⟩, computed via the displacement correlator, has the same sign as ϑ_H. Upon averaging over isotropic ripples, the time average yields a constant torque, quadratic in ripple amplitude and linear in ϑ_H. For an α-RuCl3 membrane of size 5×5 µm², the predicted torque is on the order of 2×10^{-22} N·m, exceeding the sensitivity threshold of advanced torsion sensors originally developed for gravitational wave detectors. The effect vanishes for an ideally flat membrane and reverses sign upon magnetic field inversion, enabling robust extraction by anti-symmetrization.

Implications

Our work demonstrates that static curvature serves as a knob to pull the flexural mode into the non-trivial topological sector. This opens a direct route to locally measuring phonon Hall viscosity in two-dimensional systems, complementing existing bulk methods. The revealed dissipation-free angular momentum generation mechanism is of fundamental importance for quantum thermodynamics of non-dissipative processes and broadens the analogy between the Einstein–de Haas effect and phonon transport.

Future development

Technologies originally developed for interferometric detectors can be adapted for precision mechanical readout in experiments with curved van der Waals heterostructures. In the future, the theory can be extended to dynamic curvature, nonlinear regimes, and the quantum limit, where phonon angular momentum may become quantized. Integration with quantum sensors promises new interfaces between mechanics and information.

Impact

The results will impact condensed matter physics, especially topological phononics and nanomechanics of two-dimensional materials. They directly touch upon quantum measurements of angular momentum and may stimulate the design of novel gyroscopic devices and actuators leveraging Hall viscosity.

Next steps

Immediate steps include fabricating curved membranes from magnetic layered materials such as α-RuCl3 and directly measuring the torque or shear response via interferometric or capacitive sensors.

Key open problems

The study is directly linked to the unsolved problem of survival of topological phonon effects in low-dimensional systems and to the broader question of how curvature modifies Berry phases. It also sheds light on the microscopic mechanism of the phonon Hall effect and its connection to magnetoelastic coupling.
