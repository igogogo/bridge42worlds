Atmospheric Palimpsest: Sulfur as the Key to Exoplanet Interiors

Original: "Sulfur photochemistry observationally traces mantle redox states of rocky planets"
· Ioannis Panagiotou, Tim Lichtenberg, Shang-Min Tsai, Harrison Nicholls
arXiv:2607.15204 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY · 2 min
Exoplanets Geophysics
Sulfur dioxide in the atmospheres of distant planets, contrary to expectations, does not vanish under the onslaught of stellar radiation, but serves as a reliable indicator of redox processes deep below the surface.
Abstract

Scientists studied how the interiors and atmospheres of rocky exoplanets interact. Using evolutionary modeling (PROTEUS) and chemical calculations, they showed that the overall atmospheric composition—reducing (hydrogen-rich) or oxidizing (carbon dioxide-dominated)—is determined by the redox state of the mantle and degassing history. Photochemistry under stellar radiation only alters the upper layers, but it creates a bright indicator: sulfur dioxide (SO₂), especially noticeable with intermediate and oxidized interiors. Synthetic spectra showed that these SO₂ signatures in the infrared (4 and 7-9 μm) will reach ~60 and ~100 ppm, then return to background levels of ~30 and ~50 ppm.

1

When the JWST telescope breaks the light of a distant exoplanet into a spectrum, it reads a chemical chronicle. But this chronicle is not a monolithic truth; it's an atmosphere constantly rewritten by the star's radiation. Astronomers face the task of recovering the original geological message, and sulfur compounds are the key to deciphering it. Photochemistry is especially aggressive under the radiation of red dwarfs and the young Sun — capable of erasing almost all traces, but sulfur holds the memory.

On Earth, volcanic SO₂ helps track underwater eruptions. On exoplanets, it could become a “seismograph” of mantle activity.

Using coupled numerical simulations of interior and atmospheric evolution, scientists mapped how the invisible chisel of photochemistry revises the text left by outgassing. The mantle, depending on its redox potential (from IW–4 to IW+4), exhales gases — from hydrogen and methane to carbon dioxide and water. But the upper atmospheric layers (pressure 10⁻⁵–10⁻⁸ bar) come under the onslaught of ultraviolet light: molecules break apart, radicals engage in new reactions, and the original handwriting fades. Yet sulfur behaves differently. The foundations of transit spectroscopy, laid by David Charbonneau, allow us to capture its elusive trace.

The palimpsest metaphor is literal here: photochemistry erases old chemical letters, but sulfur remains, like letters scratched too deeply into parchment.

Photochemical kinetics, calculated from equations such as the photolysis rate J = ∫ σ(λ)φ(λ)F(λ) dλ, shows that SO₂ steps out of the shadows. At intermediate and oxidized mantle states, its volume mixing ratio jumps by orders of magnitude — up to 1% — in layers transparent to transit observations. Synthetic spectra for JWST instruments reveal clear absorption dips at 4, 7.3, and 8.7 µm. For a mantle with an oxidation state of IW+0, the signal depth reaches about 60 ppm at 4 µm and 100 ppm at longer wavelengths — a reliably detectable fingerprint. Surprisingly, upon further oxidation (IW+4), the signal weakens again, returning to an equilibrium 30–50 ppm, betraying an excess of oxygen. Thus the atmosphere becomes a chemical palimpsest, where beneath the layer of new reactions, an archaic geophysical truth emerges.

This result turns sulfur dioxide into a geological compass. With it, astronomers can classify exoplanets by the oxidation state of their mantles, without looking directly under the crust. Already, JWST data can distinguish planets with reduced, intermediate, and oxidized interiors — a direct probe of geochemistry. In the future, when models include atmospheric erosion, three-dimensional dynamics, and aerosol treatment, we will be able to read these atmospheric palimpsests with even greater precision, paving the way for missions like Ariel. This brings science closer to understanding how volatile reservoirs of rocky worlds form and under what conditions they become potentially habitable.
