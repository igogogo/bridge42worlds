Sulfur Photochemistry as an Indicator of Mantle Redox States in Rocky Planets

Original: "Sulfur photochemistry observationally traces mantle redox states of rocky planets"
· Ioannis Panagiotou, Tim Lichtenberg, Shang-Min Tsai, Harrison Nicholls
arXiv:2607.15204 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY · 2 min · Exoplanets Geophysics
Photochemical reactions of sulfur-bearing molecules in exoplanet atmospheres allow remote probing of their interior geochemistry.
Abstract

The question of what determines the composition of rocky exoplanet atmospheres—interior degassing or non-equilibrium processes like photochemistry—remains open. Using the PROTEUS computational model, coupled with FastChem and VULCAN chemical codes, evolutionary simulations were conducted for a wide range of mantle redox states, irradiation fluxes, and stellar spectra (solar-type and M-dwarfs). Comparisons were made between thermochemical equilibrium, vertical transport only, and combined transport and photochemistry. They found that even with non-equilibrium chemistry, the bulk atmosphere is controlled by the mantle's redox state and degassing history: reduced interiors lead to H₂-dominated atmospheres, oxidized interiors to CO₂-dominated ones. Photochemistry affects the upper layers, depleting neutral volatile compounds and enhancing radical concentrations, especially under high irradiation. SO₂ abundance increases significantly at intermediate and oxidized mantle states. Synthetic emission spectra reveal SO₂ absorption bands at 4 and 7.3/8.7 μm with amplitudes of ~60 and ~100 ppm, which drop to ~30 and ~50 ppm for oxidized mantles.

Context

Understanding the connection between interior structure and atmosphere of exoplanets is crucial for interpreting observational data. Transit surveys have revealed thousands of planets, and spectroscopic observations with JWST allow us to peer into their interiors. The mantle redox state determines the composition of outgassed volatiles, but photochemistry driven by stellar radiation, such as from Sun and red dwarfs, can dramatically alter the upper layers. Numerical models of coupled interior-atmosphere evolution help assess these effects.

Methods

The study employs the numerical modeling framework PROTEUS, coupled with FastChem and VULCAN codes to compute equilibrium and non-equilibrium chemistry in exoplanet atmospheres. Earth-sized planet evolution was simulated with varying mantle redox states (IW–4 to IW+4), instellation flux (0.1–1000 Earth units), and host star type (Sun and red dwarf GJ 1132). For each of 50 scenarios, three post-processing options were applied: equilibrium, with vertical transport, and with added photochemistry. Synthetic spectra were then calculated for JWST bands using transit and emission spectroscopy, foundations of which were laid by David Charbonneau.

Results

The bulk atmosphere is controlled by outgassing and mantle redox state: reduced mantles (IW–4) release H₂ and CH₄, intermediate ones—CO, oxidized (IW+4)—CO₂ and H₂O. Photochemistry in the upper layers (10⁻⁵–10⁻⁸ bar) leads to molecular dissociation and formation of H radicals, especially under high instellation from red dwarfs. Sulfur spectroscopic analysis revealed that SO₂ increases by orders of magnitude for intermediate and oxidized mantle states (up to 10⁻² volume mixing ratio). Synthetic spectra for JWST show SO₂ absorption bands at 4, 7.3, and 8.7 μm. For a mantle with IW+0, depths reach ~60 ppm at 4 μm and ~100 ppm at 7.3/8.7 μm; for IW+4, values revert to equilibrium ~30 and ~50 ppm, respectively.

Implications

The results demonstrate that atmospheres of rocky exoplanets can be classified by mantle redox state even when photochemistry is considered. Spectral features of SO₂ observed by JWST directly link atmospheric composition to interior geochemistry, resolving ambiguities of equilibrium models.

Future development

Incorporating atmospheric escape, three-dimensional dynamics, and aerosol formation into the models will improve prediction accuracy for exoplanets and pave the way for missions like Ariel.

Impact

This work will influence observational strategies for JWST campaigns and methods for spectral interpretation, as well as our understanding of planetary system evolution.

Next steps

Future work will integrate photochemical kinetics into evolutionary calculations and extend the analysis to super-Earths and sub-Neptunes to test the universality of these effects.

Key open problems

The study addresses unresolved questions about the origin of volatiles on rocky exoplanets and the search for reliable geochemical markers in atmospheres, crucial for assessing potential habitability.
