Strongly Mixed Worlds: How Inflation Becomes a Cosmic Collider ✦

Original: "Pushing the Primordial Frontier: Cosmological Collider Signatures at Strong Mixing"
· Javier Huenupi, Claudio Muñoz, Gonzalo A. Palma, Spyros Sypsas
arXiv:2607.14529 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · Cosmology General Relativity HEP Phenomenology HEP Theory
Exact analytical solutions reveal extraordinarily strong signals from heavy particles in the non-Gaussianity of the cosmic microwave background, turning the early Universe into an accelerator of ultimate energies.
Abstract

An analytical approach to primordial non-Gaussianity in multi-field inflation has been developed, non-perturbative in the parameter λ describing the mixing of curvature and isocurvature modes. From exact linear solutions for perturbations ζ and σ, propagators are constructed, and exact integral representations are obtained for tree-level bispectra generated by interactions of the form ζ̇²σ, ζ̇σ², and σ³. The approach sums all orders of transfer between perturbations and is applicable for any values of the entropic mass μ. In the strong mixing regime, where standard perturbation theory breaks down, the power spectrum and bispectrum are significantly enhanced, revealing new non-perturbative scaling laws for the amplitude of non-Gaussianity. These results open up possibilities for analytical description of multi-field inflation beyond weak coupling, as well as for analyzing signals of the cosmological collider, formation of primordial black holes, and loop corrections.

Context

Tiny inhomogeneities, seeded by inflation in the first moments after the Big Bang, froze into the expanding universe and got imprinted in the cosmic microwave background. Among those imprints, non-Gaussian correlations are especially valuable because they directly depend on which quantum fields played an active role in that epoch. Usually a single field—the inflaton—dominates inflation, but in fundamental theories like the Standard Model and its extensions, extra degrees of freedom inevitably appear: candidates for dark matter particles or carriers of new interactions. Until now, their impact on observable non-Gaussianity was modeled approximately, assuming weak coupling to the inflaton. A new leap becomes possible thanks to exact solutions for the case of arbitrarily strong mixing.

Methods

The starting point was a simple but rich model: two scalar fields on an inflating background, one responsible for curvature ζ and the other for entropy perturbations σ. Their linear coupling is set by a dimensionless parameter λ and the entropy mass μ. Instead of treating this coupling as a small perturbation, the authors incorporated its exact solutions into the "dressed" propagators of the Schwinger–Keldysh formalism—in essence, a quantum-mechanical system respecting the uncertainty principle and the superposition principle. Thus, the transfer of perturbations between sectors is summed to infinite order. Then three canonical cubic interactions were added and the tree-level bispectra were computed. All calculations were reduced to elegant integral representations, which were then analyzed in the squeezed kinematic limit (one wavelength much larger than the other two).

Results

The main result is closed-form expressions for the squeezed bispectrum, displaying a non-monotonic dependence of the form (k_L/k_S)^{1/2 ± ν}, where ν = √(9/4 – µ²/H²) for the light entropy mode, or logarithmic oscillations for the heavy one. In the weak-coupling limit, the classic results of "quasi-single-field" inflation and the cosmological collider program are recovered. However, when λ ≫ 1 and µ/H is not too large, both the power spectrum and the bispectrum experience exponential enhancement ∝ e^{πλ}. Notably, the frequency scale of oscillations is set directly by µ—a key difference from the perturbative approach, which features an effective mass m²_eff = µ² – H²λ². In practical terms, this means that strong mixing regimes can generate enormous inhomogeneities capable of collapsing into primordial black holes, and the signal shape in the CMB will be qualitatively different from earlier predictions.

Implications

This work provides, for the first time, analytical control over non-Gaussianity in the non-perturbative λ regime. It paves the way for a full scan of masses and coupling constants of particles that existed during inflation, using data from the cosmic microwave background and large-scale structure. Going beyond the weak-coupling approximation is especially crucial for theories where fields interact strongly—for instance, in models with curved field space or spontaneous supersymmetry breaking. Moreover, it becomes clearer how to disentangle the contributions of different particles without confusing them with artifacts of the perturbative expansion.

Future development

The machinery built around the conformal point ν = 1/2 and the sl(2) ladder algebra strongly suggests a connection with the cosmological bootstrap method, where correlators are constructed without dynamical calculations. The hypergeometric "dressing" operator acting on seed solutions appears as a natural tool to formulate bootstrap rules at strong coupling. Technically, the next steps are to include time-dependent mixing, compute four-point functions and loop corrections, where dressed propagators should considerably simplify the calculations.

Impact

The results are important for cosmology, particle physics, and quantum field theory because they offer a unified language to describe inflationary correlators at any coupling strength. They also set new benchmarks for observational tests of fundamental theories via non-Gaussianity signatures.

Next steps

Direct comparison with Planck and future data (e.g., CMB-S4) for the whole parameter plane (λ, µ/H); develop software to accurately approximate the bispectrum in arbitrary configurations; extend to models with more than two fields.

Key open problems

The problem directly touches on the hierarchy problem: why could particle masses in the early Universe be of order the Hubble scale rather than the Planck scale? The method allows testing strongly coupled theories, which often appear in string landscapes or composite inflaton models. The enhancement of the power spectrum simultaneously addresses the puzzle of primordial black hole origin and may provide a clue to dark matter's nature if it consists of the same black holes.

🎯 To find solutions for fields of arbitrary mass, physicists used an elegant trick: they first solved the equations for a conformally coupled field (ν=1/2), then "multiplied" them using ladder operators of the sl(2) algebra, which acts like a quantum elevator between different masses. This same algebra appears in problems of supersymmetry and exactly solvable potentials in quantum mechanics.

S(k_S, k_S, k_L) \propto \sum_{\pm} C_{\pm}(\lambda,\nu) \left(\frac{k_L}{k_S}\right)^{\frac12 \pm \nu}
In the squeezed limit, the bispectrum behaves as a power law of the wave number ratio with an exponent depending on the entropy mass and mixing.
S(k_S, k_S, k_L) \propto \left(\frac{k_L}{k_S}\right)^{1/2} C(\lambda,\rho) \sin\left[\rho \ln\left(\frac{k_S}{k_L}\right) + \phi\right]
For a large entropy field mass, characteristic oscillations appear in the spectrum, with frequency reflecting the particle mass in Hubble units.
