Early Infrared Excess and the Birth of a Corona in Tidal Disruption Event 2024aepd ✦

Original: "Early Near-Infrared Excess and Rapid Disk-Corona Evolution in the Tidal Disruption Event 2024aepd"
· Yongxin Wu, Yanan Wang, Thomas M. Reynolds, Shuyuan Wei, Shiyan Zhong, Zikun Lin, Megan Newsome, Sebastian Gomez, Iair Arcavi, Panos Charalampopoulos, Chun Chen, Rongfeng Shen, Ning-Chen Sun, David Aguado, Ismael Pérez-Fournon, Frédérick Poidevin, Zhongnan Dong, Niu Li, Weijian Guo, Hu Zou, Jingbo Sun, Nieves Castro-Rodríguez, Antonio Cabrera-Lavers, Ning Jiang, Hengxiao Guo, J. P. Anderson, Tomás E. Müller-Bravo, Seppo Mattila, Claudia P. Gutiérrez, Amit Kumar, G. Pignata, Xiangkun Liu, R. Dastidar, Brajesh Kumar, Xiaowei Liu, Bin Ma, M. Dennefeld, Francesca Onori, Lydia Makrygianni, Mariusz Gromadzki, Xuan Fang
arXiv:2607.14696 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY 4.0 · 2 min · High Energy
Observations of a disrupted star unveil the secrets of accretion coronae and the mysterious infrared glow.
Abstract

We present multi-wavelength observations of the tidal disruption event TDE 2024aepd, covering primarily the first ~300 days after detection. The X-ray spectrum was initially dominated by a thermal disk component with a hard excess; starting around day 178, the spectrum became a power law and subsequently hardened, indicating rapid formation of a hot corona. An early near-infrared excess was recorded by day ~40; its nearly flat spectrum strongly differs from the Rayleigh-Jeans tail of the ultraviolet-optical blackbody emission. Although a traditional dust echo explanation cannot be entirely ruled out, free-free emission from a reprocessing photospheric shell appears more plausible. The shift in the break between UV-optical and near-IR bands, with a nearly constant density profile index, suggests evolving reprocessing conditions within a weakly changing density structure. TDE 2024aepd becomes the third event (after AT2019azh and TDE 2025abcr) to show an early IR excess; assessing the prevalence of this phenomenon requires a larger sample with near-IR observations at early epochs.

Context

When a star gets too close to a supermassive black hole, the law of universal gravitation produces tidal forces that tear it apart. Such tidal disruption events (TDEs) allow us to observe, over mere months, processes that in active galactic nuclei span millions of years. Understanding the evolution of accretion disks and the formation of coronae around black holes is one of the key goals of modern astrophysics.

Methods

A global network of telescopes tracked the event from radio to X-ray wavelengths. Key data were obtained using photometry and spectroscopy with instruments like ZTF, ATLAS, NOTCam, EMIR, and Swift. The distance to the galaxy was determined thanks to the redshift discovered by Edwin Hubble. Spectral analysis included model fitting based on Planck's law and the Compton effect to disentangle the contributions of various components.

Results

Already by ~40 days, a dust infrared excess emerged, but its spectrum is better explained by free-free emission in a plasma envelope that reprocesses hard radiation. In parallel, the X-ray spectrum underwent a dramatic transformation: around 178 days, the thermal disk emission gave way to a non-thermal power law with an index of Γ≈1.8, interpreted as the birth of a hot corona, where photons hardened by Compton scattering soar to high energies. The similarity to the low/hard state in X-ray binaries points to a universal mechanism.

Implications

The observed transition strengthens the idea that tidal disruption events are ideal proving grounds for studying accretion physics. The fact that the photon index rises with luminosity without a subsequent break challenges models developed for active galactic nuclei and binary systems, and calls for a revision of accretion disk evolution scenarios.

Future development

Future surveys, such as LSST at the Vera Rubin Observatory, will detect thousands of such events. Coordinated infrared and X-ray campaigns will help clarify whether the early excess is a universal feature and how the plasma in the corona evolves over time. As far back as the 1970s, Rashid Sunyaev laid the foundations of accretion disk theory, anticipating studies like this one.

Impact

The results will have a direct impact on accretion theory, modeling of tidal disruption events, and the interpretation of observations of intermediate-mass black holes.

Next steps

It is critically important to gather statistics on the early infrared excess and, simultaneously, to conduct detailed numerical simulations of reprocessing envelopes that account for Comptonization and free-free processes.

Key open problems

The study is directly connected to the puzzles of corona formation and evolution, the geometry of the accretion flow, and the role the environment—including cosmic dust—plays in reprocessing radiation.
