Spinning primordial black holes: a new look at dark matter through cosmic dust heating ✦

Original: "Refining primordial black hole dark matter constraints with dust heating: the role of spin and halo profile dependence"
· Shaobin Hu, Yupeng Yang, Chengjie Sun, Zihan Li, Jiafan Sun, Yuzhu Tong, Yankun Qu, Shuangxi Yi
arXiv:2607.15028 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Cosmology
Taking spin and halo profile into account refines constraints on the fraction of primordial black holes in dark matter that heat interstellar dust.
Abstract

Primordial black holes (PBHs) with masses in the range 10^15–10^18 g can heat interstellar dust through Hawking radiation. Unlike previous works, the spin of PBHs is considered, which enhances radiation intensity, and the impact of five dark matter distribution models (isothermal, Einasto, NFW, Moore, Burkert) is investigated. Full photon spectra, including primary and secondary emission, are computed. It is shown that for a fixed halo profile and mass function, higher spin values impose stricter constraints on the PBH dark matter fraction f_PBH. The tightest limits come from the isothermal profile, followed by Einasto, NFW, and Moore; the Burkert profile yields the weakest constraints. For silicate dust grains, which cool more slowly than graphite ones, the upper bound on f_PBH reaches 10^{-4} at maximal spin. Both monochromatic and log-normal mass functions are considered; for the log-normal function, increasing the width σ excludes a broader mass range, preventing massive PBHs from being the sole dark matter component. The obtained constraints are weaker than existing ones but serve as an independent check.

Context

The mystery of dark matter remains one of the biggest unsolved problems in physics. Among many candidates, primordial black holes (PBHs) draw attention because they require no new physics beyond the Standard Model and naturally explain black hole mergers detected by gravitational-wave observatories. Our entire Galaxy becomes a laboratory to test their existence, with the key being humble interstellar dust—a component of the interstellar medium.

Methods

Instead of traditional lensing or gamma-ray background, the authors used the thermal balance of interstellar dust. PBHs with masses 10^15–10^17 g emit Hawking radiation, predicted by Stephen Hawking—photons and electrons—which is absorbed by dust grains. If heating exceeds cooling via infrared emission, the dust will overheat. Cooling was calculated for two compositions: silicate and graphite, using Planck's law and the Stefan–Boltzmann law for equilibrium temperatures. Heating was estimated through the full photon spectrum of PBHs, including secondary photons from particle decays, with numerical simulations (BlackHawk code). For the first time in this method, PBH spin and five dark halo density profile models were taken into account, including isothermal, NFW, and Burkert, as well as a lognormal mass function.

Results

It turns out that even moderate spin significantly boosts the radiation flux, especially at a spin parameter a* ≈ 1. For instance, for a PBH of mass 10^15 g, the spectrum extends into the hard region, increasing dust heating. Among profiles, the isothermal one gives the strongest constraints, while Burkert’s the weakest. For a monochromatic mass function and silicate dust grains, the upper limit on the PBH fraction in dark matter (fPBH) reaches 10^{-4}, i.e., no more than 0.01%. For graphite, the limits are about twice as weak. With a lognormal distribution of width σ=2, an entire range of massive PBHs is ruled out as the dominant dark matter component. These results agree well with calculations for different halo structures, showing the method is universal though sensitive to the model choice.

Implications

The work shows that dust heating is a previously underestimated tool for probing PBH parameters. Accounting for spin opens up new constraints on nearly extremal spinning black holes, complementing data from X-ray telescopes and the gamma-ray background. The results are important for PBH formation theories: if future observations reveal that such holes have high spin, it would point to nontrivial processes in the early Universe.

Future development

Future studies could include a realistic dust distribution in the Galactic disk—this will tighten constraints in central regions by four orders of magnitude. Also promising is accounting for the electron contribution with their mean free path in the interstellar medium, and a detailed analysis of spectral features of heating by individual lines. Cross-correlation with infrared survey data will allow testing the predicted thermal anomalies.

Impact

The method will impact early Universe cosmology, black hole physics, and models of the interstellar medium. Astronomy, particle astrophysics, and dark matter theory gain a new bridge between micro- and macroscales.

Next steps

Next steps include applying to data from all-sky infrared and submillimeter surveys, and 3D modeling of radiative transfer in an inhomogeneous interstellar medium.

Key open problems

The constraints are directly linked to unsolved problems: the nature of dark matter, the mechanism of primordial black hole formation, and their spin distribution. They also touch on the black hole entropy paradox via Bekenstein–Hawking entropy, which underlies the radiation itself.

🎯 It is still unknown whether primordial black holes exist at all—none have been directly detected. But methods like dust heating turn the entire Galaxy into a kind of detector spanning a hundred kiloparsecs.
