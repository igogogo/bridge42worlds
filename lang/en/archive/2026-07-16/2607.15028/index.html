How Galactic Dust Traps Heat from Invisible Black Holes ✦

Original: "Refining primordial black hole dark matter constraints with dust heating: the role of spin and halo profile dependence"
· Shaobin Hu, Yupeng Yang, Chengjie Sun, Zihan Li, Jiafan Sun, Yuzhu Tong, Yankun Qu, Shuangxi Yi
arXiv:2607.15028 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Cosmology
The spin of primordial black holes boosts interstellar dust heating, and rotation helps to tighten constraints on their contribution to dark matter.
Abstract

The rotation of primordial black holes (candidates for dark matter) has been accounted for, which amplifies the heating of interstellar dust by Hawking radiation (the energy emitted by black holes). A comparison of five dark matter halo models showed that the isothermal profile imposes the strictest constraints, and a higher spin rules out a larger fraction of black holes. Silicate dust grains heat up more than graphite ones, and for rapidly spinning holes, the dark matter fraction is limited to 0.01%. Analogy: spin turns a black hole into an invisible space heater for cosmic dust.

Dark matter is invisible to telescopes, but its gravity was revealed half a century ago by Vera Rubin. Since then, physicists have explored dozens of hypotheses, and one of the most elegant is primordial black holes, formed in the first moments after the Big Bang. No new physics: just objects from the Standard Model and general relativity. But how do you detect something that emits no light? The answer came from an unexpected place — from cold clouds of interstellar dust, heated by the evaporation of these black holes.

The entire Galaxy is littered with microscopic dust grains — silicate and graphite particles drifting in the interstellar medium. Their typical temperature is just a few tens of kelvins, but any extra heat instantly throws them out of equilibrium. That's the key to the method: a black hole with an initial mass of about a mountain (10¹⁵–10¹⁷ g) emits Hawking radiation — a stream of photons and particles whose spectrum depends on mass and, crucially, on spin. The faster the hole spins, the harder and more abundant the radiation. The dust absorbs that energy, and if the heating power exceeds the cooling rate via infrared emission, the grain overheats. In this way, cosmic dust becomes a distributed thermometer spanning the galactic halo. A grain a thousandth of a millimeter across responds to the quantum evaporation of an object with the mass of a mountain — a meeting of the micro and macro worlds on the tip of a cosmic needle.

A black hole of 10¹⁵ g evaporates over trillions of years, but at its peak radiation it can heat a microscopic dust grain by several degrees — enough to spot an anomaly against the cold cosmic background.

For the first time, such an analysis systematically accounted for spin and five models of dark matter halo density profiles — from the steep isothermal to the shallow Burkert profile. It turned out that even a moderate spin parameter a* ≈ 0.5 multiplies the photon flux, and in the extreme case a* → 1 the heating is dramatically amplified. The strictest constraints came from the isothermal profile for silicate grains: the fraction of primordial black holes in dark matter (fPBH) cannot exceed 0.01% in the mass range 10¹⁵–10¹⁶ g. Graphite grains are slightly more forgiving — the limit is twice as high. These numbers were obtained through numerical simulations of evaporation spectra and heat transfer, and they significantly fill gaps between classical constraints from lensing and gamma-ray background.

The method turns the entire Galaxy into a detector: trillions of microscopic thermometers sensitive to ghostly heat. Mapping temperature anomalies in dust via infrared surveys could reveal clusters of primordial holes and perhaps point to their origin. This is a bridge between the physics of quantum evaporation and large-scale cosmology — fittingly built on the entropy calculated by Jacob Bekenstein and Stephen Hawking. The next step includes accounting for the actual dust distribution in the disk and 3D modeling of radiation transfer. If primordial black holes truly exist, interstellar dust may become the very signal that reveals their whereabouts and tells the story of the Universe's turbulent youth.

🎯 No primordial black hole has ever been directly detected. But methods like dust heating turn the entire Galaxy into a detector a hundred kiloparsecs across — where dust grains act as pixels on a thermal map of dark matter. And the same dust that obscures the Galactic center may yet reveal the nature of the hidden mass.
