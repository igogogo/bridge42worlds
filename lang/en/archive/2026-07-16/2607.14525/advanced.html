How to Weigh the Invisible: Redshift Tomography of Unresolved Galaxies from Euclid and LSST Data

Original: "Probabilistic redshift estimation of unresolved galaxies from multi-band background light maps"
· Shun-Sheng Li, Hendrik Hildebrandt, Ludovic Van Waerbeke
arXiv:2607.14525 · 2026-07-16 · CC BY · 4 min · Cosmology Instrumentation
Scientists have reconstructed for the first time the redshift distribution of a galaxy population that cannot be seen individually, using multi-band background maps and neural network flows.
Abstract

For unresolved galaxy populations, a methodology is proposed for estimating redshift distributions from multi-band background maps using conditional normalizing flows trained on realistic simulations. With image quality expected from the synergy of Euclid and Rubin LSST, sub-percent accuracy (mean and standard deviation of flux-weighted distributions) is achieved with statistical sample correspondence. The method is robust to photometric incompleteness through imputation. Reliability diagnostics are built in by including observational quantities as target variables. Tomography at the map level is demonstrated: pixel-level details are preserved, yielding nearly sub-percent accuracy for tomographic subsamples. This opens the way to component separation, signal interpretation, and cosmological inferences from optical background fluctuations.

Context

Why does it matter? Precise knowledge of the redshift distribution of unresolved galaxies is key to extracting cosmological information from the cosmic optical background. Unlike the well-studied cosmic microwave background, the optical background contains the combined light of all stars and star formation processes over the history of the expansion of the universe. This makes it a potential window into the reionization epoch and a testbed for dark matter hypotheses. However, the bright foreground from interstellar dust (zodiacal light) long hindered observations. Now, thanks to space telescopes like Hubble and James Webb, along with ground-based surveys like LSST, we can finally study this background. It is here that the work of Nancy Grace Roman, a pioneer of space telescopes, gains new resonance.

Methods

How was it done? Researchers created realistic simulations of multi-band images combining data from the Euclid space mission and the LSST ground survey, using numerical simulations based on the Flagship catalog. They then applied a modern machine learning technique — conditional normalizing flows — to estimate redshift distributions from pixel values in multi-band photometry. The key idea: a neural network learns to transform a simple distribution into a complex target distribution, conditioned on the observed fluxes in ten filters. The model builds upon the Friedmann equations, describing the expansion of the universe, and Hubble's law, linking redshift to distance. We also note the contribution of Vera Rubin, whose observations of galaxy rotation indicated the existence of dark matter, which can be explored through background fluctuations.

Results

What did they find? The model achieved subpercent accuracy (error less than 1%) in determining the mean and standard deviation of the flux-weighted redshift distribution on an independent sample. Even when artificially removing 20% of the data or whole photometric bands, applying simple imputation strategies (replacing missing values with the mean or extrapolating) restored accuracy to the original level. When the noise level was systematically shifted by ±3σ, the network showed noticeable deviations in predicting the observable — the root-mean-square noise deviation — providing a built-in diagnostic indicator of model unreliability. This is especially important, as it's impossible to directly verify the correctness of redshift distributions in real data. Tomographic analysis — dividing pixels into four redshift bins — demonstrated errors at the subpercent level in all bins, except for edge effects at low and high z. Thus, Edwin Hubble, who discovered the expansion of the universe, could have assessed the evolution of large-scale structure with such maps.

Implications

Significance for science: The results mean that unresolved galaxies, which make up the majority in the universe and are usually lost in deep surveys, can now be used as full-fledged cosmological tracers. This opens a new observational window, complementing traditional catalogs of individual galaxies. It becomes possible to study the reionization era, the distribution of dark matter, and to test models of star formation through the statistics of spatial fluctuations in the optical background. The proposed approach, which includes observable diagnostic quantities in the target variables, sets a new standard for uncertainty quantification in machine learning-based problems in astrophysics.

Future development

Future developments: In the future, one could train ensembles of models on various cosmological simulations (hydrodynamic, semi-analytic) to estimate systematic errors. Accounting for additional astrophysical backgrounds — from Galactic cirrus to intracluster light — would bring the method closer to real data. Increasing map resolution (pixels with fewer galaxies) will test the method's limits and provide access to small scales. Integration with radio observations and cross-correlations with catalogs of resolved galaxies promise breakthroughs in understanding the early epoch of star formation and the nature of dark matter.

Impact

Will impact observational cosmology, extragalactic astrophysics, data analysis from major surveys (Euclid, LSST, Roman Space Telescope), as well as the development of machine learning methods in astronomy.

Next steps

Next steps include validation on real Euclid Q1 data, developing methods for background subtraction, and calibration on hydrodynamic simulations with more complete treatment of the interstellar medium.

Key open problems

Connection to unsolved problems: The method directly relates to the nature of dark matter (axions, dark photons) through their hypothetical glow in the optical band, as well as the problem of missing light in the ultraviolet and infrared backgrounds. Refining redshift distributions for unresolved galaxies will help alleviate tensions in measurements of the cosmic star formation rate and better understand the expansion of the universe.

🎯 Although the Hubble Telescope can see galaxies down to 30th magnitude, about 90% of galaxies in the universe remain unresolved even for it. Their combined light forms a faint diffuse background that carries information about the very first stars.

🎬 The idea of reading the cosmic background echoes Isaac Asimov's novel 'The End of Eternity,' where observers analyze 'temporal radiation' to understand time structures. Here, astrophysicists analyze the 'optical noise' of the universe to reconstruct its history.
