The Death of Quantum Harmony: When Spin ½ Kills Supercurrent ✦

Original: "Coherent and Incoherent Interfacial Spin Transport: Quantum-to-Classical Crossover in Spin Superfluids"
· A. R. Moura, L. S. L. Barbosa
arXiv:2606.28877 · 2026-06-27 · CC BY · 3 min · Mesoscale Strongly Correlated
With minimal spin ½, a single local moment flip collapses the entire phase coherence of the condensate, nullifying the interfacial supercurrent.
Abstract

Spin transfer across the interface in a metal/magnetic insulator/metal trilayer has been investigated. It is shown that spin conductivity (a measure of transfer efficiency) consists of a coherent part, akin to a laser beam (from spin superfluidity), and an incoherent part generated by thermal magnons (quanta of vibrations). In the quantum limit S=1/2, the coherent contribution vanishes at temperature T_coh, while the incoherent one grows quadratically at low temperatures and is suppressed with increasing spin. These results link quantum fluctuations to macroscopic dynamics, paving the way for coherent spintronic devices.

Spintronics promises to transmit information not by charge but by spin, ridding microelectronics of its main enemy — Joule heating. High hopes are placed on magnetic insulators: inside them, spin current can be carried by magnons or a single spin condensate — a macroscopic quantum state, akin to a superconductor without resistance. But the eternal question: how long does such a current remain coherent amid thermal storms? The answer lies in a microscopic drama at the interface — like in a quantum choir, where one false note can ruin the entire symphony.

The superfluidity criterion, formulated by Lev Landau in 1941, states: a liquid flows without friction as long as its velocity is below a critical value. Here, the same principle applies to spins: as long as the condensate “keeps in step,” the current flows unimpeded.

Imagine a quantum choir. Each spin is a voice; in unison, they create a spin condensate — pure harmony. Interfacial conductance is like transmitting a melody into the next room: electrons in the normal metal excite magnetic oscillations that permeate the insulator. But a single spin-flip, like a false note, can destroy the entire ensemble. For a giant choir (spin S→∞), this trifle goes unnoticed, but for a chamber duet (S=½), it’s catastrophic. The calculations laid bare precisely this difference.

By applying a self-consistent harmonic approximation and microscopic linear response theory to a model of a ferromagnetic insulator, the researchers arrived at an elegant quantum-field transition in the long-wavelength limit. Here, magnon excitations obey Bose-Einstein statistics. The central discovery: the spin-mixing conductance g↑↓ splits into a condensate (g_cond) and a fluctuation (g_fluct) part. At zero temperature, fluctuations are absent, and the current flows in an orderly manner. With heating, however, incoherent excitations appear, their contribution growing quadratically ~T² — like entropic noise in a disturbed hive.

For spins S≥3/2, the coherence temperature increases with strengthening easy-plane anisotropy. In the isotropic Heisenberg limit, the condensate channel is particularly resilient: the broadened magnon band suppresses thermal depletion of the order parameter.

A tragic plot unfolds at S=½. Here, the condensate conductance collapses to zero at T_coh≈0.85 T_c — well before the Curie point. The reason: a single spin-flip at the boundary flips the local moment entirely, instantly disrupting the phase of the entire condensate. This is quantum decoherence in its purest form: the system loses its “melody” and drowns in thermal noise. For larger spins, macroscopic magnetization saves the day: like an experienced conductor, it restores order even after several false notes. Strikingly, the very spin-½ — a candidate for qubits — proves fatally fragile in a macroscopic choir; the quantum world does not forgive a small ensemble.

These results paint a clear engineering path. By choosing materials with large spin and controlling magnetic anisotropy, one can build spin-current transmission lines without Joule losses — ideal for cryogenic spintronics and magnon-based quantum computers. Moreover, this work builds a bridge between microscopic strong-correlation theory and the hydrodynamics of the spin condensate, bringing closer the moment when electrodynamics and quantum thermodynamics merge into a unified description of coherent transport.

In prospect — experimental verification of the sharp conductance jump in S=½ structures, along with studying the influence of disorder and spin-orbit interaction. Perhaps new quantum superpositions await us, defying even the second law. After all, the interface’s entropy itself grows according to quantum laws, and to control it is to control the boundary between the micro- and macroworld.
