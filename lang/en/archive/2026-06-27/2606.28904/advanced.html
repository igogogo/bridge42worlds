Three-faced superconductivity: How magnetic fields spawn new quantum phases in uranium ditelluride ✦

Original: "Unusual upper critical field in UTe2 revealed by magnetotransport measurements up to 42 T"
· Macha Méplan, Ilya Sheikin, Pierre Pugnat, Romain Barbier, François Debray, Cédric Grandclément, Steffen Krämer, Yuriy Krupko, Frédéric Molinié, Kévin Paillot, Robert Pankow, Rolf Pfister, Luc Ronayette, Benjamin Vincent, Charles Simon, Midori Amano Patino, Gérard Lapertot, Georg Knebel, Dai Aoki
arXiv:2606.28904 · 2026-06-27 · CC BY · 3 min
Strongly Correlated Superconductivity
Scientists in extreme magnetic fields up to 42 T discovered that the superconductor UTe2 exhibits three distinct superconducting phases, with two of them induced by the field itself.
Abstract

The heavy-fermion superconductor UTe2 at ambient pressure exhibits three superconducting phases, two of which are induced by a magnetic field. The goal of this work was to determine whether the SC3 phase is confined to the spin-polarized state above the metamagnetic transition (~34 T) or develops at lower fields. Magnetoresistance measurements were performed on a high-quality single crystal of UTe2 in static fields up to 42 T in the (bc) plane at temperatures down to 0.35 K. It was found that at 0.35 K, the SC3 phase is first detected when the field is tilted 20° away from the b-axis. At larger angles, a resistance maximum (indicating the onset of SC3) is observed below Hm, while zero resistance is achieved only above Hm over the entire angular range. A refined field–angle phase diagram is constructed. Moreover, it is shown that at 21°, SC3 is already suppressed at 1 K, whereas at 24° it persists down to ~1 K. Near the c-axis, Shubnikov–de Haas oscillations were detected; their frequencies agree with previous data. The field dependence of the fundamental frequency and effective mass is discussed.

Context

Superconductors usually dislike magnetic fields—they break up Cooper pairs and suppress superconductivity. However, in some strongly correlated materials like UTe2, the field not only doesn’t kill it but actually gives rise to new superconducting phases. Understanding these exotic states, where quantum superposition of spin states plays a key role, is crucial for fundamental physics and could pave the way to high-field superconductivity.

Methods

Researchers grew a high-quality single crystal of UTe2 with a record residual resistivity ratio (RRR=576) and a critical temperature of 2.05 K. The sample was placed in an electromagnetic system generating static fields up to 42 T, and resistance was measured using a four-probe method while rotating the field in the bc plane. The temperature was lowered to 0.35 K using a ³He evaporation refrigerator, allowing detection of even the slightest changes in the wave properties of electrons via Shubnikov–de Haas oscillations.

Results

The experiment confirmed that with the field oriented along the b axis, the low-field superconducting phase SC1 transitions to phase SC2 at about 20 T, which persists up to the metamagnetic transition Hm≈34 T. When the field is tilted more than 20 degrees toward the c axis, a third phase SC3 appears, with zero resistance achieved only above Hm, although a resistance maximum (a sign of an emerging superconducting state) is observed just below. These data enabled the construction of a detailed 'angle-field' diagram. Measurements of the temperature dependence at 21° and 24° showed that SC2 and SC3 can coexist in a narrow range, and the critical temperature of SC3 increases sharply with angle. Quantum oscillations (the Shubnikov–de Haas effect) with a frequency of about 3.7 kT reflect the quantization of electron orbits obeying Fermi-Dirac statistics and the Pauli exclusion principle; they correspond to heavy carriers with an effective mass of 30–40 m0 and indicate a weak field-induced modification of the condensate of electron pairs governed by Bose-Einstein statistics.

Implications

The results question the Jaccarino–Peter effect mechanism (compensation of the external field by internal exchange), since the SC3 phase does not extend beyond the spin-polarized phase. Instead, the data support a scenario in which quantum-entangled Cooper pairs form due to critical magnetic fluctuations that intensify at the metamagnetic transition. This strengthens the link between quantum fluctuations and unconventional superconductivity and adds to the family of uranium superconductors (URhGe, UCoGe) with similar behavior.

Future development

Further research will aim to uncover the microscopic nature of pairing in the SC3 phase—using neutron scattering, NMR, and heat capacity measurements in extreme fields. Theorists will need to build a model describing the evolution of the Fermi surface across Hm and its influence on the superconducting state. Of particular interest is studying the role of quantum fields near critical points where magnetic order gives way to paramagnetism.

Impact

These results are important for the physics of strongly correlated electrons, quantum materials science, and the development of devices operating in strong magnetic fields. Understanding the field evolution of entropy and energy bands in UTe2 may help create a new generation of superconducting magnets.

Next steps

Plans include extending the range of angles and fields up to 70 T using pulsed magnets, as well as investigating other heavy-fermion compounds for field-enhanced superconductivity. A key step will be the direct measurement of the spin structure of Cooper pairs.

Key open problems

The work is directly connected to the unsolved problem of the pairing mechanism in unconventional superconductors and the question of whether quantum phase transitions can enhance superconductivity. It also touches on the issue of Fermi surface topology under strong correlation.
