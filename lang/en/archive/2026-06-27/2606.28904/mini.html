The Quantum Garden of UTe2: How a Magnetic Field Cultivates Three Superconducting Phases ✦

Original: "Unusual upper critical field in UTe2 revealed by magnetotransport measurements up to 42 T"
· Macha Méplan, Ilya Sheikin, Pierre Pugnat, Romain Barbier, François Debray, Cédric Grandclément, Steffen Krämer, Yuriy Krupko, Frédéric Molinié, Kévin Paillot, Robert Pankow, Rolf Pfister, Luc Ronayette, Benjamin Vincent, Charles Simon, Midori Amano Patino, Gérard Lapertot, Georg Knebel, Dai Aoki
arXiv:2606.28904 · 2026-06-27 · CC BY · 1 min · Strongly Correlated Superconductivity
In the superconductor UTe2, an extreme magnetic field doesn't destroy but creates three distinct superconducting states—like a gardener cultivating a quantum garden.
Links in the knowledge graph 1

Usually, a magnetic field kills superconductivity. But in the crystal of uranium ditelluride, like a gardener, it cultivates three different superconducting flowers. One of them blooms only in the stormy region above 34 tesla. It's like tuning a radio: by changing the field angle, you can switch between states. Someday, we might be able to control quantum materials on demand like this.

🎯 Uranium ditelluride is one of the few materials where superconductivity not just survives in a magnetic field but literally blooms: above 34 T, it opens a window for a new superconducting phase that persists up to 70 T—an absolute record for bulk superconductors.
