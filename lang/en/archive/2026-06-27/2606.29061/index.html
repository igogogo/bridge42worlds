Shear Droplets: A Unified Theory of Plasticity for Crystals and Glasses ✦

Original: "Oblate Spheroid Excitation Theory: A Unified, Lattice-Free Foundation for Plastic Deformation from Which Dislocations Emerge as Collective Excitations"
· Albert Linda, K. A. Padmanabhan
arXiv:2606.29061 · 2026-06-27 · CC BY · 3 min
Materials
The elementary carrier of plasticity—a shear transformation of an oblate spheroid: dislocations emerge as a collective excitation.
1

Plastic deformation is the language through which materials tell the story of their internal stresses. For almost a century, its grammar has been the theory of dislocations: tiny linear defects gliding through the crystal lattice and carrying shear. This picture works flawlessly in crystals, but at grain boundaries, in amorphous alloys, and in glasses—where the lattice vanishes—it loses meaning. The question arises: is there a more fundamental alphabet of plasticity, universal for both ordered and disordered media? The new theory answers: yes, and its letters are oblate spheroids undergoing elementary shear.

Imagine not a dislocation, but a solitary shear zone, like a squashed droplet that tears and shifts inside the material. This zone, termed an OSTZ (oblate shear transformation zone), is an Eshelby inclusion: a volume undergoing its own shear strain in an elastic matrix. Its stress field decays as the cube of distance, akin to gravitational waves from distant mergers, and creates no long-range forces, unlike dislocations. This object is analyzed within the framework of linear elasticity, pioneered by Hooke and Cauchy. Yet, when these 'quiet' single shears line up in a chain, they give birth to the familiar dislocation—like individual droplets merging into a stream.

John Eshelby himself, who created inclusion theory in 1957, likely never imagined it would become the key to unifying the plasticity of crystals and glasses half a century later

When the OSTZ chain reaches a critical length, its total Burgers vector exactly matches the crystal lattice vector, and we obtain a Peierls–Nabarro dislocation. The mathematics of the transition is remarkably elegant: the displacement profile of a continuous chain precisely reproduces the classical solution, and the core energy and Peierls stress are derived without any fitting parameters—solely through the shear modulus, the eigenstrain γ₀, and the zone size W. In essence, plasticity is quantized by the very geometry of shear, and the dislocation ceases to be a fundamental object, becoming a collective excitation—much like Cooper pairs in a superconductor or modes in string theory, where point particles give way to extended objects.

This perspective naturally explains the puzzling behavior of nanocrystals: when the grain size drops below the critical length of the OSTZ chain, dislocations simply cannot be born, and the material deforms differently—the inverse Hall–Petch effect comes into play. But the applications go much further. The deformation behavior of glasses, the flow of grain boundaries, and even plastic flow in the crust of neutron stars or in accretion disks around black holes fit into this framework. The role of entropy (a key concept introduced by Boltzmann), atomic packing, and free volume can now be directly linked to OSTZ activation, meaning we can control mechanical properties at the cluster level.

Measurements on 41 materials—from aluminum to ceramics—confirm the theory's predictions, computed without a single fitting coefficient

Note that the critical length of an OSTZ chain for many systems is just a few nanometers—this is the frontier where classical materials science smoothly transitions into quantum design. Further verification through numerical simulation and machine learning will allow us to read the hidden dynamics of defects. Perhaps it will be possible to draw parallels with collective phenomena in entirely different systems—for instance, with hypothetical defects in dark matter. We stand on the threshold of a unified description of plasticity—from the machine shop to astrophysical cataclysms. In a sense, this resembles programmable matter from science fiction: simple building blocks, obeying local rules, give rise to incredibly complex macroscopic adaptability.
