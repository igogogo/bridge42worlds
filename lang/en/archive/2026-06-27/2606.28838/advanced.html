Classical Coherence and Biological Aging: A New Physical Perspective on Life and Death ✦

Original: "Classical Coherence and Biological Aging"
· Yehuda Roth
arXiv:2606.28838 · 2026-06-27 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · Biological Physics
A multicellular organism can be described as a classically coherent system, and aging as a gradual loss of this coherence, leading to the collapse of identity.
Abstract

A rigorous classical formalism is proposed to describe a multicellular organism as a holistic system with a single state vector in an abstract DNA-code space. Using Dirac bra-ket notation, applied purely classically, an analog of the center-of-mass coordinate is constructed, encoding organismal identity, and it is shown that the shared genetic code corresponds to a coherent phase in this space. The structure is mapped onto DNA sequence space by introducing a classical biological Hamiltonian, where generalized coordinates represent DNA codes and their distribution across cells. A time-dependent maintenance operator is defined with code-correcting and code-disrupting terms, weighted by parameters A(t) and B(t), reflecting the balance between restorative dynamics and code damage from the environment. Aging is described as a slow drift of the control parameters: as A(t) decreases and B(t) increases, the identity state loses stability, and the organism transitions from reliable code coherence to stochastic variability. Death in this picture is a transition at which the global identity state can no longer be maintained.

Context

The idea of a biological organism as a coherent system, where cells lose individuality and become part of a unified whole, goes back to classical mechanics by Isaac Newton—the center-of-mass analog describes collective motion without tracking individual particles. In quantum physics, superposition gives rise to entanglement, but in the warm, wet environment of a cell, quantum coherence rapidly decays—decoherence within nanoseconds. Therefore, the authors build a purely classical formalism, where the coherence of DNA codes is maintained dynamically, and aging is seen as an increase in entropy and a loss of this superposition of states. Earlier, Newton provided the mathematical framework for many-body systems, and now it is applied to genetic codes.

Methods

Using Dirac bra-ket vectors Paul Dirac as a compact notation for classical states, the model introduces a 'Biological Hamiltonian' in the space of genetic codes. Each cell i is assigned a code state gi, and energies Egi set the stability of the code. The code vector |g⟩ and the probability distribution vector |P⟩ are defined, allowing the organism to be described as a unified whole—analogous to the center of mass in code space. This approach, tracing back to the formalism of John von Neumann, turns the multicellular system into a low-dimensional object governed by classical equations. The key element is the operator O = A(t)|g0⟩⟨g| + B(t)|g⟩⟨g0|, where A(t) models code repair, and B(t) its disruption by the environment.

Results

Analysis shows that in a healthy organism, the coefficients A(t) >> B(t), and the system is in a low-entropy phase—all cells express a single code g0. Over time, A(t) falls, B(t) rises, and after crossing at some time t0, the organism loses the ability to maintain coherence. This manifests as an avalanche-like increase in code variability, analogous to decoherence in open quantum systems. The model predicts that before death, a sharp transition occurs—a collapse of the global state, when no single code dominates. The curves in Fig. 1 of the paper show that for an organism with a lifespan of about 3 years, the crossing of A(t) and B(t) occurs at around 1.2 months, marking the onset of aging. These results do not depend on molecular details but emerge from general principles of entropy and repair dynamics.

Implications

The proposed model turns aging from a collection of molecular breakdowns into a fundamental physical process—a dissipative transition in code space. This links gerontology with the physics of superposition and decoherence, enabling the search for universal aging biomarkers through loss of coherence. The organism ceases to be a sum of cells and becomes a pattern describable by a single state vector, and death is the collapse of that pattern. Such a view unites classical mechanics by Newton and information concepts by von Neumann in a new biological paradigm.

Future development

Development of the model will require numerical simulation of code dynamics in multicellular ensembles and the application of machine learning methods to extract the parameters A(t) and B(t) from real sequencing data. It may be possible to create 'coherence maps' of tissues, showing the degree of deviation from the ideal code. In the long term, this will allow not only predicting the moment of collapse of identity but also developing strategies for life extension through strengthening code-correcting mechanisms. Integration with quantum information theory could yield new insights into the nature of biological complexity.

Impact

The work will impact biogerontology, regenerative medicine, and synthetic biology by providing a quantitative language for describing health and aging. Moreover, it will stimulate the development of single-cell sequencing technologies and their analysis via machine learning.

Next steps

Immediate steps include validation of the model on time-series single-cell transcriptomics data for model organisms with known lifespans. Also needed is the development of machine learning algorithms to estimate parameters A(t) and B(t) and predict the critical transition.

Key open problems

The model directly connects biological aging to the fundamental problem of the arrow of time and the increase of entropy, embodied in the second law of thermodynamics. It also echoes the debate about the possibility of quantum effects in biology: although quantum coherence is unviable in warm environments, classical superposition of states can provide stable order. The idea of a Bose-Einstein condensate, proposed by Fröhlich to explain biological order, is here replaced by more realistic classical coherence, but remains an important historical milestone.
