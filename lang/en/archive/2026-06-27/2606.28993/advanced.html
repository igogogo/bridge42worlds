ASKAP J1745-5051: A 15,000 K White Dwarf and Sub-stellar Donor in a Radio Transient ✦

Original: "The Long-Period Radio Transient and Cataclysmic Variable ASKAP J1745-5051: Evidence for a 15,000 K White Dwarf and a Sub-Stellar Donor"
· Christian Knigge, Simone Scaringi, Noel Castro Segura, Domitilla de Martino, Martina Veresvarska
arXiv:2606.28993 · 2026-06-27 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Stellar
Broadband spectral analysis proves that the long-period radio transient ASKAP J1745-5051 is a magnetic cataclysmic variable with a hot white dwarf and a brown dwarf donor.
Abstract

Analysis of the broadband SED (far-UV to near-IR) of the LPT source ASKAP J1745-5051, which is also a magnetic cataclysmic variable with a period of ~1.3 h, revealed two components: a white dwarf with a temperature of 15,000 K, dominating in the UV-optical range, and a substellar donor (M≈0.05 M☉, T≈1800 K), noticeable in the Ks band. The difference from previous results is due to reddening correction, exclusion of contaminated photometric points, addition of VISTA/VHS data with PSF photometry, and the use of synthetic spectra based on atmospheric models instead of blackbodies. The substellar nature of the donor indicates evolution past the “period minimum” (the system is a “period bouncer”). A distance of d≈320 pc was obtained, only ~4 times farther than the nearest confirmed mCV. Since the radio beam likely illuminates only a small fraction of the sky, such objects could constitute a significant portion of the magnetic cataclysmic variable population, linking LPTs to the dearth of “period bouncers.”

Context

Why it matters: Long-period radio transients (LPTs) are a recently discovered class of objects whose nature remains a mystery. The system ASKAP J1745-5051 is the first confirmed LPT that is also an accreting magnetic cataclysmic variable (mCV), opening up the possibility of linking these populations. This discovery echoes the detection of transitional millisecond pulsars, which proved the evolutionary connection between X-ray binaries and radio pulsars. Understanding such systems is critical for the evolution of close binary stars and may help solve the problem of 'missing' period bouncers in cataclysmic variables. Here, it is fitting to recall the pioneer of radio astronomy Karl Jansky and pulsar researcher Jocelyn Bell Burnell, whose discoveries inspired searches for radio sources like ASKAP J1745-5051.

Methods

How it was done: We constructed a broadband spectral energy distribution (SED) from far-ultraviolet to near-infrared using data from ASKAP (radio), GALEX, Swift/UVOT, Gaia, VISTA/VHS. To overcome the challenge of a close (0.9″) interfering source, we applied PSF photometry on VISTA/VHS images, carefully subtracting the companion's contribution. In SED modeling, we used synthetic spectra of white dwarfs (Koester atmosphere models) and constraints from the cataclysmic variable donor sequence, which links donor mass, radius, and temperature via Kepler's third law and fundamental radiation laws (Planck's law, Stefan-Boltzmann law). Parameter fitting was performed using the MCMC method (numerical modeling), accounting for interstellar absorption and reddening. The evolution of white dwarfs in close binaries, first systematically studied by Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar, underpins our understanding of period bouncers.

Results

What we got: The best model describes the SED with emission from a white dwarf with a temperature around 14,500 K (dominating from far-UV to optical) and a sub-stellar donor with a mass of ~0.05 M☉ and temperature of ~1800 K (contributing noticeably in the J and Ks bands). This points to the companion's sub-stellar nature — likely a brown dwarf. The system is at a distance of about 320 pc, which is only ~4 times farther than the nearest confirmed mCV AM Her. The orbital period (1.37 hours) and donor mass allow us to classify the system as a 'period bouncer' — a cataclysmic variable that has passed the period minimum. A statistically acceptable fit required an intrinsic scatter of 0.26 mag, which is explainable by variability or cyclotron features.

Implications

Scientific significance: The results show that LPTs may be an integral part of the mCV population. This paves the way to solving the long-standing problem of the scarcity of period bouncers among cataclysmic variables. Moreover, it becomes clear that radio emission in such systems is likely beamed, and many mCVs could be hidden LPTs, visible only under favorable beam orientation. As predicted in the classic works of Chandrasekhar on white dwarfs, the properties of degenerate matter play a key role in the evolution of such systems.

Future development

How the topic may develop: Future observations with high angular resolution (e.g., using adaptive optics) will reliably separate the components and refine the parameters. Near-IR spectroscopy will confirm the sub-stellar nature of the donor and refine its characteristics. Searching for similar systems in the radio band will help assess the true prevalence of such objects and their role in binary evolution.

Impact

Will impact: astrophysics of close binary systems, radio astronomy of transients, theory of white dwarf evolution in cataclysmic variables.

Next steps

Next steps: Conduct high-resolution spectroscopy to measure radial velocities and refine orbital parameters. Obtain deep high-resolution images to resolve photometric ambiguity.

Key open problems

Connection to unsolved problems: The article directly addresses the problem of 'missing' period bouncers in the cataclysmic variable population and the fundamental question of the nature of long-period radio transients. Additionally, it raises questions about the mechanisms generating coherent radio emission in magnetic white dwarfs.

🎯 Fun fact: Despite its close distance (320 pc), ASKAP J1745-5051 remained unnoticed as a radio transient until recently because its narrow radio beam covers only a tiny fraction of the sky. If it weren't for the fortunate orientation of this beam, we might never have known about its radio pulses.

Key numbers

  • orbital period: 1.368 hours (82.1 minutes)
  • white dwarf temperature: 14,500 K
  • donor mass: 0.05 M☉
  • distance: 320 parsecs
  • interstellar reddening: 0.075
