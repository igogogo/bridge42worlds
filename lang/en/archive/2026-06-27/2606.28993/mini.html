Invisible Cosmic Lighthouse: Secrets of the Radio Transient ASKAP J1745-5051 ✦

Original: "The Long-Period Radio Transient and Cataclysmic Variable ASKAP J1745-5051: Evidence for a 15,000 K White Dwarf and a Sub-Stellar Donor"
· Christian Knigge, Simone Scaringi, Noel Castro Segura, Domitilla de Martino, Martina Veresvarska
arXiv:2606.28993 · 2026-06-27 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min · Stellar
Spectral analysis of the invisible radio source revealed a magnetic cataclysmic variable with a hot white dwarf and a substellar companion.
Links in the knowledge graph 1

Just 320 parsecs away, in the heart of the Milky Way, a dying pair: a white dwarf and a brown dwarf locked in a mortal pirouette, completing a circle every 1.37 hours. Their radio beam — as narrow as a blade — sweeps the sky, and we catch only a random flash. This discovery stitches together two classes of cosmic mysteries and says: the galaxy is full of invisible beacons, waiting to be finally noticed.

🎯 Despite its proximity (320 pc), ASKAP J1745-5051 remained unnoticed as a radio transient until recently — its narrow beam covers only a tiny patch of sky. Had it not been for a lucky orientation, we might never have learned of its radio pulses, and the system would have remained invisible.

L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T^4
The Stefan-Boltzmann law relates the white dwarf's luminosity (L) to its radius (R) and effective temperature (T); it allowed computing the temperature of 14,500 K from the measured flux.
P^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 a^3}{G(M_{WD} + M_{don})}
Kepler's third law for a binary system: the orbital period (P) and semi-major axis (a) determine the total mass. Combined with donor models, this yielded a brown dwarf mass of only 0.05 solar masses.
