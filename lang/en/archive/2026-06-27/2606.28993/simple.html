Cosmic Lighthouse: A Hot Dwarf with a Tiny Companion ✦

Original: "The Long-Period Radio Transient and Cataclysmic Variable ASKAP J1745-5051: Evidence for a 15,000 K White Dwarf and a Sub-Stellar Donor"
· Christian Knigge, Simone Scaringi, Noel Castro Segura, Domitilla de Martino, Martina Veresvarska
arXiv:2606.28993 · 2026-06-27 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min · Stellar
Mysterious radio bursts come from a binary system: an invisible companion transfers matter onto a searing white dwarf.
Abstract

Some stellar pairs emit radio bursts every few minutes or hours. One such pair turned out to be a system where a hot white dwarf (a star the size of Earth) pulls material from a very light companion. It’s like a cosmic “vampire.” Perhaps many similar systems are actually radio-bright, but we just haven’t noticed them yet. Are vampires hiding in the radio noise?

Links in the knowledge graph 1

The system works like a cosmic lighthouse. At its heart is a white dwarf—a dead star the size of Earth, but two and a half times hotter than the Sun. Nearby orbits a tiny companion—a brown dwarf, 20 times lighter than the Sun, that failed to ignite. It’s so close that its matter spills onto the white dwarf, forming a scorching accretion disk. The gas, spiraling inward, heats up from friction and emits a narrow radio beam. The pair rotates once every 1.37 hours, sweeping the beam across space, and we catch brief flares when it points at Earth.

That’s why the source hid for so long—its beam is too narrow. Telescopes helped capture it by analyzing light through spectral analysis and brightness measurements. Computer modeling showed that the system has passed the stage of closest approach and is fading—this is a late stage of binary star evolution. The light was distorted by the interstellar medium and cosmic dust, but corrections were made. An unexpected twist: the companion turned out to have the mass of a giant planet, yet its proximity to the white dwarf means it is evaporating, feeding the lighthouse. The discovery builds on decades of work: Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar on white dwarfs, Karl Jansky, who pioneered radio astronomy, and Jocelyn Bell Burnell, who discovered pulsars with a similar rotating beam.

🎯 The distance between the white dwarf and its companion is smaller than the diameter of the Sun—they would fit inside our star.

L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T^4
The Stefan-Boltzmann law relates the white dwarf's luminosity (L) to its radius (R) and effective temperature (T); it allowed computing the temperature of 14,500 K from the measured flux.
P^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 a^3}{G(M_{WD} + M_{don})}
Kepler's third law for a binary system: the orbital period (P) and semi-major axis (a) determine the total mass. Combined with donor models, this yielded a brown dwarf mass of only 0.05 solar masses.
