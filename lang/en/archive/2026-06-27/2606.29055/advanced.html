Machine Learning Modeling: How Defects Are Born in Diamond ✦

Original: "A Ginzburg-Landau theory of intrinsic dislocation-loop formation in diamond with machine-learned atomistic simulations"
· Xiaoya Chang, Arsalan Hashemi, Nima Ghafari Cherati, Mikko Karttunen, Adam Gali, Tapio Ala-Nissila
arXiv:2606.29055 · 2026-06-27 · CC BY · 3 min
Materials Computational Physics
The formation of dislocation loops in diamond turned out to be a first-order phase transition, driven by the rearrangement of chemical bonds rather than stress relaxation.
Abstract

It has been established that the formation of dislocation loops in diamond is a first-order phase transition. A Ginzburg–Landau model is proposed, with loop area as the order parameter; coefficients are derived from quantum-accurate simulations using machine learning. Atomistic modeling at nanometer and nanosecond scales revealed that interstitial carbon atoms aggregate via diffusion recombination and lattice exchange, forming linear defect structures that trigger a ½⟨110⟩ prismatic dislocation loop and two planar platelet-type defects. The transition kinetics follow Kramers theory. The transition is unequivocally first-order: about 98% of the driving force arises from the reorganization of chemical bond energy, not elastic relaxation. This mechanism, utilizing exclusively intrinsic carbon interstitials, presents a nitrogen-free pathway that complements classical nitrogen-mediated processes in type Ia diamonds, and serves as a transferable model for radiation-induced dislocation loops.

1

Context

Diamond is not only a precious stone but also a promising material for quantum computers and quantum sensors thanks to its optically active color centers. However, any crystal lattice defects can degrade its electronic properties, so understanding how point imperfections grow into macroscopic structural disruptions is critically important. The question of the origin of planar defects (platelets) in natural type Ia diamonds is particularly acute: it was long believed that nitrogen was necessary for their formation, but new data point to a purely carbon-based mechanism.

Methods

For the study, the authors used a machine-learned interatomic potential (neuroevolution potential, NEP), which reproduces quantum-mechanical calculation results with high accuracy but is orders of magnitude faster. This enabled large-scale molecular dynamics simulations of a system of 27,000 carbon atoms with 300 interstitials over times up to several nanoseconds. To describe the discovered phase transition, a minimal free-energy model in the spirit of Ginzburg–Landau was constructed, based on the pioneering work of Lev Landau on phase transitions, where the order parameter was the dislocation loop area. The model coefficients were directly extracted from the simulation dynamics, and Kramer's rate theory was used to estimate timescales.

Results

The modeling revealed a three-stage process: first, interstitials diffuse and swap positions with lattice atoms, forming chain-like defects; then they merge into distorted, unclassified loops; and finally, the system abruptly reorganizes into a stable prismatic loop with a 1/2<110> Burgers vector and a characteristic area of about 700 Å². This transition is accompanied by a sharp drop in potential energy (~679 eV) and pressure (~1 GPa)—a classic hallmark of a first-order phase transition. Contribution analysis showed that only about 2% of the driving force comes from elastic stress relief; everything else is due to the reorganization of covalent bond energy. Additionally, two types of planar defects were discovered: one (plane A) resembles structures known in silicon, and the second (plane B) matches the Hubble model previously attributed to nitrogen-containing diamonds. Importantly, both defects arose in the absence of nitrogen, proving the self-sufficiency of the carbon pathway. Electronic calculations showed that plane B preserves the band gap, while plane A creates localized states potentially useful for quantum information.

Implications

The work resolves a long-standing controversy by showing that nitrogen is not necessary for the nucleation of planar defects in diamond. The proposed approach—reducing atomistic dynamics to a closed thermodynamic model with an order parameter—is universal and can be applied to other materials susceptible to radiation damage, such as silicon carbide, gallium nitride, or tungsten. This gives engineers a tool to predict microstructure evolution under extreme conditions, directly impacting the development of radiation-resistant devices.

Future development

In the future, the methodology can be extended to more complex defect ensembles, including loop-impurity interactions. Including entropic contributions at finite temperatures will refine the thermodynamics of the transition under conditions closer to real technological processes. It is also natural to apply the trained potentials to study radiation damage in other superhard and wide-bandgap materials, where defects play a key role in optical and spin properties.

Impact

The results will influence the development of diamond quantum technologies (qubits, single-photon sources), diamond power electronics, and radiation-resistant materials for nuclear energy.

Next steps

The next experimental steps are: verification of the predicted nanometer-scale loops using transmission electron microscopy and comparison of growth kinetics with theoretical estimates. Simulations also need to be expanded to systems with impurities to replicate the conditions of natural diamonds.

Key open problems

The research directly addresses the unsolved problem of the origin of platelet defects in type Ia diamonds, offering an alternative nitrogen-free mechanism and thereby redefining the question: what is the true role of nitrogen—catalytic or merely stabilizing?
