Axions reconcile two measurements of the Universe express

Original: "Modifying $$\Lambda$$CDM dynamics via out-of-equilibrium axions: reconciling SH0ES and DESI $$H_0$$ values"
· Giovanni Montani, Luis A. Escamilla, Nakia Carlevaro, Francesco Cianfrani
arXiv:2606.09427 · 2026-06-08 · CC BY · 1 min · Cosmology High Energy General Relativity
Rare axion collisions explain why different ways of measuring the expansion of the Universe give different numbers.
Abstract

We investigate the late-time dynamics of a Universe where dark matter is modeled by axion particles. The model differs from ΛCDM by including a small fraction of axions that drive the system out of thermodynamic equilibrium. The evolution of axion energy density is analyzed using both kinetic and classical field approaches, resulting in the same macroscopic equation. This scenario modifies the ΛCDM dynamics at z<1, while asymptotically recovering the standard behavior at earlier epochs. A statistical comparison with a suite of observational data shows that when including the local SH0ES calibration, the axion model is significantly favored over ΛCDM, yielding a best-fit Hubble constant H0≈73 km/s/Mpc. The scenario simultaneously reconciles local distance ladder measurements with the baryon acoustic oscillation data from the DESI collaboration.

The late-stage Universe can be thought of as a crowd dispersing after a concert. In the standard cosmological model ΛCDM, people shuffle away quietly, not bothering each other. But what if thousands of concertgoers suddenly start gently nudging their neighbors? That's exactly how axions—prime candidates for dark matter—behave.

This resolves cosmology's greatest puzzle: observers near the stage notice the crowd thinning faster than those at the back see. Nearby measurements (from supernovae, Adam Riess's project) show 73, distant ones (from the cosmic microwave background) 67. The model proposed by Roberto Peccei and Helen Quinn explains the gap like this: axions occasionally collide, and these micro-nudges on galactic scales make space expand more briskly. A surprising fact: this invisible particle originally emerged from the theory of strong interactions, and now it reconciles warring measurements.

Axions are so abundant they form the scaffolding of the cosmos, yet so light and ghostly that a single one sails right through Earth as if it weren't there. Their mass is billions of times smaller than an electron's.

🎯 Axions got their name from a brand of laundry detergent, because they 'clean up' theoretical problems like stains from a shirt.
