Fully gated control of a parity-protected superconducting qubit on an altermagnetic Josephson junction ✦

Original: "Full Gate-Voltage Control of a Parity-Protected Superconducting Qubit with an Altermagnetic Josephson Junction"
· Guo-Liang Guo
arXiv:2607.04097 · 2026-07-05 · CC BY · 2 min · Superconductivity
A superconducting qubit with an altermagnetic Josephson junction is proposed, controlled solely by electric voltage, providing parity protection and coherence times up to tens of milliseconds.
Abstract

A parity-protected qubit based on a superconductor–altermagnet–superconductor Josephson junction with electrical control is proposed. Altermagnets — compensated magnets with momentum-dependent spin splitting and zero net magnetization — provide spin splitting without an external field. The two spin channels acquire opposite phase shifts; their interference is tuned by a gate voltage. In the 0-π transition regime, the first Josephson harmonic is suppressed, the second dominates, forming a double-well potential with nearly degenerate states of opposite Cooper pair parity. For parameters compatible with gatemon qubits, coherence times reach tens of milliseconds under fully electrical control. Altermagnetic Josephson junctions are promising for protected qubits with local, scalable, and fluxonium-free control.

Context

Decoherence remains the main obstacle to scaling superconducting qubits in quantum computers. Traditional architectures like transmons achieve protection from charge noise at the cost of reduced anharmonicity, complicating control. Alternative schemes, such as parity-protected qubits proposed by Alexei Kitaev, require magnetic flux and thus suffer from field fluctuations and crosstalk. This creates a need for elements that maintain protection without magnetic bias.

Methods

The authors used numerical simulation based on a tight-binding model and the Bogoliubov–de Gennes formalism, supplemented by an analytical theory of Andreev levels. The calculation is performed for a superconductor–altermagnet–superconductor junction, where a spin-dependent momentum shift creates two Josephson channels. Their interference is described by Cooper pair tunneling and leads to a harmonic expansion of the potential. The spectrum and matrix elements were computed using the KWANT package, and coherence times via the Fermi golden rule accounting for 1/f noise.

Results

At a specific chemical potential, the system undergoes a 0-π transition: the first harmonic is suppressed, the second dominates, creating a double-well potential with states close to an interferometric superposition under the superposition principle localized in phases 0 and π. This enables parity protection: the states differ by the parity of the number of pairs, and the charge operator does not cause transitions between them. Numerically, it is found that in the transmon regime the splitting energy E01 is exponentially small (∼e−√(EJ2/Ec)), and sensitivity to noise in both chemical potential and charge is minimal at the working point. Estimated T₁ and T₂ times reach tens of milliseconds for realistic parameters: EC ≈ 0.2–0.3 GHz, EJ2 ≈ 15–25 GHz.

Implications

The result shows that altermagnetic junctions enable combining decoherence protection with fully electrical control, eliminating magnetic fields. This opens the path to quantum information processing with high gate fidelity and low noise, removing the trade-off between protection and controllability typical of flux qubits.

Future development

Future research may aim at experimental realization with materials like MnTe or CrSb, optimizing junction geometry, and using strain control of altermagnetic properties. Multi-qubit circuits with individual gate addressing, as well as hybrid systems with topological qubits, could be possible.

Impact

The platform will impact quantum measurement, quantum sensing, and scaling of quantum processors, as well as the development of spintronics and superconducting electronics.

Next steps

The next steps are fabrication and characterization of the first samples, measurement of the noise spectrum, and demonstration of coherent oscillations, along with integration with existing readout and control technologies.

Key open problems

The research is connected to the fundamental problem of decoherence in quantum systems and the search for scalable physical qubits robust to local noise without complex error correction.
