How to predict a new material: a marriage of machine learning and quantum mechanics ✦

Original: "Data-Driven Prediction of NaCl-Type Entropy-Stabilized Oxide Compositions from First-Principles and Supervised Learning"
· Sebastien Junier, Celine Barreteau, David Berardan, Yann-Andrev Kerneur, Jean-Claude Crivello
arXiv:2607.04502 · 2026-07-05 · CC BY 4.0 · 5 min
Materials Disordered Systems
High-throughput screening of entropy-stabilized oxides using DFT and a neural network paves the way to accelerated design of functional materials.
Abstract

To search for high-entropy oxides with the NaCl structure, a high-throughput computational approach has been developed, combining DFT calculations, special quasirandom structures (SQS), convex hull thermodynamics, and supervised learning. Based on GGA and meta-GGA calculations, a reference database of binary and ternary oxides was first created, including disordered phases. Quinary disordered systems were described by SQS supercells and used to train models predicting the distance to the convex hull and the stabilization temperature for all 4368 equimolar compositions derived from 16 cations. The optimized multilayer perceptron showed an error of ~4 kJ/mol with explicit DFT calculations for only 10% of the systems. Comparison with experimental synthesis and calculated decomposition pathways confirms that the method captures the main stability trends and dominant competing phases, although absolute stabilization temperatures are subject to systematic thermodynamic approximations. The results offer an efficient route for the data-driven design of multicomponent oxides.

1

Context

The search for new inorganic materials with unique properties is a central challenge of modern solid-state chemistry. The traditional "synthesize–test" approach is extremely labor-intensive, especially when it comes to multicomponent systems: the space of possible elemental combinations is vast, like finding a needle in a haystack. A breakthrough came with the discovery of high-entropy oxides (HEOs)—solid solutions of five or more metals in equimolar ratios, stabilized precisely by high configurational entropy of mixing. As Ludwig Boltzmann once foresaw, disorder can be a driving force for stability—the second law of thermodynamics kicks in, making the high-entropy state favorable at elevated temperatures. Until now, however, there were no reliable methods for rapidly screening thousands of possible compositions to determine whether they form a single-phase ESO or decompose into a mixture of simpler oxides. This limitation held back practical application of the entropy-stabilization concept.

Methods

The authors built a multi-layered virtual pipeline. In the first stage, using numerical simulation within density functional theory (DFT), they calculated the formation enthalpies of all possible ordered oxides—binary and ternary—for 16 selected cations: from alkaline-earth to transition metals (including iron, cobalt, nickel, copper, zinc) and post-transition elements. Special attention was paid to elements with high metallicity—that is, metals capable of forming oxides with variable valence. Disorder in five-component solid solutions was modeled using special quasi-random supercells (SQS) of up to 60 atoms, which reproduce a statistically random distribution of cations on the sublattice against a rigid anion lattice of oxygen. To train the models, full DFT calculations were performed on 416 five-component configurations (about one million core-hours). Then machine learning entered the scene: three models (linear regression, random forest, and a perceptron neural network) were trained to predict the key parameter—the distance to the convex stability hull in multidimensional composition space and the derived stabilization temperature Tstab. The descriptors used were atomic number, radius, electronegativity, and number of valence electrons. The thermodynamic balance was described via quantum thermodynamics: the free energy of the mixture included the DFT enthalpy and the ideal configurational entropy obeying the Boltzmann distribution.

Results

The best performance was delivered by a multilayer perceptron (MLP): on a test set of 104 systems, the error was only 4.2 kJ/mol (vs. 6.1 for the linear model). This means the neural network captures subtle nonlinear relationships between the chemical nature of cations and oxide stability. Applying the MLP to all 4368 possible equimolar combinations, the researchers obtained a ranked list of candidates. Already known ESOs, such as (Co,Cu,Mg,Ni,Zn)O, were among the leaders: after correction with the more accurate r2SCAN functional, its Tstab dropped from 2745 K to 1249 K, approaching the experimental value of ~1100 K. New promising compositions included (Ca,Cu,Fe,Mg,Zn)O with a Tstab around 1676 K and (Ca,Cu,In,Sr,Zn)O at 1750 K. Experimental verification of three selected compositions confirmed the main trends: in some cases a rock-salt phase appeared (though with partial calcium segregation), in others melting occurred before the calculated stabilization, indicating the need to account for the liquid phase. The model correctly predicted the dominant competing phases.

Implications

The developed approach changes the paradigm for discovering new entropy-stabilized oxides. Instead of blind trial and error, experimenters now have a map of prospective compositions based on rigorous physical principles and supported by quantum calculations. This not only saves years of lab work but also enables rational interpretation of synthesis failures—for instance, revealing which competing phases hinder the formation of the target material. From a fundamental standpoint, the work confirms that the stability of high-entropy oxides is not solely determined by configurational entropy: enthalpic factors (charge transfer, oxidation state compatibility, local distortions) play an equally important role, and it is precisely their nonlinear combinations that the neural network successfully learns.

Future development

The next generation of models should include thermal effects beyond ideal configurational entropy: vibrational entropy (phonon calculations), magnetic contributions, and the possibility of non-equimolar compositions. It is also important to integrate the thermodynamics of the liquid phase and more accurate exchange-correlation functionals (hybrid, with strong correlations) to reduce systematic errors in absolute Tstab values. In the future, the method could be extended to other structure types (fluorite, perovskite, spinel) and even more multicomponent systems.

Impact

The results will directly influence materials design in several cutting-edge areas: in energy—the search for new cathodes for lithium-ion batteries with high ionic conductivity; in catalysis—the creation of high-temperature stable catalysts; in electronics—multifunctional oxides with tunable dielectric and magnetic properties.

Next steps

The immediate next steps will be targeted synthesis and characterization of the predicted top candidates, along with phonon calculations to refine the vibrational entropy of the selected compositions.

Key open problems

The article is directly linked to the unsolved problem of predicting phase stability in multicomponent disordered solids. An open question remains: what is the true role of configurational entropy compared to enthalpic and kinetic factors? Can universal descriptors be found that a priori assess the propensity of a set of elements to form a single-phase high-entropy oxide? These questions lie at the heart of rational materials design—one of the main goals of modern solid-state physics.
