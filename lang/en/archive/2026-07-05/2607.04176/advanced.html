How Faster R-CNN Neural Network Finds Giant Star Clumps in Hundreds of Thousands of Galaxies ✦

Original: "Star-forming clump detection in nearby galaxies using Faster R-CNN and $$ugrizy$$ imaging data from CLAUDS and HSC-SSP"
· Jürgen J. Popp, Hugh Dickinson, Stephen Serjeant, Lucy F. Fortson, Tobias Géron, Brooke D. Simmons, Vihang Mehta
arXiv:2607.04176 · 2026-07-05 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Instrumentation Galaxies
Deep learning has unlocked the automated hunt for stellar nurseries in the nearby Universe, beyond the reach of traditional algorithms.
Abstract

Giant star-forming clumps (GSFCs) — kiloparsec-scale regions of intense star formation with masses of 10^7–10^9 M⊙ — are ubiquitous in high-redshift galaxies but nearly absent in the nearby Universe (z ≲ 0.5). To find their analogs in modern galaxies, a deep learning-based object detection model was developed. The method extends the Faster R-CNN architecture to simultaneously process six photometric bands (ugrizy) from the HSC-SSP and CLAUDS surveys, enabling identification of clumps, their positions, and contaminants. A pre-trained Zoobot model was used as the feature extractor — one of its first applications to astronomical detection. Accuracy was tested on a large sample of real galaxies with simulated clumps injected: detection completeness exceeds 0.9, purity 0.8. The result opens the door to systematic searches for such structures in the local Universe and refinement of galaxy evolution models.

Context

During the peak era of star formation in the Universe (about 10 billion years ago), most galaxies were riddled with giant, bright star clumps—kiloparsec-sized regions where stars were born at rates many times the galactic average. In the modern Universe, however, such structures are rare, and their connection to young galaxies remains a mystery. To gather statistics, astronomers had to automate the search—and here machine learning came to the rescue.

Methods

The researchers took the Faster R-CNN architecture—a popular model for object detection in images—and adapted it for astronomical data. Unlike ordinary three-color images, here six photometric bands (ugrizy) are processed simultaneously, spanning from ultraviolet to near-infrared. A key step was using the pre-trained Zoobot neural network as the ‘backbone’ for feature extraction—this model already knows what galaxies look like, thanks to training on citizen science projects. The detector was then fine-tuned on 3,200 images where experts manually labeled not only clumps but also confounding sources: foreground stars, background galaxies, and artifacts. For validation, synthetic clumps with known physical properties, generated via stellar population models, were injected into real galaxy images to assess how well the model finds them.

Results

The model delivered impressive results: for clumps brighter than the detection limit (about magnitude 26 in the r-band), detection completeness exceeds 0.9 and purity reaches 0.8. Interestingly, adding the ultraviolet filter (u-band) increased the number of detections by a few percent compared to optical-only data. In total, across 710,000 galaxies with redshifts up to 0.5, about one and a half million clump candidates were identified. Analysis showed that detection efficiency drops near the centers of galaxies and for low-contrast objects, but remains stable across a wide range of parent system masses and star formation rates.

Implications

This breakthrough means that for the first time we can study star clumps not in isolated objects but in huge samples. By comparing their properties with distant analogs seen during cosmic noon, astronomers can test hypotheses about how galactic structure grew and evolved. Moreover, the work demonstrates that modern deep learning methods can effectively extract complex morphological features from multidimensional astronomical data, bridging the gap between manual classification and the needs of large surveys.

Future development

In the coming years, the method can be applied to data from new surveys like Euclid and LSST, which will offer much higher spatial resolution and depth. This will allow resolving clump substructure and more accurately determining their physical parameters. Additionally, combining with spectroscopy (e.g., via H-alpha emission lines) will help confirm the nature of the found objects and measure the gas dynamics within them. Studying stellar evolution in such extreme conditions will also receive a boost.

Impact

The developed approach will impact several fields. In galactic astronomy, it will accelerate the accumulation of statistics on non-steady star formation processes; in machine learning, it will serve as an example of successfully using foundational models for niche scientific tasks; in survey methodology, it will set a standard for automatic morphological classification.

Next steps

The next step will be the mass fitting of spectral energy distributions for all candidates to estimate their ages, metallicity, and stellar masses. The authors are already preparing a publication of a physical properties catalog for the detected clumps.

Key open problems

The work is directly connected to an unsolved problem—how exactly galaxies built up their mass over cosmic history. Giant clumps may be indicators of gas disk instabilities or mergers, and their absence in the modern Universe points to fundamental changes in the star formation mode. Understanding the evolution of these structures will help refine models of galaxy formation and the enrichment of the interstellar medium with heavy elements.

🎯 The foundational Zoobot model used in this work was trained on millions of galaxy classifications performed by volunteers of the Galaxy Zoo project—one of the most successful crowdsourcing projects in the history of science.
