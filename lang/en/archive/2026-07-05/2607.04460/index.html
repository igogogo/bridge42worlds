Cosmic Metabolism: Dark Energy Feeds Matter ✦

Original: "Strong gravitational lensing constraints on interacting dark energy models"
· F. Villalobos, T. Verdugo, P. Troncoso-Iribarren, N. Piña, J. Magaña
arXiv:2607.04460 · 2026-07-05 · CC BY · 2 min
Cosmology General Relativity
Gravitational lenses revealed a powerful outflow of energy from dark energy to dark matter — causing accelerated expansion to start two billion years earlier.
Abstract

Dark matter and dark energy, the mysterious components of the cosmos, may interact — this could solve two puzzles: the coincidence problem and the Hubble tension. Using strong gravitational lensing (where galaxy clusters act like natural magnifying glasses), astrophysicists tested a model where the interaction strength depends on the deceleration of the universe's expansion. The results confirm accelerated expansion and suggest that such a 'dark connection' can reconcile conflicting measurements of cosmic history. Like a thermostat, this interaction could fine-tune the universe.

1

The standard model of cosmology, based on the field equations proposed by Einstein, describes the Universe as a cocktail of dark matter (discovered thanks to Vera Rubin) and dark energy in roughly a 1:3 ratio. But why their densities have nearly equalized today — the so-called coincidence problem — remains a mystery. Add to this the Hubble tension — the discrepancy in the expansion rate between cosmic microwave background (Planck) and supernova data (Adam Riess) — and it's clear: cosmology is ripe for a fresh perspective.

Like the lungs of a living organism, the dark sector isn't static: it breathes, exchanging energy according to laws we don't yet know.

This 'breathing' approach is exactly what astrophysicists took, analyzing 143 galaxy lenses and the Abell 1689 cluster using the Hubble Space Telescope. They used Bayesian analysis to 'eavesdrop' on the Universe's pulse. Strong gravitational lensing works like a stethoscope here: the bent light from distant objects carries information about spacetime geometry, and therefore about mass distribution and cosmological parameters.

The results are staggering. In the sign-switching interaction model, the energy flow Q rushes from dark energy to dark matter, like an exhale that feeds the matter sector. The coupling constant β came out to -0.83—the negative sign and large magnitude point to a powerful energy drain from the dark component. This radically shifted the expansion history: the transition to acceleration happened at redshift zt=2.33, not the usual 0.64. The Universe 'started breathing' acceleration almost two billion years earlier than thought—and this changes the picture of when the first stars and galaxies were born.

An early start to acceleration means that large-scale structure formed in a much denser and rapidly expanding environment—a scenario we haven't seriously considered before.

These findings don't just deepen understanding—they challenge the Friedmann equations and demand a revision of structure growth models. Gravitational lenses are proving themselves as a precision tool: upcoming surveys like Euclid and the Vera Rubin Observatory will multiply lens samples by orders of magnitude. Then, perhaps, we will hear the full symphony of dark metabolism—and learn whether this exhale was a one-off, or if the Universe's breathing follows cycles we haven't yet imagined.

🎯 The sign-switching interaction model describes a cosmic 'breathing cycle': dark energy alternately gives and takes energy. But in the current phase, the exhale dominates—the flow is toward matter, fueling its growth.
