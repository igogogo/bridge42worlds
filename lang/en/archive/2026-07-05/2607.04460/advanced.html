Constraints on dark matter-dark energy interaction from strong lensing ✦

Original: "Strong gravitational lensing constraints on interacting dark energy models"
· F. Villalobos, T. Verdugo, P. Troncoso-Iribarren, N. Piña, J. Magaña
arXiv:2607.04460 · 2026-07-05 · CC BY · 3 min · Cosmology General Relativity
Analysis of strong gravitational lensing data points to a strong interaction between dark matter and dark energy, capable of easing the Hubble tension.
Abstract

An interaction between dark matter and dark energy, proportional to the dark matter density and dependent on the deceleration parameter q, is considered as a way to alleviate the coincidence problem and the Hubble tension. Data from strong gravitational lensing are used to constrain cosmological parameters in this model. The obtained estimates agree with an accelerating universe. The results support earlier findings that a coupling in the dark sector can ease tensions in the expansion history, and highlight the promise of strong lensing as an independent cosmological tool.

Context

The standard ΛCDM cosmological model, based on Einstein's field equations, is supported by observations of the cosmic microwave background and supernovae, but faces the cosmic coincidence puzzle — why are the densities of dark matter (whose existence was proved by Vera Rubin) and dark energy comparable today? Another problem is the Hubble tension: the discrepancy in the expansion rate of the universe between early (Planck) and late universe (supernovae of Adam Riess) measurements. The hypothesis of interaction between the dark components, proposed to alleviate these contradictions, involves an energy exchange that can synchronize their evolution.

Methods

The scientists combined two complementary datasets of strong gravitational lensing: a sample of 143 lenses on early-type galaxies (measuring velocity dispersion, Einstein radius, redshifts of lens and source) and a system of 28 multiple images from 12 families in the Abell 1689 cluster, captured by the Hubble telescope. For the galaxies, the observed ratio of angular diameter distances Dls/Ds was compared with the theoretical one derived from the Friedmann equations for the interacting model, within the singular isothermal sphere approximation. The cluster mass was reconstructed simultaneously with cosmological parameters by minimizing the deviations of image positions. The search for the best-fit values (Ωdm0, β) was performed using a Bayesian Monte Carlo method with Markov chains in the LENSTOOL software package.

Results

The joint analysis yielded a dark matter density parameter Ωdm0 = 0.253+0.018-0.004 and a coupling constant β = -0.83+0.10-0.08. The negative sign of β indicates an energy transfer from dark energy to dark matter, which accelerates the dilution of the latter and sustains a perpetually accelerating expansion. The reconstructed expansion history H(z) lies systematically below standard ΛCDM at intermediate and high redshifts, while remaining consistent with cosmic chronometer data within 3σ. The transition to acceleration, defined by q(zt)=0, occurs at zt = 2.33 — significantly earlier than in ΛCDM (zt ≈ 0.64), indicating the deep influence of interaction on the dynamics of cosmological expansion.

Implications

The results strengthen the hypothesis that interaction in the dark sector could be a real physical process, not just a mathematical trick. Such a strong negative coupling challenges the standard picture and calls for a revision of structure growth models, since the additional energy influx into matter affects the evolution of density perturbations. Moreover, gravitational lensing confirms its status as a powerful cosmological probe, capable of competing with traditional methods.

Future development

The topic can advance by including data from other probes — supernovae, baryon acoustic oscillations, cosmic microwave background — for cross-checking and parameter refinement. Full-scale hydrodynamical simulations incorporating the interaction will be needed to compare predictions with observations of large-scale structure. Future sky surveys, such as Euclid and the Rubin Observatory, will significantly increase lens samples, improving sensitivity to subtle effects.

Impact

The work impacts cosmology, gravitational lens astrophysics, and modified gravity theories, offering a new independent method for testing fundamental interactions on cosmological scales.

Next steps

Next steps involve incorporating weak lensing and cluster abundance data, as well as perturbation analysis to predict the matter power spectrum in interacting models.

Key open problems

The research is directly related to unsolved problems in modern physics: the Hubble tension and the cosmic coincidence problem. It also touches on fundamental questions about the nature of dark matter and dark energy.
