Majorana Knots on Quantum Lace: A New Platform for Topological Qubits ✦

Original: "Quasi-two-dimensional Majorana zero modes from finite-size-coupled chiral hinge states"
· Wenhao Liang, Xun-Jiang Luo
arXiv:2607.04358 · 2026-07-05 · CC BY · 4 min · Mesoscale
In thin films of higher-order topological insulators, the hybridization of chiral hinge states weaves four Majorana modes — potential qubits for a quantum computer.
Abstract

A method is proposed to create Majorana zero modes (particles that are their own antiparticles) in a thin slab of a chiral higher-order topological insulator with a superconductor. The slab’s edge states mix, forming helical channels; the proximity effect induces p-wave superconductivity, which gives rise to four Majorana modes at the corners. These modes form a second-order topological superconductor and are robust against moderate disorder. The results pave the way for building protected qubits using ordinary superconductors.

Building a quantum computer capable of solving problems beyond classical machines requires reliable qubits. One of the most elegant ideas is to use Majorana zero modes as qubits: ghostly excitations that remember their entangled paths and aren't thrown off by thermal noise. These ghost particles were predicted back in 1937, but only in 2001 did Alexei Kitaev show how to 'weave' them from a one-dimensional chain with exotic superconductivity. The trouble is, genuine p-wave superconductivity is almost non-existent in nature. Physicists found themselves like weavers given silk but no loom — the material is there, but the pattern won't form.

A new twist came with the discovery of higher-order topological insulators. These crystals are like three-dimensional lace: their bulk behaves as an insulator, surfaces are also coated with an insulating film, but along the hinges — the edges of the crystal — one-dimensional chiral currents flow, like threads embroidering the material's edge. These hinge states are ready-made wires for electrons with fixed chirality, but they don't feel superconductivity. Researchers found a way to make them 'dance' in pairs.

Take a chiral higher-order topological insulator crystal and make it thin, nearly two-dimensional, just a couple of atomic layers. Then the opposite hinge threads get close and start interacting — it's like lace making: when two threads are brought tightly together, their intertwining creates a new pattern, stronger and more beautiful than each alone. Thus two opposite hinge threads intertwine into helical channels at the sample's edges. Now add an ordinary s-wave superconductor as a coating. Through the proximity effect, superconductivity leaks into the helical channels, but because of their special structure, it behaves like effective p-wave superconductivity — exactly what's needed for Majorana modes. And then at each end of the channels, at the four corners of the crystal, appear quantum entanglement Majorana zero modes — one per corner. These corner sentinels are topologically protected and sit deep in the energy gap, like precious gems in a setting. Interestingly, four is the minimal set for one topologically protected logical qubit, able to withstand errors without continuous correction.

Numerical simulations show that this scheme doesn't require ultra-fine tuning of parameters. A phase diagram in the coordinates of chemical potential and superconducting gap size reveals a wide region of topological superconductivity where Majorana modes are born. Moreover, the wavefunctions of these modes form a superposition of localized states, which provides protection.

What's especially valuable is that the system is not finicky. Moderate disorder, inevitable in any real sample, does not destroy the Majorana states but merely shifts them slightly in energy. This gives hope for experimental realization in known materials, such as the magnetic topological insulator MnBi2Te4, where hinge currents were recently observed. Thus the crystalline lace turns into a working fabric for quantum computer and processing quantum information. The four localized Majorana modes can be woven into braids, performing logic operations, and their non-Abelian statistics serve as protection against quantum decoherence — the arch-enemy of computation. Back in the 1980s, Frank Wilczek dreamed of such anyons; now their ghosts materialize at the corners.

Although Majorana particles have yet to be found freely, their emergence in condensed matter is one of the most beautiful chapters in modern physics. The concept of higher-order topological insulators, proposed in 2017, became the missing link that turned a mathematical abstraction into a measurable signal.

Ahead lie detailed modeling of the band structure of real compounds and the development of detection schemes using scanning tunneling microscopy, which will allow quantum measurement of these states. The next logical step is creating entire networks of Majorana modes, where one can run quantum algorithm protected at the 'hardware' level. For now, it's a laboratory curiosity, but each successful work brings us closer to the day when quantum computers cease to be toys for the chosen few. And perhaps the key will be a skillfully woven lace of topological threads.

🎯 Although Majorana fermions were predicted in 1937, the first realistic model for their appearance in solids was proposed by Alexei Kitaev only in 2001, and to this day no experiment has unequivocally confirmed them — perhaps a lace of thin films will be the platform where they finally reveal themselves.

🎬 Handling Majorana modes is akin to manipulating anyons — ghosts of fractional statistics from science fiction worlds. In literature, they powered unimaginable computing machines; slowly but surely, we are approaching that same dream.
