Majorana Zero Modes in Quasi-Two-Dimensional Systems: A New Approach via Chiral Hinge States ✦

Original: "Quasi-two-dimensional Majorana zero modes from finite-size-coupled chiral hinge states"
· Wenhao Liang, Xun-Jiang Luo
arXiv:2607.04358 · 2026-07-05 · CC BY · 3 min · Mesoscale
Scientists have proposed using the hybridization of chiral hinge states in higher-order topological insulators to create four localized Majorana modes.
Abstract

A quasi-two-dimensional platform is proposed for creating Majorana zero modes: a chiral higher-order topological insulator in contact with an s-wave superconductor. In the bulk three-dimensional insulator, an anisotropic mass term provides gapless hinge states along the z-axis. In a sample with finite thickness along x and finite length along z, these hinge states hybridize into one-dimensional helical channels. The proximity effect induces p-wave pairing and opens a topological superconducting gap; as a result, four Majorana modes localize at the corners of a fully open sample, forming a second-order topological superconductor. In the three-dimensional limit, extended Majorana hinge modes are also discovered. Numerical calculations demonstrate the robustness of the corner modes against weak and moderate disorder in the presence of a non-zero superconducting gap. Thus, chiral hinge states confined by finite size represent a promising system for obtaining multiple Majorana modes via the standard proximity effect.

Context

Majorana zero modes (MZMs) have long attracted attention as building blocks for topological quantum computers due to their non-Abelian statistics and robustness against decoherence. The concept was first proposed by Alexei Kitaev in a one-dimensional chain model with p-wave superconductivity, but realizing p-wave superconductivity in real materials remains a major challenge. As a result, hybrid systems are being actively developed, where conventional s-wave superconductivity is combined with materials with strong spin-orbit coupling to mimic p-wave pairing. This work offers an elegant route by exploiting a recently discovered class of materials—higher-order topological insulators (HOTIs)—which support chiral hinge states. This connection to fundamental quantum information makes MZMs a prime candidate for protected computation.

Methods

The researchers constructed a four-band model of a chiral HOTI on a cubic lattice, where an anisotropic mass term breaks time-reversal symmetry and opens a gap on the surfaces, leaving behind gapless hinge states. They then considered a sample that is thin along the x-axis and extended along y, with a finite length along z. To account for the superconducting proximity effect, the Bogoliubov–de Gennes Hamiltonian with s-wave pairing was used. The spectrum and wave functions were computed by exact diagonalization in real space via numerical simulations. Robustness against disorder was also tested by adding random potentials to the Hamiltonian.

Results

In the thin limit (just 2 lattice constants in thickness), the wave functions of opposite hinge states overlap, leading to their hybridization and splitting of the spectrum into two helical channels localized on opposite edges along y. By tuning the chemical potential into the gap of these channels, superconducting pairing opens a topological gap, and four entangled Majorana modes emerge at the channel ends—one at each corner in the y-z plane. Numerical calculations confirm the presence of these zero-energy modes, separated from excited states, as seen in the density-of-states peaks. The phase diagram in the (μ, Δ) plane shows a robust topological superconducting region that requires no fine-tuning of parameters. Moreover, the modes prove resilient to disorder: under moderate disorder, the superposition of states persists.

Implications

The proposed mechanism establishes a new class of platforms for creating Majorana corner states by leveraging finite-size effects and the intrinsic topology of HOTIs. This expands the toolkit for engineering topological superconductivity without resorting to nanowires or quantum anomalous Hall insulators. In the long run, such systems could serve as a foundation for quantum algorithms based on braiding Majorana modes, an idea first proposed by Frank Wilczek in the context of anyons.

Future development

Future work could extend this to other classes of higher-order topological phases, including magnetic and antiferromagnetic systems; combining them with other root states like Majorana loops and networks is also promising. Moreover, advances in quantum measurement techniques will enable detection of these states with high precision.

Impact

The results may spur experimental searches in known magnetic topological insulators, such as MnBi₂Te₄, where hinge states have already been observed, and advance the development of scalable qubits for topological quantum computing.

Next steps

Immediate next steps include simulating more realistic materials with full band structures and disorder effects, as well as designing detection schemes using scanning tunneling microscopy.

Key open problems

The work is directly tied to a major unsolved problem in condensed matter physics—the experimental realization of Majorana qubits—and to the fundamental challenge of classifying symmetry-protected topological phases. Robustness to quantum decoherence remains a central hurdle for practical computation.

  • Number of Majorana zero modes: 4
  • Sample thickness in the thin direction: 2 lattice constants
  • Energy gap of the topological phase: on the order of 0.01 (in hopping units)
  • Sample size in the y-z plane: 80×80 lattice constants
