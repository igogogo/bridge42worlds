Quasi-interstellar objects: when the Solar System catches up with its own ejections ✦

Original: "There and back again: the quasi-interstellar objects"
· John C. Forbes, Michele T. Bannister, Chris Lintott, Matthew J. Hopkins
arXiv:2607.04216 · 2026-07-05 · CC BY 4.0 · 6 min · Galaxies Exoplanets
Small bodies ejected from the Solar System in the past may return after tens of millions of years, mimicking interstellar visitors.
Abstract

Interstellar objects originating from the Solar System, rather than other stars, could complicate upcoming surveys. This study examines whether unbound Solar System small bodies can dynamically evolve in the Galactic potential to re-encounter the Sun. Such 'quasi-interstellar objects' (quasi-ISOs) are primarily ejected from the outer Oort cloud through erosion in the past few hundred Myr, except the most recent ~10 Myr. After orbiting near the solar neighborhood beyond the tidal radius, objects on specific paths can return. The larger population of ISOs produced early in the Solar System's history is too dispersed to contribute noticeably. Quasi-ISOs are predicted to be rare, with hyperbolic excess velocities around 0.1 km/s, making them easily distinguishable from extrasolar ISOs and confirming the sample's Galactic origin. Detection of a quasi-ISO would suggest larger-than-expected Oort cloud losses or a catastrophic erosion event 10–300 Myr ago, otherwise undetectable.

Context

With the discovery of the first межзвёздного объекта 1I/‘Oumuamua in 2017, astronomers gained the ability to study material born around other stars. However, a question immediately arose: could some of these objects actually be "ours" — once ejected from our Solar System and returned after aeons? Such objects, called quasi-interstellar, could create a false signal of local origin, distorting conclusions about the composition and prevalence of комет and астероидов in the Galaxy. The dynamics of these close wanderers, whether метеориты or larger fragments, are determined both by the gravity of the Sun and planets and by the global galactic potential, and their fate is tightly linked to эволюцией Галактики and звёздной эволюцией of surrounding stars. The issue becomes especially acute on the eve of next-generation surveys that promise to detect thousands of interstellar visitors, and any confusion with "returnees" could seriously skew statistical conclusions.

Methods

To estimate the flux of quasi-interstellar objects, scientists used численное моделирование based on direct integration of the equations of motion, in which the Sun ejects a "flurry" of test particles at different times in the past. Various scenarios of dynamical heating were considered: from a smooth Galaxy potential without perturbations to stochastic processes with white noise and correlated kicks simulating encounters with giant molecular clouds. Ejection speeds were set in the range from 0.1 to 1 km/s, and geometry — from spherically symmetric to flattened towards the galactic plane. The resulting trajectories were analyzed using adaptive kernel density estimates in phase space, and the return frequency was computed through the flux of particles inside a sphere of radius 5 AU, taking into account the gravitational focusing by the Sun. Then, the results for individual flurries were convolved with Oort Cloud erosion models, including exponential decay, data from direct N-body simulations, and Monte Carlo analysis of encounters with field stars using Gaia data. Normalization was carried out both based on the current number of long-period comets and on the interstellar object density derived from discovery statistics. A key role in the dynamics was played by the laws of Ньютона: закон всемирного тяготения and второй закон, determining the particles' accelerations.

Results

The main result: the return probability of a single ejected object is vanishingly small — on the order of 10^-14 per year. However, the most realistic models, including correlated heating and preferential ejection in the Galactic plane, predict that the flux of quasi-interstellar objects into the inner Solar System could reach ~2 objects per decade (under optimistic normalization). This is still several orders of magnitude below the expected detection rate of genuine interstellar bodies. Quasi-returnees are characterized by extremely low excess velocities: v∞ ~ 0.1 km/s, making them virtually indistinguishable from ordinary long-period comets on hyperbolic orbits. The eccentricities of such orbits exceed unity by only ~10^-4, and the perihelion distribution turns out to be nearly flat out to 5 AU due to the dominance of gravitational focusing. On the celestial sphere, the radiants of quasi-ISOs concentrate in two spots at the intersection of the galactic plane with meridians offset by about 45° from the direction to the Galactic center — in sharp contrast to the isotropic background of "genuine" interstellar objects, which primarily come from the apex of the Sun's motion. The peak return probability occurs for ejections that took place 30–60 million years ago; older injections (including the bulk of planetesimals lost in the system's infancy) are dispersed across the Galaxy and contribute almost nothing. The most important factor turns out to be the erosion of the протопланетного диска and the Oort Cloud over the last few hundred million years, rather than events like the migration of giant planets.

Implications

This work provides the first quantitative assessment of a phenomenon previously discussed only hypothetically. The conclusion of an extremely low probability of encountering a quasi-ISO means that the sample of interstellar objects we will accumulate in the coming years will indeed be "galactic" in origin, and distortions from local debris can be neglected. On the other hand, if even one such returnee is discovered, it would point to unexpectedly high Oort Cloud loss rates — either due to unknown systematic effects or due to a catastrophic stellar encounter 10–300 million years ago. Thus, the mere absence of quasi-ISOs in LSST data will become a valuable constraint on the evolution of our system.

Future development

With the launch of the LSST survey and other instruments of the next decade, sensitivity to objects with low excess velocities will increase, and we will be able to either confidently rule out quasi-ISOs as a significant population or open a new channel for studying the Solar System's past. Improvements in models of the galactic potential and dynamical heating, along with the accumulation of Gaia astrometric data for ever more distant stars, will allow us to refine the encounter probability and thus the flux predictions. It is not excluded that over time we will learn to distinguish "our own" from "aliens" by spectra or isotopic composition, adding yet another diagnostic method.

Impact

The research touches on several fields at once: the dynamics of small Solar System bodies, galactic astronomy, stellar evolution, and even planetology, since the composition of the returned material could tell us about the early stages of planet formation.

Next steps

The main task remains the detection or imposition of upper limits on the flux of quasi-ISOs in real data, as well as improving the astrometric accuracy of candidate close encounters to narrow down the uncertainty in the Oort Cloud erosion history. In parallel, models of the joint action of galactic tides and stellar flybys in a three-dimensional potential that accounts for spiral structure should be developed.

Key open problems

The problem is directly connected to two major unresolved questions: what is the true mass and structure of the Oort Cloud (and hence the efficiency of planetary migration) and what is the role of dynamical heating in the thin disk of the Galaxy on timescales of hundreds of millions of years. Quasi-interstellar objects, if found, will become unique probes of the Sun's galactic environment in the recent past.
