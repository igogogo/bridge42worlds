Abstract

When a small object falls rapidly into a massive body, gravitational waves carry information about its nature. If this object is not a black hole but an imitator (for example, an exotic star), two characteristic features appear in the spectrum: a series of sharp peaks at certain frequencies, like a comb, and a sharp break above a critical frequency. Although the signal from a single event is weak and lost in noise, accumulating many such 'fingerprints' can reliably reveal the imitator. It's like determining the shape of a bell by its ring.
