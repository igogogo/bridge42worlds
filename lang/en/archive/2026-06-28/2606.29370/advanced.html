Breathing of Quantum Droplets: A New Look at Ultracold Dipolar Gases ✦

Original: "Breathing mode of quantum droplets in dipolar quantum gases: A sum-rule analysis"
· Xinran Zhang, Junli Liu, Huiyun Xiao, Xiao-Long Chen, Yunbo Zhang
arXiv:2606.29370 · 2026-06-28 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min
Quantum Gases Quantum Physics
An analytical method based on the sum rule accurately describes collective excitations in quantum droplets — an exotic state of matter where quantum fluctuations prevent collapse.
Abstract

The ground states and collective breathing modes of three-dimensional dipolar Bose gases in anisotropic harmonic traps are investigated, with quantum fluctuations accounted for. A Gaussian variational ansatz and non-perturbative sum-rule analysis yield explicit analytical expressions for axial and radial mode frequencies, validated by numerical simulations of the extended Gross-Pitaevskii equation. Predictions match experiments for 166Er and 162Dy with flying colors. Phase diagrams versus s-wave scattering length, atom number, and trap aspect ratio reveal first-order discontinuous transitions and smooth crossovers between a dilute Bose-condensed phase and a dense quantum-droplet phase. Quantum-fluctuation-driven incompressibility sharply hikes breathing-mode frequencies in the droplet regime. In quasi-2D and quasi-1D geometries, the system undergoes a phase transition and crossover under varying scattering length, marked by an abrupt peak-density shift and a continuous cloud-size change.

1

Context

In ultracold atom physics, Bose–Einstein condensates, obeying Bose–Einstein statistics and created via laser cooling, have long served as a playground for studying quantum phenomena. Of particular interest are dipolar gases, where long-range and anisotropic dipole–dipole interactions between atoms generate a rich phase structure. Mean-field theory predicts that when the dipole attraction and contact repulsion nearly compensate, the system should collapse; however, quantum fluctuations — in the form of the Lee–Huang–Yang (LHY) correction — provide additional repulsion, stabilizing a self-bound quantum droplet. This effect, predicted in 2015 and soon confirmed experimentally, opened a new chapter in the physics of Einsteinian condensates. In the spirit of Landau’s work on quantum liquids, studying the collective excitations of these droplets has become a key to understanding their unusual properties.

Methods

The authors used a combination of two powerful theoretical tools. For the ground state, they employed a variational approach with a Gaussian ansatz, reducing the problem to energy minimization over two parameters — the radial and axial widths of the cloud. Then, to compute the breathing mode frequencies, they invoked the sum-rule method — a non-perturbative way to estimate collective excitations, based on the Schrödinger equation and using the superposition of excited states. By choosing suitable excitation operators (sums of squared coordinates), they derived analytical expressions for ω_{b,z} and ω_{b, ho}. To verify the accuracy of the predictions, they also solved the time-dependent Gross–Pitaevskii equation with the LHY term, and the mode excitation was modeled by a weak perturbation of the trap frequencies.

Results

The results of the variational approach and sum rules revealed two distinctive features of quantum droplets. First, the breathing mode frequency — both radial and axial — in the droplet phase significantly increases compared to an ordinary weakly interacting Bose condensate, indicating a sharp rise in incompressibility due to quantum fluctuations. For typical parameters, the frequency can exceed the gas-phase value by several times. Second, depending on the geometry of the trapping potential, the transition between states occurs either as a first-order phase transition with abrupt jumps in density and cloud size, or as a smooth crossover. These theoretical predictions were confirmed by numerical simulations and direct comparison with experimental data for 166Er and 162Dy, where ultracold atomic setups were used: in the droplet regime the agreement is excellent, while in the BEC regime the formula provides a strict upper bound.

Implications

The derived analytical expressions provide experimentalists with a precise tool for diagnosing the phase state of dipolar gases. By measuring the breathing mode frequency via time-of-flight imaging, one can noninvasively determine the boundaries of droplet existence and map the role of quantum corrections. This is especially important for searching and characterizing superfluid and supersolid phases, where collective modes act as sensitive probes.

Future development

Further development of the topic involves accounting for finite temperature, which will allow describing mode damping and thermal fluctuations, as well as extending the method to recently realized ultracold polar molecules. Additionally, it opens up the possibility of studying nonlinear dynamics — droplet collisions and fragmentation.

Impact

The results will impact research in quantum simulation, superfluid and supersolid physics, and the development of precise quantum sensors based on ultracold gases.

Next steps

The next experimental step is to verify the predicted first-order phase transition in quasi-two-dimensional traps, where the jump in the breathing mode frequency will be a smoking gun. Theorists, meanwhile, need to generalize the method to mixtures and systems with synthetic gauge fields.

Key open problems

The work directly touches on the fundamental problem of the role of quantum fluctuations in stabilizing nontrivial states of matter — from quantum droplets to supersolids — and the mechanisms of quantum phase transitions in nonequilibrium systems.
