The Neutrino Echo of All Supernovae: KamLAND Sets Record Limits Using Neural Networks ✦

Original: "Search for Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background in the Full KamLAND Dataset with Neural-Network-Based Event Classification"

arXiv:2606.29381 · 2026-06-28 · CC BY · 3 min · High Energy HEP Experiment
The KamLAND collaboration used a deep neural network to search for the diffuse supernova neutrino background and set some of the most stringent limits on its flux.
Abstract

A search for the diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB) is presented using the KamLAND detector, targeting electron antineutrinos via inverse beta decay in the 8.3–30.8 MeV range. The liquid scintillator exposure was 9.02 kt·yr for 8.3–9.3 MeV and 9.42 kt·yr for 9.3–30.8 MeV. After applying a deep neural network for classification, 7 candidate events were recorded, consistent with an expected background of 16.2 ± 9.4 events. Spectral analysis showed no significant excess from DSNB. 90% upper limits on the DSNB flux were set at 38–43 cm⁻² s⁻¹ (model-dependent), along with model-independent limits on the electron antineutrino flux — among the tightest at energies below 13.3 MeV. The effectiveness of neural network classification for suppressing neutron backgrounds in liquid scintillator detectors was demonstrated.

Context

Understanding the explosion mechanism of core-collapse supernovae remains one of the key challenges. Fritz Zwicky and Walter Baade back in the 1930s suggested that neutron stars are born in such cataclysms, and Hans Bethe revealed the thermonuclear nature of stellar energy release. Neutrinos, carrying away almost all the gravitational energy of the collapse, serve as unique probes of internal processes. However, direct observations of neutrino bursts are limited to rare events in our Galaxy. An alternative source of information is the diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB), the integrated emission from all explosions over cosmic history. Thanks to Hubble's law, distant supernovae due to redshift contribute to the low-energy part of the spectrum, making the DSNB sensitive to star formation and galactic evolution in the early Universe. Its detection will allow us to test models of stellar evolution and provide an independent estimate of the explosion rate, including for "invisible" collapses into black holes.

Methods

The KamLAND experiment (1 kilotonne of liquid scintillator) detected electron antineutrinos via inverse beta decay (ν̄e + p → e⁺ + n) with a characteristic delayed coincidence signature. To suppress the main background—neutrons from neutral-current interactions of atmospheric neutrinos—a deep neural network, KamNet, was developed. The architecture combines ConvLSTM to capture spatiotemporal features and a spherical convolutional network that preserves detector symmetry. The network was trained on simulated photomultiplier hit times to distinguish compact positron events from extended neutron tracks. The full 2002–2024 dataset was analyzed using machine learning and numerical simulations, reducing the background to a record-low level.

Results

After applying all criteria, including the neural network selection, only seven candidates remained in the 8.3–30.8 MeV energy window, consistent with the expected background of 16.2±9.4 events. Spectral analysis revealed no significant excess, and the best fit corresponds to a null DSNB signal. The upper limits on the DSNB flux were 38–43 cm⁻² s⁻¹ (90% confidence level), depending on the model. The model-independent limits are the most stringent below 13.3 MeV. Interestingly, two events around 20 MeV led to an increased charged-current contribution in the best fit, illustrating the difficulty of separating components.

Implications

These limits represent an important step in supernova neutrino astronomy. Not only do they improve the previous KamLAND result by more than a factor of two, but they also demonstrate the effectiveness of the neural network approach for suppressing backgrounds related to proton recoil. This experience will be valuable in the next-generation detectors under construction, such as JUNO and THEIA, where similar methods could bring the long-awaited detection of the DSNB closer. Additionally, the strict limits on the flux below 13 MeV constrain the parameters of neutrino spectra and the fraction of collapses into black holes.

Future development

The next generation of scintillator detectors with increased luminosity and gadolinium loading for improved neutron capture, combined with enhanced neural network algorithms, promises to boost sensitivity by an order of magnitude. Joint analysis of data from different experiments will help reduce systematic uncertainties. If the DSNB is detected, its spectrum will provide information about the average neutrino temperature, the equation of state of nuclear matter, and the star formation rate during the era of peak activity in the Universe.

Impact

The results impact neutrino physics, supernova astrophysics, and cosmology, providing a new tool for studying the star formation history independently of electromagnetic observations.

Next steps

Improving the modeling of KamNet's systematic uncertainties and increasing training statistics; applying the method to KamLAND2 and JUNO data; combining with measurements from other observatories.

Key open problems

The search for the DSNB is directly linked to unresolved questions: What is the explosion mechanism of core-collapse supernovae? What is the fraction of failed supernovae? What is the equation of state of ultradense matter? DSNB observations could provide key clues.

🎯 The diffuse supernova neutrino background is sometimes called the 'neutrino cosmic microwave background.' But while the cosmic microwave background was emitted 380,000 years after the Big Bang, the DSNB is composed of neutrinos born over billions of years, and its peak is at energies of tens of MeV—trillions of times more energetic than the CMB photons.
