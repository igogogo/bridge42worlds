Stable Ballistic Cyclers in the Three-Body Problem: Stability Across Two Orders of Mass ✦

Original: "Stable Ballistic Prograde Cyclers in the Three-Body Problem"
· Shane D. Ross, Michael Roberts-Tsoukkas
arXiv:2606.29189 · 2026-06-28 · CC BY · 3 min
Chaotic Dynamics Exoplanets Classical Physics
For the first time, continuous families of stable prograde cyclers — periodic orbits linking two massive bodies — have been found.
Abstract

We present the first continuous families of stable, ballistic, direct cycler orbits in the restricted circular three-body problem: periodic trajectories that alternately undergo temporary capture and orbit each massive body. Families of symmetric cyclers are constructed from intersections of stable and unstable manifold tubes of the Lyapunov orbit near L1. Stable examples are demonstrated over a mass ratio range spanning more than two orders of magnitude: from the Sun-Jupiter regime to equal masses. Linear stability splits into planar and out-of-plane components. In each family, the branch with planar stability is born simultaneously with a hyperbolic one via a saddle-center bifurcation of the Poincaré map at the maximum Jacobi constant, while out-of-plane instability arises only in isolated parametric resonances. All computed families contain a subfamily that is linearly stable to planar and out-of-plane perturbations. These stable cyclers persist in the full three-body problem provided the third body's mass is sufficiently small. It is conjectured that saddle-center birth is universal for cycler families, pointing to their generic nature in three-body dynamics.

Context

Since the time of Poincaré, the three-body problem has been a cornerstone of celestial mechanics. Periodic orbits within it are rare islands of order in a chaotic sea. Cyclers are a special class of such orbits where a body alternately revolves around each of two massive components. Their search has gone on for decades, but until now all known prograde ballistic cyclers were unstable. Stable cyclers promise not only an efficient transport network in space but also the potential existence of exoplanets in binary star systems that periodically switch their “host.” This discovery marks a major step in understanding three-body dynamics.

Methods

The authors devised a geometric method using gravitational manifolds. It's rooted in Newton’s law of universal gravitation and Kepler’s third law for the circular motion of the primary bodies. With numerical simulation, they constructed stable and unstable invariant tubes of Lyapunov orbits around the L1 point. The intersection of these tubes on Poincaré sections yields candidates for symmetric cyclers, which were then refined by differential correction while preserving gravitational reversibility. Continuation in parameter allowed them to trace entire families in the space of initial conditions and Jacobi constant (Sun–Jupiter regime, protoplanetary limit, etc.).

Results

It was found that each cycler family is born in a saddle-center bifurcation of the return map at the maximum Jacobi constant, leading to the simultaneous appearance of stable and unstable branches. Planar stability (index sp) is typical for one branch, while vertical stability (index sv) is violated only near isolated parametric resonances. In all families studied, including (1,1), (3,2), (3,3) for mass ratios from solar (µ≈0.001) to equal masses (µ=0.5), subfamilies were found that are linearly stable both in-plane and out-of-plane. For example, in the Earth–Moon system (µ≈0.01215), the (1,1)-cycler has indices sp=0.82, sv=0.64. These stable cyclers turned out to be surrounded by invariant tori, indicating structural stability and a potential role in planetary migration — bodies can “hop” from star to star without being torn apart.

Implications

This finding means that stable cyclers are not an exotic rarity but a generic property of the three-body problem. They provide natural transportation pathways in binary star systems, valuable both for space navigation and for understanding the dynamics of exoplanets in such systems. A planet on a stable cycler orbit won’t crash into a star or get ejected — it’s a new class of planetary system architecture that could already form at the protoplanetary disk stage.

Future development

Future work will explore the influence of eccentricity in the primary orbits, as well as 3D and asymmetric cyclers. Extending the study to the full three-body problem, where the third body has a non-zero mass, is particularly exciting because it opens the possibility of real migrating planets. Simulations with actual ephemerides, such as the Earth–Moon–Sun system, will test the practical feasibility of cyclers for future space missions. Moreover, studies of stellar nurseries will reveal whether such orbits can survive in turbulent environments.

Impact

The results will influence exoplanet dynamics in binary systems, the theory of protoplanetary disks, and the design of interplanetary missions.

Next steps

Immediate next steps include stability analysis under small perturbations, including from the interstellar medium, and the search for stable cyclers in the full spatial three-body problem with non-zero masses.

Key open problems

This work is directly tied to the unsolved problem of planets on cycler orbits in binary star systems — a question that has remained open since the time of Newton. It also touches on the broader issue of structural stability of periodic orbits in the many-body problem.
