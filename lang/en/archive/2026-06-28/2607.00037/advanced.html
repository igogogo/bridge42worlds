Hawking Acoustic Radiation in Microlev Accretion: Tunneling Through the Sound Horizon ✦

Original: "Acoustic Hawking radiation as a tunnelling effect in Michel accretion"
· Nisha Jangid, Arnab K. Ray
arXiv:2607.00037 · 2026-06-28 · CC BY · 3 min · General Relativity High Energy
Spherical accretion onto a black hole creates an acoustic black hole, where phonons tunnel through the horizon, amplified by spacetime curvature.
Abstract

In the Michel accretion model, the transition from subsonic to supersonic flow occurs at a saddle point of the dynamical system. Introducing Eulerian perturbations into the stationary flow reveals the metric of an acoustic black hole. Outward-traveling acoustic waves are trapped by the sound horizon but can tunnel through it with an exponential drop in amplitude. It is established that high-frequency modes do not compromise the stability of the accretion flow. It is shown that spacetime curvature enhances both the Hawking temperature and the frequency of emitted phonons. The results are significant for analogue gravity and studies of quantum effects near real black holes.

Context

Accretion of matter onto compact objects is a fundamental process in astrophysics. Near a black hole, the flow becomes supersonic, forming a sound horizon—an analogue of the event horizon. Studying analogue gravity in such flows allows us to explore quantum effects like Hawking radiation under laboratory conditions. The Michel accretion model in curved Schwarzschild spacetime is particularly interesting because of the extra influence of curvature on phonon dynamics.

Methods

We consider a spherically symmetric steady flow of an ideal gas in the Schwarzschild metric. Small Eulerian perturbations are imposed on the transonic regime and linearized. By eliminating density and velocity perturbations, a wave equation for the variable ψ' = ρ u r^2 is derived. This equation contains the acoustic black hole metric, and a term with the derivative of ln(f+u_0^2) breaks its symmetry due to the coupling with gravity. Then the superposition principle is used to analyze wave packets: the high-frequency WKB approximation and the saddle-point method to compute the tunneling amplitude near the singularity at the sound horizon.

Results

It is shown that the steady flow is stable: high-frequency perturbations do not diverge. Near the critical point where the flow speed equals the sound speed, the wavenumber experiences an infinite blueshift. However, by applying Cauchy's residue theorem, one can bypass the singularity and show that outgoing acoustic waves can tunnel through the barrier with an exponentially decaying amplitude. The tunneling probability is ∼ exp(-ℏΩ / (2 k_B T_H)), where Ω = ω √(1+3a_c^2) is the curvature-enhanced frequency, and T_H = (ℏ G_s)/(2π k_B a_c √(1+3a_c^2)) is the analogue Hawking radiation temperature. Thus, spacetime curvature not only does not suppress but actually amplifies quantum effects in the accreting flow.

Implications

The work demonstrates that even in a purely ideal flow without dissipation, emission of phonons through the acoustic horizon is possible. This strengthens the idea of the universality of Hawking radiation as a kinematic effect in Lorentzian geometries. The enhancement of temperature and frequency due to curvature points to potentially observable signatures in real astrophysical systems, for instance, in the singular vicinity of black holes.

Future development

In the future, it would be interesting to incorporate non-ideal medium effects—viscosity and surface tension—which smooth out the singularity and could make tunneling more realistic. Also promising is the construction of laboratory analogues based on hydrodynamic jumps or Bose-Einstein condensates, where phonon quantum fields can be studied under controlled conditions.

Impact

The results are important for quantum thermodynamics of black holes and the search for analogue effects in systems ranging from microfluidics to astrophysics.

Next steps

Experimental testing in superfluid helium or ion traps will allow measuring the predicted enhancement and verifying the quantum nature of the process.

Key open problems

Tunneling through the horizon is directly connected to the information loss problem in black holes and the search for a consistent theory of quantum gravity.
