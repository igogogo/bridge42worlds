Infrared Pulsations of a Magnetar: JWST Unveils the Mystery of 4U 0142+61 ✦

Original: "Pulsed Infrared Emission from Magnetar 4U 0142+61 Detected by JWST"
· Jeremy Hare, George G. Pavlov, Bettina Posselt, George Younes, Oleg Kargaltsev
arXiv:2606.29653 · 2026-06-28 · CC BY · 4 min · High Energy
A new observation by the James Webb Space Telescope has detected periodic infrared emission from the magnetar 4U 0142+61, confirming its magnetospheric nature.
Abstract

Results from observations of the magnetar 4U 0142+61 with the JWST's NIRCam camera on August 18, 2024, are presented. Time-resolved observations (33 minutes, 2.5 s cadence) using the F410M filter (effective wavelength 4.08 μm) allowed measurement of a flux density fν = 22.9 ± 0.6 μJy. Pulsations were detected at a frequency of 115.059 ± 0.035 mHz, consistent with the magnetar's rotation period at the observation epoch. The pulse profile contains one peak per period (possibly an artifact of low time resolution), with a lower limit on the pulsed fraction of about 10%. Comparison with NICER and NuSTAR X-ray profiles shows that the infrared peak aligns with the hard X-ray peak, indicating a magnetospheric origin for the pulsed infrared emission.

Context

The puzzle of infrared and optical glow from magnetars has plagued astrophysicists since their discovery. Magnetars are neutron stars with mind-boggling magnetic field strengths, born from the core collapse of massive stars in supernova explosions. For one of the brightest of its kind, 4U 0142+61, it was previously thought that an infalling disk—debris not blown away by the blast—could explain the observed infrared emission. But to confirm or rule out this idea, high-resolution timing of the emission was needed. Back in the 1930s, Fritz Zwicky and Walter Baade predicted the existence of neutron stars, and in 1967 Jocelyn Bell Burnell discovered pulsars—regularly beaming radio waves. Now it was the infrared’s turn, and the JWST space telescope played the key role.

Methods

To tackle this, the NIRCam camera on JWST was used in time-series mode, observing the target through the F410M filter (4.08 µm) for 33 minutes with a time resolution of 2.5 seconds. Photometry measured the average flux, while earlier spectroscopy sessions (MIRI LRS) provided the power-law spectral index. To align phases, astronomers tapped into years of X-ray monitoring by NICER and NuSTAR and a global phase-connected solution covering six years of data. A powerful math toolkit, including Lomb-Scargle periodograms, helped tease out a weak periodic signal from the noise. All data processing applied barycentric time corrections to remove the influence of Earth’s motion.

Results

The result: for the first time, pulsations from a magnetar were detected in the infrared! The signal frequency was 115.059 ± 0.035 mHz, matching the star’s rotation period (8.69 seconds) with high precision. The pulse profile showed a single broad peak lasting about 0.3 in phase with a pulsed fraction of roughly 8%, but because of the modest time resolution (2.5 s), the true fraction could be much higher—possibly up to 29%, as seen in the optical. The flux in the F410M filter was 22.9 ± 0.6 µJy, about 20% lower than expected from the power-law spectrum derived two years earlier—a clear sign of variability. Phase comparison with X-ray data revealed that the infrared peak leads the hard X-ray (20–78 keV) peak by only 0.06–0.13 in phase, meaning they nearly coincide. A second harmonic also appeared in the power spectrum, hinting at a more complex pulse shape.

Implications

The near-perfect phase match between the infrared and hard X-ray signals is a strong argument against the infalling disk hypothesis. If the emission came from a disk, its phase would differ due to reprocessing geometry. Instead, we are likely seeing non-thermal emission from relativistic electrons and positrons accelerated in twisted magnetic fields near the neutron star surface. This is direct evidence that magnetars act as powerful particle accelerators, where Maxwell’s electrodynamics operates in an extreme regime. The detected variability is also easier to explain by changes in the magnetosphere than by thermal processes in a disk.

Future development

The next step is observations with much better time resolution (less than 0.1 s) to precisely reconstruct the pulse shape and measure tiny phase shifts between different energy bands. This will allow building a detailed model of the emitting region. Additionally, multi-year monitoring with JWST and future telescopes like the ELT will enable studies of long-term variability and its correlation with X-ray bursts. Theorists, in turn, will gain invaluable constraints on emission models in ultrastrong fields, including coherent curvature radiation and synchro-cyclotron processes.

Impact

This discovery impacts not only the astrophysics of neutron stars, but also particle physics in extreme fields, plasma processes, and stellar evolution. It also showcases JWST’s unique ability to study rapid variability in deep space.

Next steps

Proposals are being prepared for additional JWST observing time with optimized settings for high-speed photometry. The key goal will be quasi-simultaneous infrared and X-ray observations using NICER and NuSTAR.

Key open problems

The results are directly tied to the unsolved problem of the origin of infrared and optical emission from magnetars, as well as broader questions about non-thermal emission generation in neutron star magnetospheres.
