Abstract

Scientists have for the first time observed unusual activity from a black hole that underwent a tidal disruption event five years ago: in addition to regular X-ray flares (quasi-periodic eruptions, or QPEs) with a period of about 12 hours, they detected hotter and shorter bursts lasting from 5 to 30 minutes. These are superimposed on the main flare, especially during its rising phase, creating a unique 'double-layered' structure that doesn't fit current models. This discovery could point to extreme processes near the event horizon — for instance, a close encounter between the black hole and a compact object.
