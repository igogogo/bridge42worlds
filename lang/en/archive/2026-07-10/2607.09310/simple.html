Dancing black holes and the invisible scalar charge ✦

Original: "The significance of first post-adiabatic contributions for scalar charge measurements with intermediate and extreme mass ratio inspirals"
· Susanna Barsanti, Ollie Burke, Andrea Maselli, Thomas P. Sotiriou, Andrew Spiers, Niels Warburton
arXiv:2607.09310 · 2026-07-10 · CC BY · 1 min
General Relativity High Energy HEP Phenomenology
The future LISA detector will catch the scalar charge of black holes, even with simplified calculations.
Abstract

When black holes merge, they emit gravitational waves. Scientists added hypothetical 'fifth forces' into their calculations and refined the motion, like tuning a receiver to pick up a faint signal. Without this, you can misjudge the sizes of the holes, but a new kind of charge is measured precisely. Will the LISA detector open the door to new physics?

1

Supermassive black holes with active nuclei and accretion disks sometimes capture a neighboring hole, and the pair emits gravitational waves. These will be heard by the future space-based LISA—a giant interferometer.

Drawing a bow across a string produces sound. Pressing the string changes the pitch, and a mute on the bow adds a new overtone. So it is with black holes: theories beyond Einstein's ideas predict a 'scalar charge'—that very mute. Taking the model of a non-rotating Schwarzschild black hole, scientists performed numerical simulations of thousands of signals—akin to spectroscopy—and found: the charge is noticeable even with inaccurate data. This contradicts Hawking's 'no-hair' theorem—the scalar charge would be an exception.

The discovery accelerates the search for new fields, possibly related to dark energy or axions of dark matter. Similar effects are also sought through gravitational lensing, but it is precisely the music of black holes that gives the purest signal.

🎯 Over a year of observations, LISA will record up to a hundred thousand orbits of a small black hole around a giant, capturing how the gravitational 'note' changes.
