The Rocket Effect of Domain Walls: Vacuum Mass Asymmetry Changes the Picture ✦

Original: "Dynamics of Biased Domain Walls: The Rocket Effect"
· R. B. Vilhena, P. P. Avelino, C. dos Santos
arXiv:2607.09432 · 2026-07-10 · CC BY 4.0 · 2 min · Cosmology General Relativity HEP Phenomenology HEP Theory
A mass difference in the scalar field across vacuums propels domain walls like rockets, accelerating the decay of their network.
Abstract

The dynamics of domain walls in scalar field theories with degenerate vacua, where the scalar field's mass depends on the vacuum state, is investigated. Through analytical arguments and numerical simulations, it is shown that this dependence leads to anisotropic emission of scalar radiation from walls—predominantly into the region with lower field mass. The resulting recoil effect (reactive acceleration) creates a dynamical bias in the evolution of cosmological domain wall networks, favoring the vacuum with lower mass and promoting network decay. It is demonstrated that the mass dependence on the vacuum, rather than potential barrier asymmetry, is the main factor driving the biased evolution of walls in theories with degenerate vacua. In the more general case of non-degenerate vacua, this recoil mechanism acts as an additional source of dynamical bias, capable of accelerating or decelerating network decay compared to standard predictions based solely on vacuum energy density differences.

Context

Domain walls arise during phase transitions after the Big Bang, when discrete symmetries break. Their energy could dominate, clashing with observations of the cosmic microwave background and the expansion of the universe, including redshift data from distant supernovae — a notorious cosmological puzzle. Previously, it was thought that either an energy density difference between vacuums or an initial population bias was needed to eliminate them. However, this work shows that even with degenerate vacuums, a mass asymmetry in the quantum field can drive a directed radiation flux, producing gravitational-wave and radiative signatures.

Methods

The authors employed numerical simulations in simplified dimensions: (1+1) for flat walls and (2+1) with transverse perturbations, as well as in a cosmological setting with the Friedmann metric and Einstein's equations. High-resolution lattice field equations were solved. Across four model potentials, they varied barrier asymmetry (d3V) and mass difference (Δm²), while keeping wall tension identical. Radiation was recorded far from the walls, and recoil velocity was calculated from momentum conservation.

Results

It was found that with a nonzero mass difference, radiation becomes sharply asymmetric: it predominantly flows into the lighter vacuum. In (1+1) dimensions, drift velocities reached ~0.10 (model D), whereas the model with no mass difference (A) remained stationary. In (2+1), the effect persisted: speeds up to 0.05, and even when excitations were above both thresholds, the asymmetry didn't vanish — it merely weakened. In cosmological simulations of domain wall networks, the lighter vacuum gradually claimed a larger volume fraction, demonstrating accelerated network decay. Interestingly, the model with only a skewed barrier (B) produced a very weak drift, proving that mass asymmetry is the dominant driver.

Implications

This finding means that conservation of momentum, combined with quantum properties of the vacuum, can naturally eliminate domain walls in the early universe. The domain wall problem gets a novel solution without invoking extra physics. Moreover, the effect influences spacetime curvature near walls, potentially affecting gravitational-wave propagation. In models with nondegenerate vacuums, the rocket effect adds to the usual pressure, either boosting or suppressing decay depending on the direction.

Future development

Future work must incorporate plasma friction, realistic 3D modeling, and comparisons with gravitational-wave predictions from collapsing walls, which could be detected by observatories like LISA. The impact on primordial black hole formation also needs investigation.

Impact

The results will influence early-universe cosmology, particle physics, and the interpretation of data from upcoming gravitational-wave observatories.

Next steps

Plans include extending models to three spatial dimensions and including interactions with other fields, such as the Higgs.

Key open problems

The work directly ties to the cosmological domain wall problem and the mass hierarchy problem, as well as the search for sources of the stochastic gravitational-wave background.
