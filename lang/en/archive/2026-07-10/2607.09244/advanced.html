Controlled Criticality: How Screening Changes the Rules of Quantum Phase Transitions ✦

Original: "Screening-controlled dynamical criticality in the quantum Hall regime"
· Tanima Chanda, Simrandeep Kaur, Anantbir Virk, Kenji Watanabe, Takashi Taniguchi, G. J. Sreejith, Yuval Gefen, Aveek Bid
arXiv:2607.09244 · 2026-07-10 · CC BY · 4 min · Mesoscale Strongly Correlated
Physicists have separated the spatial and temporal aspects of the quantum Hall transition, showing that screening of the Coulomb interaction changes dynamics but not structure.
Abstract

In continuous electronic phase transitions, Coulomb interactions alter the relationship between length, energy, and temperature, making it difficult to isolate their impact on spatial and dynamic criticality. Two improvements are proposed: joint temperature and current scaling to separately extract the dynamic exponent z and the localization length exponent γ, and the use of graphene devices with a double graphite gate, where the effective screening radius is tuned geometrically via the ratio of magnetic length to gate distance. Temperature scaling yields a combined exponent κ ≈ 0.21 in the screened regime and ≈ 0.41 in the unscreened regime; combining it with current scaling shows that z changes from ≈1 to ≈2, while γ remains around 2.4. Thus, gate-controlled screening selectively modifies the dynamic sector of the transition without affecting spatial criticality. Geometric screening becomes a universal tool for controlling interactions and separating disorder and correlation effects in two-dimensional systems, including the fractional quantum Hall effect and moiré materials.

Context

The quantum Hall effect arises in two-dimensional electron systems placed in a strong magnetic field. According to [scientist:Lev Landau], the energy of electrons is quantized, forming Landau levels. When the Fermi level crosses the center of a disorder-broadened level, a localization-delocalization transition occurs between adjacent Hall resistance plateaus. This is a quantum phase transition exhibiting universal scaling behavior, similar to critical phenomena in superconductors or quantum thermodynamic systems. The driving force here is the Coulomb interaction between electrons, which can be long-range or screened. The question of how the interaction range affects critical exponents remained open for a long time. Traditional experiments on gallium arsenide could not separate the spatial and dynamic contributions, as they only measured the combined exponent κ=1/(zγ). To overcome this limitation, the authors of the new work turned to graphene—a unique two-dimensional material where screening can be geometrically controlled using graphite gates.

Methods

The researchers created a three-layer heterostructure: a graphene layer sandwiched between two layers of hexagonal boron nitride and graphite gates. The distance from the channel to the gate d sets the characteristic screening scale. The key idea—as the magnetic field changes, the magnetic length l_B varies, controlling the ratio l_B/d. When l_B/d < 1, the interaction remains almost unscreened, and when l_B/d > 1, the gate effectively cuts off the Coulomb potential. Using precision measurements of Hall resistance at temperatures down to 30 mK and various currents, the authors performed a scaling analysis of the data. Temperature scaling gave the exponent κ, and current scaling, related to electron heating, allowed independent extraction of the dynamical exponent z. Combining them, they calculated the localization length exponent γ. All measurements were carefully checked for robustness, including analysis of the resistance peak width and collapse of scaling curves.

Results

The results clearly demonstrate two regimes. In the unscreened regime (l_B/d < 1), κ ≈ 0.41±0.06, corresponding to z ≈ 1.02±0.20 and γ ≈ 2.4±0.28. In the screened regime (l_B/d > 1), κ drops to 0.21±0.03, z rises to 2.04±0.35, and γ remains 2.35±0.27 within error. Thus, screening selectively modifies the dynamic sector of the transition, leaving the spatial exponent unchanged. The statistical significance of the difference in z is about 2.5σ, confirming the reality of the effect. These conclusions are supported by several independent data processing methods, ruling out systematic errors.

Implications

The discovery has profound implications for understanding quantum criticality. It shows that entropic and energy scales in quantum transitions can be tuned separately. The experimental separation of z and γ paves the way for testing theoretical models, such as the nonlinear sigma model or network models, which predict different exponent values depending on symmetry and interactions. Moreover, the proposed method of geometric screening by a gate becomes a universal tool for exploring interaction effects in a wide class of two-dimensional materials, including systems with the fractional quantum Hall effect and moiré heterostructures.

Future development

In the future, this approach may be extended to fractional states and strongly correlated insulators. Graphene with varying spacer thickness will allow detailed study of the crossover region l_B/d ~ 1. Even finer effects might be observed, such as the influence of disorder or spin-orbit interaction on the dynamical exponent. It is also interesting to combine this technique with tunneling microscopy to directly visualize wave functions on the localization scale.

Impact

The results will impact condensed matter physics, the theory of critical phenomena, and the development of new quantum materials. They are especially important for engineering topological phases and quantum computing, where control over interactions is critically important.

Next steps

The next step will be to test predictions for the γ exponent at even lower temperatures and in samples with controlled disorder, as well as adapting the method to other two-dimensional systems, for example, transition metal dichalcogenides.

Key open problems

The study is directly connected to the unsolved problem of classifying quantum phase transitions beyond Landau theory. It contributes to understanding how interactions and disorder compete in low-dimensional systems, a central theme of modern condensed matter physics.
