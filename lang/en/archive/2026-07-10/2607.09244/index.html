Dance Without a Choreographer: How Graphene Separated Space and Time ✦

Original: "Screening-controlled dynamical criticality in the quantum Hall regime"
· Tanima Chanda, Simrandeep Kaur, Anantbir Virk, Kenji Watanabe, Takashi Taniguchi, G. J. Sreejith, Yuval Gefen, Aveek Bid
arXiv:2607.09244 · 2026-07-10 · CC BY · 3 min · Mesoscale Strongly Correlated
For the first time, physicists independently measured the spatial and temporal exponents of a quantum transition, discovering that screening changes only the rhythm of electrons, leaving their dance unchanged.
Abstract

In phase transitions of the quantum Hall effect, Coulomb interactions complicate the critical behavior, mixing spatial and dynamic effects. Scientists developed a method to separately measure the dynamic exponent z and the localization length exponent γ: they combined temperature and current scaling in graphene devices with a double graphite gate, varying the screening radius by the ratio of magnetic length to gate distance. It turned out that screening increases z from ≈1 to ≈2, while γ remains around 2.4 — much like a tone knob changes the sound color but not the volume. This paves the way to purposefully separate the influence of disorder and interactions in a wide class of two-dimensional systems.

Imagine a quantum phase transition as an intricate dance. Electrons whirl in strict order, each step representing their spatial arrangement, and the rhythm their dynamics. For decades, physicists could only see a blur of motion, unable to tell if the figures were changing or the music speeding up. It all began with Landau, whose Landau levels turned electron chaos into a series of Hall resistance plateaus. But this transition, like superconducting or critical phenomena in quantum thermodynamics, follows a hidden rhythm — universal scaling. The trouble was that experiments only yielded the overall pace κ = 1/(zγ) — the product of the spatial exponent γ and the dynamic z, making it impossible to separate them.

The γ enigma: the number 2.4 remains a mysterious constant—no theory predicts it precisely, yet experiments stubbornly reproduce it for decades. Physicists joke about a 'grand conspiracy' of errors, but to no avail.

The solution came with graphene sandwiches: a layer of graphene squeezed between two graphite gates. The gate distance d became a volume knob for the Coulomb interaction. By manipulating the magnetic field, scientists varied the magnetic length l_B = √(ℏ/(eB)) — the characteristic size of an electron cloud in a magnetic field. When l_B is much smaller than d, the electrons 'ignore' the gate and interact at full strength. But once l_B exceeds d, the gate cuts off the interaction, like muting a sound. Using temperature and current scaling (the current heats the electrons), the team computed z and γ separately for the first time. And then numerical models, which for years had insisted on a constant γ, received a brilliant confirmation.

The result was stunning: in the unscreened regime, κ ≈ 0.41 — a slow dance with z ≈ 1. But as soon as l_B/d crosses unity, κ shrinks to 0.21: the tempo accelerates to z ~ 2. And γ? Frozen at 2.4, as if nothing had happened. Screening turned out to be an elusive choreographer: it changes the rhythm of electronic fluctuations without touching the spatial figures. This ushers in an era of tuning quantum criticality — by altering the sample geometry, one can independently control the entropic and energy scales of the transition, much like adjusting the volume and tempo on a stereo system.

The idea of controlling dynamics through screening echoes the spirit of Boltzmann: entropy and disorder here become not enemies, but allies in tuning the quantum world.

This method is like a universal microscope for interactions in the flat world. It has already been applied to graphene, but the main trials lie ahead: fractional quantum states, moiré heterostructures, strongly correlated insulators. Condensed matter physics gains a key to classifying quantum phase transitions beyond Landau theory. And from there, it's just a step to engineering topological phases for quantum computing. By controlling the space and time of electron ensembles, we are for the first time trying on the role of architects of reality. Perhaps nature itself already uses such tricks to separate scales in high-temperature superconductors or even in the brain's neural networks.

🎯 The exponent γ ≈ 2.4 is one of the oldest mysteries in solid-state physics. No analytical theory dares predict it, and numerical models oscillate between 2.3 and 2.6. Experimentalists jokingly call it 'the universal constant of collective incomprehension.'
