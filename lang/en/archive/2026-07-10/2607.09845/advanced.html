Hunting for Heavy Charged Relics in Earth's Depths ✦

Original: "Searching for heavy charged relics in the Earth"
· Reza Ebadi, Peter W. Graham, Surjeet Rajendran, Erwin H. Tanin, Samuel S. Y. Wong
arXiv:2607.09845 · 2026-07-10 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · HEP Phenomenology Cosmology HEP Experiment
A method is proposed to detect heavy charged particles (CHAMPs) accumulated in Earth's rocks, water, and ice, with sensitivity down to minuscule fractions of dark matter density.
Abstract

A detection method for heavy charged particles—with masses between 1 and 10¹² TeV—that might constitute a tiny fraction of local dark matter is put forward. These particles, upon reaching Earth, slow down and get trapped, accumulating over time in high-exposure targets like water and rock. Concentration by centrifugation or gravitational settling, followed by mass spectrometry, is explored. The technique’s sensitivity can reach f_X ~ 10⁻²⁰ for the lightest particles, which is an incredibly small admixture to dark matter. A pilot experiment with merely a liter of water and a single centrifuge (or ≈1 m³ of rock without a centrifuge) over a month could achieve f_X ~ 10⁻¹⁰ and probe previously uncharted territory.

Context

Hypothetical charged massive particles (CHAMPs) are natural candidates for dark matter, arising in supersymmetric theories, extra-dimensional models, and hidden sectors. Collider searches for them are limited by collision energy, whereas the cosmological flux of such particles could carry information about physics beyond the Standard Model. Vera Rubin and Fritz Zwicky showed back in the last century that invisible matter dominates the Universe, and CHAMPs are one intriguing possibility for its nature. Previously they were sought in cosmic rays, meteorites, and lunar soil, while astrophysical constraints ranged from Big Bang nucleosynthesis to the stability of neutron stars and white dwarfs. However, a key problem—uncertainty in the exact distribution of stopped particles in natural reservoirs—left major gaps. Our approach quantitatively models, for the first time, the slowing-down and accumulation of CHAMPs in Earth, paving the way for robust terrestrial experiments.

Methods

We calculated the stopping-point distribution for CHAMPs falling onto Earth from the galactic halo, assuming a Maxwellian velocity distribution and using the Lindhard–Scharff formula for electromagnetic energy loss in matter. We then analyzed three target types: controlled water reservoirs (pools), natural materials—ice cores and ancient geological rocks. Enrichment can be done in two ways: gravitational sedimentation, leveraging the law of universal gravitation and Boltzmann distribution for heavy particles in a fluid, or centrifugation for lighter masses. Final CHAMP identification uses precision mass spectrometry, including charge-induced detection (CDMS), enabling measurement of anomalously low charge-to-mass ratios.

Results

The resulting stopping-depth map (see Fig. 3 of the original) shows that light CHAMPs (up to ~10⁴ TeV) mostly get stuck in the atmosphere and upper water layers, while particles with masses 10⁴–10⁹ TeV stop in the lithosphere at depths from meters to kilometers. For the heaviest (up to ~10¹² TeV), stopping occurs only via the low-velocity tail of the distribution. The sensitivity of the proposed method (Fig. 4) spans the CHAMP fraction in dark matter f_X from 10⁻²⁰ at low masses down to ~10⁻¹⁰ at high masses, overlapping and significantly improving on existing limits. A pilot experiment with 1 liter of water and one centrifuge over one month of exposure can achieve f_X ~ 10⁻¹⁰, while a year-long exposure of 10⁷ m³ of water with gravitational enrichment reaches f_X ~ 10⁻¹⁸. For rocks hundreds of millions of years old, even a cubic-meter sample provides competitive sensitivity.

Implications

The protocol developed here opens a new era in heavy relic searches, turning Earth into a giant dark matter particle detector. It overcomes the main drawback of previous terrestrial searches—uncertainty about sample history—and provides a reproducible, scalable approach. This method can be immediately implemented in labs using mass spectrometry and centrifugation, and it promises to greatly widen the window into physics beyond the Standard Model.

Future development

In the near future, pilot experiments with controlled water reservoirs and ice cores are planned, along with optimization of enrichment protocols using hybrid gravitational-centrifugal schemes. Further development could include dedicated underground laboratories for analyzing deep rocks and the use of quantum sensors to detect individual passages of charged particles. CDMS technology will be refined for reliable detection of particles with masses up to 10¹² TeV.

Impact

The results will impact several fields: particle physics (the search for new physics), cosmology (the nature of dark matter), astrophysics (evolution of compact objects), and geophysics (new methods for rock dating and analysis). Multidisciplinary collaborations uniting physicists, geologists, and glaciologists may emerge.

Next steps

Immediate next steps include: (1) implementing a pilot experiment with a liter water sample; (2) selecting and characterizing suitable geological samples with known depth histories; (3) developing and testing special containers with a trapping coating (e.g., tin) to prevent loss of superheavy CHAMPs.

Key open problems

The search for CHAMPs is directly linked to one of the greatest unsolved problems in physics—identifying the particles that make up dark matter. It also touches on the naturalness problem in supersymmetric theories, the origin of baryon asymmetry, and the limits of collider experiments. Detecting heavy charged particles could shed light on Grand Unified Theories and the physics of the early Universe.
