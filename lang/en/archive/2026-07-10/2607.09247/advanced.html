Time crystal on Rydberg atoms lives 17 hours ✦

Original: "Observation of a Rydberg-atom time crystal with an ultralong lifetime"
· Qi-Feng Wang, Tian-Yu Han, Ya-Jun Wang, Dong-Yang Zhu, Chao Yu, Yu Ma, Yi-Ming Yin, Guang-Can Guo, Bang Liu, Li-Hua Zhang, Dong-Sheng Ding, Bao-Sen Shi
arXiv:2607.09247 · 2026-07-10 · CC BY · 3 min
Quantum Gases Quantum Physics
Chinese physicists have created a continuous time crystal in an ensemble of Rydberg atoms, achieving a record lifetime of 16.95 hours.
Abstract

Continuous time crystals are a nonequilibrium quantum phase that spontaneously breaks time-translation symmetry without periodic driving, manifesting as stable, long-lived oscillations. Their lifetime is typically limited by the instability of the limit cycle at the edge between nonlinear feedback and dissipation. We experimentally realized an ultralong-lived time crystal using Rydberg atoms in a controlled dissipative many-body system. Long-range interactions and a specially engineered dissipative environment suppress heating and decay. The key factor is closing the Liouvillian gap and achieving a nearly zero real part of the Liouvillian spectrum. Oscillation duration exceeded 16.95 hours—orders of magnitude longer than all known counterparts. This result provides a robust platform for exploring autonomous nonequilibrium phases and opens prospects for quantum sensing and continuous quantum information processing.

1

Context

Long-lived nonequilibrium states in quantum systems challenge the foundations of statistical mechanics. According to the ergodic hypothesis, isolated systems thermalize, but strong dissipation and interactions can give rise to stable nonequilibrium phases such as continuous time crystals (CTCs). These objects spontaneously break continuous time-translation symmetry, manifesting as undamped periodic oscillations. Experimentally, CTCs have been realized in ultracold atoms and spin systems, but their lifetimes were limited to milliseconds or minutes due to dephasing and heating.

Methods

The experiment used a cloud of cesium atoms in a vapor cell, excited by three lasers (852 nm, 1470 nm, 780 nm) to Rydberg states (49P3/2). An external radiofrequency field (8.2 MHz) modulated the Rydberg levels, creating Floquet dressed states and effective nonlinearity via long-range van der Waals interactions. The dissipative environment was controlled through spontaneous decay of Rydberg states and optical pumping. The optical response was recorded via probe beam transmission, allowing spectroscopic tracking of population dynamics. System parameters (RF field amplitude and laser detuning) were carefully tuned to drive the system into a regime with a nearly zero real part of the Liouvillian spectrum, minimizing decay.

Results

At optimal parameters (field 45.6 V/m, detuning 0 MHz), self-oscillations at 15.03 kHz persisted for 16.95 hours (61,020 s), confirmed by continuous signal recording. The frequency spectrum remained stable with a maximum drift of just 30.5 Hz. Phase-wander analysis revealed superlinear growth of the mean square displacement (MSD ∝ τ^1.89), indicating correlated drift rather than simple diffusion. The Allan deviation reached a minimum of 5.3×10⁻⁵ at averaging times of 12,406 s, corresponding to an absolute frequency instability of about 0.79 Hz. The critical dependence of lifetime on field amplitude and laser detuning follows power laws, consistent with a theoretical model based on Liouvillian analysis.

Implications

Achieving a 16.95-hour CTC lifetime means the dissipative gap in the Liouvillian spectrum can be closed with high precision, realizing a practically dissipationless limit cycle. This proves the existence of stable nonequilibrium phases at macroscopic scales and enables precision sensing of weak fields through extremely long signal accumulation. The dissipationless nature implies nearly zero entropy production in the oscillatory regime. Moreover, the long-lived phase coherence could serve as a continuous quantum memory, storing information in the oscillation phase.

Future development

Further lifetime improvements may come from suppressing residual technical noise and cooling the atomic ensemble. Of particular interest is exploring the role of quantum fluctuations — transitioning from mean-field to a full quantum description, where finite entanglement may limit lifetime even with a closed Liouvillian gap. Coupling to mechanical oscillators or microwave resonators could also create hybrid time crystals.

Impact

The work impacts quantum metrology by providing a new type of oscillator with record stability, and quantum computing, where stable nonequilibrium states could serve as memory elements.

Next steps

Immediate next steps include investigating the influence of finite temperature and interparticle correlations on phase diffusion, as well as experimentally demonstrating quantum enhancement in sensor applications.

Key open problems

The results directly touch on the arrow of time and thermalization, testing the applicability limits of the second law of thermodynamics in open quantum systems. Observing suppressed relaxation ties into the general principles of the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, making time crystals a testbed for nonequilibrium quantum statistics.
