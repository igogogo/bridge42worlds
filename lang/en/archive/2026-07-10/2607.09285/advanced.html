Neutrino Growth of Primordial Black Holes: Revisiting Mass Evolution in the Early Universe ✦

Original: "Primordial Black Hole mass growth from neutrinos during radiation era"
· Maël Gonin
arXiv:2607.09285 · 2026-07-10 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · Cosmology HEP Theory
The study shows that primordial black holes can absorb neutrinos and significantly increase their mass during the radiation era, affecting their observability.
Abstract

A new picture of primordial black hole mass evolution via neutrino absorption is proposed. Using a semiclassical approach and analysis of early plasma kinetics, the thermal neutrino absorption by a population of primordial black holes in the mass range of 10^{-3} to 10^9 solar masses is recalculated. A significant mass gain is shown for intermediate and supermassive holes, altering the predicted peak of electron-positron pair annihilation in the universe's thermal history. Depending on the collapse fraction, an additional peak in the mass distribution may appear at intermediate values. Since black holes accrete thermal neutrinos, the fraction of dark matter contained in primordial black holes is also revised. These results update previous notions and are essential for interpreting observational dark matter data.

Context

Primordial black holes (PBH) are among the most intriguing candidates for dark matter. Their existence could explain not only the nature of hidden mass but also recent gravitational wave signals detected by LIGO. However, until now it was believed that during the radiation era, when the Universe was filled with plasma of particles and radiation, PBHs could not accrete enough matter due to their tiny gravitational radius compared to the mean free path of radiation. This belief was based on an argument by Stephen Hawking and colleagues, as well as classical solutions to Einstein's equations. The key scale is set by the Schwarzschild radius, which is negligibly small for light PBHs. The new study changes this paradigm by considering weakly interacting particles as a source of “food” for black holes.

Methods

The authors used a semiclassical approach, treating PBHs as black bodies that absorb neutrinos from the thermal bath of the early Universe. They computed the mass growth rate by solving an ordinary differential equation for the ratio R = M_absorbed / M_initial. The equation accounts for the geometric high-frequency radiation absorption cross-section and the kinetics of the primordial plasma, including changes in effective degrees of freedom. A key parameter is the neutrino mean free path, compared to the Schwarzschild radius; absorption is activated by a smooth weight function W = exp(-κ r_s/λ_ν). Calculations were performed for various values of parameter κ (0.5; 1; 2) and collapse fraction γ. The temperature range covers the era after the cosmological quark-hadron transition, where neutrinos contribute significantly to the energy density.

Results

The results show that significant mass growth occurs for PBHs with initial masses between 10^3 and 10^7 solar masses. Holes in the vicinity of 10^3–10^5 M⊙ grow most actively — the “sweet spot” of absorption, where the available fraction of neutrino energy is maximal and the difference between formation time and absorption onset is minimal. For γ = 0.55 and κ = 1, the final growth factor R reaches hundreds for individual masses, and the dark matter fraction in PBHs f_PBH increases from 0.1 to 0.1129. The mass spectrum is substantially deformed: the peak associated with e⁺e⁻ annihilation shifts to larger masses, and an additional peak appears in the intermediate range. For γ > 0.55, growth becomes uncontrolled — a divergence occurs, imposing an upper bound on collapse parameters. The results are insensitive to the choice of transition function model (κ).

Implications

This work demonstrates for the first time that PBHs can effectively grow during the radiation era through neutrino absorption. This changes predictions for the mass spectrum and PBH abundance, directly affecting the interpretation of observational data: from searches for gravitational lensing events to signals from merging black holes. Moreover, a new channel for probing early Universe physics opens up: the shape of the PBH spectrum can be used to reconstruct properties of the primordial plasma and the equation of state at the QCD phase transition epoch.

Future development

Future work plans to account for lepton and baryon asymmetries, which may alter the contribution of neutrinos to the energy density and mean free path. It is also necessary to consider more realistic models of the density fluctuation spectrum produced during inflation and the spatial clustering of PBHs. Observational tests with JWST and future gravitational wave observatories, such as LISA, will allow these predictions to be verified and model parameters to be refined.

Impact

The results will impact cosmology, dark matter physics, and gravitational wave astronomy. They also touch upon the formation of supermassive black holes in the early Universe and the interpretation of “little red dots” discovered by James Webb.

Next steps

A detailed comparison with observational constraints is needed, including data from microlensing and accretion radiation. The collapse criticality should also be incorporated into the calculation of the initial mass spectrum to refine the shape of the additional peak.

Key open problems

The article directly connects the nature of dark matter with Big Bang physics and inflationary cosmology. It also sheds light on the initial seeds for galaxy formation and the origin of massive black holes, which remains one of the unsolved problems in astrophysics.

Key numbers

  • range of initial PBH masses: 10^-3 – 10^9 M⊙
  • temperature of neutrino absorption onset: ~10 MeV (for masses around 10^3 M⊙)
  • masses of maximum growth ("sweet spot"): 10^3 – 10^5 M⊙
  • dark matter fraction in PBHs after absorption (γ=0.55, κ=1): 0.1129 (initial 0.1)
  • critical value of collapse parameter γ_div: ≈0.55
