Neutrons Push on Magnetic Vortices ✦

Original: "Neutron contribution to the force on a proton vortex in superconducting neutron-star matter"
· Oleg A. Goglichidze, Mikhail E. Gusakov
arXiv:2607.09468 · 2026-07-10 · CC BY · 1 min · High Energy Superconductivity Nuclear Theory
A new force has been discovered: neutrons inside neutron stars affect superconducting vortices, changing the way they move.
Abstract

In neutron stars, proton vortices feel a force not only from electrons but also from neutrons. It's like the drag of water when a boat moves. This discovery is important for understanding how pulsars spin. What else are these cosmic lighthouses hiding?

A neutron star is the ultra-dense remnant of a dead star. Deep inside, matter is like boiling soup: a liquid of protons that has become superconducting (flows without friction), and the vortices within it are like swirling bits of vegetables. Each vortex carries a magnetic field. Previously, it was thought that only electrons, scurrying through the surrounding plasma like ants between the 'vegetables', could move them. But, building on the ideas of Lev Landau and John Bardeen, researchers have discovered that neutrons, passing through the liquid like tiny bubbles, also create a force. This force emerges from the subtle effects of quantum theory and quantum thermodynamics—the laws of the microworld.

This discovery changes calculations of neutron stars' magnetic fields and explains glitches in the rotation of pulsars (cosmic lighthouses). Some stars become magnetars with fields billions of times stronger than Earth's. In an unexpected twist, the interaction is so weak that it's comparable to the pressure of light on a sail, yet it governs the life of the star—part of stellar evolution. To verify, scientists will resort to computer simulations.

🎯 If you could scoop up a teaspoon of neutron star material, it would weigh billions of tons.
