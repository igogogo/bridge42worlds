Dark Matter and MHz Gravitational Waves: A Unified Early Universe Scenario ✦

Original: "Conformal dark matter and MHz gravitational waves"
· Imtiaz Khan, Niamat Ullah, Salvatore Capozziello, G. Mustafa, Farruh Atamurotov
arXiv:2607.10607 · 2026-07-12 · CC BY · 4 min · Cosmology HEP Phenomenology
A single peak in the primordial perturbation spectrum explains the abundance of superheavy dark matter and predicts a gravitational wave background at megahertz frequencies.
Abstract

A localized enhancement in the spectrum of primordial curvature creates two relics: gravitationally produced dark matter of conformal fermions and a stochastic gravitational-wave background induced by scalar perturbations. It is shown that the dark matter density fixes the normalization of the scalar spectrum via its cubic moment, while the tensor signal is determined by a quadratic convolution, removing the normalization freedom in templates and linking the MHz signal to particle mass, peak scale, and width. A specific single-field realization with Mukhanov–Sasaki evolution yields a broad MHz background below the threshold of Gaussian primordial black hole formation. In this scenario, a null result from high-frequency searches sets a lower bound on the conformal fermion mass, while a detection should reproduce the measured dark matter abundance, peak frequency, amplitude, and width from a single primordial perturbation. Thus, a testable connection is established between small-scale inflationary structure, ultramassive dark matter, and laboratory searches for MHz-range gravitational waves.

Context

Cosmological data, including the cosmic microwave background and large-scale structure, precisely fix the properties of the Big Bang on large scales. However, many orders of magnitude in wavenumber remain unexplored. These small scales are sensitive to the late stages of inflation — the era of ultra-rapid expansion of the Universe, proposed by Alan Guth. In such models, localized enhancements of primordial perturbations can arise without affecting the already known part of the spectrum. Dark matter, whose existence we know thanks to the work of Vera Rubin, still lacks an explanation within the Standard Model. One candidate is superheavy particles born not through thermal freeze-out but from gravitational effects in the expanding Universe. The same inhomogeneity that produces these particles inevitably induces gravitational waves — ripples in spacetime.

Methods

The study relies on a precise calculation of the primordial curvature spectrum from a single-field inflationary model. To describe the enhancement, an evolution of the slow-roll parameters with a temporary suppression was used, yielding a peak in the spectrum. Then, applying Einstein’s equations and Friedmann’s equations, the production of dark matter fermions is computed. The production mechanism is purely gravitational: in a homogeneous Universe, conformally coupled quantum fields do not feel the expansion, but spatial inhomogeneities break this symmetry and lead to particle pair creation. A key role is played by the Dirac equation in curved spacetime. The production probability is proportional to the cubic moment of the curvature spectrum, fixing the dark matter abundance. Simultaneously, the same scalar perturbations at second order generate a stochastic gravitational wave background through quadratic convolution. For the radiation-dominated epoch, an exact tensor spectrum is computed.

Results

The main result is a closure relation that eliminates free normalization: the peak amplitude of induced gravitational waves h²Ω_pk_GW is uniquely expressed in terms of the observed dark matter density Ω_χh², the fermion mass M_χ, the central frequency f₀, and the peak shape parameters (width Δ and cubic form factor R₃). For a benchmark spectrum peaking at 2.98 MHz, fixing Ω_χh²=0.12, the particle mass is M_χ≈9.8×10⁷ GeV, and the peak gravitational wave amplitude is 5.33×10⁻¹² at a frequency of 2.63 MHz. The resulting signal is significantly below the threshold for primordial black hole formation, consistent with their lack of dominance in the Universe. Varying the peak shape parameters changes both the mass and amplitude, but the closure relation holds.

Implications

The work establishes a direct observational link between the microphysics of inflation on small scales and the properties of dark matter. If a gravitational wave signal is detected, its frequency, amplitude, and width must simultaneously be reproduced from the same primordial spectrum together with the correct dark matter density. This turns the search for high-frequency gravitational waves into a test of specific inflationary models. Conversely, the absence of a signal gives a lower bound on the particle mass: the lower the established upper limit on h²Ω_GW, the higher the required minimum mass to account for all dark matter.

Future development

In the coming years, the sensitivity of laboratory detectors in the 1–100 MHz range is expected to increase significantly. Facilities based on superconducting RF cavities (SRF), magnetic resonance concepts, and cross-correlation networks will either detect the predicted signal or rule out a vast region of parameter space. Further refinement of the peak shape using new inflationary models and accounting for non-zero non-Gaussianity will yield more precise predictions. Moreover, the development of multi-frequency gravitational wave astronomy will help distinguish contributions from different sources.

Impact

The results will impact cosmology, particle physics, and gravitational wave astronomy, stimulating the development of new experimental techniques.

Next steps

A targeted search with current and upcoming facilities (ABRACADABRA, DMRadio, MAGO) in the vicinity of 2–3 MHz is needed, along with an improved theoretical description of the transition from inflation to the radiation era for more precise predictions.

Key open problems

The scenario links three fundamental problems: the nature of dark matter, the shape of the primordial perturbation spectrum, and the non-detection of high-frequency gravitational waves. If no signal is found at the expected sensitivity, it will point to the need to revise the production mechanism of superheavy particles or to a significant modification of gravity on small scales.
