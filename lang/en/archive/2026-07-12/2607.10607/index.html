The Cosmic Bell: One Peak Gives Birth to Dark Matter and Gravitational Waves ✦

Original: "Conformal dark matter and MHz gravitational waves"
· Imtiaz Khan, Niamat Ullah, Salvatore Capozziello, G. Mustafa, Farruh Atamurotov
arXiv:2607.10607 · 2026-07-12 · CC BY · 3 min · Cosmology HEP Phenomenology
The same primordial peak explains the abundance of superheavy dark matter and predicts a megahertz hum of gravitational waves—two sides of the same cosmic coin.
Abstract

In the young Universe, a local surge of primordial perturbations could simultaneously spawn ultramassive dark matter (particles that only interact gravitationally) and a background of megahertz gravitational waves. The authors showed that the observed dark matter abundance rigidly fixes the amplitude of these waves, eliminating arbitrariness in theoretical models. Now the signal at MHz frequencies depends directly on particle mass, peak position, and spectral width—like a diagnosis from a pulse. If searches find nothing, we get a lower bound on the dark matter mass; if the signal is detected, it must immediately reproduce both the relic particle density and the waveform from the same single root cause.

Picture the early Universe as an unimaginably large bell. Inflation — the era of rapid expansion, dreamed up by Alan Guth, strikes it, sending a wave rippling across the fabric of spacetime. Part of that wave freezes into gravitational vibrations; another part transforms into invisible particles. That’s the essence of the new work: a single sharp peak in the spectrum of primordial inhomogeneities gives birth both to superheavy dark matter and to a stochastic background of gravitational waves at frequencies around a megahertz. Meanwhile, the formation of primordial black holes is suppressed, which agrees beautifully with observations.

The particle creation mechanism here is purely gravitational. In an expanding Universe, not even the vacuum stays calm: quantum fluctuations, blown up by inflation, materialize into particles, as if pulled out of emptiness by the curvature of space itself.

The underlying physics is simple and elegant. In the era before the Big Bang, the Universe was filled with radiation and fields. The same primordial inhomogeneities that would later become galaxies grew to enormous amplitudes on ultra-small scales. Such a peak in the curvature distribution bends space more strongly than any lens: pairs of fermions are ripped out of the vacuum. These particles have a colossal mass—on the order of a hundred million gigaelectronvolts, tens of millions of times heavier than the heaviest known quark. They could make up the hidden mass whose existence we learned about from the pioneering observations of Vera Rubin of galactic rotation. This kind of dark matter doesn’t fit into the Standard Model of particle physics, which is why new scenarios are needed.

The predicted gravitational-wave amplitude—5×10⁻¹² in units of critical density—is a trillion times smaller than the cosmic microwave background. But this tininess is no accident: it is tightly locked to the observed dark matter density through a closure equation derived by the authors for the first time. Now, knowing the particle mass and the shape of the peak, one can directly compute the expected gravitational-wave signal.

As you read these lines, a hypothetical megahertz gravitational hum, if it exists, is rhythmically stretching your body by one billionth of a proton’s radius—and it is precisely this ghostly tremor that future detectors will hunt.

But that same peak—the same “ringing string”—also inevitably generates gravitational waves. At second order in perturbation theory, scalar inhomogeneities, colliding and decaying, strike out tensor oscillations of the metric. This is the echo racing through the fabric of space after a powerful jolt. Calculations show that the frequency of these waves lands right in the megahertz range—exactly where sensitive laboratory antennas are already listening.

This coupling gives physicists a two-in-one tool. If superconducting resonators or magnetic antennas catch the sought-after “gravitational hum,” its parameters will confirm a specific inflation scenario and allow us to weigh the dark matter particles. And if they don’t catch it, then dark matter is either lighter than we think or was born in a different way. In any case, the cosmic bell will keep ringing, echoing through the greatest mysteries—from the nature of mass to quantum gravity.
