Recognizing topological quantum phases from small subsystems ✦

Original: "Learning Topological Quantum Phases from Limited Subsystems"
· Mehran Khosrojerdi, Sougato Bose, Alessandro Cuccoli, Paola Verrucchi, Abolfazl Bayat, Leonardo Banchi
arXiv:2607.10656 · 2026-07-12 · CC BY · 4 min · Quantum Physics Statistical Mech Strongly Correlated Machine Learning
A new machine learning method with a quantum kernel enables classification of quantum phases of matter from data of just a few sites.
Abstract

Characterizing quantum topological phases typically requires nonlocal string order parameters, which are accessible only for the entire system, making experiments challenging. A data-efficient supervised learning scheme is proposed that identifies phases from small subsystems (1–4 nodes). It leverages a quantum kernel based on the reduced density matrices of these subsystems, which can be practically measured in experiments. The method is tested on the phase diagrams of two spin chains: the spin-1/2 generalized cluster-Ising model and the spin-1 anisotropic Haldane chain. By restricting to local data from just a few nodes, high classification accuracy is achieved; a model trained on short chains successfully generalizes to longer ones. Thus, local reduced density matrices preserve key signatures of global topological phases, paving a practical path for characterizing rich phase diagrams of quantum many-body systems.

Context

To construct phase diagrams of quantum many-body systems, the concept of an order parameter is often invoked. In the Landau–Ginzburg paradigm (Landau), phases are identified by symmetry breaking and local parameters. However, for topological phases, such as symmetry-protected topological (SPT) states, there is no local parameter, and a nonlocal string order parameter is needed, the measurement of which is experimentally extremely difficult, as it requires access to the entire system. At the same time, machine learning methods have already proven effective in phase classification, but so far they have also relied on global data. The question arises: can a topological phase be recognized by accessing only a small fragment of the system?

Methods

The researchers developed a protocol combining ground state simulation using matrix product states (MPS) and a support vector machine (SVM) with a quantum kernel. For a target model, the ground state is computed at given parameters, after which the reduced density matrix (von Neumann) of a small block A (from 1 to 4 spins) is constructed by taking a partial trace over the rest of the system. Then the quantum kernel is defined as the scalar product of the reduced density matrices of two states: K = Tr[ρ_A ρ'_A]. This kernel, which can be estimated experimentally, for example, via a swap test or shadow tomography, is fed into a classical SVM classifier. Training is carried out on a set of points with known phases, after which the classifier predicts the phase for new parameters. All calculations were performed for chains up to 51 sites using variational MPS algorithms.

Results

The method was tested on two one-dimensional spin models, each containing an SPT phase. For the generalized cluster Ising model with spin-½ (cluster-type interaction, longitudinal and transverse fields), the phase diagram includes antiferromagnetic, paramagnetic, and SPT phases. Surprisingly, even a single central spin reliably separates the SPT phase from others (accuracy above 85%), while identifying the antiferromagnetic phase requires at least two spins. With 4 central sites, the reconstruction accuracy of the entire diagram reaches 88%. For the second model — the anisotropic Haldane chain with spin-1 — the phase diagram is even richer, including Néel, Haldane SPT, and trivial paramagnetic regions. Here, even one central spin gives accuracy above 90%, and for 4 spins it exceeds 92%. Importantly, the block's position matters: the central block gives better results than the edge block for a small number of sites, but as NA increases, the difference evens out. Moreover, the classifier trained on chains of length 31 works on chains of length 51 without loss of accuracy, demonstrating the convergence of the reduced density matrix to the thermodynamic limit already at moderate system sizes.

Implications

The results demonstrate that global topological order leaves clear fingerprints in local correlations and quantum superpositions, captured by the reduced density matrix. This means that costly measurements of string order parameters can be replaced by local measurements and subsequent processing with machine learning. Such an approach dramatically simplifies experimental mapping of phase diagrams of strongly correlated systems in quantum simulators and condensed matter.

Future development

The method naturally generalizes to two-dimensional systems and other classes of topological phases, including topological insulators and superconductors. A promising direction is implementation on real quantum processors using kernel estimation techniques such as shadow measurements, which will allow processing systems of tens of qubits in real time. Additionally, adaptation to uncontrolled environments opens a path to studying phases at finite temperature.

Impact

The development will impact quantum information science, condensed matter physics, and quantum computing technologies, providing a practical tool for characterizing complex quantum materials.

Next steps

The next step will be an experimental demonstration on ion and superconducting quantum platforms, as well as the development of unsupervised learning methods for automatic detection of phase boundaries.

Key open problems

The work directly addresses the long-standing problem of detecting topological order without nonlocal measurements — one of the key challenges in the physics of strongly correlated systems.
