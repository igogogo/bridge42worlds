A Black Hole as a Fuzzy Mirror ✦

Original: "Quantum Horizon and Quantum Membrane Paradigm from Black Hole Quantum Mechanics"
· Chong-Sun Chu
arXiv:2607.10561 · 2026-07-12 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min · HEP Theory Other Condensed Matter General Relativity HEP Phenomenology
Microscopic particles on the horizon transform a black hole from a bottomless pit into a fuzzy mirror capable of reflecting radiation.
Abstract

Imagine the black hole horizon not as a featureless boundary, but as a dynamic membrane made of myriad parton "building blocks." Their peculiar quantum organization generates electric currents, turning the membrane from a convenient fiction into a real physical object. How will this change our view of what lies hidden beyond the horizon?

Links in the knowledge graph 1

For a long time, black holes were seen as bottomless whirlpools. But Bekenstein and Hawking showed they have entropy—a measure of complexity, like a hot body. A new model turns the horizon into a 'fuzzy mirror': its fuzz comes from particles that half-fill the surface. Incoming light makes these particles move in sync, like an electric current. The resistance of this mirror fabric is a constant 377 ohms per square, whether the hole is tiny or gigantic. Reflection depends on polarization—much like velvet’s sheen changes when you stroke it in different directions. This turns a black hole into a translucent echo: some radiation bounces back, and after mergers, repeated signals linger. Gravitational observatories are already hunting for these echoes. If we catch them, we’ll for the first time feel the quantum geometry of space, merging string theory and thermal laws to solve the information paradox.

🎯 The horizon’s electric resistance is like a cosmic constant: it doesn’t change, whether the black hole is atom-sized or a billion times heavier than the Sun.
