Self-learning Wave Functions: How Neural Networks Conquer the Electron Gas ✦

Original: "Accurate Self-Attention Wavefunctions at Large Scale"
· Filippo Gaggioli, Sam Azadi, Liang Fu
arXiv:2607.08616 · 2026-07-09 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Strongly Correlated Materials
Self-attention neural networks accurately reproduce quantum behavior of electrons at scales approaching the thermodynamic limit.
Abstract

Neural network variational ansätze with a self-attention mechanism surpass traditional ones in expressivity, but their computational complexity raises questions about scalability. When applied to a two-dimensional uniform electron gas for systems of up to N=169 particles, energies obtained are systematically lower than those from cutting-edge diffusion Monte Carlo calculations. Direct access to the ground-state wave function enabled the reconstruction of the full dispersion of collective modes in the liquid phase: from the plasmon branch at small q to the roton minimum near q=2k_F. The observed quantities for N=91 and N=169 are in near-perfect agreement, indicating convergence to the thermodynamic limit.

Context

The quantum many-body world is like a giant orchestra: each electron-"musician" interacts with its neighbors, and the collective "symphony"—the wave function—dictates the material's properties. But writing out the score for dozens of particles is almost impossible: complexity explodes exponentially. Traditional numerical methods, such as variational Monte Carlo, rely on cleverly crafted ansatzes and often fall short of the required accuracy. Enter machine learning: neural networks, especially self-attention architectures, promise to build complex entangled wave functions directly from data, without manual tuning. The key question—will they lose their expressiveness when scaled to systems approaching the thermodynamic limit? This work addresses that, focusing on the two-dimensional homogeneous electron gas—a plasma where Coulomb interactions and the Pauli exclusion principle create rich physics.

Methods

The authors employed a neural network ansatz with a self-attention mechanism (transformer) that generates a superposition of Slater determinants from electron coordinates. The architecture's hallmark is spatially weighted partial attention: a term with exponential decay over distance (r_ij/λ) is added to the standard logit, encouraging local correlations without suppressing long-range ones, thanks to multi-layered structure. This design doesn't rely on predefined orbitals but creates them "on the fly" based on each particle's environment, offering exceptional flexibility. Parameter optimization (around 800,000) was performed via variational Monte Carlo energy minimization. Benchmark calculations used fixed-node diffusion Monte Carlo for comparison.

Results

The results exceeded expectations. For systems up to 169 electrons at rs=30 and 40 (density parameter characterizing interaction strength), the neural network wave function yielded energies systematically lower than the best DMC results: improvement reached up to 1.5% in correlation energy. Direct access to the wave function enabled computation of various observables: momentum distribution n(k) and static structure factor S(q). In the metallic phase (rs=30), n(k) showed a sharp Fermi-liquid discontinuity—a hallmark of a Fermi liquid, while at rs=40, Bragg peaks emerged in S(q), signaling Wigner crystal formation. But the biggest surprise was collective excitations. Using a relation proposed by Richard Feynman, they extracted the dispersion from S(q): at low momenta it matches the plasmon branch (ω ∝ √q), and near 2kF a roton-like minimum appears—a precursor to crystallization. Curves for 91 and 169 particles nearly coincided, confirming convergence to the thermodynamic limit.

Implications

The success proves that self-attentive architectures can simultaneously deliver high accuracy and scalability, opening a new frontier in computational many-body physics. The need for handcrafted ansatzes will diminish—neural networks may become a universal tool for exploring quantum phases and collective phenomena. This is especially valuable for systems where entanglement and superposition play key roles.

Future development

This field will explode. The next steps are clear: incorporating spin dynamics, accounting for defects and impurities, extending to three-dimensional systems and real materials. We might see pretrained "foundation models" of electronic states—akin to language models but for quantum matter. Integration with generative models and active learning will speed up the discovery of new phases even further.

Impact

The method will impact condensed matter physics, quantum chemistry, and computational materials science, accelerating the design of functional materials and perhaps shedding light on the nature of high-temperature superconductivity.

Next steps

Immediate research will aim to scale to thousands of particles and apply to layered semiconductor heterostructures, where exotic electron crystals and quasicrystals were recently predicted.

Key open problems

The work is directly tied to the central problem of many-body physics—accurately describing strongly correlated systems. It also lays the groundwork for tackling the mystery of high-temperature superconductivity, where phase competition demands ultra-precise calculations.
