Electron Jazz: How Neural Network Self-Attention Unveils a Quantum Symphony ✦

Original: "Accurate Self-Attention Wavefunctions at Large Scale"
· Filippo Gaggioli, Sam Azadi, Liang Fu
arXiv:2607.08616 · 2026-07-09 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · Strongly Correlated Materials
Transformers, inspired by language models, accurately reproduce the quantum dance of hundreds of electrons, bringing us closer to understanding real materials.
Abstract

Using neural network wave functions with a self-attention mechanism (the ability to highlight the most important connections), scientists studied a two-dimensional electron gas with up to 169 particles. The energies turned out to be systematically lower than those from the state-of-the-art diffusion Monte Carlo method. Analysis of the wave function allowed the reconstruction of the full dispersion of collective modes: from the plasmon branch at small momenta to the roton minimum near q=2k_F. The results for 91 and 169 particles are almost identical, indicating convergence to the thermodynamic limit.

The quantum many-body world is a jazz ensemble, where each electron improvises, continuously listening to all the others, and the overall wave function is born from this tense dialogue. But writing sheet music for hundreds of musicians is nearly impossible: complexity explodes exponentially, and traditional methods like variational Monte Carlo are often forced to play simplified notes. This is where machine learning enters: neural networks with self-attention promise to build complex entangled wave functions directly from electron coordinates, without manual tuning — as if each electron acquires a 'social graph' of interactions. The pressing question was: will these flexible architectures lose precision when scaled to systems approaching the thermodynamic limit — the boundary where a handful of atoms turns into matter of familiar sizes?

The answer came from work with a two-dimensional homogeneous electron gas — a quantum plasma, where Coulomb repulsion and the Pauli principle create extremely rich physics. The authors used a neural network ansatz based on a transformer, generating a superposition of Slater determinants. The secret is that it doesn't rely on pre-defined orbitals but builds them 'on the fly,' taking into account the environment of each particle. The key mechanism is spatially weighted self-attention: an exponential factor that decays with distance between electrons is added to standard attention. This focuses the network on local correlations, but through multiple layers, sensitivity to long-range interactions is preserved — as if each electron pays special attention to its neighbors but never loses sight of the conductor's baton of electromagnetism, which permeates all space.

The roton minimum discovered in the collective excitation spectrum is a close relative of what Richard Feynman explained for superfluid helium. The electron liquid, preparing to crystallize, mimics helium's quantum vortices — yet another reminder of the deep unity of quantum liquids.

Systems of up to 169 electrons with strong interactions (density parameter rs=30 and 40) were optimized using variational Monte Carlo. The neural network wave function surpassed the accuracy of benchmark diffusion Monte Carlo calculations — gaining up to one and a half percent in correlation energy. But the real treasure is direct access to the full wave function, which enabled computation of observables: the momentum distribution with a sharp Fermi-liquid jump (a hallmark of Fermi liquids) and the static structure factor. At low density (rs=40), Bragg peaks emerged — precursors of a Wigner crystal. And using the clever Feynman relation, ε(q) ≲ q²/(2S(q)), the dispersion of collective excitations was extracted from the structure factor: at small momenta — the plasmon branch, and near twice the Fermi wavevector — that very roton minimum, signaling the electrons' readiness to freeze into an ordered lattice.

The model contained about 800,000 trainable parameters — a lot for quantum problems, but it neither degenerated nor overfit. On the contrary, the curves for 91 and 169 particles practically coincided, confirming convergence to the thermodynamic limit. Think about it: 169 electrons is still a microscopic number, but their collective behavior is already indistinguishable from that of an infinite crystal. The neural network captures the essence, discarding inessential details — like an artist painting a portrait with a few strokes.

This success is not just another record. It proves that expressive neural network architectures can simultaneously provide high accuracy and scalability, opening a new frontier in computational many-body physics. The need for hand-crafted ansatzes will vanish — neural networks will become a universal tool for exploring quantum phases and collective phenomena. The next steps are already looming: inclusion of spin dynamics, accounting for defects, extension to real three-dimensional materials. One can imagine the emergence of pre-trained foundational models of electronic states — a sort of 'GPT for quantum matter' that will accelerate the discovery of new functional materials and perhaps help solve the mystery of high-temperature superconductivity. The electron jazz ensemble will finally allow its improvisation to be recorded without loss of nuances.

🎯 The roton minimum discovered in the electron liquid is a close relative of the one observed in superfluid helium-4. Feynman himself explained it in the 1950s using quantum vortices. It seems electrons mimic helium as they prepare to crystallize — nature loves to repeat successful solutions at different scales.

H = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_i \nabla_i^2 + \sum_{j<i} \frac{1}{|\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{r}_j|}
The first term is the sum of kinetic energies of the electrons, the second is their mutual repulsion, which decreases with distance.
\varepsilon(q) \lesssim \frac{q^2}{2 S(q)}
The excitation energy ε at wavevector q is approximately equal to the ratio of q squared to twice the structure factor S(q); this is how the neural network 'revealed' plasmons and rotons.
