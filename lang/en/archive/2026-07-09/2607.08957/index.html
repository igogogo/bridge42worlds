The Structural Dance of Water: Unraveling the Anomalous Heat Capacity ✦

Original: "Structural Origin of Water Heat Capacity Anomaly from Classical and Quantum Simulations"
· Kam-Tung Chan, Dylan A. Folkner, Margaret L. Berrens, Alexei A. Stuchebrukhov, Lee-Ping Wang, Davide Donadio
arXiv:2607.08957 · 2026-07-09 · CC BY 4.0 · 2 min
Statistical Mech Soft Condensed Matter
Quantum effects merely set the stage: the heat capacity peak arises from the dance of two local structures.
Abstract

The heat capacity of water at constant pressure is anomalously large and increases sharply upon supercooling. Calculations with a machine-learned potential show that nuclear quantum effects suppress high-frequency vibrations, but not this anomaly. The reason lies in constant structural rearrangements, akin to switching between loose and dense local environments. The 'cost' of this transition is about 4 kJ/mol, comparable to a weak hydrogen bond. A simple two-level model links these microscopic changes to the excess heat capacity from supercooled to room temperature conditions.

1

Imagine water as a ballroom, where each molecule is a dancer, constantly forming fleeting partnerships. These hydrogen bonds give rise to local patterns: some resemble a stiff waltz — an ordered network with cavities (low-density structure), others a wild jitterbug, where partners crowd into chaotic dense clusters. The ability of water to store heat is determined by how easily its molecular 'dancers' switch between these styles. Research using next-generation molecular simulations has shown that the anomalously high heat capacity and its sharp peak upon supercooling is not a consequence of quantum atomic trembling, but a direct manifestation of structural vacillations between local orders.

The team used neural network potentials (NEPs), trained on highly accurate quantum-chemical calculations. This allowed them to describe interactions in ensembles of thousands of H₂O molecules (hydrogen and oxygen). Quantum corrections accounting for the 'fuzziness' of nuclei were introduced via path integral methods — an approach tracing back to Richard Feynman. It turned out that quantum effects merely lower the overall heat capacity by a few gas constants, without affecting the shape of its temperature curve. Both classical and quantum calculations reproduced a peak near 230 K, where experiments on water in nanopores show a maximum in CP.

The key to the puzzle came from the structural order parameter SSSI(3), which counts how many 'outsiders' penetrate the second coordination shell of a molecule. Its temperature derivative and variance almost perfectly mirror the behavior of heat capacity. In the peak region — around −43°C — entropic fluctuations go off the charts: the system continuously oscillates between low-density (LDL) and high-density (HDL) local structures, like a crowd torn between a slow waltz and a fast jitterbug.

The specific heat capacity of water at 25°C is 4.18 J/(g·K) — almost double that of ethanol (2.56). It is this property that has turned the World Ocean into a giant heat buffer, absorbing more than 90% of excess heat from anthropogenic warming over the past half-century.

By building a two-level model, the authors isolated the structural contribution to heat capacity and estimated the effective enthalpy of the LDL→HDL transition: about 3–4 kJ/mol — the same as the energy difference between amorphous ices. Thus, the microscopic topology of hydrogen bonds gives rise to a macroscopic thermodynamic marvel. This result not only supports the hypothesis of a liquid–liquid critical point in supercooled water, but also paves the way for refining climate models and understanding the behavior of other anomalous liquids — like silica, phosphorus — where similar metamorphoses are suspected. So the waltz of two structures extends beyond the laboratory test tube, shaping the fate of planetary climate and opening new chapters in liquid physics.
