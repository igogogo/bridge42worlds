From Mist to Droplet: How Awareness Is Born ✦

Original: "A Non-Hermitian Potential Well Formalism for Conscious--Preconscious--Subliminal Processing"
· Vasily Lubashevskiy, Ihor Lubashevsky
arXiv:2607.08302 · 2026-07-09 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min · Neurons and Cognition nlin.AO
Researchers uncovered how the mist of impressions condenses into a droplet of clear thought—following laws familiar to physicists.
Abstract

Imagine a rolling landscape with the wind sweeping across it. When the wind (sensory information) is strong enough and finds a deep hollow, a stable vortex forms — that's the moment of awareness. The new model shows how a clear thought is born from the noise of neural signals. Consciousness is a kind of 'frozen wave' in the neural landscape.

Links in the knowledge graph 1

The brain catches a sea of signals—the whisper of the wind, a glint of light—but only a tiny fraction reaches consciousness. Scientists built a model where the brain's working area is described as a special 'mathematical space,' proposed by mathematician John von Neumann. In this space, sensations resemble a mist or cloud of probabilities: with weak attention, they remain diffuse, like vapor. An equation similar to Schrödinger's shows how the cloud moves until we direct our focus. Then it abruptly condenses into a single clear droplet-thought—like an instantaneous grasp of the essence.

The model revealed three stages. A too-weak stimulus—a vapor without a trace. A strong signal with scattered attention yields a droplet that evaporates at once: preconsciousness. Only when both stimulus and attention exceed the threshold does the droplet persist and enter consciousness. A computer experiment confirmed: the transition resembles a switch click, not a smooth crescendo. Consciousness turns out to be not a smooth stream but a series of momentary snapshots—each about 200 milliseconds long.

The droplet’s stability depends on the precision of the ensemble: a blurred signal leaves too many 'either-or' possibilities, and hidden alternatives spread apart. Noisy interference disrupts focusing. But when attention is sufficient, parts of the image coherently reinforce each other, casting off the excess. This whole mechanics rests on the mathematics of a unified mental landscape, where thoughts are but ripples on a common field.

🎯 Awareness takes about 200 milliseconds—roughly the duration of the 'now' moment, when the brain assembles scattered sensations into a single picture.

🎬 The cloud consciousness metaphor echoes the 'mental landscape' from William Gibson's Neuromancer, where the mind roams digital expanses.
