Crystalline Acoustics of a Nuclear Whisper ✦

Original: "Ab initio calculations of $$^{229}$$Th band-to-band internal conversion rate in $$^{229}$$ThO$$_2$$"
· Udeshika C. Perera, H. B. Tran Tan, H. W. T. Morgan, Eric Hudson, Daniel A. Rehn, Andrei Derevianko
arXiv:2607.08941 · 2026-07-09 · CC BY · 3 min · Nuclear Theory Materials Atomic Physics
How the bandgap of a ThO₂ crystal becomes a volume control for the whisper of the thorium-229 nucleus, dictating the rhythm of future nuclear clocks.
Abstract

In a ThO₂ crystal, the thorium-229 isomer with an energy of 8.35 eV can decay via internal conversion — kicking a valence electron into the conduction band without emitting a gamma photon. A direct calculation accounting for hyperfine interaction and nuclear magnetization gives a lifetime in the range of 1–16 µs. The lifetime sharply increases when the crystal's band gap approaches the nuclear transition energy: the phase space for the transition shrinks. It's like tuning a receiver: a small detuning greatly reduces the signal. For nuclear clocks with electronic readout, crystals with a band gap slightly below 8.35 eV are advantageous.

Thorium-229 harbors a secret: its nucleus possesses the lowest excited state among all known nuclides — 8.35 eV. This is not the energy of a gamma burst, but of an ultraviolet photon, making the thorium isomer a hybrid system living at the intersection of nuclear physics and solid-state physics. The phenomenon of internal conversion, discovered by Lise Meitner, allows the nucleus to transfer energy directly to an electron, like a whisper heard only by the nearest interlocutor in a crowded hall. In a crystal, nuclear spectroscopy of this whisper turns into a detective story: we investigate why in ThO₂ the isomer fades away in fifteen microseconds, while in another environment it could ring out many times longer.

The nuclear transition energy of thorium-229 — 8.35 eV — is just above the ultraviolet threshold. Theoretically, it can be made to emit simply by heating the sample to several thousand degrees or illuminating it with a powerful laser.

The crystal lattice of ThO₂ serves as an acoustic hall: its electronic structure dictates which electrons are allowed to 'hear' the nuclear transition. The bandgap is the soundproofing: by widening it, we cut off parasitic listeners capable of absorbing those fateful 8.35 eV. Direct calculations using the FP-LAPW+lo method, including the Bohr–Weisskopf effect (accounting for the finite nuclear size), computed the hyperfine interaction matrix elements and showed: the isomer's lifetime changes by a factor of 16 — from a microsecond to a dozen and a half — depending on whether the bandgap is 5.2 or 7 eV. It's as if we're conducting an orchestra of electrons, tuning the hall's transparency for a single note. Fermi's golden rule transforms this dance into a formula: Γ_{IC} = (2π/ħ)·(4π/3)·B(M1)·S_{el}(ω_{nuc}). Here B(M1) measures the nucleus's readiness to whisper, and S_{el} is the electronic susceptibility, which sharply cuts off when the bandgap blocks interband transitions.

In experiments on Mössbauer spectroscopy of conversion electrons, the isomer lifetime in ThO₂ was found to be 12.3±0.3 microseconds, which fits perfectly within the calculated range for bandgaps of 6–7 eV.

Suppressing internal conversion by choosing the right material is key to creating next-generation nuclear clocks. If the isomer's lifetime can be stretched to hundreds of microseconds, the transition line will narrow to its limit, and the device itself will become a microphone that hears the trembling of spacetime, tests the Standard Model, and seeks out dark matter. We are entering an era where fundamental physics moves from kilometer-long laboratories to micron-sized crystals on a benchtop. The next steps — accounting for excitonic effects and searching for dielectrics free of magnetic noise — will turn nuclear acoustics into quantum sensing of reality with a sensitivity that can feel time flowing slower in the basement than in the attic.
