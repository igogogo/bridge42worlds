Internal Conversion in ThO2: The Key to Solid-State Nuclear Clocks ✦

Original: "Ab initio calculations of $$^{229}$$Th band-to-band internal conversion rate in $$^{229}$$ThO$$_2$$"
· Udeshika C. Perera, H. B. Tran Tan, H. W. T. Morgan, Eric Hudson, Daniel A. Rehn, Andrei Derevianko
arXiv:2607.08941 · 2026-07-09 · CC BY · 3 min · Nuclear Theory Materials Atomic Physics
Ab initio calculations reveal how the band gap governs the lifetime of the thorium-229 nuclear isomer, opening the door to optimizing next-generation nuclear clocks.
Abstract

A first-principles calculation of the interband internal conversion rate for the 8.35 eV isomeric transition in ThO₂ has been performed. The transition energy lies above the band gap, allowing the isomer to decay nonradiatively by resonantly transferring a valence electron into the conduction band. The process is described as a sum over the Brillouin zone of vertical transitions weighted by hyperfine matrix elements on thorium, derived from all-electron LAPW spinors. Finite nuclear magnetization is included, regularizing the short-range behavior and the Bohr–Weisskopf effect. Applying scissor corrections to the experimental band gap widths of ThO₂ yields lifetimes of 1–16 µs. The value sharply increases as the band gap approaches the transition energy due to the shrinking phase volume. For large band gaps, the calculated lifetime is comparable to that measured for the conversion Mössbauer line. Conclusion: solid-state nuclear clocks with electronic readout are optimal when choosing a matrix with a band gap slightly below 8.35 eV.

Context

The low-energy nuclear spectroscopic transition in the thorium-229 isotope, with a unique excitation energy of about 8.35 eV comparable to electronic transitions in solids, attracts attention due to the prospect of creating nuclear clocks with unprecedented precision. Such clocks could become tools for testing the Standard Model and searching for dark matter. The phenomenon of internal conversion (IC), discovered by Lise Meitner, where nuclear energy is transferred to an electron, can compete with radiation in a solid-state environment, affecting clock performance. Understanding and controlling this process is critical for developing practical devices.

Methods

To calculate the IC rate, the authors applied a first-principles method using the full-potential linearized augmented plane-wave method with local orbitals (FP-LAPW+lo), implemented in the Elk code. This approach allows an accurate description of the electronic wave functions near the thorium nucleus, where the hyperfine interaction is localized, described by Maxwell's equations. The M1 transition matrix elements were computed directly from Bloch spinors including spin-orbit coupling, and the IC rate was determined using Fermi's golden rule by Fermi. To account for the finite nuclear size effect — the effect by Bohr–Weisskopf — a uniformly magnetized sphere model was used. The energy band gap was adjusted by a rigid shift to cover the range of experimental values.

Results

The calculated IC lifetimes range from 1 to 16 microseconds depending on the assumed band gap of ThO2 (5.2–7 eV). As the band gap increases, the lifetime sharply rises because the number of accessible interband transitions with an energy of 8.35 eV decreases. For gaps of 6–7 eV, the calculated lifetime is comparable to the experimentally measured value using Mössbauer spectroscopy of conversion electrons — 12.3(3) μs. The analysis showed that the dominant contribution to the rate comes from transitions involving thorium p-states, rather than f-states as might be expected.

Implications

The work demonstrates that internal conversion in solid-state systems is not a universal constant but is determined by the local electronic environment of the nucleus. This means that by choosing a material with a suitable band structure, the IC channel can be purposefully suppressed or enhanced. For nuclear clocks, this opens the way to creating systems with an isomer lifetime of hundreds of microseconds, which would allow readout via conversion electrons without significant line broadening.

Future development

Further development of the topic involves accounting for excitonic effects and a more accurate description of the electronic spectrum within many-body theory. It is also necessary to investigate other solid-state matrices, particularly thorium compounds with no nuclear spins on the ligands, to avoid magnetic dipole broadening. Preliminary calculations indicate the promise of materials such as sulfates, where the band gap can be just below 8.35 eV, providing an optimal balance between decay rate and lifetime.

Impact

The results will impact the development of quantum sensors and high-precision clocks, as well as experiments searching for dark matter and testing the constancy of fundamental constants.

Next steps

The immediate plans include calculations with excitonic correlations at the level of the Bethe-Salpeter equation and modeling of defect configurations typical of real experimental samples.

Key open problems

The research is directly connected to two big unsolved problems in physics: the nature of dark matter and the possible variation of fundamental constants, which can be tested using solid-state nuclear clocks.
