Electron Dance on the Sombrero: Birth of Fractional Quantum Crystals ✦

Original: "Energetics of fractional anomalous Hall crystals in rhombohedral graphene"
· Félix Desrochers, Ashvin Vishwanath
arXiv:2607.08822 · 2026-07-09 · CC BY · 3 min
Strongly Correlated Mesoscale
Physicists have shown that electrons in graphene spontaneously arrange into a crystal with a fractional topological charge, merging a Wigner lattice and a quantum Hall liquid in a single dance.
Abstract

Fractional anomalous Hall crystals (FAHC) are an exotic state where electrons spontaneously arrange into a lattice and carry fractional charges, just like in the fractional quantum Hall effect, but without needing an external magnetic field. Calculations for a realistic model of five-layer graphene show that FAHC can be energetically favorable and stable compared to other states. Their stability stems from a parent quantum liquid state and a peculiar band structure known as the 'Mexican hat'. This opens up opportunities for experimentally hunting down FAHC in graphene.

1

In the world of two-dimensional materials, states sometimes emerge that are as extraordinary as they are fragile. One of them—the fractional anomalous Hall crystal (FAHC)—is like a quantum centaur: half ordered lattice, half topological liquid. Its existence long remained in doubt, until experiments on multilayer graphene revealed a fractional quantum Hall effect in the absence of a magnetic field—an effect discovered by Klaus von Klitzing. To understand how this is possible, Harvard physicists turned to the image of a frozen dance of electrons on the brim of a 'Mexican hat'—that very dispersion curve that resembles a sombrero.

The dispersion in rhombohedral graphene is cunningly arranged: the energy minimum is not at the center of the Brillouin zone, but on a ring of finite radius. This is like a dance floor where the most favorable spots are not in the middle, but around the perimeter. Electrons, like dancers, try to occupy places on this rim, but strong Coulomb interaction forces them to line up into a rigid periodic lattice—akin to the one predicted by Wigner. The quantum ballet begins when the system's wave function takes the form of a product of a fractional quantum Hall liquid and an Abrikosov vortex lattice. This is a kind of superposition of topological order and crystalline order. It is precisely this hybrid that was named FAHC.

The name 'Mexican hat' comes from the characteristic shape of the dispersion: the energy minimum forms a ring, like the brim of a sombrero. It is this depression that resonates, selecting crystals with a specific period.

A weak periodic potential from the hexagonal boron nitride substrate plays the role of a barely audible whisper from a choreographer. It doesn't create the dance from scratch, but hints when to take the next step: if the lattice step matches the potential, the energy drops sharply. Thus arises a 'locking' effect: the electron density becomes tied to the product of the filling factor and the square of the dispersion minimum momentum (n_e ∼ ν q_⋆²). At ν=1/3, for example, the optimal density is about 0.38×10¹² cm⁻², consistent with experiments. A theoretical approach, drawing on an analogy with Landau levels and combining quantum-field methods with variational Monte Carlo, showed that FAHC withstands competition with ordinary Fermi liquid. With a favorable choice of parameters, they even win in energy, despite the fact that superconductivity could have been an alternative.

The future of these studies extends far beyond graphene. The quantum dance on the sombrero promises new collective modes—a hybrid of lattice phonons and magnetorotons, accessible to spectroscopic probes. And if we consider that such crystals support anyonic excitations—these quantum 'shards' carry fractional charge, like an echo of a whole electron divided among the dancers—then we stand on the threshold of topological qubits, protected from noise by the very geometry of the quantum world. Ahead lies the refinement of phase diagrams using neural quantum states and the search for similar phases in twisted transition metal dichalcogenides. It turns out that to assemble a perfect crystal, one must properly twist the quantum dance, and quantum thermodynamics of nonequilibrium processes will hint at how not to melt it.
