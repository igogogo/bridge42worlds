Fractional anomalous Hall crystals in rhombohedral graphene: energetics and stabilization mechanism ✦

Original: "Energetics of fractional anomalous Hall crystals in rhombohedral graphene"
· Félix Desrochers, Ashvin Vishwanath
arXiv:2607.08822 · 2026-07-09 · CC BY · 4 min
Strongly Correlated Mesoscale
Harvard physicists have shown that five-layer graphene can spontaneously form a crystal lattice with fractional topological charge, merging the physics of the Wigner crystal and the fractional quantum Hall effect.
Abstract

Fractional anomalous Hall crystals (FAHC) replicate the topological order of the fractional quantum Hall effect without a magnetic field, spontaneously breaking translational symmetry with a fractional number of electrons per unit cell. Their realization in rhombohedral five-layer graphene (R5G) was explored using variational wave functions and Monte Carlo methods, incorporating realistic dispersion and screened Coulomb interactions. It was shown that FAHC energetically compete with integer anomalous Hall crystals and Fermi liquids. Stability is ensured by mapping to a parent quantum Hall liquid, which pins the interaction energy, and kinetic energy that favors periods matching the 'Mexican hat' dispersion minimum in R5G. A weak periodic field selectively lowers the energy of commensurate fractional crystals. Predictions of stability windows upon varying twist angle and displacement field agree with experiments, hinting at fractional anomalous Hall states emerging from the continuum and interactions.

1

Context

The discovery of the fractional quantum anomalous Hall effect (FQAHE) in multilayer rhombohedral graphene, aligned with a hexagonal boron nitride substrate, posed a serious challenge to theorists. Unlike the ordinary quantum Hall effect discovered by Klaus von Klitzing, here the topological response appears without an external magnetic field. Usually such an effect is associated with an isolated topological band, but here it seems to form due to interactions. Understanding the continuum physics, where the periodic potential acts only as a 'trigger' for selecting competing states, became a central task. The new work fills this gap, viewing FAHC as a bridge between the Wigner crystal and the fractional quantum Hall liquid, uniting topological order described by long-range quantum entanglement and spontaneous symmetry breaking. Importantly, the competing superconductivity and Fermi liquid make the phase diagram extremely rich.

Methods

To estimate the energy of FAHC, the authors turned to an idealized limit of rhombohedral graphene, where the intrinsic dispersion and Coulomb electrostatic interaction are included, but the band geometry is simplified. Using quantum field parton techniques, the trial wave functions are constructed as a superposition of Slater determinants, products of a fractional quantum Hall wave function (Laughlin or Jain) and an Abrikosov lattice describing a bosonic condensate. Then, using variational Monte Carlo methods, the kinetic and exchange energies are computed. The Pauli exclusion principle is automatically accounted for in the antisymmetrized basis, and the Schrödinger equation determines the dispersion. An important simplification was the use of a correspondence between AHC correlators and lowest Landau level correlators (Landau), yielding semi-analytical formulas for the energy.

Results

The main result: FAHC turn out to be energetically very close to the Fermi liquid, and sometimes even win. Their interaction differs little from a purely quantum Hall analog, and the kinetic energy is governed by a simple mechanism. Due to the 'Mexican hat' dispersion with a minimum at finite momentum q⋆, crystals with smaller filling ν utilize this dip more effectively. This leads to locking of the lattice period: the optimal density n_e ∼ ν q_⋆^2. For example, for ν=1/3, the minimum is achieved at n_e ≈ 0.38×10^12 cm^−2. Adding a weak periodic potential of amplitude |U0|=15 meV selectively lowers the energy of crystals whose lattice constant is commensurate with the substrate. Calculations predict that the stability windows for integer and fractional anomalous Hall effects shift with twist angle and displacement field, consistent with experiments on five- and six-layer graphene.

Implications

These results change the paradigm for searching topological phases in moiré systems. Instead of requiring an isolated topological band, one can start from a strongly interacting continuum and use the periodic potential to selectively 'pin' pre-existing crystalline states. This explains why the fractional quantum anomalous Hall effect in graphene/hBN is observed in regimes where the single-particle band picture does not give an isolated Chern band. Moreover, the FAHC concept predicts new collective modes — hybrids of lattice phonons and quantum Hall magnetorotons, which can be probed in nonlinear transport or microwave spectroscopy experiments. The link to quantum information arises through non-trivial anyonic excitations, potentially useful for topological qubits.

Future development

In the future, the phase diagram needs to be refined by including spin texture, trigonal warping, and a more realistic hBN potential. The development of neural quantum states (quantum machine learning) will allow abandoning rigid variational ansatzes and exploring the true ground state from first principles. Additionally, searching for similar FAHC in other materials with non-monotonic dispersion, such as twisted transition metal dichalcogenides, could reveal a whole family of exotic quantum crystals. Further research might link to quantum thermodynamics of non-equilibrium processes, for example during current-induced melting of the crystal.

Impact

The work will impact understanding of strongly correlated topological phases in two-dimensional materials, guide experiments in scanning tunneling microscopy and inelastic light scattering, and stimulate the development of analytical methods for models with quantum geometry.

Next steps

Full-scale multi-orbital calculations using exact diagonalization or neural networks are needed, along with experimental mapping of stability windows in twist angle and displacement field space for various fillings.

Key open problems

The paper directly addresses the unsolved problem of the origin of the fractional quantum anomalous Hall effect in graphene without a magnetic field, and the more general question of coexistence of topological order and spontaneous breaking of translational symmetry.

Key numbers

  • Crystal binding energy: ~15 meV per electron for commensurate potential
  • Moiré threshold density: 0.68×10^12 cm^−2 (θ ≈ 0.47°)
