Lack of quantum advantage in approximate optimization of spin glasses ✦

Original: "Absence of quantum advantage for approximate spin glass optimization"
· Dries Sels, Flaviano Morone
arXiv:2607.08708 · 2026-07-09 · CC BY · 3 min · Quantum Physics Disordered Systems
A quasiclassical analysis of the QAOA algorithm on the Sherrington–Kirkpatrick model showed that its performance does not surpass classical methods.
Abstract

A semiclassical analysis (truncated Wigner approximation) of the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) on the Sherrington–Kirkpatrick model in the large-spin S limit. With fixed QAOA angles optimized for S=1/2, a nonmonotonic dependence of the final energy on spin magnitude is found. Small S are limited by dominant quantum noise, large S by exponential growth of initial fluctuations. For QAOA of depth p, an optimal compromise is achieved at S on the order of p, leading to convergence of energy to the Parisi value as log(p)/p. The semiclassical approach slightly outperforms the exact spin-1/2 QAOA, but both show the same convergence pattern. Eliminating initial noise and re-optimizing parameters yields an excellent result with convergence 1/p.

Context

The development of quantum computers drives the search for problems where quantum computing provides a practical advantage. The Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) is one of the main contenders for a universal combinatorial solver. However, its real effectiveness compared to classical methods remains a subject of debate. The Sherrington–Kirkpatrick (SK) model is of particular interest, as it has a known exact optimum — the Parisi value. This model is ideal for testing the performance of QAOA under conditions of strong frustration and disorder.

Methods

The researchers performed quasiclassical modeling of QAOA using the truncated Wigner approximation (TWA). This method, widely used in quantum optics and condensed matter physics, replaces quantum evolution with classical spin rotations, while the quantum fluctuations of the initial state are accounted for via the Wigner function, which for large spins reduces to a Gaussian distribution. This approach allows control over the level of quantum noise through an effective spin S: at small S the noise is large, at large S it is suppressed, but then the initial state becomes an unstable fixed point of the classical dynamics. A key parameter was the uncertainty relation, which sets the minimum level of initial fluctuations according to the Heisenberg uncertainty principle.

Results

It was found that the dependence of residual energy on S is nonmonotonic. At small S, entropic noise dominates, hindering effective optimization of the objective function, while at large S, due to superposition the initial state, close to an unstable equilibrium, leads to exponential growth of fluctuations, which also degrades the result. The optimal balance is achieved at S ∝ p (circuit depth). In this regime, the energy converges to the exact Parisi value as log(p)/p, matching the behavior of classical algorithms. Furthermore, numerical experiments showed that the quasiclassics at optimal S yields an energy even lower than the exact QAOA for spin-1/2. This means the quantum version has no advantage. If one completely removes the initial noise and reoptimizes the parameters, the classical dynamics converges as 1/p, outperforming QAOA. Measurements at the end of the evolution binarize the spin variables, finally forming the solution.

Implications

This work calls into question quantum advantage in spin glass optimization problems and points to the need for a deeper understanding of the sources of quantum speedup. If classical methods exhibit similar asymptotics, the advantage of quantum devices may lie only in constant factors that are negated by technological difficulties. The results also highlight the importance of entropic effects in the initialization of quantum algorithms.

Future development

In the future, it will be necessary to investigate other spin glass models and more complex QAOA schemes, including adaptive strategies and noise suppression. Perhaps a combination of classical and quantum approaches that takes into account features of the dynamics will yield better results. It is also interesting to explore the connection with quantum information theory and algorithmic complexity.

Impact

The findings are important for developers of quantum algorithms and optimization specialists, as well as for physicists studying nonequilibrium dynamics in quantum systems.

Next steps

Plans are to verify the observed patterns on real quantum processors and extend the TWA analysis to other variational quantum algorithms. In addition, it will be necessary to rigorously prove the log(p)/p asymptotics for depth p.

Key open problems

The paper is directly connected to the fundamental problem of quantum advantage and the boundary between quantum and classical computing. It also touches on issues of ergodicity and thermalization in many-body systems, where the uncertainty principle limits controllability.

🎯 Interestingly, the Sherrington–Kirkpatrick model was originally proposed to describe magnetic alloys, but today it is actively used in neural network theory and machine learning.
