Why the QAOA Quantum Algorithm Failed Against Spin Glass Chaos ✦

Original: "Absence of quantum advantage for approximate spin glass optimization"
· Dries Sels, Flaviano Morone
arXiv:2607.08708 · 2026-07-09 · CC BY · 3 min · Quantum Physics Disordered Systems
A quasiclassical analysis of QAOA on the Sherrington–Kirkpatrick model showed that quantum fluctuations do not speed up the search for the optimum, and a noiseless classical approach works faster.
Abstract

A semiclassical approach (truncated Wigner approximation) to analyzing the quantum optimization algorithm QAOA on the Sherrington–Kirkpatrick spin glass model revealed a nonmonotonic dependence of the final energy on the spin magnitude S. For small S, quantum noise dominates; for large S, initial fluctuations grow exponentially. For an algorithm of depth p, the optimal balance is achieved at S on the order of p, and the energy converges to the Parisi value as log(p)/p. Interestingly, the semiclassical method slightly outperforms the exact quantum algorithm for spin-1/2, but both converge in the same way. Eliminating noise and re-tuning parameters yields a convergence of 1/p — like removing friction, the mechanism starts working noticeably faster.

Searching for the global minimum in the Sherrington–Kirkpatrick spin glass model resembles climbing a glacier, where every crevasse is a treacherous local optimum, and the true bottom lies buried beneath a chaos of ice blocks. A classical climber, trudging along the gradient, would almost certainly freeze in the nearest crevice. The Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) was conceived as a lifeline: through quantum effects, it supposedly could seep through energy barriers, like superfluid helium — ignoring the friction of the landscape. However, analysis using the truncated Wigner approximation paints a different picture.

This method, borrowed from quantum optics and condensed matter physics, replaces quantum evolution with classical rotations, and initial quantum fluctuations with Gaussian noise. Here, the noise is the trembling in the climber's hands, born from the Heisenberg uncertainty principle: the more precisely one spin direction is fixed, the more the others blur. In terms of an effective spin S, the minimum trembling amplitude is inversely proportional to S — as if knowing the route robbed confidence from one's steps.

The Sherrington–Kirkpatrick model, born in 1975 for magnetic alloys, has now become a training ground for neural networks and machine learning — the icy landscape now tempers artificial intelligence.

The key result of numerical simulation is striking: the dependence of residual energy on S is nonmonotonic. For small S, the entropic noise is so strong that the climber is shaken as if caught in an ice storm, unable to discern the slope. For large S, the trembling subsides, but the initial state — a superposition of all possible spin configurations — becomes a point of unstable equilibrium, like a pencil balanced on its tip. The slightest breath of quantum wind — and the error grows exponentially. The optimal balance is achieved when S scales with the circuit depth p. Then the residual energy decreases as log(p)/p — exactly as in classical heuristics. But the bitterest pill: if the initial noise is removed entirely and the parameters reoptimized, purely classical dynamics yield convergence of 1/p, outperforming any QAOA. The final measurement merely binarizes the spins, giving the solution a finished look.

Paradox: engineers of quantum processors desperately fight noise, while here it turns out that noise — and only in a strictly measured, almost homeopathic dose — gives QAOA a chance to keep up with classical methods. But even then, it does not pull ahead — as if the universe itself whispers that on this path, quantum supremacy is a mirage.

This work strikes at the very heart of quantum ambitions in combinatorial optimization. If classical algorithms are breathing down its neck, then building multi-qubit monsters for NP-hard problems risks becoming an economic chimera. Yet science teaches not pessimism, but sobriety. Such results force a rethinking of the nature of quantum speedup: perhaps its true calling is not universal search, but the simulation of quantum matter, where classical computers are powerless in principle. Ultimately, the glacier of spin glasses has shown: quantum magic is not a panacea, but a precision tool, and sometimes its greatest strength lies in knowing when to stay silent.
