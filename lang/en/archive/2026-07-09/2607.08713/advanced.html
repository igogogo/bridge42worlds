Quantum Engine Outwits Carnot's Law: High Efficiency Without Sacrificing Power ✦

Original: "Approaching Carnot Efficiency at Finite Power in an Experimentally Feasible Quantum Heat Engine"
· Shogo Toma, Atsushi Noguchi, Ken Funo, Hiroyasu Tajima
arXiv:2607.08713 · 2026-07-09 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · Quantum Physics Statistical Mech
Japanese physicists have proposed a quantum heat engine, built on superconducting chips, that approaches Carnot efficiency while maintaining finite power, thanks to collective photon exchange.
Abstract

A fundamental question in finite-time thermodynamics is whether a heat engine can achieve Carnot efficiency at finite power. In classical Markovian engines with local interactions, the trade-off between power and efficiency prohibits asymptotic approach to the Carnot limit. It has been theoretically shown that in quantum systems, degeneracy, symmetry, and collective transitions can circumvent this constraint. In this work, a heat engine implementation using superconducting circuits is proposed, emulating collective enhancement of dissipative processes. Such a scheme allows asymptotically reaching Carnot efficiency while maintaining finite power. The result demonstrates that enhanced dissipation can overcome the classical trade-off, and paves the way for designing quantum devices using collective dissipative phenomena on the platform of circuit quantum electrodynamics.

Context

Heat engines—from steam locomotives to lasers—underpin modern civilization. Their theoretical upper limit, Carnot efficiency, stems from the second law of thermodynamics and Carnot's theorem and depends only on the temperatures of the hot and cold reservoirs. These principles trace back to the pioneering work of Ludwig Boltzmann and Max Planck. However, any real engine operating in finite time inevitably produces additional entropy due to nonequilibrium processes, lowering efficiency. Classical Markovian engines with local interactions cannot simultaneously achieve high power and near-Carnot efficiency: the SST inequality, based on the concept of "activity"—the total rate of transitions between states—forbids it. In quantum systems, degenerate levels and symmetries can cause activity to grow faster than system size, opening a loophole. Yet, until now, it was unclear whether such a mechanism could be realized experimentally.

Methods

The proposed engine is built on superconducting circuits—the same platform used in quantum computers. The working body consists of two microwave resonators with frequencies 3 and 3.8 GHz, coupled via a qubit and a Purcell filter that mediates interaction with a heat bath. The key element is the CAS-2 (coupler-assisted swap) process: when a microwave drive is applied to the qubit, two photons coherently transfer from the first resonator to the second, flipping the qubit state. Effectively, this acts as a collective quantum jump, boosting the system’s activity in proportion to the square of the total photon number N. The dynamics are described by a quantum optical Lindblad equation, and calculations used numerical simulation of the full master equation without simplifications. The working body’s entropy is computed according to von Neumann. During a cycle, the engine alternately contacts a hot (50 mK) and a cold (49 mK) reservoir, and the electromagnetic field of the resonators performs useful work. The definitions of heat and work align with the first law of thermodynamics, yielding simple expressions: each CAS event absorbs a quantum of heat and extracts a quantum of work.

Results

Analysis showed that for the CAS-2 process, activity scales as O(N^2), and the SST inequality allows efficiency η = η_C - O(1/N) with power P = O(N). In other words, by increasing the photon number N, one can approach Carnot efficiency arbitrarily closely without sacrificing power—a scenario forbidden in classical physics. Numerical simulations with N from 2 to 15 confirmed this relationship: at N=15, efficiency reaches 99.9% of the theoretical limit while power scales linearly. The engine operates cyclically, with the entropy of the working body returning to its initial value after each cycle, and entropy production remains nonnegative, satisfying the second law. Deviations from the effective description at large N stem from off-resonant processes, but the scaling holds, confirming the mechanism’s robustness.

Implications

This work blurs the line between abstract theoretical models and real devices. It demonstrates for the first time that a quantum collective mechanism for bypassing the SST limit can be embodied in a concrete scheme accessible to modern labs. This shifts the paradigm for heat engine design: instead of fighting losses, one can purposefully amplify useful quantum transitions. Moreover, the result deepens the connection between quantum information and thermodynamics, showing how coherent many-body effects can radically improve energy efficiency.

Future development

The proposed architecture is just a first step. Using higher-order CAS processes (c>2) could, in principle, yield even stronger activity amplification, up to O(N^c), and thus even more favorable power-efficiency trade-offs. Another avenue is adapting the collective enhancement idea to other platforms, such as trapped ions or cold atoms. This could lead to quantum batteries with record charging speeds or ultrasensitive sensors based on quantum heat engines. However, serious challenges remain: suppressing quantum decoherence and minimizing parasitic losses as the system scales up.

Impact

The findings will impact several fields: quantum thermodynamics, quantum technologies, and superconducting circuit engineering. In the future, they could be used to develop energy-efficient quantum devices and cooling systems for quantum computers.

Next steps

The next step is the experimental realization of the proposed engine in one of the labs working with superconducting circuits. A key challenge will be reaching a hierarchical regime where the CAS process dominates over parasitic photon loss channels.

Key open problems

The study directly addresses a fundamental question: is there a genuine quantum advantage in thermodynamics? The result shows that quantum effects can bypass constraints derived from classical stochastic thermodynamics. This calls for revisiting the limits of the SST inequality and searching for new universal relations between power, efficiency, and entropy in the quantum regime.

🎯 The proposed engine operates at temperatures around 50 millikelvin—hundreds of times colder than outer space. It uses individual microwave photons as its 'workhorses' and could, in principle, run on ambient heat inside a cryostat.

\eta_{\mathrm{C}} = 1 - \frac{T_{\mathrm{c}}}{T_{\mathrm{h}}}
The maximum theoretical efficiency of a heat engine, depending only on the temperatures of the hot (T_h) and cold (T_c) reservoirs.
P \leq \beta_{\mathrm{L}} \bar{A} \eta (\eta_{\mathrm{C}} - \eta)
Bound on power P for classical Markovian engines: the product of β_L, activity Ā, efficiency η, and the difference (η_C − η) sets an upper limit.
\bar{A} = O(N^2)
Activity (the average rate of quantum jumps) grows quadratically with the total photon number N, enabling the bypass of the classical limit.
