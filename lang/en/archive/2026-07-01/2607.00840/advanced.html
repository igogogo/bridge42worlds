Dark matter energy exchange in stars near supermassive black holes: a new path to dark stars ✦

Original: "Dark matter energy exchange in stars orbiting supermassive black holes"
· Stephan A. Meighen-Berger, R. Andrew Gustafson, Nicole F. Bell, Jayden L. Newstead, Sandra Robles, Ian M. Shoemaker
arXiv:2607.00840 · 2026-07-01 · CC BY · 4 min · HEP Phenomenology High Energy
Calculations show that elastic scattering of dark matter in the star S4714 orbiting Sagittarius A* can heat it to a luminosity comparable to its own, opening an annihilation-free path to a dark star phase.
Abstract

Stars in tight orbits around the supermassive black hole at the center of the Galaxy pass through regions of sharply increased dark matter density. For the star S4714, moving on an exceptionally close relativistic orbit around Sagittarius A*, the orbit-averaged energy exchange due to dark matter interaction has been calculated. With a spike density profile, the transferred power reaches the star's luminosity if the scattering cross-section on protons is on the order of 10⁻³⁶ cm² for particle masses in the MeV–GeV range, and on electrons—around 5×10⁻³⁸ cm² for masses sub-MeV. This opens up an annihilation-free pathway to a dark star phase, where heating is caused by elastic scattering of dark matter. The obtained cross-sections fall within the region predicted by freeze-in scenarios and do not contradict limits from experiments on dark matter reflection off the Sun and its acceleration by cosmic rays.

Context

Why it matters. The supermassive black hole at the center of our Galaxy, Sagittarius A*, is a natural laboratory for studying gravity and particle physics under extreme conditions. Observing stars orbiting it has allowed us to measure its mass and test general relativity. The recently discovered star S4714, with its record-close periastron (about 12 AU and a velocity of ~24,000 km/s), offers a unique opportunity to probe the dark matter distribution near the event horizon. In this region, the dark matter density can be enhanced by 8–9 orders of magnitude due to adiabatic growth of the black hole, forming a so-called "spike." Back in the 1930s, Fritz Zwicky proposed the existence of dark matter, and Vera Rubin later confirmed its dominant role in galaxies. Black hole physics, pioneered by scientists like Stephen Hawking, allows us to calculate the orbits of stars around them, governed by the law of universal gravitation and Kepler's third law.

Methods

How they did it. The researchers used two approaches: analytical and Monte Carlo (numerical simulation). In the analytical approach, they calculated the orbit-averaged energy transfer rate from dark matter particles to the stellar plasma by averaging over a Keplerian orbit while considering the dark matter density profile. The star was described using the stellar evolution model MESA. The Monte Carlo method simulated individual passages of dark matter particles through the star, incorporating realistic radial density and temperature profiles and a local Maxwellian velocity distribution of target particles. Gravitational capture and evaporation of light dark matter were taken into account, and energy exchange was considered in accordance with the second law of thermodynamics, as the system tends toward thermal equilibrium.

Results

What they found. For a spiked profile resulting from adiabatic compression, the orbit-averaged energy exchange reaches the total luminosity of S4714 (~17.5 solar luminosities) at a dark matter-proton scattering cross section σχp ~ 10⁻³⁶ cm² for masses in the range ~10 MeV – 1 GeV. For scattering on electrons, the critical cross section is σχe ~ 5×10⁻³⁸ cm² for masses below ~0.05 MeV. These values lie in the region predicted by freeze-in scenarios of dark matter and are not yet ruled out by direct detection experiments, including searches accounting for cosmic rays. Notably, for steeper density profiles or when considering orbital parameter uncertainties, the effect could be even stronger. Importantly, the energy exchange saturation is limited by a geometric bound: when each dark matter particle passing through the star deposits all its kinetic energy.

Implications

Scientific significance. This result offers a new mechanism for heating stars with dark matter without requiring annihilation, thus applicable to a broad class of models, including asymmetric dark matter. It paves the way for an annihilation-free "dark star" phase, where energy transferred through elastic scattering can significantly influence stellar evolution, potentially leading to observable signatures. It also connects astrophysical observations with the microphysics of dark matter particles, allowing constraints on interaction cross sections.

Future development

How the topic may develop. In the future, star models will be refined for the Galactic center environment, including gravitational acceleration during infall toward the black hole. The analysis will also be extended to other S-stars (e.g., S2, S62, S4716) to break degeneracies between dark matter parameters and astrophysical uncertainties. Of particular interest is incorporating the derived energy exchange rates into stellar evolution codes to quantitatively predict observational signatures such as anomalous luminosity or spectral features, deepening our understanding of galactic evolution. Improved astrometry and multi-epoch photometry of short-period S-stars could unveil an energy excess caused by dark matter.

Impact

Impact on other fields. This work will influence dark matter physics, stellar astrophysics, and Galactic center dynamics. It may also stimulate new searches for dark matter particles with sub-GeV masses, both in direct detectors and in indirect observations.

Next steps

Next steps. The survival of the dark matter spike under stellar relaxation needs to be clarified, as it could reduce the predicted effect by one to two orders of magnitude, and detailed modeling of baryonic feedback is required. Additionally, improved astrometric data for S4714 and other stars are crucial to narrow uncertainties in the periastron distance.

Key open problems

Connection to unsolved problems in physics: This mechanism is directly linked to the problem of the nature of dark matter and its distribution in galaxies, as well as the question of the existence and properties of dark stars. It also touches on the formation and evolution of central regions of galaxies and the feedback between dark matter and baryons.

🎯 Fun fact. The star S4714 moves so fast that at periastron its speed is about 8% of the speed of light! Due to this rapid motion, the kinetic energy of dark matter particles in the star's rest frame can be tens of times greater than the plasma's thermal energy, leading to effective heating.

🎬 In science fiction, the concept of dark stars powered by dark matter annihilation has appeared in novels such as 'Dark Star' by Oliver Langmead, but the idea of a star heated by simple friction against dark matter has not yet been widely depicted.

Key numbers

  • S4714 speed at periastron: 23,900 km/s (~8% c)
  • S4714 periastron distance: 12.6 ± 9.3 AU
  • DM-proton scattering cross section for achieving luminosity: ~10^{-36} cm^2
  • DM-electron scattering cross section for achieving luminosity: ~5×10^{-38} cm^2
  • Dark matter density enhancement in the spike: 10^8–10^9 times relative to the standard profile
