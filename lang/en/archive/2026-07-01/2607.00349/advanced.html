Wandering black holes and accretion in solitonic cores of fuzzy dark matter ✦

Original: "Variability in Supermassive Black-Hole Accretion Rates in Fuzzy Dark Matter Cores due to Black-Hole Wandering"
· Eric Ludwig, Philip Mocz, Victor Robles
arXiv:2607.00349 · 2026-07-01 · CC BY · 3 min · Cosmology
Numerical simulations show that enhanced accretion in fuzzy dark matter cores is not stable due to black hole wandering and limited gas supply.
Abstract

Simulations within the Schrödinger–Poisson framework, including isothermal gas and a moving black hole that grows via conservative mass sink, investigate how soliton cores of fuzzy dark matter influence Bondi accretion. For low-mass seed black holes (<10⁶ M⊙), the wandering and oscillations of the soliton lead only to bursty accretion without steady enhancement. Intermediate-mass black holes (~10⁷ M⊙) achieve the longest-lasting growth: amplification up to ~100 times for cs=60 km/s, but with hot gas the rate drops to background levels. At masses around ~10⁸ M⊙, black holes rapidly exhaust gas near the sink, transitioning to a starved regime. The key finding: deepening the potential well alone does not guarantee feeding—enhanced growth requires dynamically keeping the black hole within the dense gas. Relying solely on soliton amplification to form supermassive black holes in the early Universe appears insufficient.

Context

Modern telescopes, like the James Webb, have discovered many quasars at redshift z>7, when the Universe was less than a billion years old. These supermassive black holes with masses up to 10⁹ M☉ require extremely efficient accretion. Traditional estimates of the Bondi accretion rate often yield insufficient values, so alternative mechanisms are being explored. One involves fuzzy dark matter — a hypothetical form of dark matter made of ultralight axions with masses around 10⁻²² eV, whose wave nature manifests on galactic scales. In such models, solitonic cores form in the centers of galaxies, deepening the potential and potentially enhancing accretion. Previous work, including ideas by Vera Rubin and Fritz Zwicky on hidden mass, paved the way, but only recently have numerical simulations allowed these ideas to be tested dynamically.

Methods

The study is based on numerical simulation of a coupled system: fuzzy dark matter is described by the Schrödinger equation with a gravitational potential obeying the law of universal gravitation (in Poisson form), the gas evolves isothermally, and the black hole moves under the combined gravity and accretes matter through a conservative sink of fixed radius. Seeds with initial masses of 10⁶, 10⁷, and 10⁸ M☉ were considered, along with two gas temperatures (sound speeds 60 and 70 km/s). A key feature is the self-consistent wandering of both the hole and the soliton, which critically affects the local density and relative velocity of the gas.

Results

The analytical boost factor (ratio of Bondi accretion to the background rate) and the actually measured accretion rate into the sink were compared over 0.5 billion years. It turned out that for light seeds (10⁶ M☉), the boost is highly unstable: the black hole often leaves the dense solitonic core, and accretion drops to background levels. For intermediate masses (10⁷ M☉), the boost reached values of order 100 with cold gas (60 km/s) and remained stable thanks to effective retention in the core. Heavy seeds (10⁸ M☉) quickly exhausted the gas within the sink and became supply-limited, despite high instantaneous demand. Thus, stable boosting of accretion requires a fine balance between dynamic retention and gas availability, which agrees with the predictions of Hubble's law for density evolution in the Universe, but is not guaranteed by the depth of the potential alone.

Implications

The results show that solitonic cores of fuzzy dark matter cannot serve as a universal accelerator for the growth of supermassive black holes in the early Universe. To reach the observed masses of quasars, either heavier initial seeds or additional gas sources, sustained by cosmological inflows, are needed. A broader implication concerns any model with cored density profiles (e.g., self-interacting dark matter): without an effective mechanism to keep the black hole centered, accretion will be suppressed by wandering. This highlights the importance of dynamical coupling between dark and baryonic matter.

Future development

The topic will develop toward cosmological modeling that includes full physics: inflows, cooling, feedback from active galactic nuclei, and stellar dynamics. Including ensembles of black holes and accounting for mergers could yield a more realistic picture of mass growth. Additionally, future observations by JWST and gravitational-wave observatories will impose constraints on dark matter models.

Impact

The findings impact black hole astrophysics, galaxy formation theory, and dark matter models.

Next steps

Next steps include simulations with continuous gas inflow from large-scale structures and adding a stellar component for a more accurate description of dynamical friction.

Key open problems

This work is directly connected to unresolved problems of the origin of the first black holes, the nature of dark matter, and feedback mechanisms in early galaxies.
