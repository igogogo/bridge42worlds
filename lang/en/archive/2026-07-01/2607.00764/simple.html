The Oldest Stars Have Spoken: Here’s How Old the Universe Is ✦

Original: "The age of the Universe from a large sample of the oldest Galactic stars"
· Indranil Banik, Thenujaya Kudakolawa Kaluarachchige, Stephen Cookson, Harry Desmond
arXiv:2607.00764 · 2026-07-01 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min · Cosmology Galaxies Stellar
Analysis of the Milky Way’s oldest stars confirms: the Universe is about 13.9 billion years old, not 12.9.
Abstract

Like archaeologists hunting for ancient fossils, astronomers have found the oldest star in our Galaxy at 13.73 billion years old—almost the same age as the Universe itself. This coincidence challenges theories that explain expansion mysteries by inventing new physics for the infant Universe. Could the cosmos be simpler than we think?

Links in the knowledge graph 1

To learn a forest’s age, you look for the oldest tree. Astronomers took the same approach with 155,000 ancient stars in our Galaxy. They split each star’s light into colors (spectroscopy), using the shades and brightness to calculate its age via stellar aging models. Then, with the help of computer simulations, they identified the oldest one—dating back 13.73 billion years.

Adding 0.2 billion years for the first stars to form after the Big Bang (an idea from Georges Lemaître), they arrived at a cosmic age of 13.9 billion years. This matches independent measurements from the cosmic microwave background and from the redshift of distant objects.

Yet this age challenges models that proposed a younger Universe (12.9 billion years) to solve the expansion rate mystery—the “Hubble tension” (based on the discovery by Hubble and the theory of Einstein). The most surprising twist: one star could be older than 14 billion years if uncertainties are taken into account—practically a contemporary of the Big Bang.

🎯 One of the stars in the sample ignited when the Universe was only about 300 million years old—by cosmic standards, a stunningly early age.

A_U = A_\star + t_f
The age of the Universe (A_U) equals the age of the oldest star (A_\star) plus the time from the Big Bang to the birth of the first stars (t_f).
