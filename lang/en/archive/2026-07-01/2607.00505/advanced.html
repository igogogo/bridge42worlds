FRB 20250613A: Key to the Mysteries of Fast Radio Bursts and Plasma Physics ✦

Original: "FRB20250613A: a remarkable repeating FRB with apparent millisecond-timescale scattering variations"
· T. Dial, A. T. Deller, Alexa C. Gordon, P. A. Uttarkar, R. M. Shannon, Ziteng Wang, M. Caleb, Wen-fai Fong, Marcin Glowacki, Kelly Gourdji, Joscha N. Jahns-Schindler
arXiv:2607.00505 · 2026-07-01 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · High Energy
A new repeating fast radio burst exhibits exotic properties pointing to a neutron star in a binary system with a massive star.
Abstract

FRB20250613A is a repeating fast radio burst discovered in a dwarf galaxy with low metallicity. Observations with ASKAP, MeerKAT, and Parkes revealed that burst dispersion varies sharply over minutes to hours, while polarization changes over days, including rotation measure jumps of ~300 rad/m². This indicates a turbulent magnetoionized environment. In multicomponent signals, a preferred peak separation of 6.8±0.8 ms is found, likely reflecting an intrinsic emission mechanism. For individual bursts, propagation effects differ on millisecond timescales — this behavior is naturally explained by nonlinear plasma effects from the intense FRB radiation, rather than a changing line of sight. All data are consistent with the hypothesis that the source is embedded in the dense stellar wind of a Be star companion, typical for such low-mass galaxies.

Context

Fast radio bursts (FRB) are enigmatic extragalactic millisecond-duration pulses discovered thanks to radio astronomy, whose foundations were laid by Karl Jansky. Their ability to probe the distribution of ionized gas in the Universe makes them potential cosmological tools, but uncertainty about the nature of their sources limits model accuracy. Repeating bursts like FRB 20250613A offer a unique chance to study the local environment of the progenitor by tracking rapid changes in the propagation of the signal. This object, localized in a dwarf galaxy with low metallicity and active star formation, stands out for record-breaking scattering variations and signs of nonlinear interaction with plasma, which could shed light on the physics of neutron stars and magnetars.

Methods

Observations were carried out with the ASKAP, MeerKAT, and the 64-meter Parkes radio telescopes using spectroscopy and photometry to analyze the radio data. ASKAP provided high-precision localization via interferometry, while MeerKAT and Parkes enabled high-time-resolution monitoring. Optical observations at SOAR and Gemini South provided the host galaxy's redshift and characteristics. The analysis included measuring the dispersion measure (DM) and rotation measure (RM) based on the Faraday effect, fitting profiles with scattering in the interstellar medium, modeling spectral depolarization, and numerically simulating radiation propagation in turbulent plasma. To identify nonlinear effects, the wave strength parameter was evaluated, determining the transition to relativistic electron acceleration.

Results

FRB 20250613A exhibited exceptional activity: over 60 bursts in 5 months. The rotation measure (RM) varied by ~300 rad/m² over weeks, and scattering by nearly two orders of magnitude (0.14–7.2 ms at 1 GHz) within minutes. In multi-component pulses, a strict periodicity with an interval of 6.8±0.8 ms was found, possibly reflecting the spin of the neutron star. Most striking is the behavior of the brightest bursts: their subcomponents, separated by milliseconds, show different scattering and polarization, which cannot be explained by a change in the line of sight. Instead, the data point to nonlinear interaction of the powerful radiation with the plasma of the stellar wind. The host galaxy is a dwarf with a mass of ~2×10⁸ M⊙, metallicity about 0.2 solar, and signs of recent quenching of star formation.

Implications

The set of properties of FRB 20250613A is naturally explained if the source is a neutron star (possibly a magnetar) in a binary system with a Be star, surrounded by a dense and turbulent wind. This supports the scenario in which FRBs are born in extreme magneto-ionic environments, and their variability reflects orbital motion and wind inhomogeneities. The detection of nonlinear plasma effects—relativistic acceleration of electrons by radiation—provides a new method for estimating the distance to the scattering screen and testing models of the interaction of intense radio pulses with plasma.

Future development

Future observations with higher time resolution (e.g., with the upcoming SKA) will enable detailed study of the burst microstructure and the evolution of nonlinear effects. Long-term monitoring of the FRB will help determine the orbital parameters of the binary system, and polarization measurements will map magnetic fields in the stellar wind. Theoretical work should refine the role of magnetars in generating coherent emission and the mechanisms of nonlinear dissipation in plasma.

Impact

The results are significant for neutron star physics, high-energy astrophysics, and cosmology, as FRBs become precise probes of baryonic matter. The methods of analyzing propagation in a turbulent medium are applicable to pulsars and other sources.

Next steps

A priority task is to obtain phase-connected polarization measurements on orbital cycle timescales and search for similar nonlinear effects in other repeating FRBs using high-resolution spectroscopy.

Key open problems

The study is directly linked to unsolved problems: the nature of fast radio bursts, coherent emission mechanisms in magnetars, the physics of collisionless shock waves and nonlinear processes in plasma, as well as the evolution of dwarf galaxies with active star formation.
