Neutron Star Lighthouse in a Cosmic Storm ✦

Original: "FRB20250613A: a remarkable repeating FRB with apparent millisecond-timescale scattering variations"
· T. Dial, A. T. Deller, Alexa C. Gordon, P. A. Uttarkar, R. M. Shannon, Ziteng Wang, M. Caleb, Wen-fai Fong, Marcin Glowacki, Kelly Gourdji, Joscha N. Jahns-Schindler
arXiv:2607.00505 · 2026-07-01 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min · High Energy
A repeating radio burst reveals a neutron star spinning alongside a massive star.
Abstract

Repeating radio burst FRB20250613A from a dwarf galaxy: signal delay (dispersion) changes minute by minute, polarization day by day. Peaks in complex signals are separated by ~6.8 ms — likely the emission rhythm. Rapid variations point to nonlinear plasma effects in the wind of a massive companion star.

Links in the knowledge graph 1

A repeating radio signal has been captured from a distant dwarf galaxy. It’s a fast radio burst — a burst of energy comparable to the Sun's daily output, but squeezed into milliseconds. It was revealed by radio astronomy, born from the discoveries of Karl Jansky. Frequency analysis of the signal and redshift measurement (a shift of light indicating distance) showed that it comes from a system where a superdense stellar remnant — a neutron star — orbits a massive companion. Its beam pierces through scorching gas of charged particles — a stellar wind — and the interstellar medium, like a lighthouse beam in a storm. Signal distortions change within minutes, exposing violent turbulence. In the burst structure, a rhythm of 6.8 milliseconds emerged — likely the neutron star’s "pulse." Perhaps it’s a magnetar with a monstrous magnetic field. The system resides in a small galaxy poor in heavy elements (metallicity several times lower than solar) but with pockets of star formation. Such conditions are an ideal lab for extreme physics. The bursts themselves are so powerful that they accelerate charged particles to nearly the speed of light, altering the surrounding plasma.

🎯 In just a couple of milliseconds, one burst releases more energy than the Sun does in days.

🎬 The bursts’ ability to change the plasma around them echoes Vernor Vinge’s novel "A Fire Upon the Deep," where signals from supercivilizations rewrote the laws of physics.
