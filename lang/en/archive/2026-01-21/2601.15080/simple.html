How to Cool Light to Quantum Order ⚡ экспресс

Original: "Stimulated cooling in non-equilibrium Bose-Einstein condensate"
· Ka Kit Kelvin Ho, Vladislav Yu. Shishkov, Mohammad Amini, Leonie Teresa Wrathall, Evgeny Mamonov, Darius Urbonas, Ioannis Georgakilas, Tobias Herkenrath, Michael Forster, Ullrich Scherf, Tapio Niemi, Päivi Törmä, Anton V. Zasedatelev
arXiv:2601.15080 · 2026-01-21 · CC BY 4.0 · ⏱ 1 min · Quantum Gases Quantum Physics
Physicists cooled a quantum mix of light and matter to 20 degrees above absolute zero and witnessed the moment when chaos gives way to a synchronized dance.
Abstract

Scientists cooled an exotic gas of quasiparticles from nearly room temperature to 20 K (-253°C). It's as if a swarming crowd suddenly froze into perfect order. An astonishing self-sorting mechanism of particles was discovered, which governs the birth of quantum coherence. Could cooling be not just a loss of heat, but a key to a new state of matter?

Links in the knowledge graph 1

Cooling usually brings order: water freezes into ice. But in the quantum world, order takes unexpected forms. Experimenters cooled polaritons — particles that are both light and matter — to near absolute zero. Instead of simple freezing, the cloud began to 'dance,' synchronizing the oscillations of all particles into a single rhythm. But then the dance split: the cloud broke into two 'dance floors' with different tempos, each obeying the laws discovered by Bose and Einstein. Most strikingly, the temperature of these regions turned out to be not a result of heating, but a direct reflection of the number of particles in each clump. The tighter the crowd, the hotter the dance. This unexpected principle is universal for quantum systems far from equilibrium and may find application in future devices operating at the edge of quantum effects.

🎯 Polaritons are billions of times lighter than atoms, so cooling them takes just moments even at temperatures relatively 'high' for the quantum world.
