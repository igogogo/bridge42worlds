How an AI Scientist Taught a Robot Fish to Swim express

Original: "Self-Evolving Scientific Agent Discovers Generalizable Physically-Reasoned Fluid Control"
· Boai Sun, Wenjin Guo, Zongmin Yu, Liu Yang
arXiv:2606.08405 · 2026-06-07 · CC BY · 1 min · Artificial Intelligence Fluid Dynamics
Without any hints, an AI devised a universal algorithm for a robot fish—one you can read like a cooking recipe.
Abstract

Engineers created an agent based on language models that autonomously develops controllers for physical systems while maintaining interpretability. The task was set using a two-joint model of a shark-like fish with underactuation, interacting with fluid. The agent analyzed simulations, iteratively improved the code, and obtained a universal algorithm that guides the fish to static and moving targets without retraining. Principles such as traveling wave and adaptive heading control emerged in the final controller — just like in living organisms. This shows that AI can not just tune weights, but discover physically meaningful strategies, keeping a "log" of its reasoning.

In the watery environment, vortices batter the mechanical fish. The AI agent, like an experimental chef, ran simulations, tried out 'recipes' of movements, and improved the program. Each experiment was like spectroscopy: the agent broke down behavior into elements, uncovering patterns. At first, the fish only paddled in one direction. But after trying hundreds of combinations, the agent derived a universal algorithm for any direction. The result: a clear instruction—body wave, aim, stabilize. This reduced the entropy (disorder) of movements to a minimum.

Astoundingly, the agent invented a swimming method not found in nature: a simultaneous body wave and target tracking, like a homing torpedo. It logged all its 'recipes,' making the AI's logic more transparent than any neural network.

🎯 Unlike neural networks, whose inner workings remain hidden, this agent left a detailed diary of all decisions, like an open cookbook.
