Gravitational Duet: How Entangled Masses Listen to the Whisper of Other Dimensions ✦

Original: "Entanglement probes of gravitational Kaluza-Klein spectra: signal hierarchy and model discrimination"
· Yi Zhong, Tao-Tao Sui, Ke Yang
arXiv:2605.00749v1 · 2026-05-01 · CC BY · ⏱ 3 min · General Relativity
Massive objects in quantum entanglement become a sensitive tuning fork, capturing the whisper of hidden dimensions through a shift in gravitational phase.
Abstract

Scientists investigated how quantum entanglement of massive particles could reveal the influence of extra dimensions on gravity at microscopic scales. Three scenarios were compared: RSII, ADD, and a model with an energy gap (Pöschl–Teller potential). Analysis at distances of 40–80 µm showed that ADD gives the strongest signal, RSII the weakest. With optimistic parameters, all scenarios are detectable, and the shape of the phase response distinguishes RSII from ADD and the gap scenario, which are almost indistinguishable from each other. This opens the possibility of experimentally testing multidimensional theories of gravity.

The universe, according to the Standard Model, seems to be a three-dimensional canvas upon which familiar physics unfolds. Yet deep down, beyond the threshold of the everyday, there may be curled-up, invisible dimensions — six or seven tightly wound loops, as predicted by string theory. Detecting them directly is as impossible as hearing an orchestra in the next room through a concrete wall. But Newtonian gravity, like a low-frequency hum, can seep through any barrier — and if other dimensions exist, they must distort that hum at short distances.

Two microscopic diamonds, suspended in a vacuum, become the strings of an unseen duet. The protocol of quantum-induced entanglement of masses (QGEM) places them in a spatial superposition — each body simultaneously exists in two locations separated by tens of microns. Thanks to the wave-particle duality of massive objects, their gravitational interaction generates a phase shift that depends on the branches of the superposition — the very note we’re trying to catch. If gravity obeys the old laws from Einstein to Newton, the duet sounds predictable. But add hidden dimensions, and the chord changes key. A tiny correction Δ(r) to the potential, like a misadjusted tuning peg, shifts the phase Φ(d) by fractions of a radian, and the derivative of the potential only amplifies this discord.

The relative phase shift acts as a tuning fork for multidimensional theories. In the ADD model with two large extra dimensions, the signal is loud and distinct; for the gapped continuum, it’s softer; and in the Randall–Sundrum II scenario, it’s barely audible without special conditions. The three scenarios form a clear hierarchy, like musical intervals of varying expressiveness.

Numerical results paint an almost detective-like picture. Under conservative parameters (mass ~10⁻¹⁴ kg, interaction time 0.1 s), ADD confidently exceeds the quantum entanglement threshold, while the gapped continuum only steps onto the stage at the edge of the measurement window, and RSII remains backstage. In an optimistic scenario, all three models are detectable, and ADD even enters the strong-entanglement regime. Most intriguing: the normalized phase-response profile clearly separates RSII from the other two, but ADD and the gapped continuum are almost indistinguishable — their “melodies” differ by no more than 0.38%, like two variations on a single theme.

This is more than academic elegance. The experiment directly tackles the hierarchy problem and the quantum nature of gravity, a puzzle that Schrödinger himself pondered. Detecting modifications to the Newtonian potential would confirm the existence of extra dimensions, turning a quantum interferometer into a tabletop detector of spacetime curvature at submillimeter scales. Remarkably, reading out this phase would be the first direct evidence of the quantum nature of gravity — not via elusive gravitons, but through the interference of spacetime trajectories. The next challenge is to overcome decoherence and noise, boosting phase sensitivity. Once these disturbances are tamed, the experiment, like a supremely precise seismograph, will begin to register vibrations from the very fabric of reality. And then, perhaps, we will at last hear the true music of a multidimensional cosmos.
