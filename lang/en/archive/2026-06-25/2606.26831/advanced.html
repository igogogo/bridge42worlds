Hubble Constant: The Distance Ladder, Systematics, and JWST Prospects ✦

Original: "Distance-Ladder Measurements of the Hubble Constant: Recent Progress, Systematics, and Prospects"
· Xiaodian Chen, Shu Wang
arXiv:2606.26831 · 2026-06-25 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Cosmology Stellar
The precision of the local Hubble constant measurement has reached 1%, but the discrepancy with predictions from the cosmic microwave background challenges the standard cosmological model.
Abstract

The Hubble constant H0 ties the local distance scale to the current expansion rate of the universe. Measurements based on the distance ladder achieve sub-percent precision and systematically exceed the value inferred from relic radiation within the ΛCDM standard model by more than 5σ—this is the gist of the Hubble tension. In this work, the ladder is presented as a covariance network that combines geometric anchors, stellar distance indicators (Cepheids, tip of the red giant branch, asymptotic giant branch stars, Mira variables), and probes of the Hubble flow (surface brightness fluctuations, Tully-Fisher relation, type II supernovae). A combined analysis of seven independent routes yields H0 = 73.30 ± 0.92 km/s/Mpc, which is 5.6σ above the Planck ΛCDM value. The James Webb Space Telescope is already checking crowding effects for Cepheids and makes independent H0 measurements from TRGB possible. To achieve robust one-percent precision within the next five years, larger calibrator samples, cross-validation of zero points, explicit covariance treatment, and reproducible, predefined selection criteria—potentially using AI—are needed.

Context

Modern cosmology faces the Hubble tension — a discrepancy between local measurements of [tag:expansion_of_the_universe] and predictions from [tag:cosmic_microwave_background] within the standard ΛCDM model, which includes [tag:dark_energy] and [tag:dark_matter]. [law:hubbles_law] linearly relates [tag:redshift] and distance, but the absolute scale remains under debate. The local distance ladder relies on geometric anchors, stellar standard candles, and Type Ia supernovae ([tag:supernova]), while the early universe is probed via CMB anisotropies. Resolving this conflict will either reveal systematics in one of the methods or require new physics.

Methods

The measurements are built as a three-rung network. The zeroth rung — geometric distances: Gaia trigonometric parallaxes, eclipsing binaries in the Large Magellanic Cloud, and masers in NGC 4258. The first rung — stellar indicators: classical Cepheids, the tip of the red giant branch (TRGB), JAGB carbon stars, and Miras, whose calibration depends on [tag:metallicity] and models of [tag:stellar_evolution]. The second rung — standardized [tag:supernova] Ia, plus alternatives: surface brightness fluctuations, the Tully–Fisher relation, and Type II supernovae. Observations are performed with [tag:hubble] and [tag:jwst] in the near-infrared, reducing dust and crowding effects. Precise [tag:photometry] and spectroscopy enable accounting for [tag:redshift] and peculiar velocities. The key law is [law:hubbles_law], and the cosmological context is set by [law:friedmann_equations].

Results

The flagship Cepheid–Type Ia supernova route, executed by the SH0ES collaboration led by [scientist:Adam Riess], yields H0 = 73.04±1.04 km/s/Mpc based on 42 calibrator supernovae in 37 galaxies. This value remains ~5σ above the Planck value of 67.36±0.54 km/s/Mpc for [tag:cosmic_microwave_background]. [tag:jwst] observations showed that Cepheid crowding in [tag:hubble] images cannot explain the discrepancy: the distance difference was −0.01±0.03 mag. Alternative routes (TRGB, JAGB, Miras, surface brightness fluctuations, Tully–Fisher, Type II supernovae) mostly agree with the high local value, and their covariance-weighted average across seven paths gives H0 = 73.30±0.92 km/s/Mpc, still 5.6σ above the ΛCDM prediction.

Implications

If the tension is not caused by hidden systematics, it will be a direct indication that the standard cosmological model is incomplete. Possible extensions include early dark energy, extra relativistic species, or modifications to gravity. The consistency of various indicators within the local network, verified with [tag:jwst], strengthens confidence in the measured distance scale. Thus, the weight shifts toward revising the physics of the early universe, described by [law:friedmann_equations] and the properties of [tag:dark_matter].

Future development

In the coming years, [tag:jwst] will continue cross-calibrating Cepheids, TRGB, and JAGB in the same galaxies, while the Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope and the Rubin/LSST observatory will expand calibrator supernova samples. Gaia DR4 will improve parallax precision to 0.5%, reducing the zero-point error. The adoption of [tag:machine_learning] methods will make variable star selection reproducible and enable automatic handling of covariances. Numerical simulations ([tag:numerical_simulation]) will help standardize the detection of luminosity function edges and crowding effects.

Impact

Refining the Hubble constant directly impacts cosmology: the age of the universe, large-scale structure, and the nature of [tag:dark_energy]. The results are also critical for interpreting data from future missions like Euclid and SPHEREx, and for testing modified gravity theories.

Next steps

We need to increase the number of calibrator Type Ia supernovae from 42 to ~100, perform a joint Bayesian analysis of all indicators with an explicit covariance matrix, and develop reproducible, predefined source selection criteria using [tag:machine_learning].

Key open problems

The Hubble tension is among the most pressing unsolved problems in physics, alongside the nature of [tag:dark_matter] and [tag:dark_energy]. Resolving it may require a revision of the cosmological model, including inflation, and will raise new questions about fundamental interactions.
