Dark Energy: A New Way to Peer Inside ✦

Original: "Collider Probes of Dark Energy Microphysics"
· Chaitanya Bashyam, Alfredo Gurrola, Andres Florez, Cristian Rodriguez
arXiv:2606.27441 · 2026-06-25 · CC BY · 1 min
HEP Phenomenology Cosmology General Relativity HEP Experiment
Particle collisions will help us 'hear' the hidden voice of dark energy.
Abstract

Dark energy is a mysterious force accelerating the expansion of the Universe. Scientists have proposed searching for its traces in particle collisions at a collider. They built a theory where dark energy influences a new mediator particle. The decay properties of this particle depend on the 'speed of sound' in dark energy, making it possible to distinguish models that are indistinguishable by cosmic observations.

1

The universe is expanding at an accelerating pace, as if driven by an unheard melody — this invisible force was dubbed dark energy. The discovery earned a Nobel Prize for Saul Perlmutter and Adam Riess, yet its inner workings remain a mystery.

Usually, dark energy is inferred from the rush of galaxies away from each other — it's like trying to guess the instruments of an orchestra through a closed door. You hear the rhythm, but not the timbre. Now, physicists propose to 'open the door' using the Large Hadron Collider. There, we can produce fleeting particles that lie beyond the Standard Model (the core theory of the microworld). Their decay patterns depend on the speed of sound in the dark energy medium: it's as if the tonality shifts.

The speed of sound could be thousands of times slower than light. If it were extremely low, dark energy would start to clump, casting invisible acoustic shadows across the cosmos.

In this way, proton collisions help us 'hear' the timbre of the force driving the cosmos apart, and pick the correct theory from many — much like tuning a radio to the right frequency.
