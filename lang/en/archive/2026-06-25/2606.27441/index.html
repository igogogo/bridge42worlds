Dark Energy Symphony: LHC Catches the Unheard Sound ✦

Original: "Collider Probes of Dark Energy Microphysics"
· Chaitanya Bashyam, Alfredo Gurrola, Andres Florez, Cristian Rodriguez
arXiv:2606.27441 · 2026-06-25 · CC BY · 3 min · HEP Phenomenology Cosmology General Relativity HEP Experiment
Resonances of new particles at the Large Hadron Collider promise to measure the sound speed of dark energy for the first time—a key to its unsolved nature.
Abstract

Physicists have figured out how to catch dark energy at the Large Hadron Collider. Dark energy makes the Universe expand at an accelerating rate. A theory has been created linking dark energy to a mediator particle. The decay characteristics of this particle depend on the 'speed of sound' in dark energy — a property that couldn't be measured before. This will help distinguish between different versions of dark energy, indistinguishable by cosmic observations, and connect the micro and macro worlds — like ripples on water betray a hidden current.

In the late 1990s, the night sky delivered a surprise: Saul Perlmutter and Adam Riess found that the expansion of the Universe is accelerating. Ever since, the greatest riddle has been dark energy—a substance making up nearly 70% of the cosmos, yet evading laboratory study. Cosmological observatories have drilled into its equation of state, staring into the cosmic microwave background, but it's not enough. The true nature hides in the sound speed of its fluctuations, \(c_s\). So far its value is almost unconstrained, and different models sound different: some are mute, others hum like an organ pipe. This is what decides the fate of the Universe.

Theoretically \(c_s\) can drop to 10⁻⁴ of the speed of light—then dark energy begins to clump, casting 'acoustic shadows' across the cosmos. In such a Universe, sound crawls slower than a snail, turning a uniform void into a textured pattern.

Now a team of physicists proposes to hear this silent music at the Large Hadron Collider. The key instrument is a pseudoscalar mediator \(a\), arising in the extended Higgs sector 2HDM+a. This particle, akin to the axion, interacts with dark energy through derivative couplings, much like phonons in a crystal. The background kinetic field of dark energy acts as a medium, pulling an invisible string: it renormalizes the mediator's mass and coupling, and fluctuations spawn real pairs of particles. Peter Higgs, unwittingly, set the stage for this drama: without the Higgs boson, such extensions of the Standard Model would be unthinkable. It turns out that the decay width \(a\to\phi\phi\) depends catastrophically on \(c_s\): \(\Gamma \propto (1+c_s^2)^2 / c_s^3\). At \(c_s^2=0.001\), the width explodes nearly a hundred-thousand-fold, turning a narrow peak into a broad hum—silence begets a roar, and the resonance shape reveals hidden parameters.

In numerical simulations (numerical simulation with MadGraph) at a mass around 365 GeV and \(c_s^2=0.001\), the resonance swells hundreds of times, slipping past standard searches. The collider echo-sounder catches this echo of the dark symphony.

This approach marries collider physics and cosmology into a single experiment for the first time. By measuring the width and transverse momentum distribution, we can distinguish models that are indistinguishable by the equation of state \(w\). This is not just a thought experiment: the HL-LHC and future facilities will yield the first real constraints on \(c_s\), outlining the contours of the dark sector. On the horizon—accounting for phase transitions in the early Universe and gravitational waves, closing the loop between the lab and the sky. The same mechanism could unite dark matter and dark energy, and we stand on the brink of a paradigm shift: from fragmented puzzles, a unified theory of all things dark emerges—a symphony that has finally found its voice.

🎯 The sound speed of dark energy may be thousands of times lower than that of light. At \(c_s=10^{-4}c\), it starts to clump, creating 'acoustic shadows'—as if an unheard hum turns the void into an invisible relief.
