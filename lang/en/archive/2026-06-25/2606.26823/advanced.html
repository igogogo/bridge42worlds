Search for Inflation Particles in the CMB: New Constraints and Hints ✦

Original: "Probing inflationary particle production with the CMB power spectrum"
· Hidde T. Jense, Luca H. Abu El-Haj, J. Colin Hill, Oliver H. E. Philcox
arXiv:2606.26823 · 2026-06-25 · CC0 · 2 min · Cosmology
Analysis of Planck and ACT data reveals a weak hint of a signal from massive particles born during inflation.
Abstract

We consider an inflation scenario where the inflaton is coupled to an extremely massive field (with mass mχ ≳ O(100 HI)), leading to impulsive particle production at a characteristic moment of conformal time η*. Full two-point functions of CMB temperature and polarization are computed, predicting localized features in the power spectrum. A joint analysis of Planck and ACT data reveals a weak hint (~2σ) of a signal corresponding to scales 3 ≤ η* ≤ 10 Mpc, but individually neither dataset reaches statistical significance. A Fisher forecast for a Simons Observatory-like experiment promises detection at 3–5σ if the signal is real. Comparing constraints from the power spectrum with previous matched-filter results shows that for relatively light particles (mχ ≲ 200 HI), the spectrum analysis yields an order of magnitude tighter constraints, while in the rare-production regime the matched filter is more sensitive.

Context

Inflation, proposed by Alan Guth, explains the large-scale structure of the universe, but its microphysics remains unknown. The cosmic microwave background, predicted by George Gamow and discovered by Arno Penzias, carries imprints of the inflationary era. Searching for deviations from the standard power spectrum could point to the birth of massive particles. This work explores a scenario where such particles with masses much larger than the Hubble scale during inflation leave a trace as localized features. This connects ultra-high-energy physics with the observed large-scale structure and the redshift of the CMB.

Methods

The authors derived the contribution of pair production of particles to the two-point curvature function within general relativity. The resulting correction to the power spectrum depends on the scale η* and amplitude. A Bayesian MCMC analysis was used to compare with data. Radio observations from the Planck satellite and ACT telescope were used, including lensing and baryon acoustic oscillation data. The background cosmology was set by the Friedmann equations, and the Hubble law accounted for expansion.

Results

The joint analysis of Planck and ACT did not yield a significant signal but revealed a weak hint (~2σ) in amplitude at η* = 3–10 Mpc. Upper limits on 2g^2P* were 0.26 ± 0.11 (η* = 10 Mpc). For masses m0 ≲ 200 HI, constraints from the spectrum are an order of magnitude stronger than from the matched filter method. The forecast for Simons Observatory promises detection at 3–5σ.

Implications

The work showcases the power of spectroscopy of the CMB to probe inflation physics. The hint correlates with the discrepancy in the spectral index ns between Planck and ACT.

Future development

Future studies will include higher-order correlators and models with periodic production. Experiments like Simons Observatory and CMB-S4 will provide a decisive test.

Impact

The results will impact models of inflation and particle physics, as well as joint analyses with large-scale structure.

Next steps

Perform matched filtering on ACT polarization maps at small scales and combine constraints.

Key open problems

The study touches on the nature of inflation and unsolved problems in fundamental physics, such as quantum gravity and a unified field theory.

Key numbers

  • Characteristic scale η* for the detected hint: 3–10 Mpc
  • Signal amplitude 2g^2P* at η* = 10 Mpc: 0.26 ± 0.11
  • Significance level of the hint: ~2σ
  • Predicted significance for Simons Observatory: 3–5σ
  • Particle mass mχ: ≳100 HI (around 10^13 GeV)
