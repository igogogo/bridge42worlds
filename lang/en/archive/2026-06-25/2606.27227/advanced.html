GlitchGAN: Synthesizing Temporal Glitches of Gravitational-Wave Detectors ✦

Original: "Realistic Time-Domain Synthesis of Gravitational-Wave Detector Glitches using Class-Conditional Derivative Generative Adversarial Networks"
· Tom Dooney, Mees de Boer, Harsh Narola, Melissa Lopez, Stefano Bromuri, Daniel Stanley Tan, Chris Van Den Broeck
arXiv:2606.27227 · 2026-06-25 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Instrumentation
GlitchGAN is a generative neural network that recreates realistic noise artifacts (glitches) from LIGO directly in the time domain.
Abstract

We present GlitchGAN — a conditional generative model that synthesizes realistic glitches directly in the time domain. The model is trained on high-quality reconstructions of seven common glitch types from the LIGO O3 observing run. GlitchGAN generalizes effectively and can generate hybrid or transitional forms via interpolation of the class condition vector. Generating 1000 glitches takes under 22 seconds on a CPU, suitable for large-scale synthesis in detector simulation, mock data challenges, and pipeline validation. Synthetic glitches were validated by the Gravity Spy classifier (majority correctly classified) and unsupervised UMAP analysis, showing significant overlap with real samples in the latent space. A critical issue is highlighted: classifiers relying solely on amplitude Q-transforms can confidently assign the correct class to physically meaningless glitches, necessitating validation methods that preserve phase information.

Context

Gravitational-wave astronomy, enabled by LIGO and Virgo detectors, has allowed us to observe mergers of black holes and neutron stars across the Universe. But the unprecedented sensitivity of these instruments makes them vulnerable to transient noise glitches—whose varied morphologies can masquerade as astrophysical gravitational-wave signals. As detection rates rise and next-generation observatories (Einstein Telescope, Cosmic Explorer) come online, glitches will overlap with real signals more frequently, threatening the accuracy of source parameter estimation. Realistic glitch synthesis with machine learning has become critical for validating analysis pipelines and generating mock data.

Methods

GlitchGAN is built on a class-conditional generative adversarial network based on the cDVGAN architecture. The generator takes a noise vector and a class vector and outputs a time series of 8,192 samples. Training used 31,511 glitch reconstructions of seven types (Blip, Fast Scattering, Koi Fish, Low Frequency Burst, Scattered Light, Tomte, Whistle) produced by the DeepExtractor neural network from LIGO O3 data. Unlike standard GANs, cDVGAN includes an additional discriminator operating on the signal's time derivative, which stabilizes training and suppresses unphysical high-frequency artifacts. The model trained for 500 epochs on an NVIDIA A100 GPU, and epoch 210 was manually selected from checkpoints for best visual quality and UMAP distribution overlap.

Results

The Gravity Spy classifier, widely used by the gravitational-wave community to label glitches, correctly identifies 79% of GlitchGAN's synthetic samples with mean confidences above 90%. Q-transform analysis (time-frequency spectrograms) and UMAP projections show significant overlap between real and synthetic data distributions for most classes. The exception is the high-frequency Whistle glitch, whose structure isn't fully reproduced—likely due to insufficient receptive field size of convolutional layers. GlitchGAN can also generate transitional and hybrid morphologies by interpolating the class vector, opening new avenues for testing algorithm robustness. A key limitation is demonstrated with a diffusion model: glitches unrealistic in the time domain nonetheless earned high Gravity Spy confidence because phase information is lost in spectrograms. This underscores the need to supplement validation with phase-preserving methods like UMAP on raw signals.

Implications

This work shows that a single generative model can capture the complex, multi-modal noise distributions of detectors without resorting to parametric approximations. GlitchGAN enables scalable synthesis of instrumental artifacts for realistic numerical simulation of detectors and elaborate mock-data challenges—crucial for preparing for next-generation observatories. The failure of purely magnitude-based validation forces a rethink of standard quality assessment for synthetic data in astrophysics and beyond.

Future development

Future improvements may include attention mechanisms and dilated convolutions to better capture structures across scales. Plans include expanding the glitch class set to include rare types and developing metrics that directly assess phase consistency. Conditional generation will allow controlling not just glitch type but also duration and signal-to-noise ratio, making the tool even more versatile.

Impact

The approach directly impacts gravitational-wave astronomy (pipeline validation, data simulations), time-series analysis in particle physics, and the field of machine learning (evaluating generative models).

Next steps

Next steps include hyperparameter optimization for the Whistle class, integration into LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA mock-data pipelines, and testing on data from the fourth observing run O4.

Key open problems

The challenge of realistic noise synthesis ties into fundamental issues of non-stationary and non-Gaussian noise in precision measurements, echoing unsolved problems in quantum metrology and weak-signal detection theory. The ability to fool magnitude-based classifiers raises a philosophical question about the 'realism' of synthesized data, demanding objective similarity measures that preserve causal-phase structure.

🎯 LIGO's glitch class names sometimes draw from animal life and folklore: Blip is a burst, Koi Fish resembles a carp, and Whistle is, well, a whistle.

🎬 GlitchGAN's ability to blend classes echoes the 'glitch in the Matrix' concept, where anomalies in a simulation reveal its artificial nature.

Key numbers
