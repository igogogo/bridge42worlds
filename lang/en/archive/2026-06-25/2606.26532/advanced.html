Viscous Cosmology: How Bulk Viscosity Changes the Birth of Primordial Black Holes ✦

Original: "Primordial black hole formation in bulk-viscous cosmology"
· Zi-Yan Yuwen, Cristian Joana, Shao-Jiang Wang, Rong-Gen Cai
arXiv:2606.26532 · 2026-06-25 · CC BY · 4 min
General Relativity Cosmology
Accounting for bulk viscosity in the cosmic fluid raises the collapse threshold and increases the mass of forming primordial black holes, as shown in numerical simulations.
Abstract

The formation of primordial black holes (PBHs) in a cosmological medium with bulk viscosity is investigated. The collapse threshold and mass of forming PBHs are determined via numerical simulations. The critical threshold μ_c retains a dependence on the equation-of-state parameter w similar to the inviscid case, but increases by an amount comparable to the strength of bulk viscosity ε. For fixed w, μ_c grows roughly linearly with ε. Fitting the critical scaling law for near-threshold collapse shows that viscosity leads to an increase in PBH mass. Thus, bulk viscosity systematically alters both the threshold and mass scaling of PBHs in the early Universe.

1

Context

One of the leading candidates for dark matter is primordial black holes, hypothetically born from the collapse of overdense regions after inflationary expansion. Their prediction is linked to the names of Stephen Hawking and other pioneers. However, the standard analysis assumes an ideal cosmic fluid, whereas in the extreme conditions of the Big Bang, dissipative effects—in particular, bulk viscosity—may emerge. This work for the first time applies numerical simulation in full general relativity to determine how viscosity changes the collapse threshold and the mass spectrum of primordial black holes, which is crucial for testing the dark matter hypothesis and interpreting data from gravitational microlensing.

Methods

Within the three-dimensional BSSN formalism (Baumgarte–Shapiro–Shibata–Nakamura), the system of Einstein field equations and hydrodynamic equations with viscous pressure according to Eckart's theory was solved. The stress-energy tensor contained a term Π = −ε ρ^{1/2} Θ_f, where ε is the dimensionless viscosity strength and Θ_f is the fluid expansion. The background was described by the Friedmann equations with an effective equation of state w_eff = w − ε√(24π). Initial overdensities were set with two curvature profiles: a sinc function (characteristic of a sharp spectral peak) and a Gaussian. Evolution was computed on a non-uniform radial grid using an explicit fourth-order Runge–Kutta method. Black hole formation was identified by the appearance of a trapped surface, and its mass by the Misner–Sharp mass. An analytical estimate of the threshold shift relied on the Jeans argument about the balance between gravitational collapse and sound pressure.

Results

It was found that the critical threshold µ_c increases with viscosity almost linearly: for ε=0.01 and w=1/3, the increase is ~0.04 (from 0.60 to 0.64 for the sinc profile, from 0.80 to 0.83 for the Gaussian). The dependence on the equation-of-state parameter remains monotonic, similar to the inviscid case. A power-law fit µ_c − µ_{c,0} = K ε^Γ yields Γ ≈ 1 and K of order unity (Table II). Near the threshold, the PBH mass follows the scaling M_PBH/M_H = k |µ−µ_c|^γ. Viscosity increases the amplitude k: for example, for the sinc profile, k changes from 8.30 to 10.22, while the exponent γ decreases slightly (from 0.37 to 0.34). This result is consistent with the physical picture: dissipation slows collapse, requiring a larger initial density contrast to overcome gravitational collapse, analogous to the effect of Jeans instability in a viscous medium.

Implications

The results demonstrate for the first time that even a small bulk viscosity (ε ~ 0.01) can systematically change the formation threshold of primordial black holes and their mass function. This means that reliable predictions of PBH abundance as dark matter must account for the non-ideality of the primordial plasma. Viscosity shifts the distribution toward larger masses, which could ease tensions with observational constraints. Moreover, the work expands the toolkit of numerical cosmology, demonstrating the applicability of the BSSN formalism to dissipative media, and paves the way for more realistic modeling of extreme gravitational processes.

Future development

Future studies could incorporate second-order viscosity (Israel–Stewart theory) with a finite relaxation time, eliminating acausalities. Full 3+1 simulations with anisotropy would allow computation of spins and ellipticities of newborn black holes. The aspect of Hubble tension is also intriguing: viscosity softens the background equation of state, potentially reducing the discrepancy between local and global measurements of the Hubble constant, creating a nontrivial link between collapse microphysics and large-scale cosmology.

Impact

The study will impact the interpretation of data from missions searching for dark matter through gravitational microlensing (Subaru, OGLE, future space telescopes), as well as the analysis of the stochastic gravitational-wave background, where the PBH mass function is important. For high-energy physics, it exemplifies the significant role of dissipation in gravitational collapse, which may also be relevant in astrophysical contexts.

Next steps

Conduct simulations with a wider range of ε and w parameters, as well as with nonlinearities in the power spectrum. Develop robust schemes for viscosity evolution in arbitrary gauges, suitable for negative and large perturbations.

Key open problems

The work directly addresses the nature of dark matter: if primordial black holes explain it entirely, then the mass function, determined by the collapse threshold, must satisfy numerous astrophysical constraints. The viscous modification shifts the theoretical predictions and may improve agreement. Moreover, the study fits into the broader context of the problem of the initial conditions of the universe: non-ideal effects could have played a role in structure formation, smoothing or sharpening inhomogeneities.

🎯 Although viscosity is often associated with a slowing action, in the context of black hole birth it plays a stabilizing role: the 'thick honey' of the cosmic medium requires a greater density excess to collapse. Only in 2026 did numerical simulations enable a quantitative assessment of this effect—more than half a century after the pioneering works of Hawking and Carr.

Key numbers

  • Collapse threshold increase: Δµ_c ≈ 0.04 at ε=0.01 (w=1/3)
  • Effective equation of state parameter: w_eff ≈ 0.25 at w=1/3, ε=0.01
  • Mass scaling amplitude (sinc): k increases from 8.3 to 10.2 at ε=0.01
  • Mass scaling exponent (sinc): γ ≈ 0.37 (ε=0) and 0.34 (ε=0.01)
  • Power-law index for threshold: Γ ≈ 1
