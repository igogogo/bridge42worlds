The 'Gaia Sausage' star cloud turned out to be not one, but several traces of ancient collisions.
Abstract

The GSE structure in the Milky Way was long believed to be the aftermath of one ancient smash-up. But a new analysis, like teasing apart a tangled ball of yarn, has uncovered four distinct star groups ranging in age from 7 to 12 billion years. Think of it as a cosmic layer cake, built from different epochs. This means our Galaxy was assembled through a series of mergers, not a single blow. Just picture how many more tales are still buried in the stars!

It was once thought that the Milky Way grew by gobbling up other galaxies. The outskirts of our Galaxy are littered with leftovers from such meals. The most prominent is the 'Gaia Sausage' cloud. For a long time, it was taken as the scar of a single powerful impact. But the DESI telescope, splitting starlight into colors, revealed four distinct streams within. Their ages range from 7 to 12 billion years, and their chemical makeup varies more than the recipes for a layer cake. Some are rich in carbon and oxygen, while others contain iron forged in supernova explosions and the disruption of stellar remnants—white dwarfs. Each stream is a separate layer in this cosmic pie, adding its own ingredient to the overall structure.

This discovery flips the picture: instead of one collision, a series of small mergers. The oldest group of stars predates our galactic disk: they ignited when the Universe was just 1.5 billion years old. The layered structure helps us understand the distribution of invisible dark matter that holds everything together. The James Webb Space Telescope will in the future discern even finer layers of this cosmic pastry. The foundations of knowledge were laid by Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin, who deciphered the composition of stars; Fred Hoyle, who explained element synthesis; and Chandrasekhar, who found the mass limit for explosion.

🎯 The structure was nicknamed 'Sausage' because of its shape on a velocity map: if you plot the stars' speeds in three dimensions, they stretch out like a sausage.

🎬 It's reminiscent of Asimov's 'Foundation': a galactic empire grows by absorbing neighbors, but here gravity replaces politics, and stellar streams replace worlds.
