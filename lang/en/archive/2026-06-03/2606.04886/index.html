Dark energy may be losing energy: explaining strange behavior ⚡ экспресс

Original: "Dissipative Dark Energy can explain the DESI phantom crossing"
· Prolay Chanda, Subinoy Das, Suratna Das
arXiv:2606.04886 · 2026-06-03 · CC BY 4.0 · ⏱ 1 min · Cosmology General Relativity HEP Phenomenology HEP Theory
A subtle friction in dark energy explains why it changes — hinted at by the DESI telescope.
Abstract

Data from the DESI telescope suggest that dark energy—the engine behind the universe's accelerating expansion—isn't constant. Instead, it evolves, possibly crossing what's called the phantom divide (a threshold where its properties flip). A simple scenario is proposed: slight dissipation (energy loss) within the dark energy field naturally triggers both evolution and the crossing, without needing exotic phantom fields. This mechanism could explain observations while respecting known physics.

In the late 1990s, astronomers including Saul Perlmutter discovered that the universe is expanding at an accelerating rate. The cause was dubbed dark energy — an invisible force stretching the cosmos. For a long time, it was thought to be constant, but fresh data from the DESI telescope, which studies millions of galaxies via their light, showed: around 6 billion years ago dark energy changed its behavior, crossing a critical threshold beyond which its influence becomes even stranger. Physicists described this field like a pendulum. In a standard model, a pendulum meets no resistance and swings forever. But if you put it in a very thick fluid, it starts losing energy due to friction. Similarly, dark energy experiences an ultra-weak ‘friction’ that dissipates its energy and makes the expansion of the universe accelerate differently. No exotic hypotheses — just a natural energy loss. Previously, such a transition would have doomed the universe to a ‘Big Rip’ — a catastrophic end, but the subtle friction cancels this scenario.

🎯 The density of dark energy is so tiny that within the volume of Earth, it would amount to just a couple of kilograms — yet on cosmic scales, it determines the fate of the universe.
