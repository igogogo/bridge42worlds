The Fourth Planet in the Kepler-51 System: An Unexpected Discovery by the JWST

Original: "A Fourth Planet in the Kepler-51 System Revealed by Transit Timing Variations"
· Kento Masuda, Jessica E. Libby-Roberts, John H. Livingston, Kevin B. arXiv:2410.01625 · 2024-10-02 · CC BY 4.0 · ⏱ 3 min · Exoplanets
Observations by the James Webb Space Telescope revealed a significant deviation in the transit time of Kepler-51d, pointing to the presence of a previously unknown planet, Kepler-51e, and forcing a revision of the masses of the inner 'super-puff' worlds.
Abstract

The roughly 1-billion-year-old Kepler-51 system hosts three transiting planets with radii of 6–9 R⊕ and periods of 45–130 days. Previous TTV analysis using Kepler and HST data indicated low masses and extremely low densities (≲0.1 g/cm³). However, a transit of Kepler-51d recorded by JWST a decade after the Kepler mission showed a significant departure from the three-planet model. To account for the discrepancy, a fourth planet — Kepler-51e — has been introduced, with a mass up to that of Jupiter and an orbital period up to 10 years. All coplanar solutions obtained through brute-force search keep the inner planets at low masses (≲10 M⊕) and thus low densities, albeit with increased uncertainties. The most likely scenario (a 2:1 resonance with Kepler-51d) yields low eccentricities (≲0.05) and similar masses (~5 M⊕) for all four planets — a hallmark of compact multi-planet systems. This result underscores the value of sustained TTV monitoring for detecting long-period planets.

Context

Exoplanets with densities lower than water—so-called 'super-puffs'—challenge our current understanding of planet formation. The Kepler-51 system, with three such planets (b, c, d) sized ~7–9 R⊕ and orbital periods of 45, 85, and 130 days, has become the benchmark for studying this phenomenon. Previous work on transit timing variations (TTVs) by Eric Agol and others, using data from the Hubble Space Telescope and photometry from the Kepler mission, initiated by William Borucki, allowed estimates of their masses (<10 M⊕) and densities. However, the precise prediction of a future transit time, needed for observations by JWST, turned out to be wrong, opening the door to detecting a hidden neighbor.

Methods

Researchers combined photometric data from transits of the three planets, collected over 14 years: from the Kepler, TESS, Hubble, and JWST space telescopes, as well as ground-based observatories (APO, Palomar, etc.). For each light curve, spectroscopy and Gaussian process methods were applied to account for stellar activity, including spots. Transit timing variations were modeled using numerical N-body simulations of gravitational interactions between planets, employing techniques developed by David Charbonneau for precise transit photometry. To find parameters for the fourth planet, a grid search over periods and phases was performed, followed by optimization and assessment of dynamical stability.

Results

The key result: the transit of Kepler-51d, recorded by JWST in 2023, occurred two hours earlier than predicted by the three-planet model, with a prediction uncertainty of just 2.7 minutes—a discrepancy of over 40 sigma. Adding a fourth planet, Kepler-51e, to the model reconciled all the data. Its possible parameters span a wide range: from a low-mass (~5 M⊕) planet near a 2:1 resonance with Kepler-51d to a massive (~ M_Jup) planet on a distant, elongated orbit. However, in all coplanar solutions, the inner planets' masses remain below ~10 M⊕, though for Kepler-51b the upper limit increased to ~13 M⊕. The 2:1 resonance solution is noteworthy because all four planets have similar masses (~5 M⊕) and low eccentricities, reminiscent of the 'peas in a pod' architecture typical of many compact multi-planet systems.

Implications

The discovery of Kepler-51e rewrites our understanding of the system, which now appears more 'crowded' than previously thought. It highlights that many exoplanetary systems may harbor unseen planets that influence TTVs. For modeling planetary migration and stellar evolution theories, it is important that the super-puff planets' masses remain low, but with increased uncertainties, leaving room for mechanisms that explain their bloated atmospheres.

Future development

Future transit observations of all three planets, especially Kepler-51c and d, will refine the orbit and mass of Kepler-51e. According to predictions, differences in TTV between possible solutions will reach tens of minutes in the coming years, making monitoring critical. Also promising are searches for transit duration variations (TDVs), which could hint at mutual orbital inclinations.

Impact

The results are important for interpreting data from exoplanet search missions (PLATO, ARIEL) and for understanding planet formation in general. They also demonstrate how long-term TTV campaigns can probe the outer regions of systems, inaccessible to direct transits.

Next steps

Regular transit observations (especially at epochs predicted by various models) are needed to narrow down the possible orbits of Kepler-51e. Attempts to measure planet masses via radial velocity, though challenging due to the star's faintness and activity, could provide independent verification.

Key open problems

The discovery directly connects to key questions: Why do some planets have extremely low density? How do compact multi-planet systems form? Is there a 'cliff' of planetary systems beyond the transiting planets, or do undiscovered worlds lurk there?
