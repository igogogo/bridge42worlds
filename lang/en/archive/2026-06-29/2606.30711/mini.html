A gas cocoon around a black hole fools telescopes into mistaking small objects for supermassive giants.

The red dots caught by Webb may not be supermassive black holes, but 'tiny tots' wrapped in a dense gas cocoon. This fog inflates their sizes and tricks telescopes. Perhaps this is how future monster quasars are born, hidden for now in their own cradle.

🎯 In 1843, the star Eta Carinae became the second brightest in the sky, even though it was 7,500 light-years away. Its light was amplified by a gas shell — just like the 'baby' black holes we see in the red dots.
