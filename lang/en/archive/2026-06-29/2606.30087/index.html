Branching Inflation: How Stochastic Trees Revealed Exotic Black Holes ✦

Original: "Compaction function in stochastic inflation: a \texttt{FOREST} of type I and II primordial black holes"
· Chiara Animali, Pierre Auclair, Baptiste Blachier, Eemeli Tomberg, Vincent Vennin
arXiv:2606.30087 · 2026-06-29 · CC BY · 3 min · Cosmology General Relativity
A new stochastic tree method computes the compaction function and shows: in quantum inflation regimes, exotic type II black holes predominate—a challenge to established scenarios.
Abstract

Scientists used the method of stochastic binary trees (think branching random chains) to calculate the compression function—a crucial value for predicting how primordial black holes form in an inflating universe. This approach let them clearly distinguish two types of fluctuations: type I, where the region’s radius grows steadily, and type II, which is more complex. In the classical regime, black holes have a narrow range of masses and type II barely shows up. But when quantum diffusion runs wild, masses vary by orders of magnitude, and type II fluctuations take over. This leads to a ‘cloud-in-cloud’ effect, meaning we need a deeper understanding for precise predictions.

After the Big Bang, the universe underwent a rapid expansion—inflation, smoothing out the cosmic microwave background and sowing the seeds of galaxies. But at the quantum level, chaotic fluctuations brewed in the inflaton field. Once a fluctuation crossed a critical threshold, a primordial black hole was born—an object whose gravity curves spacetime to the extreme, potentially shedding light on the nature of dark matter and becoming a source of gravitational waves. The trouble was that standard perturbation theory recipes fell short against the wild nonlinearity of collapse. So researchers turned to the language of stochastic trees.

Imagine a family tree where each Hubble volume is a separate family. Time passes, volume doubles, and the family splits into two daughter lines—that’s how inflation branches. Nodes are moments of division, leaves are the ends of epochs. To predict collapse, scientists introduced the compaction function—a kind of 'family inequality coefficient.' At a node b_L it takes the form C_l = 2z(w)[1 - log_2(V_{b_R}/V_{c_R})], where V_{b_R} is the volume of the 'uncle' (right neighbor) and V_{c_R} is the volume of the 'nephew' (right descendant). If the inheritance gap is too large, spacetime can’t hold and collapses into a black hole.

The compaction function is like the scales of Themis: if the imbalance between the 'uncle' and 'nephew' volumes exceeds the critical value C_c = 1/2, a black hole is born.

Hunting for such events required numerical simulations over tens of billions of trees. And here the dual nature of collapse revealed itself. Black holes appeared in two guises. Type I — classical collapsars: the perturbation dives under the horizon and sets a trap for light. Type II — exotic creatures with a non-monotonic radius function; their throat seems to connect an inflating bubble to the outside universe. In the calm classical limit, type I holes dominate, while the weird type II are suppressed. But turn on strong quantum noise—and the picture flips.

This flip forces a rethinking of the hunt for dark matter. In phases of ultra-slow roll, where quantum kicks overpower classical drift, type II holes are mainly born—so their signatures in gravitational waves could be entirely different. Instead of the familiar ring of mergers, we might hear the whisper of wormholes or the echo of decaying throats. Stephen Hawking and Roger Penrose proved the inevitability of singularities, but the second type of hole hints that the horizon is more complex than a simple trap. Stochastic trees give the key to this symphony: from the tree’s topology one can reconstruct the geometry of all spacetime and predict where and which primordial black holes we will encounter in future surveys. And just as tree rings preserve memories of droughts and fires, branching statistics may hide imprints of quantum phase transitions—perhaps even traces of other universes that brushed against ours in infancy.

🎯 A stochastic tree is a genealogical tree of space volumes, where each node inherits the 'state' of its parent Hubble region. Branching occurs when the volume doubles, and the compaction function becomes a measure of 'family inequality' between related nodes.

C_{l,\ b_L} = 2z(w) \left[ 1 - \log_2\!\left( \frac{V_{b_R}}{V_{c_R}} \right) \right]
The linear compaction function C_l at node b_L is determined by the ratio of volumes arising from its two 'side' branches: the right neighbor by parent (b_R) and its own right descendant (c_R). Here z(w) = 3(1+w)/(5+3w) is a factor depending on the equation of state w.
C_c = \frac{1}{2}
If the compaction function value C_l at a node exceeds 1/2, the corresponding region of space collapses into a black hole.
