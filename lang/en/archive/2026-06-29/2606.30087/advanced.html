Branching Worlds of Inflation: Stochastic Trees Distinguish Two Types of Primordial Black Holes ✦

Original: "Compaction function in stochastic inflation: a \texttt{FOREST} of type I and II primordial black holes"
· Chiara Animali, Pierre Auclair, Baptiste Blachier, Eemeli Tomberg, Vincent Vennin
arXiv:2606.30087 · 2026-06-29 · CC BY · 4 min · Cosmology General Relativity
A new method based on stochastic trees computes the compaction function and reveals an unexpected dominance of exotic type II black holes in quantum regimes of inflation.
Abstract

It is shown how to compute the compression function within the framework of stochastic inflation by solving the dynamics of a random field on stochastic binary trees. In this setup, the compression function is related to the ratio of volumes exiting from adjacent branches of a given node. This approach also determines whether the areal radius of the perturbation grows monotonically with the radial coordinate, allowing one to distinguish between type I and type II fluctuations. As an application, the formation of primordial black holes is investigated in a single-field model with a constant potential slope, using realizations of stochastic trees generated by the FOREST code. In the classical regime, where quantum diffusion is suppressed, the black hole mass function is narrow, and type II fluctuations are strongly suppressed compared to type I. In the quantum and near-critical (close to eternal inflation) regimes, the mass distribution spans several orders of magnitude, the overall abundance of black holes is enhanced, and type II fluctuations outnumber type I. In these cases, cloud-in-cloud effects are important, requiring a better understanding of the evolution and collapse of type II perturbations for reliable predictions.

Context

The inflationary model of Alan Guth explains the uniformity of the cosmic microwave background and the formation of large-scale structure, predicting a nearly scale-invariant perturbation spectrum. According to Einstein's field equations, these perturbations grow over time, and on small scales quantum fluctuations can become dominant. In this regime, rare but extreme density spikes can collapse into primordial black holes — objects considered as candidates for dark matter and sources of gravitational waves. However, standard methods based on perturbation theory are inapplicable to strongly nonlinear fluctuations. Stochastic inflation offers a non-perturbative approach, treating expansion as a random process, but until now it could not directly compute the compaction function — a critical indicator of collapse.

Methods

The method is based on the separate-universe picture, where space is partitioned into causally disconnected regions of Hubble radius. In the stochastic tree formalism, the inflaton's evolution in each region is described by a Langevin equation, and tree branching occurs whenever the physical volume of a region doubles. Tree nodes correspond to branching moments, and leaves to the end of inflation. The compaction function is computed through the volume imbalance between neighboring branches: for node b_L it is proportional to the logarithm of the ratio of volumes of its 'uncle' (b_R) and right child (c_R). This discretization preserves all symmetries of the continuous limit and automatically accounts for the 'cloud-in-cloud' effect. Numerical simulations were performed using the open-source code FOREST, generating populations of up to 10¹⁰ trees.

Results

Depending on the potential parameters, three regimes are identified. In the classical limit (classicality parameter dμ² ≫ 1) perturbations are nearly Gaussian, and black holes form rarely. Their mass function is narrow, and the vast majority of objects are type I: their horizon forms in the standard way after the perturbation re-enters the Hubble radius. Type II (with a non-monotonic radius function in the metric) is exponentially suppressed. In the quantum regime (dμ² ≪ 1) and near the eternal inflation boundary, the picture changes radically: abundant pumping by quantum fluctuations generates heavy tails in the perturbation distribution. The mass function stretches over several orders of magnitude, and the fraction of type II objects sharply increases, often exceeding that of type I. Also important in this regime are 'cloud-in-cloud' effects, where larger-scale collapses engulf smaller ones.

Implications

The discovery of type II black holes as the dominant population in the strong quantum regime challenges established ideas about their rarity. Since such objects may have a fundamentally different horizon structure — a throat connecting the inflating bubble to the external universe — their subsequent evolution and observational signatures (e.g., in gravitational waves) require reexamination. The results also emphasize that the non-Gaussianity naturally arising in stochastic inflation cannot be reduced to simple parameterizations like the local f_NL.

Future development

The proposed method opens the way to more realistic inflation modeling, especially scenarios with an ultra-slow-roll phase, where quantum effects are particularly strong. The next step could be constructing a consistent geometry of the entire spacetime based on the tree topology, enabling studies of primordial black hole clustering and their spatial correlations.

Impact

The results will impact cosmology, astrophysics, and gravitational-wave astronomy, providing new guidelines for the search for primordial black holes as a component of dark matter and the interpretation of merger signals.

Next steps

Detailed numerical simulations of type II perturbation collapse in general relativity are needed to determine their final fate — whether they form black holes, wormholes, or something else. In parallel, the FOREST algorithm should be adapted to more complex potentials, including multi-field models.

Key open problems

The work touches on fundamental problems: the nature of dark matter — primordial black holes remain a viable candidate; the quantum-to-classical transition in the early Universe — where exactly stochastic dynamics gives way to deterministic; and the final stage of gravitational collapse of strongly nonlinear perturbations, at the interface with quantum gravity.

🎯 The stochastic tree can be visualized as a family tree of space volumes, where each node inherits the 'state' of the parent Hubble patch. Branching occurs when the volume doubles, and the compaction function turns out to be a measure of 'family inequality' between sibling nodes.

C_{l,\ b_L} = 2z(w) \left[ 1 - \log_2\!\left( \frac{V_{b_R}}{V_{c_R}} \right) \right]
The linear compaction function C_l at node b_L is determined by the ratio of volumes arising from two of its 'side' branches: the right sibling of its parent (b_R) and its own right child (c_R). Here z(w) = 3(1+w)/(5+3w) is a factor depending on the equation of state w.

Key numbers

  • Collapse threshold for compaction function: C_c = 1/2
  • Number of e-folds between tree branchings: ΔN = ln(2)/3 ≈ 0.231
  • Equation of state during radiation domination: w = 1/3
  • Maximum number of simulated trees: 10¹⁰
  • Classicality parameter (transition to classical regime): dμ² ≫ 1
