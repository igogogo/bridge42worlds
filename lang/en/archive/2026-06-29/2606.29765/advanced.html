Ferron Hall Effect: Transverse Accumulation of Polarization in Ferroelectrics ✦

Original: "Ferron Hall effect: Transverse accumulation of polarization driven by thermal gradients in ferroelectrics"
· Daniel A. Bustamante Lopez, Verena Brehm, Dominik M. Juraschek
arXiv:2606.29765 · 2026-06-29 · CC BY · 5 min · Materials
A new Hall effect for electric polarization has been theoretically predicted, arising in ferroelectrics under the combined action of a temperature gradient and a magnetic field.
Abstract

The phonon Hall effect consists of the emergence of a transverse heat flow under a longitudinal temperature gradient in a magnetic field. It was theoretically shown that when the deflected lattice excitations carry an electric dipole moment, their transverse motion leads to the accumulation of electric polarization in ferroelectrics. This mechanism is called the ferron Hall effect because the polarization carriers are ferrones. The effect is illustrated using atomistic lattice dynamics with parameters derived from density functional theory, on the example of the classic ferroelectric BaTiO₃. The results identify ferrones as the electric analogue of magnons in transverse transport and open a path to thermomagnetic control of ferroic order.

Context

Hall effects are a cornerstone of modern condensed matter physics. From the classical electromagnetic Hall effect to exotic quantum, spin, and orbital versions, they all describe a transverse response to a longitudinal perturbation. In recent years, this family has gained bosonic members: Hall effects for photons, magnons, and phonons. The phonon Hall effect, in particular, manifests as a transverse heat flow under a magnetic field. However, one fundamental quantity remained uncovered: electric polarization in dielectrics. Ferroelectrics, where elementary excitations — ferrons — carry a dipole moment, turned out to be an ideal platform. Similar to how the solar wind is deflected by the magnetosphere, or plasma in an accretion disk forms jets under the action of a magnetic field, the thermal flow of ferrons can be redirected transversely. The study harnesses the power of numerical simulation, allowing a glimpse into the microworld of lattice dynamics without oversimplified analytical approximations. A key role is also played by entropy — the temperature gradient creates a non-equilibrium state, and the fluctuation-dissipation theorem links random forces to dissipation. The work continues the traditions of phase transition theory of Lev Landau, who laid the foundations for describing ferroelectrics, and traces back to the ideas of Albert Einstein on Brownian motion.

Methods

To demonstrate the ferron Hall effect, the authors constructed a microscopic model of barium titanate (BaTiO₃) — a classic ferroelectric. The lattice dynamics are described by a collective soft-mode coordinate responsible for polarization. The model parameters — force constants, Born effective charges, dielectric permittivities — were obtained from first principles using density functional theory. Thermal excitation is imposed by a temperature gradient: a hot spot at the center of the sample with T=50 K surrounded by cold edges at near-zero temperature. An external magnetic field is introduced phenomenologically via a gyroscopic term in the Langevin equations, which mimics the Zeeman interaction of the phonon angular momentum with the field. Calculations were performed using stochastic molecular dynamics for a three-dimensional lattice of 101×85×13 unit cells with periodic boundary conditions along the gradient and open boundaries in the transverse direction. Averaging over 200 independent realizations allowed for the extraction of a weak signal, odd in both magnetic field and temperature gradient.

Results

The key result is the prediction of transverse accumulation of electric polarization caused by the joint action of heat and a magnetic field. In zero field, the temperature gradient only generates a phonon Hall angular momentum and a slight redistribution of kinetic energy. Turning on the field for B∥z leads to an asymmetric shift of polarization: opposite-sign charges accumulate on opposite transverse edges of the sample with a density of ±15.7 µC/m². This shift reverses sign upon reversing either the magnetic field or the longitudinal temperature gradient, which is the hallmark of a true Hall effect. The magnitude of the effect is about four orders of magnitude smaller than the spontaneous polarization of BaTiO₃ (~0.35 C/m²), but it is quite measurable by modern methods, such as second-order optical spectroscopy and polarization microscopy. The physical mechanism is transparent: the redirection of the ferron flow by the magnetic field is analogous to the deflection of charged particles in the classical Hall effect, but here the carriers are not electrons but lattice excitations carrying a dipole moment.

Implications

The discovery of the ferron Hall effect introduces electric polarization into the family of transverse transport phenomena, on par with heat and spin. It lays the foundation for a new direction — ferronics, where information is carried and processed not by magnetic but by electric dipole excitations. This promises a breakthrough in creating devices with ultralow power consumption, since ferroelectrics are insulators and polarization transport does not involve ohmic losses. Moreover, the effect blurs the line between thermal, magnetic, and electric phenomena, opening up possibilities for multiferroic devices where all three influences are intertwined. Technologically, this could lead to new types of heat flow sensors switchable by a magnetic field, and memory elements based on polarization domains.

Future development

Further development of the topic will require calculations with realistic domain structures, accounting for surface bound charges and screening. The primary task is to obtain microscopic magneto-phonon coupling coefficients from first principles for specific materials, which will allow a transition from qualitative demonstration to quantitative predictions. Experimental realization could follow the adaptation of phonon Hall effect schemes: creating a thermal Hall bar on a ferroelectric film with lateral electrodes to measure transverse polarization. Of particular interest is the search for materials with strong magneto-phonon coupling, possibly among multiferroics or ferroelectrics with magnetic impurities, where the effect could be enhanced by orders of magnitude.

Impact

The effect will impact the physics of ferroelectrics, thermoelectricity, spintronics, and multiferroics. Engineering applications may encompass thermal diodes switchable by polarization, and magnetic storage devices based on ferroelectrics.

Next steps

The next steps include verifying the effect in a simplified geometry by means of piezoresponse force microscopy and nonlinear optics, as well as more accurate first-principles calculations of magneto-phonon coupling to search for optimal materials.

Key open problems

The ferron Hall effect is directly connected to the fundamental problem of interconversion of thermal, magnetic, and electric fluxes in solids. It raises the question of the existence of topologically protected edge states for polarization excitations, analogous to those observed in the quantum Hall effect, and prompts the search for materials with nontrivial phonon band geometry. An important role is played here by the second law of thermodynamics, which determines the direction of flows, and the fluctuation-dissipation theorem links non-equilibrium transport to equilibrium correlations.
