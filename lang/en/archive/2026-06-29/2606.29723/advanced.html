How to See the Invisible: Latent Atlases of Multiscale Fields ✦

Original: "ScaleAware-JEPA: Latent Representation for Discovery in Multiscale Physical Fields"
· Guang-Xing Li
arXiv:2606.29723 · 2026-06-29 · CC BY · 3 min · Machine Learning Instrumentation Computer Vision Computational Physics
The ScaleAware-JEPA method links machine learning with the physical hierarchy of scales to find structures in complex environments without supervision.
Abstract

For analyzing continuous physical fields whose structures are organized into a multi-scale hierarchy without known coordinates in advance, standard self-supervised methods with a fixed context size are poorly suited. We propose ScaleAware-JEPA, which forms dense latent coordinates without labels. The constrained diffusion decomposition (CDD) procedure breaks the field into scale components, defining the masking geometry; the JEPA architecture then predicts the hidden structure using a context window tied to the diffusion scale. Experiments on magnetohydrodynamic turbulence, interstellar molecular gas, and night-time city illumination structure show that the learned geometry reproduces coherent morphology and creates detailed structural atlases without segmentation rules. Scale-aware prediction enables exploring complex physical patterns before they are formally defined.

Context

Imagine trying to understand how an orchestra works by listening only to the sum of all instruments. It's similar in science: physical fields, whether turbulent plasma or the interstellar medium, contain nested structures at different scales, but extracting them isn't easy. Traditional methods either compress data into averaged spectra, losing spatial organization, or impose rigid definitions, like searching for filaments via gradients. The key difficulty, as Stuart Kauffman put it, is that for complex systems you can't specify the phase space in advance—the relevant variables are unknown. Hence, we need a way for the data to suggest how to organize itself.

Methods

The researchers created a tool to “hear the individual instruments”: the ScaleAware-JEPA method. First, the field is decomposed into scale components using CDD (Constrained Diffusion Decomposition). This resembles how an artist applies blur to emphasize large shapes. Then, a neural network with a JEPA architecture learns to predict hidden parts of the field, not in pixels, but in the so-called latent space—a compact representation where nearby points correspond to similar morphology. The key innovation is that the mask size (the prediction region) is tied to the component's scale: for fine details, a small context window is used; for large features, a larger one. This allows the network to capture relationships spread across different scales, much like Riemann taught about manifolds where the local intertwines with the global.

Results

The method was tested on three arenas. The first was a numerical simulation of compressible magnetohydrodynamic turbulence, where plasma obeys Maxwell's equations (which unified electricity and magnetism through the work of Maxwell) and the Navier-Stokes equations. Although the network saw only the density distribution, it unsupervisedly identified regions corresponding to magnetic and kinetic regimes—structures that had previously been detected only with the help of magnetic field data. In the second example—a map of Chengdu’s nighttime lights—the algorithm separated bright centers, roads, and diffuse outskirts without knowing what a city is. The third example—molecular gas in the galaxy NGC 3627, observed by the ALMA telescope. Here, the interstellar medium appeared as a latent atlas: compact clouds—potential cradles of star formation, fibrous dust structures, and diffuse nebulae. Interestingly, the method also captured recently discovered molecular “contrails”—long, narrow bridges that don't fit into the bright spiral arms.

Implications

The main lesson: meaningful representation can arise from data without human hints. This changes the approach to studying complex systems where there are no ready-made “phase diagrams.” Now we can build latent atlases and search for new patterns without fear of missing the unknown. The discovery that magnetic reconnection or turbulent cascades leave a recognizable imprint in density suggests that many physical processes are implicitly encoded in observed fields.

Future development

In the future, ScaleAware-JEPA can be extended to three-dimensional fields and incorporate time—then we'll be able to track the evolution of structures like in a movie. Combining with physics-informed neural networks, where Maxwell's equations or other laws are embedded into training, will boost interpretability. It's already clear that this approach will take root not only in astrophysics but also in Earth sciences, materials science, and even urban dynamics analysis.

Impact

The method will impact all fields where data are continuous fields: from astrophysics and plasma physics to climatology and urban studies.

Next steps

The next step is to include additional physical fields in training, such as magnetic field and velocity, and to move to time sequences.

Key open problems

This work is directly linked to one of the eternal problems in physics: how to select relevant variables to describe a complex system far from equilibrium. It also resonates with questions about the nature of turbulence and why matter organizes into hierarchical structures from the cosmic web down to molecular clouds.
