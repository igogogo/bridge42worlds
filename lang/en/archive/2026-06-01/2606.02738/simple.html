Why Do Early Galaxies Shine Brighter Than Expected? express

Original: "Signature of Bursty Star Formation in the High-Redshift Galaxies Detected with JWST"
· Rupam Sarkar, Saumyadip Samui
arXiv:2606.02738 · 2026-06-01 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min · Galaxies Cosmology
The James Webb Telescope revealed that in the young universe, stars were born in bursts — faster, but not more efficiently.
Abstract

The Webb telescope’s data have baffled astronomers: the young Universe is brimming with overly bright galaxies. New modeling shows the trick isn’t how much gas gets used up, but how fast. Ancient star systems didn’t burn steadily like a light bulb—they flared up in brilliant bursts, like fireworks. Could those cosmic sparks be what lit up the early cosmos?

Links in the knowledge graph 1

Images from James Webb captured the unexpectedly bright glow of the first galaxies. A toaster browns a slice in a minute, while an oven bakes a loaf in an hour—the result is the same. So it is in space: young galaxies put out powerful light quickly, like a toaster, rather than baking stars slowly, like an oven.

The answer lies in the bursty birth rhythm. In the early universe, gas compressed into stars not continuously but in short bursts. Yet the fraction of gas turned into stars remained almost the same as today. The toaster used as much dough as the oven.

Neither cosmic dust nor giant stars alone could explain such brilliance—only the accelerated pace. A surprising twist: supermassive black holes at the centers of galaxies also played a part. As matter fell into them, it heated up and added light, making the baby galaxies even brighter.

🎯 The most distant known star cluster ignited when the universe was only 300–400 million years old—an infant age by cosmic standards.
