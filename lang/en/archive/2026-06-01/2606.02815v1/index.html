Cosmic Vermicelli: A Carbon Chain in a Protoplanetary Disk

Original: "First Detection of HC5N in a Class II Disk around TW Hya"
Astronomers have for the first time found a long carbon molecule in a disk where planets are forming—it's like discovering a complex ingredient in a cosmic soup that's still being cooked.

Imagine that the star TW Hydrae is a cosmic kitchen where planets are cooked from gas and dust. Around it spins a protoplanetary disk—a giant cloud, like pizza dough. Scientists using the radio telescope ALMA found in this disk a molecule resembling a long spaghetti of five carbon atoms with hydrogen and nitrogen at the ends—HC5N. Previously, it was thought that such long chains couldn't survive near young stars due to ultraviolet radiation, but they do exist.

How did they spot such a faint signal? Astronomers used a clever computational technique—matched filtering—like searching for a familiar tune in noise. They knew what the emission of a similar but shorter molecule looked like and used that pattern as a template. And voilà—the HC5N signal was 5.3 times stronger than noise, meaning it's almost certainly real. It turned out that for such a chain to form, the disk must have more carbon than oxygen—like a sweet syrup where sugar outweighs water.

Why is this cool? Long carbon chains are building blocks for more complex organics. They can settle on dust and ice particles, later entering the atmospheres of forming exoplanets. Perhaps this is how the first molecules leading to life appeared on Earth. We are now observing chemistry that happened 4.5 billion years ago in our own solar system. And this is just the beginning—scientists are already searching for even longer chains.

🎯 The HC5N molecule consists of a chain of five carbon atoms—the same structure has been found in meteorites that fell to Earth. So we can witness how the building blocks of life are being created in space right now.
