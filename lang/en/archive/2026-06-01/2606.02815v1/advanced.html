First Detection of HC5N in the TW Hya Disk: Long Carbon Chains in the Cradle of Planets

Original: "First Detection of HC5N in a Class II Disk around TW Hya"
Astronomers have for the first time detected the HC5N molecule in a protoplanetary disk, showing that complex carbon chemistry is possible even in cold planet-forming regions.

Context

Protoplanetary disks contain molecular reservoirs from which planets, moons, and comets form. Understanding the chemical composition of disks is necessary to connect with exoplanet atmospheres. However, complex organic molecules are rarely found in old T Tauri star disks. High-resolution spectroscopy allows the detection of even weak signatures of such molecules. The star TW Hya, the nearest analog to the young Sun, provides a unique laboratory for studying carbon, hydrogen, and nitrogen chemistry in conditions similar to the early Solar System.

Methods

Observations were conducted using the radio telescope ALMA at 3 mm as part of Cycle 6. Data were calibrated and further self-calibrated. To extract the weak HC5N signal, matched filtering was used, with the emission of the well-known HC3N molecule as a template. The shift-and-stack method (GoFish) was also applied to account for Keplerian rotation of the disk. Chemical modeling was performed with a two-dimensional time-dependent model incorporating gas-phase reactions, photochemistry, and adsorption onto dust and ice.

Results

Two HC5N transitions were detected at significance levels of 3.4σ and 4.2σ, with a combined stack yielding 5.3σ. Measured integrated fluxes were 3.7±3.5 and 8.3±6.3 mJy·km/s. Population analysis gave a column density NT ~ 10^12 cm^-2 at rotational temperatures of 20–50 K. This is 1–2 orders of magnitude lower than for HC3N, consistent with a rapid decrease in abundance with carbon chain length. Models with an enhanced C/O > 1 reproduce the observed values.

Implications

The detection of HC5N expands the known inventory of carbon chains in Class II disks. This indicates that long cyanopolyynes can form and persist in planet-forming environments despite the star's harsh ultraviolet radiation. Exoplanets forming in such disks may inherit complex organics, important for understanding prebiotic chemistry.

Future development

Future observations with higher angular resolution, such as with ALMA at longer wavelengths or using interferometry, will allow spatially resolving the HC5N emission and determining its distribution in the disk. Improved chemical models with more detailed disk physics will help clarify the formation and destruction mechanisms of long carbon chains.

Impact

The results will impact astrochemistry, planetary science, and the study of exoplanet atmospheres, providing a more complete picture of chemical evolution from disk to planets.

Next steps

A search for even longer cyanopolyynes, such as HC7N and HC9N, in the TW Hya disk and other nearby disks is necessary. It is also important to measure the isotopic ratios 13C/12C and 15N/14N in HC5N to constrain formation conditions.

Key open problems

This discovery links the scarcity of complex organic molecules in old disks to the problem of the origin of life: how and where are the building blocks for biomolecules formed?

🎯 The HC5N molecule contains a chain of five carbon atoms with a nitrogen at the end—a structure also found in some prebiotic molecules discovered in meteorites.
CN + C_4H_2 \rightarrow HC_5N + H
The CN radical reacts with diacetylene to produce HC5N and a hydrogen atom

Key numbers

  • Column density of HC5N: ~10^12 cm^-2
  • HC3N/HC5N ratio: 0.9–63 (at 20–50 K)
  • Distance to TW Hya: 60 pc
  • Combined significance level: 5.3σ
  • Integrated flux of HC5N J=41-40: 8.3±6.3 mJy·km/s
