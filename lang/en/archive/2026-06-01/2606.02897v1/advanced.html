Cosmic Birefringence: Hybrid ILC Breaks the Degeneracy

Original: "Field-level constraints on cosmic birefringence with a hybrid $$E$$-$$B$$ internal linear combination"
A new hybrid ILC method measures cosmic birefringence by separating it from instrumental effects, yielding a 2.7σ hint of its existence in Planck data.

Context

Measuring the cosmic microwave background (CMB) is key to understanding the early universe. Of particular interest is CMB polarization, which may carry imprints of physics beyond the Standard Model. General relativity predicts certain polarization properties, but if axion-like particles (candidates for dark matter) exist, they can violate parity and rotate the polarization plane — the effect of cosmic birefringence. However, this signal is degenerate with rotation due to inaccurate instrumental polarization angle calibration. Separating these effects is a key challenge, and a new method is proposed to address it.

Methods

The author developed a blind map-based method — hybrid ILC (internal linear combination). Standard ILC constructs a cleaned CMB map as a weighted sum of observations at different frequencies, requiring the sum of weights to equal one. This preserves achromatic signals like cosmic birefringence. Instrumental rotation, however, is chromatic (frequency-dependent), so after ILC it is suppressed and becomes multipole-dependent. A hybrid E–B ILC is developed that combines E- and B-mode maps at different frequencies to separate correlated and uncorrelated components. This allows constructing an estimator for the angle β via spatial linear regression. The method is tested on LiteBIRD computer simulations and applied to Planck PR4 data.

Results

In LiteBIRD simulations with known β = 0.3°, the method gave β̂ = 0.31° ± 0.02° (>15σ), and with β = 0 gave β̂ = 0.01° ± 0.02°, confirming no bias. In Planck PR4 data, the angle is β̂ = 0.32° ± 0.12° (2.7σ). The diagnostic ratio Rℓ (EB cross-spectrum normalized by the EE–BB difference) is flat across multipoles, characteristic of an achromatic effect. The result is robust to changes in the sky fraction analyzed, supporting a cosmological origin. This is an important step in understanding inflation and the expansion of the universe, as well as a possible connection to the Big Bang.

Implications

The result points to the existence of a parity-violating pseudoscalar field, which could be related to axion dark matter or other beyond-Standard Model scenarios. The method does not require explicit modeling of Galactic EB correlations, making it more robust. In the future, it can be applied to data from upcoming missions like LiteBIRD to achieve higher precision.

Future development

The method can be extended to account for anisotropic birefringence, which could provide information on spatial fluctuations of the axion field. It can also be combined with cross-correlation methods with large-scale structure. The development of CMB polarimetry with new instruments (Simons Observatory, CMB-S4) will allow testing the signal at high significance.

Impact

Impact on cosmology, particle physics, and astrophysics: the discovery of cosmic birefringence would confirm physics beyond the Standard Model and provide a new tool for studying dark matter.

Next steps

Necessary steps: apply the method to Simons Observatory and CMB-S4 data, and investigate possible systematic effects related to foreground polarization. Cross-validation with other methods is important.

Key open problems

The method brings us closer to solving the problems of the nature of dark matter and CP violation in the early universe.
