Cosmic Twist: How the Universe Twists Light

Original: "Field-level constraints on cosmic birefringence with a hybrid $$E$$-$$B$$ internal linear combination"
A new method has measured a hint of a strange effect — cosmic birefringence — which could open a door to dark matter.

Imagine you are looking at the night sky through a perfectly clear glass, but suddenly you notice that the stars are slightly shifted. You don't know whether the glass is to blame or space itself is curved. A similar problem faced cosmologists studying the cosmic microwave background — the echo of the Big Bang. This radiation is polarized, and its polarization can be slightly rotated by hypothetical particles that violate parity. But the same rotations can be caused by imperfect telescope calibration. How to separate these two effects?

The author of the work devised an elegant, almost detective-like technique. He used the fact that true cosmic birefringence does not depend on the observation frequency, while instrumental rotation does. It's like looking at an object through different glasses: if the image shifts the same in all glasses, it's the object; if differently, it's the glasses. The hybrid ILC (internal linear combination) method combines data at different frequencies to extract the achromatic component. Tests on simulations of the future LiteBIRD mission showed accuracy to hundredths of a degree.

Applying the method to data from the Planck satellite, scientists obtained a hint of a rotation of 0.32° ± 0.12° (2.7σ). This is not yet a discovery, but an intriguing hint. If the effect is real, it could be caused by axion-like fields — candidates for dark matter. Moreover, this is directly linked to inflation — the era of rapid expansion of the Universe when these fields could have been born.

Practical benefit: the method provides a new tool for searching for new physics. In the future, with data from the Simons Observatory and CMB-S4, we will be able to either confirm or refute cosmic birefringence with high precision. And if it is confirmed, it would become a window into the world beyond the Standard Model, possibly explaining dark matter. Philosophically, this reminds us that even in the most precise measurements we must separate reality from the instrument, and sometimes the answer lies in subtle patterns, like a rotation of the polarization plane by a third of a degree.

🎯 If cosmic birefringence really exists, the rotation of the CMB polarization is only about a third of a degree — that's the angle at which a coin is seen from arm's length.
C_\ell^{EB,\text{rot}} = 2\beta (C_\ell^{EE} - C_\ell^{BB})
This formula relates the measured cross-spectrum (correlation of E and B polarization modes) to the cosmic birefringence angle β and the difference of the E and B mode auto-spectra. The larger β, the stronger the signal.
