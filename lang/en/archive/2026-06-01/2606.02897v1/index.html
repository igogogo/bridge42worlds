Is the Universe Slightly Twisting Light? A Discovery on the Edge

Original: "Field-level constraints on cosmic birefringence with a hybrid $$E$$-$$B$$ internal linear combination"
Scientists found a way to distinguish real polarization rotation of light from telescope errors and spotted a faint hint of new physics.

Imagine looking at the world through defective glasses: the image is slightly shifted. How do you know whether it's the glasses or the world itself? Scientists faced the same challenge with the cosmic microwave background—the afterglow of the Big Bang. This light is polarized (it oscillates in a specific plane), and according to theory, this plane might rotate slightly due to hypothetical particles. But imperfect instruments can do the same thing. They needed to separate these effects.

The author devised a clever approach: true cosmic rotation does not depend on the color (frequency) of light, while instrumental rotation does. Tested on computer simulations of the upcoming LiteBIRD mission, the method showed accuracy down to hundredths of a degree.

Applying it to data from the Planck satellite, researchers saw a hint of a rotation of just 0.32°—that's like the angle at which you see a coin from an outstretched arm. For now, it's only a hint (2.7σ statistical significance), but if real, it could be caused by dark matter—axion-like fields born during inflation (the rapid expansion of the universe). If the effect is confirmed, we'll open a window to new physics beyond the Standard Model.

🎯 If cosmic birefringence exists, the measured rotation is just one-third of a degree: roughly the angle formed by your fingertips when looking at them from arm's length.
