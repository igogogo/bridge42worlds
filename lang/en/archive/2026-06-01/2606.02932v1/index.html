The Culinary Secret of Neutron Stars

Original: "On a re-examination of neutron star cooling in transient sources -- No shallow heating required?"
Neutron stars cool slower thanks to thermonuclear cooking in their crust.

Imagine you've taken a pot of soup off the stove. Usually it cools down, but if embers are still smoldering underneath, the soup stays hot. Astrophysicists have solved just such a 'culinary' puzzle while studying neutron stars. These stars are ultra-dense objects packed with neutrons, protons, and electrons.

When such a star pulls in material from a companion (hydrogen and helium), its crust heats up. After the inflow stops, the star should cool quickly, but it stays hot longer. New computer simulations accounting for gravity and general relativity (the theory of Einstein) showed that at the boundary between the crust and the outer layer, nuclear fusion occurs—hydrogen and helium merge to form heavier elements like iron and release heat. Those are the 'smoldering embers.'

The model builds on ideas from Chandrasekhar about dense matter. Now astronomers better understand the thermal evolution of neutron stars, and thus the physics of extreme objects. Future models will become even more precise, and we will finally crack the recipe for these cosmic 'pots.'

🎯 The model is based on a 'watershed' idea proposed back in the 1980s, but it had not been applied to star cooling before.
