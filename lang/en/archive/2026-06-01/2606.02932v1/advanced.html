Neutron Star Cooling: A New Explanation Without 'Surface Heating'

Original: "On a re-examination of neutron star cooling in transient sources -- No shallow heating required?"
A new boundary condition accounting for thermonuclear burning of hydrogen and helium explains neutron star cooling after accretion outbursts without invoking an unknown heat source.

Context

When a neutron star in a binary system accretes matter, its crust heats up. After the outburst ends, the star cools, but observed high temperatures required an additional heat source of unknown origin—'surface heating.' Einstein's general relativity is crucial for modeling these dense objects.

Methods

Using the relativistic code NSCool, the team simulated the cooling of eight outbursts from seven sources. They implemented a new boundary condition that depends on the accretion rate and thermonuclear burning of hydrogen and helium in the envelope, allowing heat to flow into the crust (the 'watershed' model). Computer simulations with Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) fitting were used.

Results

For seven out of eight outbursts, the model matched observations without surface heating. The inferred accretion rates matched the observed ones. Nuclear fusion in the envelope provides the necessary heat. Only EXO 0748-676 required additional physics. Gravity and relativistic effects were accounted for.

Implications

This suggests that surface heating might be an artifact of simplified boundary conditions. Electrons, protons, and neutrons in the crust and envelope behave differently under accretion. The results are consistent with Chandrasekhar's work on degenerate matter.

Future development

Future models will include time-dependent accretion rates and different neutron star masses. The role of iron and other elements in the crust will be refined.

Impact

Impacts modeling of thermal evolution of neutron stars, observations of X-ray binaries, and nuclear physics of dense matter.

Next steps

Include variable accretion rates and explore more massive neutron stars to further test the model.

Key open problems

Links the problem of neutron star cooling and the nature of heat sources in their crust.

🎯 The new boundary condition was inspired by the 'watershed model' proposed in the 1980s but previously not applied to cooling curves.
