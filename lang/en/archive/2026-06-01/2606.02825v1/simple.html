Conductor of the Cosmic Orchestra: How to Synchronize Telescopes to Femtosecond Precision

Original: "A High-Bandwidth Backplane for Wideband Radio Interferometers and Integration with the CHORD Telescope Correlators"
A new combiner panel for radio interferometers acts like a virtuoso conductor, synchronizing hundreds of telescopes with sub-picosecond precision.

Imagine an orchestra where every musician plays at their own tempo—instead of harmony, you get chaos. That’s roughly how radio interferometers combining signals from hundreds of telescopes would work without precise synchronization. Each telescope catches the faint whisper of distant galaxies, but to combine these signals into a clear image, you need timing coherence at the picosecond level. The new combiner panel is like a conductor who, with a wave of the baton, sets a unified rhythm for all instruments.

At the heart of the system is a passive combiner board with 25 Gbps transmission lines and blind-mate connectors. Time distribution is done via LVDS with a 10 MHz reference signal. For precise delay estimation, the GCC-PHAT method is used—it's as if the conductor not only watches the bow movements but also predicts their position from the phase of the sound. The result is impressive: jitter is only 200–500 femtoseconds, and delays are stable within tens of picoseconds. No transmission errors occur even under loads exceeding 10 terabits per channel.

The practical benefits of such technologies are enormous. They allow us to study cosmic microwave background and the structure of the expanding Universe, and to search for traces of dark matter through the 21-centimeter hydrogen line. In the future, this architecture will form the basis of giant arrays like SKA, where thousands of telescopes must work as a single organism. Meanwhile, engineers are preparing for integration with the CHORD project—the first instrument capable of observing the birth of stars and supernova explosions in minute detail.

By the way, did you know that the data transfer speed through this panel is equivalent to downloading a thousand ultra-high-resolution movies every second? Such volumes of information are not a luxury but a necessity for modern astrophysics. And although we are just beginning to understand how this 'nervous system' of telescopes works, it is already clear: the future of science lies in synchronization at the edge of physical possibilities.
