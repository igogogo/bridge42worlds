Nereid: The Last Fragment of Neptune's Lost Family

Original: "Nereid as a Regular Satellite of Neptune"
New JWST data and simulations reveal that Nereid is not a captured interloper but a surviving regular moon of Neptune, enduring after Triton's catastrophic invasion.

Imagine a harbor where ships drift calmly—that's the system of regular moons around Neptune. But one day, a massive pirate ship storms in—the gravitational capture of Triton. It wreaks havoc, throwing some vessels onto the rocks and others beyond the horizon. Nereid is the lone ship that neither crashed nor sailed away forever, but ended up on a wild, elongated orbit, as if catapulted.

For a long time, astronomers considered Nereid a captured Kuiper Belt object—its orbit is a roller coaster: eccentricity 0.75, inclination 28°. But new spectroscopy from JWST revealed its surface is covered in pristine water ice with deep absorption bands—unlike any captured icy body. Nereid's ice is too virgin, too 'planetary'.

Computer simulations confirmed: in 20% of capture scenarios, Triton ejects one of the regular moons into an orbit resembling Nereid's. The other moons die, colliding with Triton or falling onto the planet. Thus, Nereid is the last survivor of the original siblinghood—a shard of a lost system. Studying it is key to understanding how icy giant moons formed and how catastrophic events reshape entire worlds.

Practical takeaway? We can finally reconstruct Neptune's history and its retinue. Moreover, if we send a mission to Nereid, we'll find pristine materials left over from the ice giant's formation. It's like finding a diary that miraculously survived a library explosion. Nereid teaches us: even in destruction there are survivors, and they hold the most precious secrets.

🎯 Nereid was discovered in 1949 by Gerard Kuiper—the same who predicted the Kuiper Belt. He initially mistook it for an asteroid.
