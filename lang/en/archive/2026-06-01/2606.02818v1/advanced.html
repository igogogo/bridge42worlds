Nereid: The Last Surviving Regular Moon of Neptune

Original: "Nereid as a Regular Satellite of Neptune"
New JWST data and dynamical modeling show that Nereid is not a captured Kuiper Belt object but the sole surviving regular moon of Neptune.

Context

Neptune is the only giant planet lacking an intact system of regular moons. Its largest moon, Triton, has a retrograde orbit, indicating capture. The seven inner moons are likely collision fragments. Nereid, long classified as an irregular captured moon due to its highly eccentric orbit, is an anomaly given its size and proximity to the planet. New data challenge its captured origin.

Methods

The team used near-infrared spectroscopy with the NIRSpec instrument on JWST in IFU Prism mode (0.6–5.3 µm). They compared Nereid's spectrum with data from Kuiper Belt objects and irregular moons. They also performed N-body simulations of the evolution of Neptune's satellite system after Triton's capture using the REBOUND integrator with adaptive timestepping. The simulations modeled Triton's collisional circularization through a disk of protomoons.

Results

Nereid's spectrum shows deep water ice bands, a negative near-infrared slope, and a flat continuum at 3 µm. Comparison with three spectral classes of Kuiper Belt objects shows mismatch: Nereid has a bluer slope and deeper ice bands than any known KBO. Nereid's icy surface is unique. Dynamical simulations show that in about 20% of cases, Triton passing through the regular moon system can eject one moon onto an orbit similar to Nereid's (semimajor axis within 10% of current, eccentricity ~0.705, inclination ~40°). In 60% of simulations Triton disrupted, and in 20% it removed all moons except the inner ones. Gravitational interactions in the simulations were reproduced via computer modeling.

Implications

If Nereid is indeed a regular moon, it means it is the sole surviving intact moon from Neptune's original system. This changes our understanding of the evolution of gas giant satellite systems and the role of Triton's capture. Additionally, it may explain why Neptune lacks a full system of regular moons like other giant planets.

Future development

Further studies could include measuring isotopic ratios (D/H, carbon) in Nereid's spectrum and searching for signs of ancient geological activity. Future missions to the Neptune system could test the hypothesis of Triton's collisional origin and evidence of the destruction of the original moons.

Impact

The results will impact planetary science and the study of the Solar System, especially models of moon formation and evolution around ice giants.

Next steps

The mass of Nereid and the exact configuration of Neptune's original moon system, as well as the timing of Triton's capture, need to be refined. This will allow more accurate modeling of the process.

Key open problems

The work is connected to the problem of the origin of irregular moons and the evolution of satellite systems around giant planets.
