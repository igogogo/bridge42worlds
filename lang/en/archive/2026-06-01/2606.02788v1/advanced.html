Neutrino Fingerprints: Images for Direction Reconstruction in IceCube

Original: "Neutrino Fingerprints: Image-Based Encodings of IceCube Events for CNN Direction Reconstruction"
Encoding IceCube detector data into compact RGB images allows a convolutional neural network to achieve neutrino direction reconstruction accuracy close to complex ensemble models.

Context

The IceCube neutrino telescope detects Cherenkov radiation emitted by charged particles produced when neutrinos interact with the ice. Photons are captured by a network of 5160 photomultipliers. Neutrinos are fermions participating only in weak interaction. Reconstructing neutrino direction is key for identifying their astrophysical sources.

Methods

The authors developed a method called neutrino fingerprints — compact RGB images of size 72×72×3, where each pixel corresponds to one detector. Color channels encode normalized arrival time, total charge, and their difference. This representation transforms sparse pulse sequences into dense images suitable for convolutional networks. Training was performed on 140 million simulated events from the Kaggle competition using a ResNet18 network and various loss functions, including angular loss and combined angular loss with von Mises–Fisher distribution.

Results

The best model achieved a mean angular error (MAE) of 1.10 radians on the test set. For comparison, top Kaggle solutions using graph neural networks and transformers with ensembling achieved around 0.95 radians. Thus, the proposed simple convolutional approach lags behind the state-of-the-art by only 0.15 radians, demonstrating that proper data representation allows the Standard Model of particle physics to be competitive. Interestingly, the model was trained without complex techniques — learning rate schedulers, restarts, or augmentations.

Implications

The work shows that abandoning complex architectures in favor of simple convolutional networks with thoughtful data encoding can be an effective strategy. The weak interaction of the Standard Model, through which neutrinos interact, makes their detection difficult, but this study simplifies the reconstruction step. The proposed 'neutrino fingerprints' are intuitive: each pixel corresponds to a specific detector, facilitating error diagnosis. This could be important for interpreting AI decisions in high-energy physics.

Future development

Further improvement is possible by using space-filling curves to better preserve locality, adding extra color channels (e.g., energy), and applying more modern architectures such as Vision Transformer or depthwise-separable convolutions. Electroweak interaction may be considered in modeling finer effects, although not addressed in this work. Also promising is the use of von Mises–Fisher distribution for calibrated directional uncertainties.

Impact

The results can be applied in astrophysics for automatic processing of neutrino telescope data, and in machine learning for parameter reconstruction from sparse signals.

Next steps

Integration of the proposed encoding method into the real IceCube data processing pipeline, and testing on data from other neutrino observatories such as KM3NeT. Use of space-filling curves and augmentations may further improve accuracy.

Key open problems

This work is related to the unresolved problem of accurate reconstruction of high-energy neutrino direction, which is critical for multi-messenger astronomy.
