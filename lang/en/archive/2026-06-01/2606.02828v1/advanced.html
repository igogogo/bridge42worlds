Neutron Star Mass in 4U 1820–30: A Fresh Perspective

Original: "The mass of the neutron star in 4U 1820-30 revisited"
Analysis of gravitational redshift, pulsations, and bursts reveals contradictions in neutron star mass estimates.

Context

Neutron stars are among the densest objects in the Universe, and precisely determining their mass and radius is critical for understanding the equation of state of superdense matter. The system 4U 1820–30 is a unique laboratory: it contains a neutron star and a lightweight white dwarf with an orbital period of just 11 minutes, allowing several independent methods to estimate the properties of the accreting object.

Methods

Researchers used data from the NICER X-ray telescope, spectroscopy, and timing analysis. They plotted the mass–radius (M–R) plane from three approaches: 1) gravitational redshift from the Fe line (1+z=1.72), 2) the touch method based on thermonuclear bursts, and 3) interpretation of the upper quasi-periodic oscillation (QPO) at 1065 Hz as a signal from the innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO). For rotation (716 Hz), Kerr and Hartle–Thorne metric approximations were used.

Results

Assuming a low maximum neutron star mass (2.3 M_⊙), the overlap between the redshift region and the 95% reference contour from the equation of state is almost nonexistent. Allowing a higher limit (up to 2.6 M_⊙) yields a region of agreement at radii ~12 km, but this increases the discrepancy with the touch method measurements (mass 1.77+0.25-0.28 M_⊙). Thus, the three methods point to different parts of the M–R plane, and their joint interpretation requires accounting for systematic errors.

Implications

The work demonstrates that even using the same data, different methods give mutually contradictory mass estimates. This questions the possibility of a simple combination of results without detailed modeling of systematics. Confirming the line as Fe and its formation on the star's surface would impose strong constraints on the equation of state but requires additional observations.

Future development

Further development involves confirming the absorption line in other bursts, refining atmospheric models, and accounting for rotation and system inclination. New NICER data and future missions (e.g., STROBE-X) are expected to allow a joint inference of mass and radius with controlled systematics.

Impact

The results will affect neutron star physics, the equation of state of superdense matter, and the interpretation of accretion processes in binary systems.

Next steps

A joint Bayesian analysis incorporating all three methods is needed, including a prior distribution for the maximum neutron star mass. Additionally, analogous lines should be searched for in other LMXBs.

Key open problems

The work directly relates to the unresolved problem of the maximum neutron star mass and the shape of the equation of state at supra-nuclear densities.
