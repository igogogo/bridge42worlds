Three Ways to Weigh a Neutron Star, and They All Differ

Original: "The mass of the neutron star in 4U 1820-30 revisited"
Astronomers are trying to determine the mass of a neutron star paired with a white dwarf, but different methods yield different answers.

Neutron stars are the densest objects after black holes. In the system 4U 1820–30, such a star orbits a lightweight white dwarf with a period of just 11 minutes—like two peas in a cosmic whirlwind. Scientists want to know its mass and radius to understand the nature of superdense matter.

They applied three independent methods—like weighing a watermelon on different scales. The first uses spectroscopy of iron lines (atoms absorb light). The second relies on thermonuclear bursts (small explosions from nuclear fusion of hydrogen and helium into carbon and oxygen). The third analyzes X-ray pulsations. Using computer simulations, each method yielded a different mass: from 1.77 to nearly 2.6 solar masses. It's like a scale showing 2, 3, and 5 kilograms for the same watermelon!

Why the discrepancy? Perhaps each method has its own biases, or maybe we're misjudging the star's spin (716 rotations per second!). General relativity Einstein's theory helps link mass and size, but the disagreements remain large. Chandrasekhar would have been intrigued too. Solving the puzzle requires even more precise observations—for instance, with the future STROBE-X telescope.

🎯 The neutron star in 4U 1820–30 orbits the white dwarf in just 11 minutes—a record among known binary systems.
