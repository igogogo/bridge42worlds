ExoVeil: How to Predict a Star and Find an Invisible Planet

Original: "One Transit Is All You Need: Detecting Exoplanets Through Learned Stellar Behaviour with EXOVEIL"
The ExoVeil system learns to predict a star's brightness and detects single transits that were previously invisible.

Imagine watching a calm lake and knowing every ripple. Suddenly, a single wave appears—and you're sure it's not the wind, but a fish swimming by. That's roughly how the ExoVeil algorithm works: it predicts what a star should look like without planets, then hunts for tiny deviations—single transits.

Traditional methods require a planet to pass in front of its star multiple times to stack signals. But what if the orbit is longer than a year? Then the transit method sees only one dip in brightness over years of observation. ExoVeil tackles this differently: it trains on thousands of light curves from the Kepler telescope, masking known transits to learn to predict the 'norm.' Then it compares prediction to reality and uses a matched filter to find residual signals. An XGBoost classifier weeds out false positives.

Results are impressive: in a blind search, ExoVeil found 179 new anomalies missed by the DR25 catalog. On artificial single transits with a depth of 1000 ppm, the system recovered 32%—while classical pipelines yield 0%. And on TESS data without fine-tuning, it found all 47 confirmed planets in the PLATO LOPS2 field. With the future PLATO mission's cadence (25 seconds), sensitivity reaches 100 ppm—that's already super-Earth and Earth analog territory.

ExoVeil's philosophy—'predict, not classify'—changes the game for world hunting. Now we can catch planets with a single transit that were once ghosts in the data. Next up: swapping the transformer for a linear Mamba architecture and integrating spectroscopy. Maybe this is how we'll find the first Earth-like planet around a Sun-like star—by a single, solitary shadow.

🎯 ExoVeil is the first to apply conformal prediction to transit vetting, giving a formal coverage guarantee of 95.9%—like insurance against false signals.
\text{SNR} = \frac{d}{\sigma}
Signal-to-noise ratio, where d is transit depth (dip in brightness) and σ is noise level. For reliable detection, SNR > 3 is typically required.
