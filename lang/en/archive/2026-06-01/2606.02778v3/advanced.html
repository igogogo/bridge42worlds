ExoVeil: Detecting Exoplanets by Single Transit via Self-Supervision

Original: "One Transit Is All You Need: Detecting Exoplanets Through Learned Stellar Behaviour with EXOVEIL"
The ExoVeil system learns to predict stellar brightness and finds single transits that classifiers miss.

Context

Modern machine learning methods for transit detection, such as ExoMiner and AstroNet, require a known orbital period and phase folding. This makes them useless for single-transit planets, which are a priority for missions like PLATO. Exoplanets with long periods are the main target, but a structural flaw in pipelines prevents their detection.

Methods

The author trained a transformer world model on 16,499 Kepler light curves with self-supervised learning, masking known transits. The model predicts the expected flux, and a matched filter with variance weighting extracts signals from residuals. Then an XGBoost classifier on 21 features separates planets from false positives. Conformal prediction is used for uncertainty estimation.

Results

A blind search of 3,737 Kepler stars revealed 179 transit-like anomalies not in the DR25 catalog. When injecting single transits, the system recovers 32% at 1000 ppm depth, while classifiers give 0% by design. With zero-shot transfer to the TESS mission, all 47 confirmed planets in the PLATO LOPS2 field are recovered (100% recovery). At PLATO's 25-second cadence, sensitivity reaches 100 ppm, approaching Earth analogs.

Implications

The results show that the prediction paradigm, rather than classification, can fill the gap in detecting exoplanets with few transits. Stellar brightness is modeled via learning, paving the way for detecting Earth-like planets with next-generation telescopes.

Future development

Plans include replacing the transformer with a linear architecture (Mamba) to process full light curves without windows, adding automatic rejection of four classes of false positives (post-flare bump, rotation tracking error, etc.), and extending to the multi-objective PLATO mission with 24 cameras.

Impact

It will impact data processing pipelines for PLATO and other surveys, as well as candidate selection strategies for ground-based confirmation.

Next steps

Conduct large-scale injections with PlatoSim, include centroid analysis and spectroscopy for candidate verification.

Key open problems

This work directly addresses the problem of detecting Earth-like exoplanets around Sun-like stars, which is central to the PLATO mission and is being tackled using machine learning methods.

🎯 The ExoVeil system applies conformal prediction to transit vetting for the first time, providing a formal coverage guarantee of 95.9%.
