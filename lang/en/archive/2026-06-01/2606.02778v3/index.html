ExoVeil: How to Find a Planet by a Single Shadow

Original: "One Transit Is All You Need: Detecting Exoplanets Through Learned Stellar Behaviour with EXOVEIL"
A new algorithm detects single transits of planets that were previously invisible.

Imagine you're looking at a calm lake and you know every ripple. Suddenly a single wave appears — and you're certain it's not the wind but a fish. That's roughly how the ExoVeil algorithm works: it learns to predict how a star should look without planets, then looks for the slightest deviations — single transits.

Standard methods require a planet to cross the star several times, but if its year is longer, we see only one dimming. ExoVeil solves the problem differently: it learns from Kepler telescope data, masks known transits to predict the "norm." Then, using a matched filter, it finds residual signals and filters out false ones. As a result, ExoVeil discovered 179 new anomalies that older methods missed. On synthetic single transits, it found 32% — while conventional algorithms found 0%.

On TESS data (another telescope), the system found all 47 confirmed planets. And in the future, with the PLATO mission, it could detect even super-Earths and Earth analogs. Scientists plan to improve the algorithm, but for now ExoVeil is already helping to find worlds with a single dimming — for example, via spectroscopy (studying light) we could confirm such finds. Perhaps this is how we will find the first Earth-like planet around another star.

🎯 ExoVeil uses conformal prediction — it's like an insurance against errors: the system guarantees that 95.9% of detected signals are correct.
