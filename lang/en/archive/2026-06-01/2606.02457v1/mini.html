Original: "Size limits on tidal debris around white dwarfs: the km-size barrier"
Tiny cohesive forces inside an asteroid become a kilometer barrier: a dead star cannot grind it to dust — only city-sized boulders remain.

Tiny van der Waals forces, binding sand grains together, clash with the titanic gravity of a dying star — and win. The asteroid tears apart, but the fragments remain skyscraper-sized: this is the kilometer barrier. Like a coral reef, it breaks into massive blocks rather than crumbling into roe. Thus dust disks are born around white dwarfs, heralding the finale of our planetary system.

🎯 As they dive toward the Sun, Kreutz sungrazers after tidal disruption leave a swarm of kilometer-sized boulders — exactly as the model predicts.

\frac{GM\rho s^4 r}{(r^2 - s^2/4)^2} = \sigma s^2 + G\rho^2 s^4
The left-hand side — the tidal force tearing apart a fragment of size s at distance r from a white dwarf of mass M; the right-hand side — the binding sum of cohesion (strength σ) and self-gravity. From this balance arises the kilometer-scale minimum size of a stable fragment.
