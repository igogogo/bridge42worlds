AI Seismologist Protects Gravitational Wave Search

Original: "Data-Driven Forecasting of three-Component Seismograms Using Transformer Architectures"
Neural network SeismoGPT predicts earthquakes to help gravitational wave detectors work without interference.

Imagine trying to hear a whisper in a noisy hall—that's how scientists search for gravitational waves, predicted by Albert Einstein. The main obstacle is Earth's seismic noise. To protect future observatories like LIGO or the Einstein Telescope, scientists created SeismoGPT—an AI that predicts earthquakes.

SeismoGPT works like an experienced sailor who predicts a storm's development from its first signs. Only instead of wind, it's computer simulations of seismic waves. The neural network based on transformers (yes, the same ones as in ChatGPT) learns to anticipate the next tremors. Its experience: 3.9 million synthetic records, equivalent to 50 years of continuous shaking.

The results are impressive: the similarity measure (NCC) exceeds 0.93—almost a perfect match. The model predicts strong earthquakes best. Why does this matter? Gravitational observatories are extremely sensitive. Active suppression of seismic noise is key to registering faint signals from merging black holes and neutron stars. Kip Thorne believes this will help peer into spacetime curvature with unprecedented precision.

🎯 Training SeismoGPT required 3.9 million synthetic seismograms—equivalent to recording earthquakes for 50 consecutive years.
