SeismoGPT: Predicting Earthquakes with AI to Protect Gravitational Wave Observatories

Original: "Data-Driven Forecasting of three-Component Seismograms Using Transformer Architectures"
The neural network SeismoGPT, based on transformers, learned to predict seismic waves, which could help future gravitational wave detectors.

Context

Accurate prediction of seismic waves is critical for early earthquake warning and protecting sensitive equipment. This problem is especially acute for future gravitational wave observatories like the Einstein Telescope, predicted by Albert Einstein. Seismic noise (Newtonian noise) dominates the noise budget. Current numerical methods are too slow for real-time. Computer simulation requires huge computational resources. So researchers turned to machine learning.

Methods

The authors created SeismoGPT—a transformer-based algorithm that predicts seismograms as a continuation task. The seismogram is divided into tokens (patches of 16 samples). The model uses causal self-attention to predict the next token. Training was done on synthetic data generated with Instaseis and AxiSEM. A combined loss function including time and spectral components was used.

Results

The model achieved median normalized cross-correlation (NCC) above 0.93 in all configurations. Doubling the forecast horizon from 120 to 240 seconds dropped NCC by 2%, but doubling the context restored quality. Best results were for strong earthquakes at short distances; at large distances and small magnitudes (Mw<4) quality degraded. Importantly, the model generates physically plausible waves, but sometimes with a phase shift.

Implications

SeismoGPT proves that transformers can learn stable continuation of seismic wave fields without explicitly solving equations. This opens the way to next-generation gravitational wave observatories with active Newtonian noise suppression. The method could also be applied in early warning systems.

Future development

Further development of SeismoGPT includes: (1) transition to real data accounting for noise and 3D Earth heterogeneity; (2) scaling up the model and data volume; (3) using more sophisticated tokenization, e.g., latent representations. Kip Thorne emphasizes the importance of such methods for LIGO and the Einstein Telescope.

Impact

The results will impact seismology, early warning systems, design of gravitational wave observatories, and machine learning for physical simulations.

Next steps

The next step is validation on real seismometer recordings and extension to multi-station arrays. Also planned is investigating the connection between predictions and Earth's internal structure.

Key open problems

The work addresses the problem of accurate real-time prediction of seismic waves to protect infrastructure.

🎯 SeismoGPT was trained on 3.9 million synthetic seismograms, equivalent to about 50 years of continuous recording.
