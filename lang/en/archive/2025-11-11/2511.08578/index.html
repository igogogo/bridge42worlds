Original: "Early Formation of Supermassive Black Holes via Dark Star Gravitational Instability"
Full version coming soon
Abstract

Dark stars are hypothetical objects of the early universe, powered by dark matter annihilation. Unlike ordinary stars, they can grow by accretion up to 10^4–10^7 solar masses, avoiding disruption from nuclear processes. When the mass becomes critical, relativistic instability kicks in, and the star collapses into a black hole. This scenario turned out to be remarkably robust to details—it doesn't depend on the initial mass, birth time, or accretion history. The resulting black holes serve as seeds for supermassive giants (over 10^9 M⊙) observed in the early universe.
