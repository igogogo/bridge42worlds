Abstract

For the first time, a method of conversion electron spectroscopy was demonstrated for the thorium-229 nucleus: a laser excites the isomer, and the energy is transferred to an electron, which is ejected from the material (internal conversion). This works even in samples opaque to nuclear light, such as a thin ThO₂ film, where the small band gap facilitates the process. The short isomer lifetime (~10 μs) and the ability to use spinless isotopes allow the interrogation time of nuclear clocks to be reduced by a factor of 10⁸ and their instability to be lowered by 10⁴ — as if an ultra-precise stopwatch stopped getting thrown off by trembling parts.
