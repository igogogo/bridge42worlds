Little red galaxies discovered by the James Webb telescope turned out to be nothing like bright dusty quasars.
Abstract

«Little Red Dots» (LRDs) are compact dusty galaxies with a V-shaped spectrum, discovered by JWST. They resemble «blue-excess dusty quasars» (BHDs)—rare objects accreting near the limit onto a black hole. But detailed spectral analysis showed: LRDs contain less dust, their infrared emission is bluer, indicating no hot dust from accretion, and the blue excess is not scattered light from the nucleus. Unlike BHDs, which are born in galaxy collisions, LRDs are compact and rarely detected in X-rays, suggesting a different evolutionary path.

The James Webb telescope discovered a multitude of small red galaxies in the early Universe—far too many to be a fluke.

At first, they were mistaken for rare 'furnace' galaxies, where a black hole runs at full throttle, heating dust until it glows. Both types show a V-shaped dip in their spectrum—dust muffles some colors while letting others through. But precise brightness measurements revealed a difference. In the 'furnaces,' the dust is so hot that radiation spills deep into the red and bursts out as X-rays. In the red dots, the dust is cooler, and there's almost no X-rays.

It's like two cars with similar bodies but different engines: one a roaring supercar, the other a silent electric vehicle.

It seems their 'engines' never experienced violent flares after galaxy collisions and grew gradually. This hunch changes the picture of how galaxies matured after the Big Bang.

🎯 The first Webb images were so thick with these dots that astronomers jokingly called them 'polluters' of galactic surveys.
