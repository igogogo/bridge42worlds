When Repulsion Gives Birth to Pairs: A New Look at Unusual Superconductivity ✦

Original: "When Repulsion Creates Pairing: A New Perspective on Unconventional Superconductivity"
· Patrick Navez
arXiv:2607.03022 · 2026-07-03 · CC BY · 3 min · Superconductivity
Dynamic screening of Coulomb repulsion in cuprates can create effective attraction for Cooper pair formation, explaining the phase diagram of high-temperature superconductors.
Abstract

Even with the Coulomb repulsion and Pauli exclusion trying to keep electrons apart, many-body systems with valley potential landscapes can cook up an effective attraction between electrons or holes. The Kohn-Luttinger mechanism (1965) shows that dynamic screening of the repulsive interaction can coax electrons into Cooper pairs. This study extends that framework to cuprates, where screening emerges from the superexchange dance of virtual holes in the oxygen orbitals of the CuO₂ planes. Using BCS theory, they map out a temperature-doping phase diagram that captures the pseudogap, strange metal, antiferromagnetic, superconducting, and normal states—all lining up nicely with experimental data.

Context

For almost four decades, high-temperature superconductivity in cuprates has remained a mystery. Unlike ordinary metals, where electron pairing into Cooper pairs is mediated by phonons, the isotope effect here is weak. This points to the dominant role of electronic interactions, despite Coulomb repulsion and the Pauli exclusion principle, which forbids two electrons from being in the same state. As early as 1965, John Bardeen (who co-created BCS theory with Cooper and Schrieffer) and other scientists pointed out the possibility of dynamic screening, turning repulsion into effective attraction. New theoretical approaches based on quantum correlations in the CuO2 plane may finally unveil this mechanism.

Methods

To capture the essence of pairing, the authors considered a minimal model — a triad of two copper atoms (d-orbitals) connected by an oxygen (p-orbitals). Using a quantum-field formalism of second quantization and perturbation theory in the hopping amplitude t_pd, they computed the energies of states with different numbers of holes. Virtual transitions of a hole from copper to oxygen contribute quadratically in t_pd. The key idea is that quantum fluctuations allow particles to momentarily violate energy conservation, which is taken into account in second-order perturbation theory. The presence of additional 'screening' holes changes the energy balance, as virtual transitions now involve coherent superpositions of states with different orbital occupations. This leads to a net attraction between two holes on neighboring copper sites.

Results

Calculation within the triad model showed: in the absence of additional holes, the pairing energy U = E2+E0−E1−E1' is positive — no attraction. However, adding at least one extra hole to a neighboring site flips the sign of U to negative, indicating effective attraction. In the regime of strong on-site Coulomb repulsion on copper Ud (~ 6.6 eV), the effect is enhanced. Extending the approach to the entire CuO2 plane through quantum-statistical averaging allowed constructing a temperature-doping phase diagram. With εp = 0.4 eV, Upd = 1.55 eV, the model predicts a superconducting dome with a maximum Tc, as well as transitions to pseudogap and strange metal phases, qualitatively consistent with experiments on La2-xSrxCuO4.

Implications

The proposed scenario indicates that the Coulomb interaction in a many-body environment can not only be screened but also generate attractive forces. This shifts the paradigm for searching new superconductors: one can purposefully design materials with strong superexchange and a suitable energy gap εp. The theory also naturally explains the coexistence of antiferromagnetism and superconductivity, as well as the emergence of the pseudogap as a result of competition between delocalization and correlations.

Future development

Further research could focus on the role of oxygen p-orbitals in other families — nickelates and ferropnictides. Numerical simulations by exact diagonalization or quantum Monte Carlo will test the robustness of the mechanism. If predictions are confirmed, the combination of superexchange and screening will pave the way for engineering superconductors with critical temperatures approaching room temperature, radically transforming energy and transportation.

Impact

The work will impact the physics of strongly correlated systems, quantum phase transitions, and solid-state theory. Practical applications range from lossless power lines to highly sensitive sensors.

Next steps

Precision measurements of ARPES spectra and tunneling microscopy are needed to capture the predicted modification of hole wave functions as doping changes. This will be a direct test of the screening mechanism.

Key open problems

The article directly addresses the unsolved problem of the microscopic mechanism of high-temperature superconductivity, listed as one of the major mysteries in condensed matter physics. It also touches upon the nature of the pseudogap phase, which, according to the authors, may be due to partial condensation without superfluidity.

Key numbers

  • on-site repulsion on copper (Ud): 6.6 eV
  • energy difference oxygen-copper (εp): 0.4 eV
  • interorbital Coulomb repulsion (Upd): 1.55 eV
  • maximum Tc in cuprates under pressure: ~135 K
  • typical hole doping for SC phase: p ~ 0.05-0.25
