Discovery of a Low Surface Brightness Galaxy in the Zone of Avoidance Using VVVX Deepstack ✦

Original: "Discovery of a low surface brightness galaxy in the Zone of Avoidance using VVVX Deepstack imaging"
· J. L. Nilo-Castellón, L. Baravalle, D. Minniti, M. V. Alonso, G. Galaz, C. Villalon, R. Hinojosa, E. O Schmidt, C. Valotto, M. Soto, E. G. Rivera, V. Valdivia-Rojas, D. Alvarez-Rocha, P. Marchant Cortés, N. J. G. Cross
arXiv:2607.03489 · 2026-07-03 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · Galaxies
A new breakthrough in exploring the Zone of Avoidance: deep infrared images from VVVX have revealed the first low surface brightness galaxy, uncovering the hidden structure of the local Universe.
Abstract

We report the discovery of VVVX J080705.96-273823.6, the first candidate low-surface-brightness galaxy in the Zone of Avoidance from the VVVX survey. The object was identified by visually inspecting Deepstack images obtained after improved processing that enhanced depth and uniformity. Photometric parameters were measured using SExtractor and PSFEx after star subtraction; structural properties were derived by fitting surface brightness profiles (J, H, Ks) with exponential, Sérsic, and composite models. In all three filters, the profile is consistent with an exponential disk with a scale length of 32–36″ and a central surface brightness of 19.82–22.35 mag arcsec⁻² (absorption-corrected). The proximity to ESO 494-25 and ESO 494-26 suggests a physical association; at a distance of 11–12 Mpc, the scale length is ≈1.8–2.0 kpc, typical for nearby LSB galaxies, although no spectroscopic redshift is available. This result demonstrates that the VVVX Deepstack products enable the detection of diffuse galaxies at low Galactic latitudes, bringing us closer to a complete census of the nearby Universe hidden behind the Milky Way.

Context

The Zone of Avoidance remains a major gap in our attempts to build a uniform picture of matter distribution in the local Universe. Dense layers of cosmic dust and the high concentration of stars in the Milky Way's disk cause strong absorption and scattering of optical light, hiding up to 20% of the extragalactic sky. Although previous surveys, especially at radio wavelengths, have uncovered thousands of galaxies behind this veil, most are bright and massive objects. The population of faint diffuse galaxies, including low surface brightness objects, remains largely unexplored, introducing a systematic bias into our understanding of galactic evolution and dark matter distribution. The lack of a complete census of such systems in the interstellar medium can distort dynamical models and cosmological conclusions based on large-scale structure mapping.

Methods

The key to the breakthrough was a new reduction of VVVX (VISTA Variables in the Vía Láctea eXtended Survey) data, named Deepstack. Unlike standard reduction, Deepstack employs advanced alignment and calibration algorithms that increased the effective image depth and improved spatial uniformity. For sky tile e0778, between 24 and 264 individual exposures were combined in three near-infrared bands (J, H, and Ks), yielding a 3σ limiting surface brightness of 25.6 mag/arcsec². To extract the faint diffuse object against foreground stars, a two-pass procedure using SExtractor was applied: first a mask of compact sources was created, then extended emission was searched on the cleaned image. Point-spread function (PSF) modeling was done with PSFEx, and quantitative morphology used direct fitting of azimuthally averaged surface brightness profiles with exponential and Sérsic functions. Photometry was calibrated against 2MASS stars and corrected for Galactic extinction.

Results

Galaxy VVVX J080705.96−273823.6 is confidently detected in all three infrared bands as a diffuse elongated system with a position angle of about 70 degrees. Automatic modeling showed disk dominance: in the J and Ks bands the disk fraction is 0.996 and 0.968, respectively. Azimuthally averaged profiles are well described by an exponential law with effective scale lengths h ≈ 32–36 arcseconds. After correction for internal extinction within the Milky Way (AJ ≈ 0.38 mag, AH ≈ 0.24 mag, AKs ≈ 0.16 mag), the central surface brightnesses are µ0 ≈ 19.82 (J), 19.81 (H), and 22.35 (Ks) mag/arcsec². These values fall into the typical range for low-brightness dwarf galaxies. Remarkably, the object lies only 11 arcminutes from the galaxy pair ESO 494−25 and ESO 494−26, whose radial velocities correspond to a distance of ≈11–12 Mpc. If VVVX J0807 is at the same distance, its disk scale length is about 1.8–2.0 kpc, typical for nearby disk LSB galaxies. However, without spectroscopic redshift confirmation, this association remains tentative.

Implications

This discovery shows that the new Deepstack image archive can pierce the Milky Way's dusty veil and reveal a population of faint galaxies previously inaccessible. It paves the way for a more complete census of extragalactic objects in the Zone of Avoidance, directly improving the accuracy of baryonic and dark matter distribution maps in the local volume. Moreover, finding LSB galaxies in such obscured regions can help refine the luminosity function and constrain models of hierarchical galaxy formation, where such objects are natural building blocks.

Future development

Systematic scanning of the entire Deepstack archive covering over 1700 square degrees is expected to yield hundreds of new LSB galaxy candidates, including extremely faint and dwarf systems. Combined with future spectroscopic surveys on next-generation telescopes, such as spectroscopy on the VLT or ELT, this will not only confirm their nature but also measure kinematics and dark matter content, critically testing theories of galactic evolution.

Impact

The results will impact extragalactic astronomy and cosmology by providing data to refine models of matter distribution in the local Universe and improve methods for correcting Galactic extinction.

Next steps

The most critical step is obtaining optical spectra to measure redshifts and confirm the galaxy's physical association with the ESO 494 group. In parallel, automated search for LSB candidates across the full VVVX Deepstack coverage will be conducted using machine learning algorithms.

Key open problems

The incompleteness of galaxy samples behind the Milky Way is directly linked to the missing baryons problem and the need to close the visible matter budget in the local volume. Additionally, studying LSB galaxies in the Zone of Avoidance may shed light on the nature of dark matter, as such objects are believed to be embedded in massive dark halos.
