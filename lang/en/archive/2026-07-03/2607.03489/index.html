Dim Ghost Galaxy: First Success of VVVX Deepstack in the Zone of Avoidance ✦

Original: "Discovery of a low surface brightness galaxy in the Zone of Avoidance using VVVX Deepstack imaging"
· J. L. Nilo-Castellón, L. Baravalle, D. Minniti, M. V. Alonso, G. Galaz, C. Villalon, R. Hinojosa, E. O Schmidt, C. Valotto, M. Soto, E. G. Rivera, V. Valdivia-Rojas, D. Alvarez-Rocha, P. Marchant Cortés, N. J. G. Cross
arXiv:2607.03489 · 2026-07-03 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · Galaxies
The infrared 'developer' VVVX Deepstack pierced through the Milky Way's dust shield and revealed for the first time a ghost galaxy hidden in the Zone of Avoidance.
Abstract

In the Zone of Avoidance — the region hidden behind the bright stars and dust of the Milky Way — the first candidate for a low-surface-brightness galaxy (dimmer than the night sky background) has been discovered. It was accidentally found in enhanced infrared images from the VVVX survey. Its brightness profile in all three near-IR bands fits an exponential disk with a scale length of 32–36 arcseconds. If it lies at the distance of its neighbors (11–12 Mpc), its size is typical for nearby faint disks. This discovery proves that the new deep VVVX maps can reveal hidden galaxies, like dusting off a long-forgotten painting.

The Zone of Avoidance is the most stubborn gap in our knowledge of the local Universe, like pages blotted out with ink in an ancient chronicle. Up to 20% of the extragalactic sky is reliably hidden behind dense cosmic dust and swarms of stars in the Milky Way's disk. Cosmic archaeologists had long to make do with occasional surviving lines — bright galaxies that pierced the veil in radio waves, but the vast majority of faint, diffuse systems remained invisible. Now a tool has arrived, akin to a digital restorer: it doesn't just peer under the stain, but reconstructs the erased text from infrared traces.

Dust in the Milky Way's disk absorbs optical light like a thick fog, but near-infrared light passes through it far more freely — and we are now, for the first time, exploiting this property systematically, like an infrared 'decryption' of concealed texts.

The VVVX Deepstack archive is the result of painstakingly stacking thousands of individual images, much like archaeologists piecing together a shattered fresco from fragments. The limiting photometry reaches an unprecedented depth — down to 25.6 mag/arcsec² in the J-band at the 3σ level. It was this depth that plucked a barely noticeable wisp from the interstellar medium: VVVX J080705.96−273823.6, the first LSB galaxy in the Zone of Avoidance discovered in infrared light.

The object appears as an elongated ghostly disk with a position angle of about 70 degrees. Its azimuthally averaged profile fits an exponential law perfectly, typical of disk systems. The central surface brightness, corrected for Galactic extinction, is about 19.8 mag/arcsec² in the J and H bands, and the disk scale length is 32–36 arcseconds. Assuming it lies at the distance of the neighboring pair ESO 494−25/26 (roughly 11–12 Mpc), the physical size of the disk comes out to a typical value for dwarf LSB galaxies — 1.8–2.0 kpc. It's like finding a half-erased entry in an archive, written in a familiar script: the shape of the characters is recognizable, but to accurately date it we need the missing key — spectroscopic redshift.

The central surface brightness of the newly discovered galaxy in the J-band is about 19.82 mag/arcsec² after correcting for extinction — like a candle at a distance of tens of millions of light-years, shining through a dusty veil.

This first find confirms that the two-step search — star masking and automated modeling — works. Now on the agenda is a systematic scan of all 1700 square degrees of the VVVX survey. We stand on the threshold of a true census of the hidden population: hundreds, if not thousands, of faint dwarf galaxies and LSB disks could fill the gaps in maps of large-scale structure. And this is directly tied to the problem of missing baryons and the distribution of dark matter — because such ghostly systems often reside in massive dark halos. Each faint wisp is a potential marker of a massive dark halo, changing our understanding of the gravitational framework of the local Universe. Future spectroscopy with telescopes like the VLT or ELT will turn each wisp into a full-fledged physical object with measured kinematics, and then the restored chronicle of the local Universe will become far more complete. This isn't just deciphering one line — it's the beginning of reading the missing chapter of galactic evolution.

🎯 It is estimated that entire massive galaxy clusters, comparable to the Great Attractor, may lie hidden behind the Milky Way's dust curtain, and our galactic disk literally blocks giant cosmic continents from our view.
