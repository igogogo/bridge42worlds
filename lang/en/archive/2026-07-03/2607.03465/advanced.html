From Packing Disorder to Cubic Order: Crystallization of Ice from Deeply Supercooled Water ✦

Original: "From Stacking Disorder to Cubic Order: Ice Crystallization from Deeply Supercooled Water"
· Yulin Lin, Weimin Guo, Suvo Banik, Tao Zhou, Thomas E. Gage, Lei Yu, Maksim A. Sultanov, Martin Holt, Subramanian Sankaranarayanan, Peng Zhang, Ilke Arslan, Jianguo Wen
arXiv:2607.03465 · 2026-07-03 · CC BY · 4 min · Materials
How atomic disorder during freezing of supercooled microdroplets gives rise to macroscopic cubic symmetry
Abstract

Using in situ cryogenic transmission electron microscopy on a platform with membrane-encapsulated microdroplets, the freezing of deeply supercooled water was visualized with molecular resolution. Homogeneous nucleation leads to the formation of ice with stacking faults—a mixture of hexagonal and cubic sequences, where cubic ice initially exists only as isolated monolayers. The gradual thickening of these cubic layers is a key kinetic mechanism: on their basal planes, defect-free cubic nanonuclei emerge, acting as face-oriented kinetic seeds that induce cubic twinning and sequential multiplication of growth branches. This kinetic pathway reproducibly generates eight-armed dendrites with global cubic (octahedral) symmetry, despite strong disorder within each arm. In later stages, the release of latent heat triggers a transition to the thermodynamically favorable hexagonal phase, yet the pre-established cubic symmetry is preserved.

Context

Far from equilibrium, the second law of thermodynamics does not prohibit the formation of ordered structures, yet the mechanisms linking atomic chaos to macroscopic symmetry have long remained unexplored. The classic works of Johannes Kepler on hexagonal snowflakes and Michael Faraday's experiments on ice regelation laid the foundations of crystallization science, but direct observation of water nucleation under deep supercooling was missing. Paradoxically, strong kinetic excitation often gives rise to long-range order—for instance, polytypic transitions in carbon (diamond) and silicon (SiC) materials, which, like ice, exhibit mixed hexagonal-cubic stacking sequences. Ludwig Boltzmann provided a statistical interpretation of entropy, but how microscopic fluctuations grow into global symmetry is a question that demands direct observations.

Methods

Experiments were conducted at −40 °C using a cryogenic transmission electron microscope and a special liquid cell sealed with amorphous carbon membranes. High spatial resolution was achieved due to the short de Broglie wavelength of accelerated electrons. Cooling trajectories were repeated many times, enabling the capture of homogeneous nucleation. Crystal structure was determined by electron diffraction in a three-dimensional variant (3D-ED) with reciprocal space reconstruction. Automated phase classification was performed using unsupervised machine learning, complemented by molecular dynamics simulations with a coarse-grained potential and numerical modeling of heat transfer in droplets.

Results

Initially, disordered ice (water with a cubicity of 0.35) forms with isolated monolayers of cubic ice. As growth proceeds, the thickness of cubic segments increases, reaching ~1 nm, and cubicity rises: up to 0.53 in primary dendrites and 0.62 in tertiary ones. Nanoscale nuclei of pure cubic ice, ranging from 3 to 20 nm, were directly visualized; according to Bragg's law, they produce diffraction patterns without the characteristic streaks from stacking faults. These cubic 'embryos' act as face-oriented kinetic seeds, inducing cubic twinning at an angle of 109.5° and successive branch multiplication, leading to a persistent eight-rayed octahedral dendrite despite local disorder. Numerical simulations confirm the preferential nucleation of cubic grains on the basal planes of the disordered matrix, while latent heat at later stages triggers a transition to thermodynamically stable hexagonal ice, with the cubic symmetry preserved.

Implications

The uncovered mechanism, in which nanoscale cubic grains act as face-oriented kinetic seeds, explains how strong kinetic supercooling transforms microscopic disorder into macroscopic cubic symmetry. It links Kepler's observations of snowflake symmetry with modern concepts of non-equilibrium crystallization. A similar pathway may be universal for a wide range of covalent materials, including diamond and silicon carbide, where polytypic transitions govern mechanical and electronic properties. The results also emphasize that global order does not require local perfection—collective behavior can give birth to symmetry from chaos.

Future development

Future research may explore the role of impurities and pressure in controlling the polytypic makeup of ice, as well as apply similar approaches to synthesizing metastable phases in semiconductors and ceramics. Technical advances in numerical simulations with machine-learned potentials will allow prediction of disorder evolution at microscopic scales and linking it to macroscopic responses. The development of in situ time-resolved techniques will enable capturing intermediate states of nuclei during their formation.

Impact

The results will impact the understanding of rapid crystallization in climatology (formation of ice clouds), cryobiology (vitrification), and materials science (controlled synthesis of metastable structures). Moreover, realizing how water transitions from chaotic packing to long-range order may inspire new strategies for designing materials with hierarchical architecture.

Next steps

Immediate steps include investigating the dependence of dendrite branching on cooling rate and droplet size, as well as detecting intermediate states using ultrafast diffraction. There are also plans to study how the presence of dissolved gases or solid particles affects the kinetics of cubic grain emergence.

Key open problems

The article touches on the fundamental problem of nucleation and growth under large deviations from equilibrium, linking it to a specific mechanism of polytypic ordering. This resonates with unsolved questions in prebiotic chemistry (emergence of chirality) and the general theory of non-equilibrium phase transitions, as well as with the problem of universal behavior during ultrafast solidification, where local disorder can become a constructive element of self-organization.

🎯 Interestingly, cubic ice Ic, though metastable under ordinary conditions, can exist in the upper atmosphere, and the octahedral shape observed in the experiment is reminiscent of diamond crystals. What's more, the dendritic growth with twinning creates a structure similar to a Maltese cross, making these icy microparticles extraordinarily beautiful objects.

🎬 Kurt Vonnegut's 'Cat's Cradle,' with its ice-nine capable of instantly crystallizing all water, echoes the idea of catastrophic freezing driven by a non-standard crystal phase. The novel also plays with the role of symmetry and the unpredictability of polymorphic transformations, directly resonant with the uncovered pathway of polytypic ordering.
