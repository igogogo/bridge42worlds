How to Create the Perfect Alloy from Scrap Metal ✦

Original: "Data-driven multi-objective optimization for alloy recycling using factorization machines and quantum annealing"
· Thomas Plehn, Katrin Bugelnig, Silvana Tumminello, Daniel Barragan-Yani, David Melching
arXiv:2607.03208 · 2026-07-03 · CC BY · 1 min · Materials Quantum Physics
A quantum computer has learned to find the best mixture of aluminum waste for strong and thermally conductive parts.
Abstract

Scientists used quantum annealing—a method for solving tough problems—to find the best alloys from recycled metal, balancing strength and cost. Like a chef turning leftovers into a tasty, healthy meal, the algorithm with a quantum computer hunts for the perfect balance. Could such quantum cuisine transform industry?

Links in the knowledge graph 1

Finding the perfect alloy from scrap metal is like baking a cake from leftover ingredients without an exact recipe. You need to mix pieces of different aluminum alloys to get a strong and thermally conductive material. Scientists assigned this task to a quantum computer.

Unlike a conventional computer, which explores options one by one, the quantum computer 'tastes' many mixtures simultaneously thanks to superposition — the ability to be in multiple states at once. And quantum entanglement links parts of the recipe so that changing one instantly affects the others. But if the mixture is too complex, interference occurs — quantum decoherence, and the machine makes mistakes.

To get around this, they added machine learning to the process — a program that predicts the alloy's properties from its composition. The cycle is simple: the program suggests recipes, the quantum algorithm picks the best ones, and numerical simulation (a virtual experiment) checks the result. By repeating these steps, scientists found the ideal metallicity — the precise chemical composition where high strength meets excellent thermal conductivity. The key was not to get stuck on one variant but to maintain diversity in the search — what physicists call entropy. This approach gives a second life to cans and profiles, saving almost all the energy needed for primary aluminum production.

🎯 In the 19th century, aluminum was more expensive than gold: at a banquet of Napoleon III, the most honored guests used aluminum cutlery, while the rest used gold.

🎬 The method resembles the replicator from 'Star Trek': the machine assembles material from an atomic recipe.

f_{\mathrm{FM}}(\mathbf{x}) = w_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i x_i + \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{j=i+1}^{N} w_{ij} x_i x_j
w_0 is the global bias, w_i are linear weights for the elements, w_{ij} is the strength of pairwise interactions between alloy components, x_i are binary variables indicating presence of an element
f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^{\mathsf{T}} \mathbf{Q} \mathbf{x} + \lambda C(\mathbf{x})
Matrix Q is extracted from the trained factorization machine, C(x) is a penalty function that keeps the mixture proportions within reasonable limits, λ is the penalty strength
