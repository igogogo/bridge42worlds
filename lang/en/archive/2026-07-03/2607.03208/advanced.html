Quantum annealing in alloy design: a second life for scrap metal ✦

Original: "Data-driven multi-objective optimization for alloy recycling using factorization machines and quantum annealing"
· Thomas Plehn, Katrin Bugelnig, Silvana Tumminello, Daniel Barragan-Yani, David Melching
arXiv:2607.03208 · 2026-07-03 · CC BY · 3 min
Materials Quantum Physics
Quantum computing and machine learning pave the way for efficient aluminum waste recycling, creating alloys with an optimal balance of strength and thermal conductivity.
Abstract

A systematic evaluation of a hybrid approach combining factorization machines and quantum annealing (FM+QA) for multi-objective Pareto optimization in creating alloys from recycled metal is conducted. To handle the non-convex nature of the Pareto front, a data-driven Tchebycheff scalarization (DDTS) scheme is employed. Results show that FM+QA extends QUBO optimization to materials search with multiple competing criteria. The scalability of the approach is analyzed, and quantum annealing is compared with classical simulated annealing under plain binary and one-hot encodings. A critical perspective is offered on problem sizes and encoding strategies where quantum-assisted optimization may become practically useful in the near future.

1

Context

The global economy faces resource scarcity and the imperative to reduce carbon footprints. The aluminum industry consumes enormous amounts of energy: producing secondary aluminum requires only 5% of the energy needed for primary metal. However, creating high-quality alloys from heterogeneous scrap is a task with multiple conflicting requirements, where metallicity (chemical element composition) nonlinearly influences strength and thermal conductivity. Here, machine learning and quantum technologies come to the rescue, capable of accelerating the search for optimal recipes in gigantic combinatorial spaces.

Methods

The researchers used active learning with a surrogate model based on a factorization machine (FM), which predicts alloy properties from its encoded composition. At each step, a quantum computer of the D-Wave Advantage type solves a discrete optimization problem (QUBO), finding the next candidate for validation. Scrap mixing proportions were encoded in two ways: binary (compact) and one-hot, where each bit corresponds to a logical qubit. For the multi-objective task of maximizing yield strength (YS) and thermal conductivity (TC), the Chebyshev scalarization (DDTS) technique was applied, transforming multiple goals into one by iteratively varying weights, thereby exploring the entropy of the solution front.

Results

During single-objective optimization of YS, binary encoding consistently outperformed one-hot, achieving rapid convergence to the global optimum (436 MPa at 100% AlSi12Cu3Ni2Mg1Fe0.5) in less than 25 iterations. For a 5-alloy system with 3.2% resolution (25 qubits), the quantum annealing algorithm showed results comparable to classical simulated annealing (SA). Time-to-solution (TTS) analysis revealed that at small scales (up to 16 qubits) the quantum sampler had an advantage, but at 60 qubits with one-hot encoding performance collapsed due to quantum decoherence and noise. In the multi-objective case (20 qubits, binary encoding), the DDTS method successfully recovered a non-convex Pareto front, discovering complex four-component mixtures that cannot be found by standard weighted sums. The hypervolume of the front grew at the same rate for the quantum variant as for the classical one, proving the practical applicability of superposition and quantum entanglement in industrial tasks.

Implications

The results confirm that quantum annealing can already serve today as a competitive tool for multi-objective optimization in materials science, without requiring fundamental quantum supremacy. This extends the methodology of numerical simulation with active learning to circular economy tasks, where each iteration involving expensive experiments or Thermo-Calc calculations must be maximally informative. Overcoming the limitations of one-hot encoding points to the need for hybrid strategies to handle constraints on NISQ devices.

Future development

As quantum processors improve (increased connectivity, reduced thermal noise) and quantum error correction algorithms advance, such methods will be able to operate with tens of chemical elements and mixture resolutions of fractions of a percent. Promising areas include combining the quantum sampler with classical local optimization to solve the embedding packing problem, as well as transitioning to gate-based architectures (QAOA), where encoding constraints can be naturally incorporated.

Impact

The work will influence accelerated materials development for the aerospace industry and additive manufacturing, as well as stimulate the adoption of quantum computing in industrial recycling chains.

Next steps

Next steps include experimental validation of the proposed alloys using synchrotron methods and integrating more realistic microstructure models into the feedback loop.

Key open problems

The study is directly connected to the unsolved problem of achieving practical quantum supremacy in noisy systems, as well as the fundamental question: how to efficiently encode continuous variables into discrete ones to circumvent the “curse of dimensionality” in quantum machine learning.
