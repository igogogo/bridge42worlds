Photosynthesis as a Biomarker: What Will Give Away Life on Other Worlds ✦

Original: "White Paper on Phototrophic Biosignatures: Research Priorities for the Search for Life on Other Worlds"
· Nancy Y. Kiang, Christopher Gisriel, Robert E. Blankenship, Mary N. Parenteau, Hazel A. Barton, Oded Béjà, Anthony J. Burnetti, Ligia F. Coelho, Saleheh Ebadirad, Nathan M. Ennist, Yuka Fujii, Colin Gates, R. J. Graham, Keiichi Inoue, Betül Kaçar, Yu Komatsu, Émilie A. Laflèche, Nicoletta La Rocca, Elisabetta Liistro, Jonathan Lindsey, Timothy Lyons, Carolina A. arXiv:2607.03106 · 2026-07-03 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min
Instrumentation Exoplanets
Photosynthesis can create global biosignatures, such as oxygen and the spectral ‘red edge’ of vegetation, which could be detected by future telescopes on exoplanets.
Abstract

On Earth, oxygenic photosynthesis creates two global biomarkers: oxygen and the red edge of vegetation (a jump in reflectance from red to near-infrared due to chlorophyll a). However, the diversity of phototrophic organisms with other pigments that produce spectral jumps in other ranges suggests that on exoplanets the signatures may be different. To predict such biosignatures, critically important questions have been identified: about the origin, key features, and alternative adaptations of light harvesting, electron transport, rhodopsin-dependent proton pumping, and carbon fixation. The need to consider evolution and ecology when moving from molecular mechanisms to detectable planetary scales is discussed. The questions are linked with the NASA Astrobiology Strategy 2015 and the future Decadal Astrobiology Exploration Strategy.

1

Context

The search for life beyond the Solar System relies on biosignatures — remotely observable signs of biological activity. Biosignatures of oxygenic photosynthesis, primarily atmospheric oxygen and the spectral ‘red edge’ of vegetation (VRE), have already transformed Earth and are visible from space. Yet the astrobiology community cannot yet robustly link observational data from exoplanets to the presence of photosynthetic life, especially in the context of different atmospheric compositions and spectra of host stars, the study of which began with the pioneering work of Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin. It is critically important to understand how universal Earth’s photography mechanisms are and what alternatives might have arisen on planets around red dwarfs or Sun-like stars, largely discovered thanks to missions by William Borucki.

Methods

To build a predictive model of phototrophic biosignatures, an interdisciplinary approach is used, combining spectroscopy with Doppler measurement of radial velocities, genomics, synthetic biology, and planetary modeling. Natural pigments — from chlorophylls to retinal-containing rhodopsins — are studied using Einstein's photoelectric equation and the Planck–Einstein relation, which define the energy thresholds of photoconversion. Laboratory experiments simulate illumination from M-dwarfs for cyanobacterial cultures, while climate models calculate the available photon flux and surface chemical context. Space missions such as JWST and future direct-imaging observatories test the detectability of spectral features under noisy conditions.

Results

A wide range of spectral adaptations has been revealed: in oxygenic phototrophs, the long-wavelength limit is extended from 700 nm (chlorophyll a) to roughly 800 nm thanks to chlorophylls d and f, while anoxygenic bacteria can use near-IR up to 1020 nm. Oxygenic photosynthesis likely emerged in the Archean, but atmospheric O2 accumulated only hundreds of millions of years later due to strong geochemical sinks — pointing to the risk of false negatives in the search for life. It has been shown that rhodopsin-based proton pumps, which do not require complex redox chains, are widespread across all domains of life and can dominate certain spectral niches, though their link to autotrophy remains unclear. The analysis also highlights that the dominant pigments on a planet may not coincide with the first evolutionary innovations due to ecological priorities and planetary catastrophes.

Implications

These results are of fundamental importance for planning missions like JWST and the proposed Habitable Worlds Observatory. They show that unambiguous interpretation of biosignatures requires a comprehensive consideration of stellar spectrum, planetary atmosphere, availability of water, and evolutionary context. Understanding the physicochemical constraints on photoconversion allows us to outline a photosynthetic ‘habitable zone’ — the region around a star where energetically efficient photography is possible. Moreover, the research justifies the need to search not only for Earth-like but also for agnostic biosignatures, such as spectral shifts of pigments not tied to a specific chemical composition.

Future development

The topic will evolve toward integrating systems biology and planetary science. Future work includes creating quantum-chemical models to predict absorption spectra of hypothetical pigments in various solvents and protein environments, as well as conducting evolutionary experiments on the synthetic redesign of reaction centers. An important step will be developing a scaling theory that links molecular innovations to the global proliferation of phototrophs and their reflection in the spectra of exoplanets.

Impact

The results will influence the scientific programs of next-generation space telescopes, criteria for selecting target planetary systems, and the interpretation of spectral data. They also stimulate synthetic biology, since the search for alternative photography pathways will require designing and testing artificial photosystems.

Next steps

It is necessary to refine the minimum photon flux supporting growth and the upper illuminance limit causing photodamage for diverse phototrophs under exoplanet-like conditions. A large-scale campaign for spectral screening of terrestrial microorganisms and metagenomic search for new photopigments will also be key.

Key open problems

The research is directly connected to unresolved questions in astrobiology: how did oxygenic photosynthesis arise and why did it appear only once; what delayed the oxygenation of Earth's atmosphere; can photosynthesis be considered an expected step in the evolution of complex life; what are the true boundaries of the habitable zone considering phototrophic productivity.

🎯 The vegetation red edge, which gives away Earth's plants from space, is not just a ‘green’ effect: it results from a sharp drop in chlorophyll absorption in the near-IR and strong scattering inside the leaf — that's why vegetation looks bright red on satellite images!

🎬 In the movie ‘Avatar,’ Pandora's flora has unusual colors, echoing the idea that photosynthetic life on other planets may use pigments different from Earth's and create unique spectral biosignatures, similar to what scientists are searching for in real exoplanetary systems.

E = h\nu
E is photon energy, h is Planck’s constant, ν is radiation frequency. It determines which photons can excite electrons in photosynthetic pigments.

Key numbers

  • vegetation red edge (VRE): 680–760 nm
  • record long-wavelength limit of oxygenic photosynthesis: ~800 nm (chlorophyll f)
  • maximum wavelength of anoxygenic photosynthesis: ~1020 nm
  • minimum photon flux density for survival of oxygenic phototrophs: 0.04 mmol m⁻² s⁻¹
  • delay period of O₂ accumulation after the emergence of oxygenic photosynthesis: hundreds of millions of years
