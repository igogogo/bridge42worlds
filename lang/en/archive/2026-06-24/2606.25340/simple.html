Halfway Melting: How Fractional Vortices Destroy Order ✦

Original: "Fractionalized Vortices Drive Kosterlitz-Thouless Transitions in Dipole-Conserving Systems"
· Han-Xie Wang, Shuai A. Chen, Zheng Yan, Peng Ye
arXiv:2606.25340 · 2026-06-24 · CC BY · 1 min
Quantum Gases Statistical Mech Strongly Correlated
In some quantum media, the transition from order to chaos is driven not by whole vortices, but by half-vortices tied to a single axis.
Abstract

In a two-dimensional superfluid where the dipolar moment is conserved, ordinary vortices cannot wreak havoc because their energy is limited. The researchers demonstrated that a vortex splits into two pieces, each carrying unbounded energy. These 'half-vortices' are the ones responsible for the transition. So the system dodges the prohibition by breaking apart what's permitted, like a key that snaps into twin halves.

1

A mesh of interwoven threads doesn't tear all at once: cut one vertical thread, and the horizontal ones still hold the shape. A similar principle works in certain two-dimensional quantum media. There, defects—fractional vortices—can only move along a single axis, like a cut that doesn't affect the whole fabric.

As temperature rises, more of these vortices appear—as dictated by the second law of thermodynamics. At the critical point, melting begins. But if the vertical and horizontal bonds differ, the system melts in two steps: first the weak threads snap, then the strong ones. In between, a curious state emerges: the material flows in one direction but remains solid in the other.

This discovery deepens our understanding of superfluidity and superconductivity. Computer simulations confirmed this scenario. It can be witnessed firsthand in Bose-Einstein condensates—clouds of atoms cooled to ultra-low temperatures. There, fractional vortices act like singular points, rearranging quantum fields. The revealed mechanics echo processes in cosmic plasma, where similar vortices take part in magnetic reconnection and even tidal disruption of stars.

🎯 The authors compare fractional vortices to spaghetti strands in soup—each moves only along its own axis, but together they create a tangled pattern, like when stirred.
