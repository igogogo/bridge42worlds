Spaghetti in Soup: How Fractional Vortices Make Superfluidity Melt Twice ✦

Original: "Fractionalized Vortices Drive Kosterlitz-Thouless Transitions in Dipole-Conserving Systems"
· Han-Xie Wang, Shuai A. Chen, Zheng Yan, Peng Ye
arXiv:2606.25340 · 2026-06-24 · CC BY · 4 min
Quantum Gases Statistical Mech Strongly Correlated
In media with dipolar constraints, whole vortices shatter into fragments, and each fragment awakens at its own temperature — hence superfluidity melts in two steps.
Abstract

Usually in 2D systems, the Kosterlitz–Thouless (KT) phase transition is driven by vortices. But if the dipolar moment is conserved, an ordinary vortex has finite energy and the KT transition vanishes. The authors found a way out: the vortex splits into two 'half-vortices' with infinite energy, and their unbinding triggers the transition. Monte Carlo simulations revealed a single KT transition in the isotropic case, while anisotropy leads to two transitions, with an intermediate phase. This is akin to particle splitting in hadron physics. The results are important for systems with multipole conservation laws.

1

Two-dimensional worlds never cease to amaze: here even crystal melting is not a symmetry breaking but a vortex drama discovered by Kosterlitz and Thouless. At the heart of this theater are topological singularity, whose energy and entropy bargain for order. This language is common to superconductivity. But impose a constraint—to conserve the dipole moment of particles—and the familiar stage collapses. The usual order parameter, the superfluid wave, simply cannot be born. That is how Bose-Einstein condensate work, candidates for realization in optical lattices. In them, vortices lose their punch: their energy is finite, the logarithmic divergence needed for the transition is absent. It would seem the Kosterlitz-Thouless transition is forbidden. But nature, as always, finds a loophole.

In the dipole-conserving XY model, the energy of an ordinary vortex is constant (πJ) — it does not grow with size. Such a vortex cannot trigger a transition: entropy cannot overcome the fixed energy. But for fractional descendants, the energy soars as 2πJ ln(L/a), and the real magic begins.

Imagine a bowl of tomato soup with long spaghetti. Each noodle can slide only along itself — that's the dipole constraint: particle motion is allowed only collinearly. A vortex in such a world is not a compact whirlpool but a chain of spaghetti that can kink while conserving dipole moment. A whole vortex Θ₀ is like a tangled clump of vermicelli — its energy does not intimidate the system. But pull out individual strands and you get fractional vortices Θ(1)₁ and Θ(2)₁ — each stretching along its own axis. Their birth demands a logarithmic growth of energy, exactly as in the classical KT transition. The key to the puzzle is Quantum Field, intertwining vortices with elastic moduli. In the isotropic case, both species of fractional vortices break free simultaneously at T≈1.495 (in units of J) — and superfluidity vanishes in a jump. Large-scale numerical simulation on lattices up to 64×64 confirms: densities of both types grow synchronously, and generalized elastic moduli — sensitive to bending under the dipole yoke — exhibit a universal jump.

Break the isotropy, and the dance of noodles stratifies. If couplings along the axes are unequal (J₁ ≠ J₂), vortices Θ(2)₁ break free first — at T_c^(2)≈1.20, while their siblings Θ(1)₁ are still shackled. Between the two KT transitions, an exotic phase emerges: one dipole field retains algebraic order, the other is already destroyed — superfluidity becomes asymmetric, like a liquid crystal. The analogy with plasma is transparent: free vortices screen the interaction, much like mobile charges, and each species acts on its own. Strikingly, switching off the ring exchange around a plaquette (J₃=0) again merges the two transitions into one — geometry intertwines the fates of the vortices.

In the limit of strong anisotropy, when J₂→0, the logarithmic contribution to the energy of Θ(2)₁ disappears, and these vortices drop out of the game. Only one active species remains — and a single KT transition, as if nature mercilessly trims the excess pasta.

This work is not a curiosity but a universal principle. Conservation of dipole moment spawns two species of fractional vortices; conservation of quadrupole moment creates an entire hierarchy, which even under full isotropy yields two transitions due to differing energies. Thus a ladder of fracton criticality is built: the higher the multipole, the more tangled the skein. Soon these predictions will be tested in ultracold atoms on tilted optical lattices, where lasers will allow us to witness the split transition firsthand. Similar motifs likely play out in layered magnets with ring exchange. And magnetic reconnection during pair annihilation — a process akin to tidal disruption event of stars — still awaits detailed investigation. Ultimately, a conservation law ceases to be an enemy of order and turns into a conductor of an intricate topological ballet. And perhaps the main lesson is that even absolute prohibitions can give birth to new degrees of freedom — one only needs to find a crack in the armor of symmetry.
