Topological entanglement in a monitored Rashba nanowire ✦

Original: "Long-lasting Topological Entanglement in a Monitored Rashba Nanowire"
· Emanuele Guida, Giulia Salatino, Gianluca Passarelli, Angelo Russomanno, Procolo Lucignano
arXiv:2606.25653 · 2026-06-24 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · Quantum Physics Mesoscale
Scientists showed that non-local quantum entanglement persists for a long time in an open system due to ground state degeneracy.
Abstract

We study the topological dynamics of a Rashba chain under continuous monitoring, described by quantum jump trajectories. The disconnected entanglement entropy (DEE) retains its initial topological value for a time linearly proportional to the system size, even with boundary dissipative processes affecting edge Majorana modes. The resilience stems from the lack of particle number conservation and the degeneracy of the topological manifold: stochastic quantum jumps induce transitions that create and annihilate Majorana modes in alternation. Simultaneously, ballistically propagating quasiparticles with finite energy are injected, and their accumulation eventually demolishes the topological entanglement structure. These findings set the timescale for topological order preservation in open systems, vital for engineering noise-resilient quantum protocols.

Context

In condensed matter physics, topological phases are a revolution: they are protected by global symmetries and have no local order parameters. A key feature is the presence of edge modes, such as Majorana fermions in superconducting nanowires based on the proximity effect with an ordinary superconductor (BCS theory, John Bardeen). These modes promise error-resistant quantum computation. However, real systems always interact with the environment, and the question of how decoherence affects topological protection remains open. Disconnected entanglement entropy (DEE) — a recently proposed tool for detecting non-local topological entanglement — becomes a powerful probe here.

Methods

The researchers considered a Rashba nanowire model near an s-wave superconductor with a magnetic field. Its dynamics was described by the Lindblad equation with local spin-dependent particle loss operators. Underpinning this is the Schrödinger equation for a pure state, extended to a stochastic equation, and the superposition principle, allowing a pure state to be a coherent sum. Instead of averaging over all trajectories, they applied the quantum jump (unraveling) method, tracking the evolution of pure states along individual stochastic trajectories. Since the Hamiltonian is quadratic, the state remains Gaussian, which made it possible to compute correlation matrices and entropies for a large number of sites (up to 120) using numerical simulations. DEE was computed using the standard formula, where subsystems A and B are two disconnected blocks of the chain, in order to cancel area-law terms and isolate the topological contribution. The computation of entropy relies on the concept of quantum entanglement, first formulated by Erwin Schrödinger.

Results

The main result: regardless of whether the loss acts on the whole chain, only in the bulk, or only at the edges, the ensemble-averaged DEE remains at the level log 2 ≈ 0.693 for a long time. The characteristic time t_c, defined by the deviation of DEE from the topological value by more than 0.01, grows linearly with system size N (e.g., t_c ≈ 5–10 in dimensionless units for N~100). This is confirmed for weak disorder in the chemical potential. Statistics of DEE changes under individual quantum jumps showed a sharp peak at zero: jumps do not instantly alter the non-local entanglement. Physically, unlike the SSH model where a jump at an edge immediately destroys the edge mode, here a quantum jump toggles the system between two degenerate topological ground states with different fermion parity, alternately creating and destroying a Majorana mode. This is a direct consequence of the Pauli exclusion principle for fermions and the absence of particle number conservation. However, each such jump also creates a finite energy — a parasitic quasiparticle near the edge. This quasiparticle moves ballistically at a speed on the order of the Fermi velocity and reaches the other boundary only after a time ~L/v. It is then that it introduces an additional entropy contribution, disrupting the delicate cancellation in DEE. Thus arises delayed poisoning, explaining the linear increase in the lifetime of topological entanglement. In the thermodynamic limit, DEE becomes eternal.

Implications

These results change the perception of the vulnerability of topological quantum correlations. It turns out that boundary dissipation is not necessarily fatal for topological protection if there is ground state degeneracy and no particle conservation law. This opens new perspectives for designing robust topological quantum devices, where monitoring could even be used to manipulate Majorana modes without immediate wave function collapse. The method also shows that DEE is a sensitive tool for distinguishing types of edge entanglement in open systems.

Future development

The topic may evolve towards studying the influence of different types of jumps (e.g., parity-preserving), as well as interactions and non-Markovian dynamics. It is important to understand whether the linear scaling holds for more realistic models of quasiparticle poisoning that account for Coulomb interaction. Another front is experimental realization: although DEE is hard to measure directly, recent advances in quantum tomography inspire hope.

Impact

The work will impact the development of topological quantum computers based on Majorana zero modes, as well as the general understanding of quantum phase transitions in non-equilibrium conditions and the physics of open quantum systems.

Next steps

Near-term steps include simulating larger systems to verify scaling and analyzing sensitivity to the dissipation profile, as well as experimental verification in hybrid semiconductor-superconductor nanowires.

Key open problems

This research is directly related to a major unsolved problem — creating stable topological qubits and understanding decoherence in multi-qubit systems. It also sheds light on the fundamental question of how quantum information spreads in open many-particle systems, including the paradox of how the classical world emerges from the quantum through measurements.

🎯 Interestingly, the disconnected entanglement entropy equals log 2 regardless of the exact model, as long as it is in a topological phase — this is the quantum analog of the universal conductance in the quantum Hall effect.

Key numbers

  • topological value of DEE: log 2 ≈ 0.693
  • sizes of studied systems: N = 88, 96, 104, 112, 120
  • loss rates: γ↑ = 1.0, γ↓ = 0.9 (in arbitrary units)
  • characteristic time t_c for N=104 (uniform dissipation): ~5 (in inverse energy units)
  • ballistic excitation propagation speed: ~1 (in units of lattice constant/time)
