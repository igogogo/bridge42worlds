Differentiable DFT: Inverse Materials Design via Gradient Composition Optimization ✦

Original: "A Differentiable DFT-Based Framework for Inverse Materials Design"
· Kohei Ishii, Hisazumi Akai, Tetsuya Fukushima, Hikari Shinya, Koji Inui
arXiv:2606.25502 · 2026-06-24 · CC BY · 4 min · Materials Computational Physics
A new method combines automatic differentiation with the KKR-CPA method to directly search for alloy compositions with desired magnetic and electronic properties.
Abstract

Designing materials with targeted properties remains a central challenge in computational materials science. Existing approaches—high-throughput screening, surrogate optimization, generative models—require large datasets and extrapolate poorly to unknown compositions. We propose a first-principles inverse design platform that embeds reverse-mode automatic differentiation into the KKR-CPA (Korringa-Kohn-Rostoker with coherent potential approximation) method. This allows treating atomic concentrations as continuous variables and computing gradients of objective functions with respect to composition at a cost independent of the number of candidate elements. Gradient-based optimization efficiently explores spaces of dozens of elements. Two tasks are chosen for demonstration: searching for magnetic alloys and half-metals, resulting in compositions (Lu₀.₅₅₃Yb₀.₄₄₇)(Co₀.₇₅₉Fe₀.₂₄₁)₂Fe₃ and FeZr(Sb₀.₉₄Te₀.₀₆). This approach provides a physically justified route from a target property to the material that realizes it.

Context

Searching for new functional materials is a central task in solid-state physics. First-principles methods, pioneered by quantum solid-state theory founders such as John Bardeen, can predict properties from composition, but the inverse problem—from property to composition—remained unsolved due to the enormous number of possible element combinations. Even with a fixed crystal lattice, the composition space is astronomically large. High-throughput screening requires huge resources, and approaches based on machine learning (surrogate models and generative design) are unreliable outside the training set. This work proposes a fundamentally different path—using gradient optimization within first-principles calculations, made possible by treating composition as continuous variables in the KKR-CPA method, where, unlike discrete supercells, the wave nature of electrons is described via Green's functions, and configurational averaging is done analytically.

Methods

The method is based on integrating numerical simulation using density functional theory with reverse-mode automatic differentiation. Instead of conventional plane-wave DFT, where swapping an atom changes the system discretely, the authors use the Korringa–Kohn–Rostoker method with the coherent potential approximation (KKR-CPA). This approach has its roots in the multiple scattering theory of Hans Bethe and uses Green's functions. Here, each crystallographic site can be occupied by a mixture of elements with continuous concentrations, and configurational averaging is done analytically. The computational graph is traversed twice: the forward pass computes the objective function (e.g., magnetic moment), and the backward pass yields exact gradients with respect to all concentration parameters, with the cost of this step independent of the number of parameters. The calculations employ Bloch's theorem (formulated by Felix Bloch) to describe electronic states in a periodic lattice and the Schrödinger equation with an exchange-correlation functional. The Pauli exclusion principle governs state occupation and spin polarization, crucial for magnetic materials.

Results

The method was tested in two contrasting scenarios. When maximizing the magnetization of the FeCoNi ternary alloy, gradient ascent stably converges to the well-known Slater–Pauling peak (Fe0.87Co0.13) from three different starting points. In a 30-dimensional space of elements from B to Te, the optimizer automatically suppresses all components except iron and cobalt, singling out the same optimal composition. For the multi-site LnT5 system with 43 parameters across three sublattices, a series of permanent magnet candidates were found, such as PrFe5 (12.15 μB/f.u.) and (Lu,Yb)(Co,Fe)2Fe3. In the second scenario, the target function was the density of states shape, and the optimization synthesized the half-metal FeZr(Sb0.94Te0.06)—close to the known FeZrSb—with a 97.6% suppression of states at the Fermi level in one spin channel. All candidates are metallic phases with pronounced magnetic properties. In all cases, each optimization step is backed by a full DFT calculation, and the final compositions were cross-checked for convergence.

Implications

This work demonstrates for the first time that combining KKR-CPA with automatic differentiation yields a universal first-principles inverse materials design tool. The fact that you can set any differentiable quantity as a target—from integral characteristics like magnetic moment to spectroscopic details of the electronic structure—opens the way to goal-oriented searches for materials with desired transport, optical, or catalytic properties. Moreover, the method requires no training data and avoids the extrapolation errors characteristic of machine learning models, which is crucial when exploring uncharted regions of phase space.

Future development

In the future, this approach can be extended to optimize not only composition but also lattice geometry and external parameters such as magnetic and electric fields. This would enable a unified computational platform for materials design “from target to substance,” where both composition and structure are jointly tailored. Developing full-potential calculation versions and incorporating thermodynamic stability are the next steps that will bring the method closer to real experimental challenges.

Impact

The method will impact the development of magnetic materials, especially high-performance permanent magnets and spintronic devices, as well as the search for new superconductors and thermoelectrics. Integration with calculations of electromagnetic properties, such as magnetic anisotropy and transport phenomena, will make materials design goal-oriented.

Next steps

Immediate tasks include implementing Curie temperature calculations via the Liechtenstein formalism, incorporating elemental cost targets into the objective function, and extending the method to strongly correlated systems.

Key open problems

This work directly addresses the fundamental problem of predictive materials design—minimizing blind screening. Using continuous composition variables combined with exact quantum field methods (Green's functions) bypasses the limitation of discrete supercells and enables exploration of spaces with dozens of parameters, previously inaccessible to first-principles methods.
