Neural Network Surrogate for Predicting Elastic Properties of Digital Rocks ✦

Original: "A convolutional neural network surrogate for hierarchical homogenization: fast elastic moduli prediction of digital rocks"
· Hanfeng Zhai, Rasool Ahmad, Tapan Mukerji, Wei Cai
arXiv:2606.25275 · 2026-06-24 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min
Computational Physics Materials Geophysics
A lightweight convolutional neural network coupled with hierarchical homogenization speeds up elastic modulus calculations for porous rocks by millions of times while preserving direct simulation accuracy.
Abstract

To determine the effective elastic properties of rocks from micro-CT images, an approach combining a lightweight 3D convolutional neural network and hierarchical homogenization has been developed. The image is divided into subcubes; the network predicts their elastic moduli, replacing resource-intensive direct simulation (DNS). Using a single convolutional architecture, three training target representations are systematically compared: the full 6×6 anisotropic stiffness tensor, a pair of isotropic moduli — bulk modulus and shear modulus (K, G), and HS-normalized coefficients. For several rock types, all models showed good agreement with DNS results at substantially lower computational cost. Training from scratch for each rock type is fast; transfer learning is not needed. The best combination of speed, accuracy, and physical consistency is achieved with HS-normalized factors; a slightly more accurate alternative is the isotropic pair (K, G).

1

Context

Digital rock physics allows mechanical properties to be calculated without destructive lab tests, but direct numerical modeling on high-resolution micro-images is prohibitively expensive. Hierarchical homogenization simplifies the problem by splitting a large rock into subvolumes, but the first step—solving the elasticity problem on thousands of subvolumes—still demands enormous computational resources. To accelerate this step, physicists increasingly turn to machine learning, but previous attempts were either too "heavy" or did not systematically evaluate the choice of target variables. The new approach uses a lightweight numerical scheme based on a convolutional neural network that learns to replace direct simulation on subvolumes. The original equations of linear elasticity, discovered by Hooke and formalized by Cauchy, describe the isotropic behavior of rock at the macroscale, but anisotropic effects can appear at the subvolume level.

Methods

The scientists analyzed X-ray microtomographic images of five sandstone samples (Berea, Castlegate, Fontainebleau) with a resolution of 900×900×900 voxels. X-ray microtomography relies on differential absorption of rays in mineral and pores, akin to spectroscopy. Each sample was divided into subvolumes of 75×75×75 voxels. A lightweight 3D convolutional neural network was trained on these subvolumes with three output variants: the full 6×6 stiffness tensor (Model 1), isotropic bulk and shear moduli (Model 2), and factors normalized to the Hashin–Shtrikman bounds (Model 3). The network architecture, reminiscent of a simplified visual analyzer, contains only about 57 million parameters. After training, the network was embedded in a hierarchical homogenization pipeline to predict effective moduli of large fragments (up to 600³ voxels). Accuracy was compared with direct numerical simulation using the fast Fourier transform method.

Results

All three models showed excellent agreement with benchmark calculations at the subvolume level (R² > 0.99). When moving to larger volumes (300³ and 600³ voxels), Models 2 and 3, which use the isotropic approximation, slightly outperformed Model 1, confirming that the rock behaves isotropically at the macroscale. Model 3 with normalized factors trained the fastest—the limited output range [0,1] improved gradient optimization convergence. Interestingly, the overall pipeline error was determined mainly by the upscaling error during homogenization, not by replacing the simulator with a neural network. This allowed a total speedup of 10⁵–10⁶ times compared to full DNS without significant loss of accuracy. The observation that most predictions satisfy the Hashin–Shtrikman bounds—a kind of elasticity limit, analogous to the mass limit for white dwarfs—underscores the physical consistency of the result.

Implications

The study shows how smart incorporation of physical knowledge into machine learning architecture accelerates training and ensures reliability. Success on five different sandstones proves the approach's universality. In the future, digital twins of reservoirs could be created in minutes instead of days and used to optimize oil recovery or assess CO2 storage capacity. The similarity to how gravitational lensing reconstructs dark matter distribution suggests common principles of tomographic reconstruction across fields.

Future development

The next step is to adapt the method for multiphase flow and multicomponent media. Accounting for nonlinear elasticity and plasticity will enable modeling of fracture processes. Combining with Bayesian methods will provide quantitative uncertainty estimates—an essential condition for engineering decisions. Just as astrophysicists studying dark matter through its gravitational influence, geophysicists will be able to "feel" pore space through effective moduli.

Impact

Direct applications are in oil and gas, hydrogeology, and site evaluation for greenhouse gas storage. The technology is also important for materials science, where predicting composite and porous media properties is needed.

Next steps

The immediate task is to test the method on more heterogeneous rocks (carbonates, shales) and implement transfer learning for cases with scarce data. Integration with generative microstructure models will allow creating virtual rock ensembles for accelerated screening.

Key open problems

The work directly connects to the fundamental problem of multiscale modeling in heterogeneous media. It demonstrates how a "coarse-grained" description, similar to effective field theory, can accurately capture macro-characteristics while avoiding detailed simulation of all scales. This resonates with challenges in describing turbulence, climate systems, and quantum gravity.

🎯 The neural network itself has fewer parameters than many mobile apps (~57 million), yet replaces hours of supercomputer computation. And training a model for a new sandstone type only requires a regular laptop—transfer learning wasn't even needed.
