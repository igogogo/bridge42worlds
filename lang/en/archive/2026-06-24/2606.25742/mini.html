The neural network tuner: how five quantum calculations open the door to Freon-free fridges ✦

Original: "Barocaloric phase transformation from data efficient fine-tuning of machine learned interatomic potentials"
· Ludwig Hedin, Johan Klarbring
arXiv:2606.25742 · 2026-06-24 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min
Materials
A pre-trained model fine-tuned on just 5–10 examples reproduces the phase transition of ammonium sulfate—like a tuner tweaking the strings of a finished grand piano.
1

A piano is tuned for centuries, but to strike a new note, the master only needs a couple of moves. So it is with a neural network: having absorbed quantum harmony, it learns from just 5 examples to model cooling crystals. Ordinary salt, ammonium sulfate, has a surprise—its pressure-driven phase transition promises refrigerators without Freon. The future of materials science is fine-tuning, not building from scratch.

🎯 The original model would have misjudged the transition temperature by tens of degrees, but five fine-tuning examples reduced the error to 60 K (compared to the experimental value of 219 K)—like a string tuned to within a quarter tone.

\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{\Delta S}{\Delta V}
The Clapeyron–Clausius equation for a first-order phase transition relates the slope of the equilibrium line to the ratio of entropy and volume jumps—showing how sharply the transition temperature shifts with pressure, like a tight string whose pitch changes with the tuning peg.
\Delta T_{ad} = -\frac{T}{C_p} \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial P} \right)_T \Delta P
Adiabatic temperature change under rapid loading: estimates how many degrees a crystal cools upon a sudden pressure drop—a key metric for a refrigerant.
