Dissipative Structures and Self-Oscillations in Magnets with Spin Crossover ✦

Original: "Dynamic dissipative structures in bistable magnetic ordered spin crossover systems: self-oscillations of magnetization"
· Yu. S. Orlov, N. N. Paklin, S. V. Nikolaev, E. I. Shneyder, V. A. Dudnikov
arXiv:2606.25506 · 2026-06-24 · CC BY · 3 min · Statistical Mech
A theoretical study predicts spontaneous self-oscillations of magnetization in bistable magnetic systems with spin crossover, analogous to the Belousov–Zhabotinsky reaction.
Abstract

Dissipative systems demonstrate a wide range of behavior — from complex chaos to the spontaneous formation of ordered structures, such as Bénard cells or more complex examples like the Belousov–Zhabotinsky reaction. Of particular interest for the formation of spatiotemporal dissipative structures are bistable systems with spin crossover. This work theoretically investigates the possibility of observing self-oscillations of magnetization in magnetically ordered systems with spin crossover. The results of calculations of nonlinear dynamics of bistable magnetic systems under nonequilibrium conditions are presented. It is shown that such systems can serve as a platform for studying self-organization in magnetic materials.

Context

Spin-crossover (SC) materials, in which transition metal ions switch between high-spin (HS) and low-spin (LS) states, have attracted scientists' attention for almost a century with their prospects for creating sensors, displays, and memory devices. However, their non-equilibrium dynamics, especially in magnetically ordered compounds, remain poorly studied. Meanwhile, systems far from equilibrium are capable of self-organization, forming dissipative structures—from Bénard cells to lasers and biological life. Understanding how entropic and thermodynamic factors, combined with numerical simulations, lead to the spontaneous emergence of order in magnetic SC systems may not only expand fundamental concepts but also suggest pathways to new types of nanoelectronic devices.

Methods

The approach is based on a lattice pseudospin model, where each site is assigned an operator that distinguishes HS and LS states. The Hamiltonian includes elastic interaction between ions and exchange interaction within the spin subsystem, enabling a unified description of both paramagnetic and antiferromagnetic SC compounds. Within the mean-field approximation, evolution equations for the HS-state population and magnetization are derived, based on the formalism of Roy Glauber and satisfying the principle of detailed balance. Additionally, a heat balance equation is introduced, accounting for coupling with a thermostat, photoheating by external radiation, and the release of latent heat of the phase transition. Analysis of stationary states and bifurcations is performed using methods from dynamical systems theory developed by Lev Landau and Ludwig Boltzmann for phase transitions and Boltzmann distribution.

Results

Two qualitatively different regimes have been discovered. When the elastic interionic interaction dominates, self-oscillations of population and temperature are possible only with constant external illumination providing photoheating. The second scenario occurs when exchange interaction dominates and elastic interaction is negligible. In this case, the system spontaneously, without external irradiation, exhibits stable self-oscillations of magnetization and HS-state population—an analog of autocatalytic reactions like the Belousov–Zhabotinsky type. Phase diagrams have been numerically constructed, showing regions of bistability, critical points, and lines of first- and second-order phase transitions. For parameters close to FeBO3, limit cycles are found: at a thermostat temperature of 75 K and a relaxation rate of about 4.5 s⁻¹, magnetization oscillations with an amplitude of about 1.3 μB per ion occur. This effect is directly related to the second law of thermodynamics and demonstrates how magnetic order generates feedback capable of driving the system into a regime of undamped oscillations.

Implications

The work shows for the first time that magnetization self-oscillations are possible in magnetically ordered SC systems without the need for an external periodic drive. This is a fundamentally new mechanism for generating magnetic oscillations, complementing known spin waves and stochastic resonance. The theoretical prediction prompts a targeted search for such materials and paves the way for non-volatile memory elements operating on a "write-erase" principle via a phase transition, and even neuromorphic computing networks where oscillations play the role of spikes.

Future development

The topic may develop towards experimental realization of the predicted regimes, primarily in FeBO3 single crystals, and also in a broader class of transition metal oxides. Extending the approaches to nanostructures is also of interest—individual molecules or clusters on surfaces, where intermolecular interactions can be tailored so that magnetization oscillations become coherent over macroscopic distances. Moreover, studying spatiotemporal dissipative structures promises to reveal analogies with out-of-equilibrium Bose-Einstein condensates and lasers, where the collective phase is maintained by constant pumping.

Impact

The results will impact condensed matter physics, nonlinear dynamics, and materials science.

Next steps

The next step is experimental verification of self-oscillations in a suitable SC magnet using magneto-optical and calorimetric measurements.

Key open problems

The phenomenon is directly related to the fundamental problem of self-organization in open systems and to understanding how macroscopic order emerges from microscopic reversibility under conditions far from equilibrium. It also resonates with unresolved questions in the dynamics of phase transitions under rapid heating or cooling, when a system crosses lines of bistability.
