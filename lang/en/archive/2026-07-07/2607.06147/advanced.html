Coupled Dark Matter and Early Dark Energy: Key to Cosmological Mysteries ✦

Original: "Imprint of swampland-inspired coupled early dark energy"
· Hao Wang, Yun-Song Piao
arXiv:2607.06147 · 2026-07-07 · CC BY · 3 min · Cosmology
A new study explores the coupling between dark matter and early dark energy to explain the discrepancy in measurements of the Hubble constant and the evolution of dark energy.
Abstract

Inspired by the Swampland Distance Conjecture, the authors explore cosmological implications of a fractional coupling between dark matter and early dark energy in light of new DESI DR2 baryon acoustic oscillation data. A conditional normalizing flow network was used for efficient high-dimensional parameter space sampling. A joint analysis of Planck CMB, DESI DR2 BAO, PantheonPlus supernovae, and SH0ES data was performed. It is found that the detailed structure of the early dark energy potential, beyond its mere existence, can alter cosmological constraints on late-time dark energy properties when the interaction between dark matter and early dark energy is considered.

Context

Modern cosmology is experiencing a crisis known as the Hubble tension: measurements of the Hubble constant, initiated by Edwin Hubble, from the cosmic microwave background (Planck) give about 67 km/s/Mpc, while those from supernovae (SH0ES), calibrated by Cepheids, obtained by Adam Riess and colleagues yield about 73 km/s/Mpc. These discrepancies, exceeding 5σ, demand new physics. One leading explanation is early dark energy (EDE)—a component that briefly influenced the expansion of the Universe before recombination, reducing the acoustic horizon. However, recent data on baryon acoustic oscillations (DESI DR2) additionally hint at a possible evolution of dark energy in the late Universe, further complicating the picture.

Methods

The authors applied cutting-edge machine learning methods—conditional normalizing flows—to explore the high-dimensional parameter space (up to 14 dimensions). This approach, unlike traditional Markov chain Monte Carlo methods, enables rapid and accurate posterior distribution construction while avoiding volume effects. They considered two EDE models: an axion-like one, where the scalar field settles at the bottom of the potential, and a model with an anti-de Sitter potential (AdS-EDE), where the field continues to evolve. Both models included an exponential coupling between the mass of dark matter and the EDE field, arising from the swampland conjecture (Swampland Distance Conjecture). The analysis combined data from Planck, DESI DR2 BAO, PantheonPlus, and SH0ES.

Results

In the axion-like EDE model, the coupling strength c was found to be close to zero within 1σ, consistent with previous works and indicating a disfavor of such coupling. Moreover, the coupling does not alleviate the S8 problem—the discrepancy in the amplitude of matter clustering. In the AdS-EDE model, on the contrary, the mean value of c is positive, and the fraction of coupled dark matter f* > 0 at the 1σ level. This is explained by the fact that in AdS-EDE, the field continues to shift at late times, allowing data to better constrain the coupling. Both models yield higher values of the Hubble constant H0 (around 72 km/s/Mpc) than standard ΛCDM, but the coupling slightly reduces H0, leading to an enhanced preference for evolving dark energy.

Implications

The results indicate that the details of the EDE potential, not just its presence, can radically change our conclusions about late dark energy. The coupling between dark matter and EDE, predicted by string theory, becomes not merely a theoretical curiosity but a potentially testable ingredient capable of reconciling the Hubble tension with DESI data. The work also sheds light on the fundamental nature of dark energy, whose accelerated expansion was first firmly established by Saul Perlmutter and others.

Future development

In the future, with data from upcoming surveys like Euclid and LSST, as well as more precise measurements of the cosmic microwave background (e.g., CMB-S4), we will be able to constrain the coupling parameters and EDE dynamics much better. Advances in normalizing flows will allow exploring even more complex models, including multi-component couplings and non-standard potentials.

Impact

This work will influence the development of models that unify quantum gravity and cosmology, as well as data analysis strategies in major collaborations.

Next steps

The next step will involve incorporating weak lensing and cosmic microwave background polarization data, as well as developing self-consistent models where the EDE scalar field simultaneously accounts for both dark matter and late dark energy.

Key open problems

The work is directly connected to the problems of the nature of dark energy and dark matter, as well as the Hubble tension, testing Hubble's law on different scales, while remaining within the framework of cosmology described by Friedmann equations.
