The Southern Ring of the Crab Nebula: a Counterjet Signature in the Supernova Explosion Mechanism ✦

Original: "Identifying a circum-jet southern ring counterpart to the northern jet of the Crab Nebula"
· Noam Soker
arXiv:2607.05963 · 2026-07-07 · CC BY 4.0 · 3 min · High Energy
A ring discovered in the Crab Nebula points to the existence of a counterjet, supporting the jittering-jet explosion mechanism of supernovae.
Abstract

Using new three-dimensional hydrodynamic simulations of the jittering-jets explosion mechanism (JJEM), images of the Crab Nebula — a core-collapse supernova remnant — were analyzed. A ring was found in the southern part, opposite the bright northern jet. It is interpreted as the trail of a counter-jet. Previously, a point-symmetric structure was noted in the nebula: seven pairs of 'bays' and two pairs of filaments, but no paired jets were found. JJEM simulations predict that jets participating in the explosion form such rings. Thus, the southern ring indicates the existence of a southern jet — paired with the northern one — supporting the jet-driven explosion model for the Crab Nebula.

Context

The origin of core-collapse supernovae is one of the central problems in astrophysics. Two competing mechanisms—the neutrino-driven and the jittering-jet (JJEM)—predict different remnant morphologies. The presence of point-symmetry in about twenty remnants, including the Crab Nebula, provides crucial evidence for JJEM, which involves multiple pairs of jets. However, direct identification of counterjets has been challenging, and the identification of the southern ring as a counterjet signature fills this gap.

Methods

To search for jet-related structures, multiwavelength images of the Crab Nebula were used: optical images from Hubble, infrared data from James Webb, and radio telescope observations. These observational data were compared with results from three-dimensional hydrodynamical simulations of JJEM, which reproduce the formation of rings and radial filaments by expanding jets. The models account for Kelvin–Helmholtz instabilities that lead to wavy structures.

Results

The analysis revealed a clear elliptical ring and radial filaments south of the northern jet. The axis ratio of about 2:1 indicates that the jet axis was inclined by 30° to the plane of the sky. This ring is interpreted as a signature of the counterjet, paired with the northern jet. The center of the jet pair coincides with the position of the pulsar — a neutron star that likely launched the jets after receiving a natal kick. The angle between the jet axis and the pulsar's proper motion direction is 54°, typical for post-kick pairs in JJEM. The observed structure forms as the ejecta interact with the surrounding interstellar medium inside the remnant.

Implications

Identifying the southern ring as a counterjet significantly strengthens the case for JJEM. It shows that the Crab Nebula supernova was exploded by jets, and the leftover pulsar acquired its velocity through asymmetric ejection. Multiwavelength observations, including data from JWST and Hubble, confirm simulation predictions that such rings are typical for late jets in JJEM.

Future development

Further deep observations along the southern ring's direction could reveal jet remnants beyond the main nebula. A systematic search for asymmetric ring-like structures in other supernova remnants will test the universality of the mechanism. Developing numerical models that account for inhomogeneities in the interstellar medium will improve predictions.

Impact

The results impact our understanding of massive star explosions, neutron star formation and velocities, and the interpretation of remnant morphology. Modeling turbulent jets finds applications in high-energy astrophysics.

Next steps

Next steps include hydrodynamical simulations with varying jet parameters and conducting spectroscopic observations of the southern ring to measure its kinematics. It is also important to search for similar jet pairs in other point-symmetric remnants.

Key open problems

This discovery is directly linked to the fundamental problem of the core-collapse supernova explosion mechanism. It also touches on the nature of pulsar kicks and the behavior of matter under extreme conditions during collapse, which remains an unsolved problem in astrophysics.
