Star Explosion: Twin Jet Leaves a Twin Ring ✦

Original: "Identifying a circum-jet southern ring counterpart to the northern jet of the Crab Nebula"
· Noam Soker
arXiv:2607.05963 · 2026-07-07 · CC BY 4.0 · 1 min
High Energy
A paired ring has been discovered in the Crab Nebula — evidence that the star exploded with two opposite jets.
Abstract

In the Crab Nebula, besides the northern jet, a ring was found in the south. It looks like the trail of a similar jet, only aimed the opposite way. So, during the supernova explosion, two jets were at work, creating a symmetrical pattern — like a cosmic butterfly.

1

In 1054, astronomers spotted a new star — a supernova explosion, whose remnant we call the Crab Nebula. At its center lies a neutron star, a city-sized core spinning wildly (a pulsar). For many years, we only saw one jet — a thin stream of gas blasting out at nearly light speed. But Hubble, Webb, and radio telescopes revealed a second ring: an identical jet burst out from the opposite side and, slamming into the surrounding gas, left its mark.

The find confirms that the blast wasn't spherical but followed a 'jittering jet' scenario from computer models. It's not a round firework burst but more like a popped paint balloon: the stuff was ejected in two jets going opposite ways. That's why the nebula has such a lace-like pattern. And the recoil from the jets gave the pulsar tremendous speed — like a paint balloon, shooting out jets, also flies in the opposite direction.

The term 'supernova' was coined by Fritz Zwicky. Later, Jocelyn Bell Burnell discovered a pulsar at the nebula's heart, proving the existence of neutron stars.

Now astronomers know what to look for and will check other supernova remnants for paired rings.

🎯 The 1054 explosion was so bright it was visible during the day for an entire month, and at night it lit up the ground — historians find records of it from Chinese and Arab astronomers.
