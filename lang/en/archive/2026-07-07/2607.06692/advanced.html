Multi-channel hunt for the Big Bang's echo: lenses, gas, and X-rays versus primordial non-Gaussianity ✦

Original: "Primordial Physics in the Nonlinear Universe: Towards particle constraints using the Weak lensing, Thermal SZ, and X-ray fields"
· Dhayaa Anbajagane
arXiv:2607.06692 · 2026-07-07 · CC BY 4.0 · 4 min · Cosmology
Joint analysis of weak lensing maps, the Sunyaev-Zeldovich effect, and X-ray emission improves constraints on inflationary model parameters by a factor of two.
Abstract

Primordial non-Gaussianities (PNG) link the initial density field to the particle physics of the early Universe. Using a new class of simulations, the impact of PNG on nonlinear structure formation is investigated, and the constraining power of second- and third-moment statistics of weak lensing, thermal Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect (tSZ), and X-ray emission fields is assessed. Semi-analytical baryon models are applied, consistently incorporating astrophysical effects, along with background mitigation methods that account for explicit component correlations. It is shown that tSZ and X-ray data contain significant PNG information and enable self-calibration of nuisance parameters, breaking degeneracies. The joint analysis yields a twofold improvement in constraints over lensing alone; further gains are expected when more scales and other statistics are included. Code available: https://github.com/DhayaaAnbajagane/Vaanam.

Context

Primordial non-Gaussianity is a deviation of the initial density field's statistics from a Gaussian distribution. It arises in various inflation scenarios and is sensitive to particle physics at energies around 10^15 GeV, making it a unique window into fundamental theory. Traditional methods using cosmic microwave background and galaxy clustering have already achieved high precision, but they make little use of nonlinear structures. Meanwhile, massive galaxy clusters are especially sensitive to the non-Gaussian tails of the density distribution. Thanks to the expansion of the universe, described by Hubble's law, new sky surveys like the Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST) at the Vera Rubin Observatory, the Simons Observatory, and eROSITA will for the first time study these structures using three observables at once: weak gravitational lensing, thermal Sunyaev-Zeldovich effect, and X-ray emission.

Methods

The authors built synthetic sky maps using N-body simulations from the Ulagam suite, where the initial conditions were modified to reproduce six different models of primordial non-Gaussianity (local, equilateral, quasi-single field, and others). Using numerical simulation and the semi-analytic BaryonForge model, they accounted for the effects of dark matter and gas on the observables: weak lensing was modeled via the density integral along the line of sight, clusters produced a microwave signal through photon scattering off hot electrons, and the X-ray flux was calculated from thermal emission of the gas taking metallicity into account. Realistic noise components and correlated background sources, including active galactic nuclei, were also added. To compress the information, second- and third-order moments of the smoothed fields were computed, and then the Fisher method was used to estimate the potential accuracy of future measurements.

Results

The analysis showed that the weak lensing data from LSST (14,000 sq. degrees) alone can provide constraints on the non-Gaussianity amplitude f_NL comparable to the best results from the Planck satellite, and for some models—twice as good. However, the need to simultaneously fit cosmology and baryonic physics parameters reduces accuracy by about 2–3 times. Maps of the Sunyaev-Zeldovich effect and X-ray emission individually give weaker constraints, but using them jointly with lensing breaks degeneracies between parameters. As a result, combining all three fields provides a 2-fold improvement in precision compared to lensing alone, even after marginalizing over astrophysical uncertainties. A particularly strong improvement comes from cross-calibrating the gas ejection radius around clusters. Important contributions came from pioneers: Fritz Zwicky first pointed out the role of dark matter in clusters, Rashid Sunyaev predicted the scattering of CMB photons off hot gas, and Alan Guth laid the foundations of inflationary theory.

Implications

The results demonstrate that future surveys can turn constraining primordial non-Gaussianity into a multi-instrumental challenge, much like today's combination of cosmic microwave background, supernova, and baryon acoustic oscillation data for the standard cosmological model. This paves the way for detailed testing of inflation scenarios involving multiple fields and heavy particles that were previously nearly inaccessible. Moreover, joint analysis automatically reduces systematic errors associated with accounting for ordinary baryonic matter.

Future development

In the coming years, precision will improve with higher resolution maps (NSIDE 4096) and the inclusion of fourth-order moments and topological statistics. Additional observables such as the kinematic Sunyaev-Zeldovich effect and fast radio bursts will help pin down the baryonic physics model even more tightly. New simulations with better resolution will refine the contribution of low-mass clusters, and machine learning will efficiently extract information from the full variety of maps.

Impact

The work will impact cosmology, hot gas astrophysics, and particle physics, linking the observed structure of the universe to processes at Planck-scale energies.

Next steps

Next steps include applying the developed pipeline to real data from DES, DECADE, and eROSITA DR1, as well as integrating it into the analysis of future projects like SPHEREx and Athena.

Key open problems

The research directly addresses the problem of the nature of inflation and physics beyond the Standard Model, including mass hierarchy and dark energy, since the shape of non-Gaussianity may point to new fields and interactions in the early universe.
