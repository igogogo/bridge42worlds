The Bubble That Could Change the World ✦

Original: "Evidence for renormalized instantons in real-time simulations of vacuum decay"
· Emilie Hertig, Alexander C. Jenkins, Hiranya V. Peiris, Andrew Pontzen, Matthew C. Johnson
arXiv:2607.06680 · 2026-07-07 · CC BY · 1 min
HEP Theory Cosmology General Relativity
Physicists have found a way to reconcile computer simulations with the theory of how our Universe could suddenly transition into a new state.
Abstract

Imagine our universe as a supercooled liquid—it might suddenly 'freeze' into a new state. Physicists simulated this decay and found it didn't align with old theories. But adding quantum corrections straightened everything out, linking the math to the simulations. It’s a step toward testing quantum decay in the lab. Will we one day witness the birth of a new universe?

1

The Universe is permeated by quantum fields—invisible substances resembling a fluid. One such field can get trapped in a 'false vacuum,' like soda in a sealed bottle, ready to release bubbles at any moment. A random quantum event gives birth to a bubble of true vacuum, whose wall races at nearly the speed of light, wiping out the old world without warning.

The theory behind such processes was laid by Alan Guth and Andrei Linde in the context of inflation—the ultra-fast expansion of the early Universe. However, computer simulations long showed bubbles appearing more often than the equations predicted. The solution lies in feedback: the field inside the bubble changes the environment, much like how in soda the liquid's composition changes as gas escapes. Accounting for this effect reconciled theory and computations.

The agreement allows us to better assess the stability of the Higgs boson within the Standard Model, search for gravitational waves from ancient transitions, and analyze the cosmic microwave background. Most surprising: experiments with ultracold atoms (Bose-Einstein condensates) will soon create artificial 'cosmic' bubbles in the lab—to study a force capable of erasing reality.

🎯 If the Higgs vacuum were unstable, its decay could start anywhere, and we'd get no warning: light signals can't outrun the expanding bubble.

🎬 The idea of vacuum decay inspired Geoffrey Landis's story 'Vacuum States' and is reflected in the movie 'Interstellar'.

Equation for the bubble profile φ_b in Euclidean space: x is the radial coordinate, V' is the derivative of the potential, determining how the field 'rolls' from the false vacuum to the true one.
B = S_E[\phi_b(x)] - S_E[\phi_{\text{FV}}]
Exponent factor in the decay probability: B is the difference in Euclidean actions between the bubble configuration and the plain false vacuum, dictating how suppressed the process is.
