Abstract

The James Webb Telescope's sharper look at supernova 1987A has confirmed a neutron star hiding in its core. Narrow emission lines from ionized argon, sulfur, calcium, and chlorine (gas glowing under the star's radiation) are blueshifted, revealing a 'kick' at birth: the star is hurtling away at ~510 km/s. Dust in the shell scatters short wavelengths, like frosted glass—so part of the spectrum is still hidden. As the dust clears, new lines will emerge, helping to decide between a pulsar wind nebula and a cooling neutron star model.
