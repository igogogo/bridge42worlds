Spin is like a tiny top that every particle has, but it doesn't actually spin. It's just an intrinsic property, similar to rotation, but without motion.
💡 An electron behaves as if it's spinning, but it really isn't – it's just its inherent property, like eye color.
Spin is the intrinsic angular momentum of a particle, similar to how Earth rotates about its axis and possesses angular momentum. But unlike Earth, spin is quantized and not related to actual rotation in space.

History

The idea of spin arose in the 1920s, when Otto Stern and Walther Gerlach discovered the splitting of a beam of silver atoms in a magnetic field. Later, Wolfgang Pauli and Paul Dirac developed its theoretical description.

How it works

Spin manifests in magnetic properties: for example, in permanent magnets, electrons align their spins in one direction, creating a net magnetic field. It's like many tiny magnets oriented the same way.

💡 Thanks to spin, magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) works: hydrogen nuclei in our body have spin and, placed in a magnetic field, emit signals that the scanner reads.
Spin is a fundamental quantum number characterizing the intrinsic angular momentum of a particle. Unlike orbital angular momentum, spin is not associated with motion in space but is an internal property described by the SU(2) algebra. For fermions (electrons, protons), spin takes half-integer values (e.g., 1/2), for bosons (photons) – integer (0, 1). Spin determines particle statistics and their behavior in a magnetic field via the magnetic moment. Measurement of a spin component always yields discrete values, and its state is described by spinors.

History

The experimental discovery of spin is linked to the Stern–Gerlach experiment (1922), where a beam of silver atoms split into two components in an inhomogeneous magnetic field, contradicting classical physics. The theoretical explanation was given by George Uhlenbeck and Samuel Goudsmit in 1925, proposing an electron’s own momentum. Later, Pauli introduced spin matrices, and Dirac in 1928 naturally incorporated spin into the relativistic equation for the electron. Subsequently, the concept of spin became key for all quantum field theory and particle physics.

How it works

Spin is described by operators obeying the commutation relations [Ŝ_i, Ŝ_j] = iħ ε_{ijk} Ŝ_k. For spin-1/2 particles, the Pauli representation with matrices σ_x, σ_y, σ_z is often used. The Hamiltonian for interaction with a magnetic field: H = –μ·B, where the magnetic moment μ = g (e/2m) S. Quantum spin correlations (entanglement) are tested in Bell's inequalities, demonstrating the nonlocality of quantum mechanics. Spin is also responsible for the fine structure of spectra and plays a central role in spin electronics (spintronics).

|\mathbf{S}| = \sqrt{s(s+1)} \hbar
|S| is the magnitude of spin angular momentum; s is the spin quantum number (for an electron s = 1/2); ħ is the reduced Planck constant, ħ = h/(2π) ≈ 1.054571817×10⁻³⁴ J·s.
