Vector Calculus

📊 0 articles
This is the mathematical language for describing flows, vortices, and fields, for example, how water flows in a river or how air swirls in a tornado.
💡 Vector calculus helped engineers create the first computed tomography (CT) scan, which 'sees' the human body using the mathematics of fields.
Vector calculus is a branch of mathematics that studies vector fields, such as wind speed or Earth's magnetic field. It allows calculating how much 'substance' passes through a surface (flux) or how strongly a field swirls (curl). For example, it can be used to calculate how much water flows through a dam per second.

History

The foundations of vector calculus were laid by James Clerk Maxwell and Oliver Heaviside in the 19th century to describe electromagnetism. Maxwell used it for his famous equations, which unified electricity and magnetism.

How it works

Imagine you are standing in a wind field: at each point, the wind blows with a certain strength and direction. Vector calculus provides tools to measure how the wind 'diverges' from you (divergence) or how it 'swirls' around you (curl). It is like looking at a whirlpool: you can understand where water flows in and out.

💡 Without vector calculus, modern GPS systems would be impossible, as they use calculations of Earth's gravitational field.
Vector calculus is a branch of mathematics that studies vector fields and scalar fields, and operations on them: gradient, divergence, curl, and Laplacian. It provides a language for formulating physical laws, such as Maxwell's equations in electrodynamics and the Navier-Stokes equations in fluid dynamics. The main theorems are the Gauss-Ostrogradsky theorem (relating the flux of a field through a closed surface to the divergence inside the volume) and Stokes' theorem (relating the circulation of a field around a contour to the curl through a surface). Vector calculus is indispensable in continuum physics, field theory, and differential geometry.

History

Vector calculus developed in the 19th century thanks to the work of James Clerk Maxwell, who in the 1860s used quaternions to formulate electromagnetism. Later, Oliver Heaviside and Josiah Willard Gibbs simplified the notation, introducing modern vector operations. Maxwell, being one of the key scientists, laid the foundations, while Gibbs and Heaviside popularized vector calculus as an independent discipline.

How it works

Vector calculus operates with vector fields F(x,y,z), where a vector is assigned to each point in space. The gradient of a scalar field f indicates the direction of greatest increase of f. Divergence ∇·F measures how much the field 'diverges' from a point (sources/sinks). Curl ∇×F measures the 'swirl' of the field. These operations are related by integral theorems: the flux through a closed surface equals the integral of divergence over the volume (Gauss' theorem), and the circulation around a closed contour equals the flux of curl through the surface (Stokes' theorem).

\oint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \int_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}) \, dV
The flux of a vector field F through a closed surface S equals the integral of the divergence of the field over the volume V bounded by that surface.
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S}
The circulation of a vector field F around a closed contour C equals the flux of the curl of the field through the surface S spanned by that contour.
