Qubit — a tossed coin: in flight it is simultaneously heads and tails. Unlike an ordinary bit, which always lies strictly with one side up.

In practice: Qubits enable quantum computers to solve in seconds problems that would take conventional supercomputers billions of years: for example, modeling new drugs or cracking cryptographic codes.
Qubit — the elementary carrier of quantum information. Unlike a bit, rigidly fixed at 0 or 1, a qubit realizes superposition — a mathematical combination of two states described by complex amplitudes. Measurement collapses the superposition into one of the basis states with a probability equal to the squared modulus of the amplitude.

History

The term 'qubit' was proposed by physicist Benjamin Schumacher in 1995, inspired by work on quantum computing. The very idea of quantum information was developed by Richard Feynman and David Deutsch since the 1980s.

How it works

Any interaction with a qubit is compared to a hand touching a spinning top: it immediately changes its rotation. In the quantum world, qubit control is designed so that they 'feel' each other without direct contact — thanks to entanglement (a special connection at a distance).

💡 Even the simplest operation on a qubit is mathematically equivalent to rotating the Bloch sphere — an imaginary three-dimensional sphere on whose surface all possible states live.
A qubit is like a ballerina spinning on pointe shoes. While the dance lasts, her pose continuously changes between classical positions. Stopping the dance means forcing her to freeze in one pose, similarly measuring a qubit yields either 0 or 1. But before measurement, the qubit exists in quantum superposition (a blend of states 0 and 1 simultaneously).

Qubit — a quantum system with a Hilbert space of dimension 2. Mathematically described by a state vector |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ in C², normalized by ⟨ψ|ψ⟩ = 1. Physically realized, for example, by two levels of a superconducting circuit, electron spin, or photon polarization. Superposition and entanglement of multiple qubits lead to exponential growth of the computational space.

History

The predecessor of the qubit was the concept of the Feynman quantum computer (1982). David Deutsch formalized the concept of a universal quantum computer, and the term 'qubit' was introduced by Benjamin Schumacher in 1995, generalizing the idea of a quantum bit for information theory. John Archibald Wheeler earlier proposed the doctrine 'it from bit' about the fundamental role of information, which resonates with the modern view of the qubit as the basic informational atom of the universe.

How it works

The evolution of a qubit is unitary and reversible: operators transform the state through rotations in state space. Measurement breaks unitarity — the superposition collapses into one of the eigenvectors of the measured observable with probability determined by Born's rule. Physically, qubit control is achieved with resonant microwave pulses (for superconducting systems) or laser fields (for ions) implementing rotations by the required angle.

|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle
|\psi\rangle — qubit state vector; |0\rangle, |1\rangle — orthonormal basis vectors; \alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{C} — probability amplitudes; |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1 — normalization condition, where |\alpha|^2 is the probability of obtaining state |0\rangle upon measurement in the computational basis, |\beta|^2 is the probability of obtaining |1\rangle.

Open problems & fun facts

Decoherence — loss of quantum properties due to interaction with the environment; Qubit lifetime (coherence time) remains below the threshold for scalable computing; Difficulty of accurate state readout during measurement without destroying information
