Hubble Space Telescope

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
12

An orbiting telescope above the atmosphere turned hazy spots into clusters of distant galaxies and showed how the Universe is expanding.

In practice: Thanks to Hubble images, the age of the Universe has been refined to 13.8 billion years, and water and methane were directly detected for the first time in the atmospheres of distant exoplanets.
The Hubble Space Telescope is an 11-ton reflector in orbit at an altitude of about 540 km. Its Ritchey–Chrétien optical design provides ideal image quality in ultraviolet and visible light. Thanks to the absence of atmosphere, it achieves a resolution of 0.05 arcseconds — equivalent to the ability to discern a coin from 100 kilometers away. Main instruments: WFC3 camera, COS spectrograph, ACS camera, and others.

History

The idea of a space telescope was voiced as early as the 1940s, but Hubble was launched in 1990 on the shuttle Discovery. Named after Edwin Hubble, who proved the expansion of the Universe. Right after launch, it was discovered that the primary mirror had a defect, and the first repair mission in 1993 installed corrective optics, 'putting glasses' on the telescope.

How it works

Hubble works like a giant bucket collecting raindrops: its concave 2.4-meter mirror captures the faint light of distant stars and directs it to electronic detectors. The cameras accumulate photons for hours, just as water drop by drop fills a bucket.

💡 Hubble does not see stars twinkling — on Earth, twinkling is caused by atmospheric turbulence, but in orbit the air does not interfere, and images are crystal clear.
Hubble is a real observatory launched into Earth orbit (altitude about 540 km). It photographs stars and splits their light into spectra — from ultraviolet to infrared. Because there is no atmosphere above it, its images are tens of times sharper than ground-based telescopes of the 20th century could achieve.

The Hubble Space Telescope (HST) is an automated observatory in a circular orbit at an altitude of ~540 km with an orbital period of ~95 minutes. The optical system is a Ritchey–Chrétien design with aplanatic correction for coma and astigmatism; the primary mirror is 2.4 m in diameter, made of glass with a low expansion coefficient. The mirror coating is aluminum with magnesium fluoride, optimized for the ultraviolet region. Photometric and spectroscopic channels cover wavelengths from 115 nm (far ultraviolet) to 2.4 μm (near infrared). The three-axis pointing system uses star trackers and gyroscopes, with a stabilization precision of 0.007″. Power is supplied by solar panels and six nickel-hydrogen batteries.

History

The Large Space Telescope project was developed since the 1960s. The launch was repeatedly postponed, including due to the Challenger shuttle disaster in 1986. On April 24, 1990, Discovery placed the telescope into orbit, but it was discovered that the primary mirror suffered from spherical aberration — a shape error. The first repair mission (STS-61, 1993) installed the COSTAR unit with tiny corrective mirrors and replaced the camera, revitalizing the project. Subsequent missions added ever more sophisticated instruments: the ACS camera (2002), the COS spectrograph and WFC3 camera (2009). From the outset, the scientific inspiration behind the project was Lyman Spitzer, and the name of Edwin Hubble recalls his epoch-making discovery of the expansion of the Universe. It was with Hubble that Adam Riess and his colleagues discovered that this expansion is accelerating.

How it works

HST uses a Ritchey–Chrétien optical system with an effective focal length of 57.6 m (f/24). Light is collected by the primary mirror, reflected to the secondary hyperbolic mirror, and then through the central hole in the primary projected onto the focal plane, where instruments are located. Cameras use CCD sensors; spectrographs use diffraction gratings. Fine pointing is achieved by the Fine Guidance Sensors, which use 14th-magnitude stars. The onboard computer executes observation scripts; data transmission occurs via radio link through TDRS satellites.

\theta = \frac{1.22 \lambda}{D}
θ is the minimum angular size in radians, λ is the wavelength of observed light, D is the diameter of the aperture (primary mirror) of the telescope. For HST: D=2.4 m, at a wavelength of 500 nm θ≈0.05 arcseconds.

Open problems & fun facts

Gradual degradation of gyroscopes limits maneuverability — HST now operates in single-gyro mode.; The end of servicing missions due to the cessation of shuttle flights means no repair or upgrade capability.; HST does not cover the mid-infrared range, so it cannot see very distant (redshift > 10) galaxies, limiting the study of the early Universe.
