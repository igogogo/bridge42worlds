Hubble Space Telescope

📊 12 articles
The Hubble Space Telescope is like a huge eye in space that looks at stars without interference from clouds and air. It orbits the Earth and takes pictures of distant galaxies, beautiful nebulae, and even shows how stars are born and die. It's like a spyglass raised high into the sky to see the most incredible things.

History

The telescope is named after Edwin Hubble, who proved the expansion of the universe. It was launched in 1990 by the Space Shuttle Discovery. Immediately after launch, it turned out that the mirror had a defect, and the first images were blurry. In 1993, astronauts installed corrective optics, and Hubble began working at full capacity. Since then, it has been repaired and upgraded several times.

How it works

Hubble works like a giant reflecting camera: light falls on the primary mirror, reflects to the secondary, and then goes to scientific instruments — spectrographs and cameras. It can stare at one patch of sky for hours, accumulating light from faint objects. Data is transmitted to Earth by radio, and it is controlled from the Goddard Space Flight Center.

💡 Hubble takes such sharp pictures that if it were looking at Earth, it could see a firefly at a distance of 10,000 kilometers! And it also showed that our universe is expanding faster than we thought.
Imagine you're photographing the night sky through a dirty window. The Hubble Space Telescope is a camera placed outside the atmosphere, where the air is clear and doesn't shimmer. It orbits at an altitude of about 540 km, and its 2.4-meter mirror collects light from incredibly distant objects. Thanks to it, we have seen the birth of stars in nebulae, galaxy collisions, and traces of black holes.

The Hubble Space Telescope (HST) is an automated observatory in low Earth orbit, operating in the visible, ultraviolet, and near-infrared ranges. It is equipped with a Ritchey-Chrétien reflector with a 2.4 m primary mirror and a set of interchangeable instruments: the Wide Field Camera 3 (WFC3), the spectrographs STIS and COS, and the Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS). The absence of atmospheric distortions provides an angular resolution of about 0.05 arcseconds. Major scientific results: refining the Hubble constant, discovering the accelerated expansion of the universe, the Hubble Deep Field images, observations of exoplanet atmospheres, confirming the presence of supermassive black holes at the centers of galaxies, and studying star formation processes.

History

The concept of a space telescope was proposed by Lyman Spitzer in 1946. The project was approved in the 1970s, and construction began in 1979. Launch took place on April 24, 1990, aboard the Space Shuttle Discovery (mission STS-31). After the discovery of spherical aberration in the mirror, the STS-61 mission in 1993 installed the COSTAR corrective system. Subsequent servicing missions (1997, 1999, 2002, 2009) replaced gyroscopes, batteries, and installed new instruments. In 2009, the WFC3 camera was installed and the STIS spectrograph was repaired. Hubble continues to operate, but without the possibility of servicing. Its scientific legacy has underlain many discoveries, including dark energy.

How it works

The telescope uses a Ritchey-Chrétien optical design with hyperbolic mirrors to minimize coma and spherical aberration. Light is collected by the primary mirror, reflected to the secondary, and through a hole in the primary reaches the focal plane, where interchangeable instruments are located. Precision pointing is provided by gyroscopes and star trackers. A cooling system maintains instrument temperatures. Spectrographs split light into a spectrum for analyzing chemical composition and velocities via the Doppler effect. Wide-field cameras take direct images. Data is encoded and transmitted via S-band through TDRSS satellites. Processing includes removing artifacts and photometric calibration. The Hubble law, relating the recession velocity of galaxies to distance, was refined using observations of Cepheids with Hubble.
