graphene

📊 0 articles

A carbon film one atom thick—like honeycomb, but flat. Stronger than steel, conducts electricity without losses. The basis of flexible electronics and ultrafast transistors.

In practice: Flexible displays, ultrafast transistors, reinforced composites, high-capacity batteries.
Graphene is a two-dimensional form of carbon, where atoms are bonded in hexagons, like a beehive honeycomb. Such a lattice makes it both stiff and flexible. Electrons in it behave like massless particles, providing exceptionally high mobility (over 200,000 cm²/V·s) and the ability to move without scattering over large distances.
💡 A sheet of graphene the size of a football field would weigh less than a cat's whisker.
Graphene is a plane of carbon atoms bonded in hexagons, like a wire mesh. It is only one atom thick, yet it is hundreds of times stronger than steel. Electrons in it race as if they had no mass, making it a conductor better than any metal.

History

In 2004, Andre Geim and Konstantin Novoselov obtained graphene by peeling layers of graphite with ordinary Scotch tape. Discovering a film one atom thick, they made a breakthrough that earned the Nobel Prize.

How it works

Carbon atoms in graphene are tightly bound into a flexible network. Free electrons zip through it with minimal hindrance, like a beam of light. Such behavior is usually characteristic of particles at near-light speeds, but in graphene it appears already under ordinary conditions.

💡 Graphene one atom thick is impermeable even to helium atoms—the smallest particles of inert gas. Yet it lets water through, acting as a molecular sieve.
Graphene is a monolayer of graphite, a flat hexagonal lattice of carbon atoms with sp²-bonds. Each atom contributes one electron to the π-system, forming an electronic spectrum that near the Dirac points is linear: E = ħ v_F |k|. Therefore, electrons behave as massless fermions. Such a band structure (a semimetal with no bandgap) explains the extremely high mobility, as well as the quantum Hall effect and ballistic transport at room temperature. The mechanical strength (Young's modulus of about 1 TPa) is due to the strength of covalent bonds; graphene is impermeable to any gases, including helium. Thermal conductivity (up to 5000 W/(m·K)) is carried by phonons. Films are transparent (absorbing only about 2.3% of visible light) and flexible.

History

Theoretical studies of graphite layers began with P.R. Wallace in 1947. The term 'graphene' was introduced in 1987. The breakthrough occurred in 2004, when Andre Geim and Konstantin Novoselov, using Scotch tape, exfoliated a monolayer of graphite, proving the stability of two-dimensional crystals. In 2010 they received the Nobel Prize in Physics. This discovery triggered a wave of studies of other two-dimensional materials.

How it works

The electronic properties of graphene are determined by the symmetry of its lattice and the tight-binding approximation. Near the vertices of the Brillouin zone, the dispersion is linear: E = ħ v_F |k|, where v_F is about 10⁶ m/s. The chirality of the carriers (pseudospin rigidly linked to the direction of motion) suppresses backscattering, ensuring ballistic transport. Mechanical strength is provided by the high energy of sp²-bonds. By applying a voltage, one can change the type of conductivity (electrons or holes), so graphene is an ambipolar semimetal. The gapless band structure hinders application in digital logic.
