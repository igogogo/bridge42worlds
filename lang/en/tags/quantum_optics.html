quantum optics

25

Light can be broken down into individual bricks — photons — and each can be controlled like a grain of sand.

In practice: Secure laser communication lines where eavesdropping changes the photon's state and is immediately detected.
Quantum optics is a branch of physics that studies the interaction of light and matter in a regime where quantum effects become decisive. Unlike classical optics, where light is described by electromagnetic waves, here it is considered as a stream of photons. Experiments achieve control at the level of single quanta, demonstrating phenomena such as photon antibunching (photons avoid arriving in pairs) and squeezed light (suppressed quantum noise in one of the characteristics). In practice, this leads to quantum cryptography and high-precision interferometers.

History

It all began with the invention of the laser in 1960 by Charles Townes and his colleagues. The laser provided a pure stream of identical photons, which made it possible to conduct experiments with single particles. Later, Alain Aspect in the 1980s demonstrated the reality of quantum entanglement of photons.

How it works

Imagine shooting single photon bullets from a light gun. Upon hitting a special crystal, one bullet sometimes splits into two smaller entangled ones. If you send one to a friend and keep the other, then by measuring yours, you instantly know what your friend received — even without a phone call. This is how quantum communication works.

💡 There exists light 'quieter' than absolute darkness — squeezed vacuum, in which quantum fluctuations of the field can be smaller than in empty space.
In everyday life, light behaves like a wave: it bends around obstacles, creates rainbows. But if you take very weak light, it breaks down into portions — photons (light particles). Quantum optics studies how to control these individual particles and make them interact without intermediaries, creating, for example, pairs of entangled photons (linked so that a change in one instantly affects the other). It's like two jugglers who always catch each other's clubs, even if they are in different rooms.

Quantum optics is a fundamental discipline at the intersection of quantum electrodynamics and optics, describing the quantization of the electromagnetic field. Basic field states are Fock states |n⟩ with a definite number of photons n. Single-photon sources (e.g., quantum dots) enable the generation of single-photon pulses. Nonlinear crystals in the process of spontaneous parametric down-conversion produce pairs of entangled photons. Measurements with single-photon detectors allow the study of fundamental quantum phenomena, including violation of Bell inequalities, quantum teleportation, and Hong-Ou-Mandel interference. Important tools include beam splitters, phase plates, and homodyne detectors for measuring field quadratures. Quantum optics underlies technologies for quantum communication and quantum computing on photonic chips.

History

Einstein's light quantum hypothesis (1905) laid the foundation, but only with the creation of the laser (Townes, 1960) did a tool for precision quantum experiments appear. Roy Glauber developed the quantum theory of coherence in 1963. Alain Aspect's experiments (1981–1982) with entangled photons confirmed the nonlocality of quantum mechanics. Since the 1990s, single-photon sources and quantum cryptography have rapidly developed.

How it works

Interaction of an intense laser beam with a nonlinear crystal (e.g., beta-barium borate) leads to spontaneous parametric down-conversion: a pump photon with frequency ω_p decays into two entangled photons, signal and idler, with ω_s + ω_i = ω_p. Entanglement manifests in the correlation of polarizations or emission directions. This state is described by a vector in Hilbert space and obeys the superposition principle. Measurement on one photon projects the pair into a definite state according to the reduction postulate. Antibunching is tested in a Hanbury Brown–Twiss interferometer: the correlation function g^(2)(0) < 1 proves the quantum nature of the source.
