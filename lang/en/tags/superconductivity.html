Superconductivity

📊 0 articles
PopularSimpleAdvanced
A phenomenon in which the electrical resistance of some materials drops to zero when cooled below a certain critical temperature. Current in a superconductor can flow forever without loss. Imagine a train moving without friction—electrons move without resistance.
💡 Superconducting magnets are used in MRI scanners and the Large Hadron Collider, creating fields hundreds of thousands of times stronger than Earth's magnetic field.
A phenomenon in which the electrical resistance of some materials drops to zero when cooled below a certain critical temperature. Current in a superconductor can flow forever without loss. Imagine a train moving without friction—electrons move without resistance.

History

Superconductivity was discovered by Heike Kamerlingh Onnes in 1911, cooling mercury to 4 K. The theoretical explanation (BCS theory) was given by John Bardeen, Leon Cooper, and Robert Schrieffer in 1957. In 1986, high-temperature superconductors operating above 77 K were discovered.

How it works

At low temperatures, electrons form Cooper pairs that do not scatter off the lattice, providing zero resistance. Analogy: dancers on ice moving in pairs do not trip over bumps. Superconductors also expel magnetic fields (Meissner effect), allowing magnets to levitate.
