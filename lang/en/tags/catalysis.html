catalysis

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

Reaction accelerator that is not consumed itself — like a matchmaker bringing molecules together to create new bonds.

In practice: Catalytic converters in cars transform toxic exhaust gases into safe nitrogen and water.
Catalysis is the increase in the rate of a chemical reaction under the action of a substance that is not part of the final products. The catalyst lowers the activation energy (energy barrier), opening an alternative reaction pathway. It can be homogeneous (in the same phase as the reactants) or heterogeneous (usually a solid in a liquid or gaseous medium).

History

The word 'catalysis' was proposed by Jöns Berzelius in 1836, observing the acceleration of reactions in the presence of certain substances.

How it works

The catalyst offers molecules a new route where less push energy (activation energy) is needed to start the reaction. Molecules dock on its surface, weaken their bonds, and transform into products faster.

💡 The same catalyst can accelerate both the forward and reverse reactions — it does not shift the equilibrium, only helps to reach it faster.
Catalysis is when a special substance (catalyst) helps a chemical reaction proceed faster or at a lower temperature, but is itself not consumed. Like a bridge connecting two shores, it provides a path with less effort.

Catalysis is a process underlying most industrial chemical technologies and biological systems. The mechanism of heterogeneous catalysis includes adsorption of reactants on the active sites of the catalyst surface, their chemical activation (weakening of intramolecular bonds), migration, reaction, and desorption of products. Homogeneous catalysis is often realized through the formation of an intermediate complex of the catalyst with the substrate, which then decomposes releasing the product and regenerating the catalyst. Enzymatic catalysis in living organisms is distinguished by exceptional selectivity and regulability. The rate of a catalytic reaction usually follows the Arrhenius equation with a reduced effective activation energy. The most important indicators of a catalyst are activity (TON, turnover frequency), selectivity, and stability (service life). Modern directions: nanocatalysis, photocatalysis, electrocatalysis for water splitting.

History

The term 'catalysis' was introduced by Swedish chemist Jöns Jacob Berzelius in 1836 to describe reactions accelerated in the presence of substances that themselves do not change. In 1895, Wilhelm Ostwald gave a quantitative definition of a catalyst as a substance that accelerates a reaction but does not shift equilibrium. In the early 20th century, Paul Sabatier discovered hydrogenation on metallic nickel, for which he received the Nobel Prize. Fritz Haber and Carl Bosch developed catalytic synthesis of ammonia, revolutionizing agriculture. Further development of enzymatic catalysis is associated with the names of Michaelis, Menten, Briggs and Haldane. Today, catalysis permeates petrochemistry, energy, and environmental protection.

How it works

The catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. In heterogeneous catalysis, chemisorption plays a decisive role — the formation of chemical bonds between the reactants and the active sites on the surface. This weakens certain interatomic bonds in the reactant molecules, facilitating their breaking. Then the adsorbed particles interact, forming products that desorb, freeing the sites. For example, in ammonia synthesis on an iron catalyst, N₂ and H₂ molecules dissociate into atoms, which requires high energy without a catalyst. The reaction rate is often described by the Arrhenius equation: k = A exp(-Ea/RT), where Ea is the activation energy. The catalyst lowers Ea. In enzymes, an additional role is played by the complementarity of the active site to the substrate (lock-and-key model) and stabilization of the transition state.

k = A e^{-\frac{E_a}{RT}}
k — reaction rate constant, A — pre-exponential factor (collision frequency), E_a — activation energy (minimum energy required for reactants to start the reaction), R — universal gas constant (R ≈ 8.314 J/(mol·K)), T — absolute temperature (in Kelvin)
