Bose-Einstein condensate

📊 3 articles
Imagine a flock of birds that suddenly starts moving as a single whole — each bird repeats the movement of its neighbor, and the flock turns into a giant wave. A Bose-Einstein condensate is a 'flock' of atoms cooled to unimaginably low temperatures, where millions of particles lose their individuality and merge into one quantum droplet. In this state, atoms behave not as a chaotic crowd, but as one super-atom.
💡 The condensate is the coldest substance in the Universe, only a few billionths of a degree above absolute zero. If you place a laser beam into it, light can slow down to the speed of a cyclist (about 60 km/h), or even come to a complete stop, as if frozen inside ice.
Bose-Einstein condensate (BEC) is an exotic state of matter that occurs when a dilute gas of bosons (particles with integer spin) is cooled to ultra-low temperatures. All the atoms as if 'agree' to occupy the same quantum state — like many frightened penguins huddling together in a tight bunch, only here they are not warming themselves, but achieving maximum quantum order. As a result, a substance appears that is at once a single giant matter wave, capable of flowing without friction and exhibiting strange quantum properties on a macroscopic level.

History

In 1924, Indian physicist Satyendra Nath Bose sent Einstein an article on a new statistics for particles of light. Einstein immediately recognized the importance of the idea and extended it to atoms, showing that with sufficient cooling a gas should 'condense' into the lowest energy state. But experimental realization proved extremely difficult. Only in 1995 did Eric Cornell and Carl Wieman obtain the first condensate from rubidium atoms, cooling them to 170 nanokelvins. For this discovery, they (together with Wolfgang Ketterle) were awarded the Nobel Prize.

How it works

Imagine a group of musicians tuning their instruments. While it's warm (ordinary temperature), each plays its own note — complete chaos. But as it cools, their 'breath' slows down, and they begin to synchronize until they sound in unison as one pure note. So too atoms: at room temperature, they rush around at different speeds, but upon cooling to nanokelvins, their quantum waves smear out and overlap, forcing all particles to occupy a single energy level. The condensate behaves as a single object — it can even be 'shaken' and made to vibrate as a single drop.

💡 Bose-Einstein condensate can be used to create an 'atom laser' — a beam of atoms moving in perfect synchrony. Such a beam is already used in ultra-precise atomic clocks and interferometers.
Bose-Einstein condensate (BEC) is a phase state of an ideal or weakly interacting Bose gas, in which a macroscopic fraction of particles occupies the ground (lowest energy) quantum state. The phenomenon is described by Bose-Einstein statistics and occurs at temperatures below the critical Tc, when the thermal de Broglie wavelength λdB = h/√(2π m k_B T) becomes comparable to the average distance between particles. The wave functions of individual atoms overlap, forming a single macroscopic wave function — the condensate. BEC exhibits superfluidity, a high degree of coherence, interference of atom waves, and suppression of inelastic scattering. Interaction in the condensate at low densities is well modeled by mean-field theory — the Gross-Pitaevskii equation. Condensation is analogous to the emergence of coherence in lasers (with the difference that photons condense there). Cooper pairs in superconductors also exhibit properties of a Bose condensate.

History

The theoretical prediction of BEC dates back to the works of Satyendra Nath Bose (1924) and Albert Einstein (1925). Bose derived a distribution for photons without a classical limit, and Einstein applied this statistics to non-relativistic atoms. He showed that below a certain temperature, the gas should dramatically change its properties due to the accumulation of particles in the lowest energy state. For many decades, the search for the right conditions (necessary to avoid condensation into liquid and solid) yielded no results. The breakthrough came in 1995: the group of Eric Cornell and Carl Wieman from JILA (USA) first cooled a cloud of rubidium-87 atoms to a temperature of 170 nanokelvins and observed the phase transition to BEC, and soon Wolfgang Ketterle from MIT obtained a condensate from sodium atoms. These experiments opened the field of quantum gases and earned the authors the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2001.

How it works

A Bose gas at high temperatures is described by Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics. As the temperature decreases, the de Broglie wavelength of the particles increases: λdB ∝ 1/√T. When λdB exceeds the average interparticle distance d (i.e., when nλdB³ ~ 1, more precisely nλdB³ = ζ(3/2) ≈ 2.612), the wave packets of atoms begin to overlap, and one must apply quantum Bose-Einstein statistics. For bosons, unlike fermions, the Pauli principle does not prohibit multiple occupation of a single quantum state; moreover, at low temperatures this becomes energetically favorable. A phase transition occurs, and a macroscopic number of particles accumulates in the ground state. This condensate is described by a single wave function, which satisfies the second law of thermodynamics: the entropy of the system decreases during the transition, but is compensated by heat removal to the external environment. The Gross-Pitaevskii equation incorporates this wave function and accounts for weak interaction between atoms.

n \lambda_{dB}^3 \approx 2.612
n — atom concentration (m⁻³); λdB = h/√(2π m k_B T) — thermal de Broglie wavelength (m), where h — Planck constant, h ≈ 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s, m — atom mass (kg), k_B — Boltzmann constant, k_B ≈ 1.381×10⁻²³ J/K, T — absolute temperature (K). When this equality holds, a Bose-Einstein condensate forms.
