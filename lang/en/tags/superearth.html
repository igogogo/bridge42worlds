Super-Earth

📊 1 articles
This is a planet larger than Earth but smaller than Neptune, composed mainly of rock and metal.
💡 Super-Earths are the most common type of exoplanet discovered to date.
A super-Earth is an exoplanet with a mass from 1 to 10 Earth masses, which may be rocky like Earth or have a thick atmosphere. Imagine Earth enlarged several times. Some super-Earths are in the habitable zone of their stars, making them candidates for the search for life.

History

The first super-Earth was discovered in 1992 around a pulsar, but the first around a normal star (Gliese 876 d) was in 2005. Since then, thousands of such planets have been detected.

How it works

Super-Earths form from a protoplanetary disk, accreting more material than Earth. Their composition can vary from rocky to water-rich. Due to their larger mass, they have stronger gravity and possibly a thicker atmosphere.

💡 Some super-Earths may be 'ocean planets' completely covered in water.
A super-Earth is a class of exoplanets with masses from 1 to 10 Earth masses and radii up to 2 Earth radii. They can be rocky like Earth or have a significant gas envelope. Super-Earths are often found in systems of red dwarfs. Their study is important for understanding the diversity of planets and conditions for life. Examples: Kepler-452b, TRAPPIST-1e.

History

The first super-Earth (around pulsar PSR B1257+12) was discovered in 1992. The first super-Earth around a normal star (Gliese 876 d) was in 2005. The Kepler mission (2009-2018) discovered thousands of super-Earths, showing they are a common type of planet.

How it works

Super-Earths form in a protoplanetary disk by accretion of solid material, and then may capture a gas envelope. Their internal structure depends on mass and composition: rocky planets have an iron core and silicate mantle; planets with a thick atmosphere may be mini-Neptunes. Surface gravity is higher than on Earth, affecting geology and climate.

Open problems & fun facts
