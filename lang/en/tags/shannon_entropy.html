Shannon entropy

📊 0 articles
Shannon entropy is a measure of uncertainty: the more possible outcomes, the higher the entropy. For example, a fair coin has an entropy of 1 bit, while a coin with two heads has 0.
💡 Shannon entropy coincides in formula with Boltzmann entropy in statistical physics.
Shannon entropy is the average amount of information carried by one symbol of a message. For a random variable X with distribution p(x), it is H(X) = -∑ p(x) log p(x). The more uniform the distribution, the higher the entropy.

History

Introduced by Claude Shannon in 1948 as a measure of information. The name 'entropy' was suggested by John von Neumann due to its similarity to thermodynamic entropy.

How it works

If an event has probability p, its information content is -log p. Entropy is the average of such values. For example, for a uniform distribution with 8 outcomes, entropy is 3 bits.

💡 Shannon entropy is used in machine learning to build decision trees (information gain criterion).
Shannon entropy is a fundamental measure of uncertainty in information theory. For a discrete random variable X with values {x_i} and probabilities p_i, entropy H(X) = -∑ p_i log_b p_i, where the base of the logarithm determines the unit (bit, nat, hartley). Properties: non-negativity, maximum for uniform distribution, additivity for independent variables. Entropy is a special case of Rényi entropy. It is related to the minimum lossless code length (Shannon's source coding theorem).

History

Claude Shannon introduced entropy in 1948. John von Neumann suggested the name 'entropy' because it resembles Boltzmann's formula S = k log W. In the 1950s, entropy became a key concept in coding theory. Later it was generalized to continuous distributions (differential entropy) and quantum systems (von Neumann entropy).

How it works

Entropy is computed from the probability distribution. For the discrete case, H = -∑ p_i log_2 p_i (bits). For the continuous case, differential entropy h(X) = -∫ f(x) log f(x) dx. Entropy can be conditional H(Y|X) and joint H(X,Y). Mutual information I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X|Y) = H(Y) - H(Y|X).

H(X) = -\sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \log_2 p_i
Average amount of information (in bits) per symbol for a source with distribution p_i.

Open problems & fun facts

Generalization of entropy for non-extensive systems (Tsallis entropy); Connection between Shannon entropy and computational complexity
