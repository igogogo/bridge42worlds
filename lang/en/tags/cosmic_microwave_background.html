Cosmic Microwave Background

📊 3 articles
Electromagnetic radiation filling the entire universe, left over from the early epoch of the Big Bang. It is thermal radiation with a temperature of about 2.725 K, corresponding to the microwave range. Imagine a faint glow that we see as 'noise' on an old TV — that is the echo of the birth of the universe.
💡 The cosmic microwave background is the oldest radiation that can be observed: its photons have been traveling to us for almost 13.8 billion years.

History

Discovered accidentally in 1965 by Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson (Nobel Prize 1978) as isotropic noise. Theoretically predicted by George Gamow, Ralph Alpher, and Robert Herman (1948). The COBE satellite (1989) showed the spectrum is blackbody, and WMAP (2001) and Planck (2009) measured anisotropy with high precision.

How it works

About 380,000 years after the Big Bang, the universe cooled enough for protons and electrons to form hydrogen atoms (recombination). Photons stopped scattering and began to travel freely. Since then, they have cooled due to the expansion of the universe to 2.7 K. Anisotropy (temperature fluctuations) reflects inhomogeneities in the early universe, from which galaxies formed.
