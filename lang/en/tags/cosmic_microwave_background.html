cosmic microwave background

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
📊 33
After the Big Bang, the Universe was hot, like a red-hot stove. Then it cooled and expanded like a balloon. Now, from that heat, a faint glow remains, but we can hardly see it — it has turned into invisible microwaves. Imagine a campfire that has long gone out, but the stones around are still warm. The cosmic microwave background is that kind of "heat" from the birth of the Universe.

History

In 1948, Ralph Alpher and Robert Herman predicted the existence of such radiation based on the Big Bang theory. In 1965, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson accidentally discovered it using a large antenna, for which they later received the Nobel Prize.

How it works

After the Big Bang, the Universe was a plasma of particles and light. When it cooled down about 380,000 years later, electrons were able to combine with nuclei (the era of recombination), and light began to travel freely. Since then, it has "cooled" as space expanded and now reaches us as microwaves. Its almost perfect uniformity with tiny ripples shows where galaxies later formed.

Imagine you are baking raisin bread. When the dough was compressed and hot, there was a lot of light in it. As it expanded, the light stretched and turned into microwaves. The cosmic microwave background is the most ancient light that has reached us. It fills the entire Universe as a uniform background with a temperature of about –270°C.

The cosmic microwave background (CMB) is thermal radiation left over from the recombination era in the early Universe. It is nearly isotropic blackbody radiation with a temperature of 2.72548 K and tiny anisotropies that carry information about the physics of the early Universe, its composition, and cosmological parameters. The spectrum of the radiation matches the Planck law to an extremely high degree, which is the strongest confirmation of the hot Big Bang model.

History

The theoretical prediction was made in the works of George Gamow, Ralph Alpher, and Robert Herman in 1948. They estimated the temperature of the remnant radiation at several kelvins. The experimental discovery occurred in 1965 by Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson, who detected excess noise in an antenna, which turned out to be radiation with a temperature of about 3 K. Subsequent satellite missions (COBE, WMAP, Planck) measured the CMB spectrum and anisotropies with extreme precision, turning cosmology into an exact science.

How it works

The CMB arises at the epoch of recombination (about 380,000 years after the Big Bang), when the Universe became transparent to photons. Its spectrum is described by the Planck law for a black body. Temperature anisotropies reflect initial quantum density fluctuations amplified by inflation. Acoustic oscillations in the photon-baryon plasma produce characteristic peaks in the angular power spectrum, from which cosmological parameters (baryon and dark matter densities, the Hubble constant, and others) are determined. These processes are described by the Friedmann equations and the physics of non-equilibrium thermodynamics.
