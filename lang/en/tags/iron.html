iron

📊 0 articles
Iron is the strongest metal from which airplanes and towers are built. But it is also inside us: tiny particles of iron in the blood grab oxygen and carry it to every cell, like tiny trucks delivering fuel throughout the body.
💡 In ancient times, blacksmiths forged weapons from meteoritic iron that fell from the sky. Such iron does not rust and was more valuable than gold! And without impurities, it is viscous like plasticine—the famous Eiffel Tower is made of almost pure iron mined from local deposits.
Iron is the fourth most abundant element in the Earth's crust and the main metal of civilization. It is like the backbone of our technosphere: bridges, rails, skyscrapers depend on it. In living nature, iron is a key player: iron atoms in hemoglobin reversibly bind oxygen, working like magnets that either attract or release their load.

History

The Iron Age began around 1200 BC, but the first iron used was from meteorites. Smelting iron from ore was learned by the Hittites in Asia Minor. In 1747, French physician Pierre Menard de la Grosse proved the presence of iron in blood, and a century later Henry Bessemer invented the converter for mass steel production.

How it works

In a blast furnace, resembling a red-hot volcano, iron oxides are reduced by carbon—coal 'takes away' oxygen, leaving pure metal. In our body, iron, changing its charge, 'charges' with oxygen in the lungs and 'discharges' in tissues, like a battery ensuring continuous respiration.

💡 Pure iron is soft: its hardness on the Mohs scale is only 4 out of 10. But adding 0.5% carbon yields steel that cuts glass. And the Earth's core is a solid iron ball the size of the Moon, heated to 5500°C, rotating inside a liquid outer core, creating the planet's magnetic field.
Iron (Fe) is a transition metal of group VIII with atomic number 26. It is a ferromagnet with a Curie temperature of 770°C, possessing high saturation magnetization. In the Earth's crust, it occurs as oxides (hematite Fe₂O₃, magnetite Fe₃O₄). In biochemistry, iron is part of heme—the prosthetic group of hemoglobin, myoglobin, cytochromes. Its coordination number of 6 allows reversible binding of O₂, CO₂, and other ligands.

History

Iron smelting began in Anatolia around 1500 BC. Meteoritic iron was used as early as the 4th millennium BC. In 1747, Menard de la Grosse discovered iron in blood ash. In 1932, Hans Fischer deciphered the structure of heme. Iron's magnetism was studied by Gilbert (1600), but only Maxwell (1873) gave a rigorous theory of the electromagnetic field, explaining ferromagnetism as the result of microscopic currents.

How it works

Ferromagnetism of iron is due to exchange interaction, aligning the spins of unpaired d-electrons. This creates macroscopic domains that reorient in an external field. According to Maxwell's equations for media with magnetic hysteresis, the hysteresis loop determines the coercive force and remanent magnetization. Upon heating above the Curie point, thermal fluctuations destroy magnetic order (Boltzmann statistics). In biology, oxidation of Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺ allows cytochromes to transfer electrons in the respiratory chain.

\chi = \frac{C}{T - T_C}
\chi is magnetic susceptibility, C is the Curie constant (characteristic of the substance), T is absolute temperature, T_C is the Curie temperature (for iron ≈ 1043 K)
