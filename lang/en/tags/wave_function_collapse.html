Wave Function Collapse

📊 0 articles
The process by which a quantum system transitions from a superposition of all possible states to a single definite state as a result of measurement. It's like flipping a coin: while in the air, it is both heads and tails, but once caught, it becomes either heads or tails.
💡 In Wheeler's delayed-choice experiment (1978), collapse can be 'delayed' by making a photon decide to behave as a wave or particle after passing through the slits.
The process by which a quantum system transitions from a superposition of all possible states to a single definite state as a result of measurement. It's like flipping a coin: while in the air, it is both heads and tails, but once caught, it becomes either heads or tails.

History

The concept was introduced by Werner Heisenberg and Niels Bohr within the Copenhagen interpretation (1927). John von Neumann mathematically described collapse as the reduction of the wave packet. Later, alternative interpretations emerged: the many-worlds interpretation (Everett, 1957) denies collapse, while objective collapse (GRW) posits spontaneous localization.

How it works

Before measurement, the system is described by a wave function that evolves deterministically according to the Schrödinger equation. Upon measurement, the wave function abruptly 'collapses' into one of the eigenstates of the measuring device. The probability of each outcome is given by the squared modulus of the amplitude. The mechanism of collapse remains unclear.

Open problems & fun facts

What causes collapse? The observer's consciousness? Interaction with the environment? Or is there no collapse, but a splitting of universes? (the measurement problem)
