Wave Function Collapse

📊 11 articles
When a particle is measured, it is as if it suddenly realizes it is being observed and stops playing tricks, choosing one specific state. This instantaneous choice is the wave function collapse.
💡 If you measure identical particles many times, the distribution of results will match the probabilities predicted by the wave function before collapse.
The wave function is a cloud of possibilities. When measured, it collapses to a single point, like a soap bubble that pops, leaving a single drop. Collapse is the transformation of the probable into the actual.

History

Erwin Schrödinger introduced the wave function in 1926 but did not explain the measurement mechanism. Max Born proposed a probabilistic interpretation, and John von Neumann formalized collapse as a projection. However, the nature of collapse is still debated.

How it works

Like a die in a cup: while it's in the cup, its face is unknown (superposition of all faces). Collapse happens at the moment it lands on the table—a specific number comes up. Only in the quantum world, the die can land on several tables at once.

💡 There is an interpretation of quantum mechanics in which there is no collapse at all, and the universe splits into many worlds at each measurement.
Wave function collapse is a process by which a quantum superposition is reduced to a single definite state as a result of measurement. Mathematically, it is described as the projection of the state vector onto the eigensubspace of the measured observable. Collapse occurs instantaneously and irreversibly, breaking the unitary evolution given by the Schrödinger equation. This is one of the central problems in the interpretation of quantum mechanics.

History

Erwin Schrödinger introduced the wave function in 1926. Max Born at the same time gave a probabilistic interpretation, but the measurement mechanism remained a mystery. John von Neumann in 1932 introduced the projection postulate (collapse) as a separate process, distinct from unitary evolution. John Stewart Bell in 1964 showed that any hidden-variable theory reproducing quantum predictions must be nonlocal, which was experimentally confirmed by Alain Aspect in the 1980s. The question of whether collapse is a physical process or merely an update of knowledge remains open.

How it works

According to the measurement postulate (Born's rule), if a system is in superposition |ψ⟩ = Σ c_i |φ_i⟩, and observable A with eigenvectors |φ_i⟩ is measured, then the probability of outcome i is |⟨φ_i|ψ⟩|² = |c_i|². As a result of measurement, the state collapses to |φ_i⟩. This process is described by the projection operator P_i = |φ_i⟩⟨φ_i|. Mathematically, collapse represents an irreversible transformation that turns the superposition into a statistical mixture described by the density matrix ρ = Σ |c_i|² |φ_i⟩⟨φ_i|. In dynamical collapse models (e.g., Ghirardi-Rimini-Weber), nonlinear stochastic terms are added to the Schrödinger equation so that collapse occurs objectively.
