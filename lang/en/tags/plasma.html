plasma

Plasma is a gas in which electrons are torn from atoms, turning it into a soup of charged particles. Lightning and the Sun are examples of plasma in action.

In practice: Plasma is used in fluorescent lamps and plasma televisions, and in the future will enable clean energy through nuclear fusion.
Plasma is an ionized gas of free electrons and positive ions. Unlike a neutral gas, it is electrically conductive and subject to the influence of electromagnetic fields. More than 99% of the visible matter in the Universe is plasma: stars, interstellar gas, accretion disks. On Earth, it can be found in lightning, auroras, and artificial devices from neon signs to tokamaks.

History

The word 'plasma' (from Greek 'molded') was proposed by American physicist Irving Langmuir in 1928 while studying electrical discharges in gases. But the phenomenon has long been known: lightning and St. Elmo's fire are plasma.

How it works

When strongly heated, collisions between atoms knock electrons out of their orbits. A mixture of charged particles forms that collectively responds to electric and magnetic fields — much like a flock of birds simultaneously changing direction.

💡 Plasma can spontaneously form structures — 'plasma crystals,' ordered lattices of charged dust particles floating in the plasma.
When a gas is heated to a very high temperature, its atoms break down into positive ions (atoms that lost electrons) and free electrons. This 'electric gas' is called plasma — the fourth state of matter after solid, liquid, and ordinary gas. Plasma conducts electricity and responds to magnetic fields, like a crowd at a disco that moves chaotically but can flash synchronously to the music (electric field).

Plasma is the fourth state of matter, characterized by the presence of free charged particles (electrons and ions), due to which it exhibits collective electromagnetic properties. Quasi-neutrality on macroscopic scales is combined with local electric fields that give rise to complex behavior: waves, instabilities, and self-generation of magnetic fields. Plasma is described by magnetohydrodynamics equations and kinetic theory.

History

Although gas discharges were studied by Michael Faraday in the 19th century, the term 'plasma' was introduced by Irving Langmuir in 1928 while working with ionized gases in electron tubes. Langmuir noticed a similarity between the behavior of the multi-electron medium and living tissue and used the Greek word meaning 'form' or 'molding'. The development of plasma theory in the mid-20th century was stimulated by astrophysics and thermonuclear research; major contributions were made by James Clerk Maxwell (equations of electrodynamics) and Ludwig Boltzmann (statistical kinetics).

How it works

The plasma state is achieved when the thermal motion energy exceeds the binding energy of electrons in atoms. A mixture of charged particles arises, interacting via long-range Coulomb forces, leading to collective effects: Debye screening and plasma oscillations. In a magnetic field, plasma is frozen into field lines, which explains its structures in space. To describe plasma, Maxwell's equations are used in combination with equations of particle motion; in thermodynamic equilibrium, the particle distribution follows the Boltzmann distribution.
