gluon

Gluons are invisible threads that glue quarks in protons and neutrons, like superglue. Without them all matter would crumble into dust.

History

In 1979 at the PETRA collider in Germany, physicists first observed three-jet events in electron-positron collisions — direct evidence of gluon radiation by quarks.

How it works

When quarks exchange gluons, they change their color, like players in tag exchanging colored ribbons. This vigorous exchange prevents quarks from flying apart, holding them inside hadrons like in a tight sack.

💡 Gluons are so sticky that they cling not only to quarks but also to each other! This makes the strong interaction very unusual.
The gluon is like a courier that continuously carries a 'color package' from one quark to another. Unlike the photon (the electric charge courier), the gluon can change the color of the package and even create new identical packages.

Gluons are the gauge bosons of the strong interaction, massless particles with spin 1 and negative parity. Unlike photons, gluons carry a color charge (combination of color and anticolor), leading to the existence of eight independent types of gluons. This feature makes QCD a non-Abelian theory, where gluons interact with each other, giving rise to asymptotic freedom and confinement.

History

The concept of gluons emerged with quantum chromodynamics in the early 1970s. The works of Gross, Wilczek, and Politzer (Nobel Prize 2004) demonstrated the property of asymptotic freedom, explaining the weakness of interaction at short distances. First experimental confirmation was obtained in 1979 at DESY (Hamburg) by observing three-jet events in e⁺e⁻ annihilation. Later, gluon properties were studied in detail at LEP, HERA, and LHC colliders.

How it works

The gluon field is described by the tensor G_{μν}^a within QCD with the gauge group SU(3). The Lagrangian contains gluon self-interaction terms (triple and quartic vertices), which leads to saturation of the gluon field at high energies and confinement. The exchange of gluons between quarks generates a force whose potential grows linearly with distance: V(r) ~ kr. When trying to isolate a color charge, the field energy reaches the threshold for quark-antiquark pair production, leading to hadronization and jet formation. Asymptotic freedom ensures that at ultra-short distances the interaction weakens, allowing the application of perturbation theory.

Understanding the mechanism of confinement and the structure of the QCD physical vacuum.; The role of gluons in the spin structure of the nucleon (spin crisis problem).; Description of processes in the gluon saturation regime at extreme energy densities (e.g., Colour Glass Condensate).
