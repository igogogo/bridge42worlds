dark photon

📊 2

The dark photon is an elusive "cousin" of ordinary light: while ordinary photons bind charges, the dark photon binds dark matter particles, remaining invisible to us.

In practice: The discovery of the dark photon would help explain the nature of dark matter and possibly create new communication technologies through the bulk of the Earth.
The dark photon is a hypothetical vector boson associated with a U(1) symmetry in the hidden sector of particle physics, analogous to the photon for electromagnetism. It interacts with Standard Model particles through kinetic mixing with the ordinary photon — a small parameter ε, a "leakage" between dark and ordinary electrodynamics. The mass of the dark photon can range from micro-electronvolts to hundreds of GeV.

History

The idea of the dark photon emerged at the end of the 20th century when physicists were looking for ways to explain dark matter.

How it works

If dark photons exist, they can occasionally mix with ordinary photons, becoming briefly visible. It's like two parallel roads with very rare pedestrian crossings.

💡 The dark photon could explain excess radiation in the centers of galaxies that cannot be attributed to ordinary astrophysics.
Ordinary light consists of particles — photons. They carry the electromagnetic interaction (what makes a magnet stick to a refrigerator). Scientists think there might be a similar particle — a dark photon. It "works" like an ordinary photon, but only for dark matter (invisible substance that does not emit light but has mass). That's why we don't see it.

The dark photon (A') is a massive gauge boson of an additional U(1)' symmetry, broken at some scale. In the minimal model, it arises from adding a "dark sector" to the Standard Model: dark matter fermion fields interacting through a dark photon. Kinetic mixing with the ordinary hypercharge field, described by the operator (ε/2 cosθ_W) F_μν F'^μν, induces a weak coupling of the dark photon to ordinary matter. The mass is generated by the Higgs or Stückelberg mechanism.

History

The concept emerged in the 1980s as a byproduct of grand unified theories. In the 2008–2010s, interest revived thanks to experiments searching for light dark photons (LSND, MiniBooNE) and anomalies in cosmic rays. Leading laboratories, such as CERN (NA64) and SLAC (HPS), are actively searching.

How it works

If the dark photon is light (meV–MeV), it could manifest as an additional radiation source or affect star cooling. In experiments, it is searched for via radiation processes (e.g., e⁺e⁻ → γ γ', where the signal is a single photon with missing energy) or through resonant conversion of ordinary photons into dark ones in vacuum chambers (the "light through a wall" scheme). Heavier dark photons decay into charged leptons and can be detected by colliders.
