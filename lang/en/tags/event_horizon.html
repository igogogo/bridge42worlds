event horizon

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
The boundary of a black hole from which not even light can escape. Like the edge of a waterfall: as you approach, the boat can still row, but beyond a certain line the current relentlessly carries it down. Here the falling speed of spacetime equals the speed of light.

History

Karl Schwarzschild in 1916 found an exact solution to Einstein's equations for a point mass and obtained a radius at which the metric becomes unphysical. Later, in 1974, Stephen Hawking showed that the horizon emits faint radiation due to quantum effects.

How it works

A massive object bends spacetime, like a ball on a rubber sheet. At a certain distance, the slope becomes so steep that even light cannot get out. That's the horizon. For a distant observer, the time of a falling object slows down and stops right at the boundary.

💡 The Sun, compressed into a black hole, would have a horizon only 6 kilometers in diameter. Earth would continue to move in the same orbit, but eternal darkness and cold would set in.

💡 From the point of view of a distant observer, any object will never reach the horizon — it will infinitely redshift and freeze. But the falling observer crosses the horizon in finite time, noticing nothing special (except catastrophic tidal forces in small holes).
The event horizon is a closed null hypersurface in spacetime that bounds a region from which no causal signals can reach a distant observer. For a static uncharged black hole, its radius equals the Schwarzschild radius: R_s = 2GM/c². In general relativity, it is a global concept defined as the boundary of the past for light rays that escape to infinity.

History

The first solution of Einstein's equations containing a horizon was found by Karl Schwarzschild in 1916. Initially it was considered a mathematical fiction, but in 1939 Oppenheimer and Snyder showed the reality of gravitational collapse. Key discoveries in singularity theory and horizon properties were made by Roger Penrose and Stephen Hawking in the 1960s-70s. Jacob Bekenstein and Hawking linked the horizon to thermodynamics, proving that black holes have entropy and temperature.

How it works

According to Einstein's equations, a massive body curves spacetime. During collapse, a region forms where light cones tilt inward, and any path leads to the singularity. At the horizon, the coordinate singularity of the Schwarzschild metric disappears in suitable coordinates (e.g., Kruskal–Szekeres). Quantum effects near the horizon lead to Hawking radiation, with its temperature and entropy related to the horizon area by the Bekenstein–Hawking formulas.

R_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}
R_s is the event horizon radius, G is the gravitational constant (≈6.674×10⁻¹¹ m³/(kg·s²)), M is the object's mass, c is the speed of light (≈3×10⁸ m/s)
S = \frac{k_B A}{4 l_P^2}
S is the black hole entropy, k_B is the Boltzmann constant (≈1.38×10⁻²³ J/K), A is the event horizon area, l_P is the Planck length (≈1.616×10⁻³⁵ m)
