lepton

Leptons are tiny building blocks of matter that do not feel the strong nuclear force. The most familiar is the electron, which runs around the nucleus in atoms. There are also elusive neutrinos, which fly through the Earth almost without noticing it.
💡 About 10¹¹ neutrinos from the Sun pass through your fingernail every day, but you feel nothing — so ghostly are leptons.
Leptons are a family of light particles (from the Greek 'light'), including the electron, muon, tau, and three types of neutrinos. They are like schools of fish in an ocean of fields: each lepton has its own field, just as each fish species has its own school, and they interact electromagnetically (if charged) and weakly, but avoid strong interactions.

History

The electron was discovered by J.J. Thomson in 1897. The muon was found by Carl Anderson in 1936 and initially mistaken for a meson. The tau lepton was discovered in 1975 at the Stanford Linear Accelerator. Neutrinos were first detected in 1956. Wolfgang Pauli predicted the existence of neutrinos in 1930.

How it works

Leptons manifest themselves in decays and collisions. The muon is a heavy cousin of the electron, lives for a couple of microseconds, and decays into an electron and neutrinos. Neutrinos oscillate, changing their flavor along the way like a chameleon changing color. All this is governed by the weak interaction, and for charged leptons, also by electromagnetism.

💡 In neutrino observatories like IceCube, they use a cubic kilometer of Antarctic ice to catch a flash from a single neutrino colliding with an atom.
In the Standard Model, leptons are fundamental fermions with spin 1/2, not participating in strong interactions. Six flavors: electron (e⁻), muon (μ⁻), tau (τ⁻) and corresponding neutrinos (ν_e, ν_μ, ν_τ). Each charged lepton carries a charge of -1e, while neutrinos are neutral. Leptons are conserved by generation in an axial form in the Standard Model, but neutrino oscillations violate lepton number by flavor. Neutrino masses are extremely small, pointing to physics beyond the Standard Model.

History

J.J. Thomson identified the electron as a particle in 1897 by measuring its e/m ratio. The muon was discovered in 1936 in cosmic rays and was first confused with Yukawa's meson. The tau lepton was detected by Martin Perl's group in 1975. The existence of neutrinos was postulated by Wolfgang Pauli in 1930 to save the energy conservation law in beta decay; neutrinos were first captured by Reines and Cowan in 1956.

How it works

Leptons interact through the weak interaction (exchange of W±, Z bosons), and charged ones also electromagnetically (photon). The Dirac equation describes free charged leptons. Neutrino oscillations require nonzero mass and mixing, described by the Pontecorvo-Maki-Nakagawa-Sakata matrix. Charged leptons are stable (electron) or decay via the weak interaction (muon, tau). The Pauli principle forbids two identical leptons from occupying the same quantum state, which determines the structure of atoms and neutron stars.

i\hbar \gamma^\mu \partial_\mu \psi - m c \psi = 0
ψ is the four-component spinor of the lepton field, i is the imaginary unit, ħ is the reduced Planck constant (ħ ≈ 1.05×10⁻³⁴ J·s), γ^μ are the Dirac matrices (4×4), ∂_μ is the partial derivative with respect to coordinates, m is the lepton mass, c is the speed of light (c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s).
