Radioactivity

📊 0 articles
Spontaneous transformation of unstable atomic nuclei into other nuclei, accompanied by emission of particles or electromagnetic radiation. This phenomenon underlies nuclear energy, medicine (radiation therapy), and dating. Imagine a nucleus as an unstable tower of blocks that eventually collapses, throwing out blocks.
💡 Radioactivity is used to determine the age of archaeological finds (radiocarbon dating)—up to 50,000 years.

History

Antoine Henri Becquerel discovered radioactivity in 1896 while studying uranium salts. Marie and Pierre Curie isolated new radioactive elements—polonium and radium. Ernest Rutherford identified alpha and beta radiation, and in 1911 proposed the planetary model of the atom.

How it works

Radioactive decay occurs randomly, but for a large number of atoms, the half-life is constant. Analogy: popcorn in a microwave—you don't know which kernel will pop next, but you know that after a certain time half will pop. There are alpha, beta, and gamma decay.

N = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}
The number of undecayed nuclei N decreases exponentially with time t, where λ is the decay constant.
