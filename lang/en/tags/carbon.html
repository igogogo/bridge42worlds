Carbon (C) is an element with atomic number 6, the basis of organic chemistry and biochemistry. Its unique ability for catenation — formation of stable chains and rings through covalent bonds — generates an enormous variety of compounds. Carbon allotropes exhibit a striking contrast of properties: from electrically conductive graphite to superhard diamond and fullerenes. In astrophysics, carbon is synthesized in stars via the triple-alpha process, which requires fine-tuning of a resonance level predicted by Fred Hoyle — a vivid example of the anthropic principle.

History

The elemental nature of carbon was established at the end of the 18th century by Antoine Lavoisier, who obtained carbon dioxide by burning diamonds and proved that diamond, graphite, and coal are the same substance. Lavoisier introduced the term 'carbon' in 1789. The development of organic chemistry in the 19th century solidified the central role of carbon. Johann Balmer, by creating a formula for the spectral lines of hydrogen, opened the way for spectral analysis of carbon in flames and stars. Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin in the 20th century confirmed that carbon is one of the main elements of stellar composition.

How it works

The chemical flexibility of carbon is provided by four valence electrons capable of orbital hybridization (sp, sp², sp³), which determines bond geometry and types of allotropes. In stars, carbon is formed in the triple-alpha process: three helium-4 nuclei fuse into carbon-12, which requires precise energy matching described by quantum mechanics (the Schrödinger equation). The stability of carbon nuclei is also dictated by the Pauli principle, which forbids fermions from occupying the same state, playing a role in stellar evolution and preventing the collapse of white dwarfs.
