History

The idea of an object from which light cannot escape dates back to the 18th century (John Michell and Pierre-Simon Laplace). However, modern understanding began with solutions to Einstein's equations found by Karl Schwarzschild in 1916 — he obtained an exact solution for a spherically symmetric black hole. The term 'black hole' was coined by John Wheeler in 1967. In the 1970s, Stephen Hawking showed that black holes can emit energy (Hawking radiation), and Jacob Bekenstein linked them to entropy.

A black hole is an astrophysical object predicted by Einstein's general relativity, representing a region of spacetime with such a strong gravitational field that the escape velocity exceeds the speed of light. Key elements: singularity (a point of zero volume with infinite density and curvature), event horizon (a surface bounding the region from which there is no return), photon sphere, and ergosphere for rotating black holes. Solutions to Einstein's equations: Schwarzschild (non-rotating), Kerr (rotating), Reissner–Nordström (charged), and Kerr–Newman (charged rotating). Black holes emit Hawking radiation due to quantum effects near the horizon, leading to their evaporation and the information paradox.

History

In 1783, John Michell suggested the existence of 'dark stars', and in 1796 Pierre-Simon Laplace independently put forward a similar idea. In 1916, Karl Schwarzschild found the first exact solution to Einstein's equations for a point mass, describing a static black hole. A few decades later, in the 1960s, Roy Kerr obtained the solution for rotating objects. Jacob Bekenstein in 1972 proposed that a black hole possesses entropy proportional to the horizon area, which Stephen Hawking confirmed in 1974 by discovering quantum-mechanical radiation. Also crucial were the works of Roger Penrose and John Wheeler, who cemented the concept of a black hole in astrophysics.

How it works

A black hole is described by the metric of the corresponding solution to Einstein's equations. The gravitational field is so strong that no material particle or electromagnetic radiation can leave it after crossing the event horizon. According to the second law of black hole thermodynamics, the event horizon area never decreases, which is analogous to entropy. Hawking radiation arises from quantum vacuum fluctuations: near the horizon, particle-antiparticle pairs are created, one of which may fall into the hole while the other escapes to infinity, carrying away energy and reducing mass. Also at work are Newton's law of universal gravitation for distant effects and the constant speed of light principle, which defines the horizon boundary.
