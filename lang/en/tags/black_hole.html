Black Hole

📊 1 articles
A region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape. Imagine a whirlpool in the ocean that sucks everything around it — only instead of water, it's curved space, and instead of a funnel, it's the event horizon.
💡 If the Sun became a black hole of the same radius, its gravity at Earth's orbit would not change — we would simply freeze in the dark.
A region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape. Imagine a whirlpool in the ocean that sucks everything around it — only instead of water, it's curved space, and instead of a funnel, it's the event horizon.

History

The idea of objects with such gravity appeared as early as 1783 with John Michell and later with Laplace. In 1915, Karl Schwarzschild found an exact solution to Einstein's equations describing a black hole. In the 1960s, John Wheeler popularized the term 'black hole'. In 2019, the Event Horizon Telescope obtained the first image of a black hole's shadow in the galaxy M87.

How it works

When a massive star exhausts its nuclear fuel, its core collapses under its own gravity, forming a black hole. Gravity is so strong that space and time swap roles: inside the event horizon, the singularity is not a point in space but a moment in the future. Analogy: if you fall into a black hole, to an outside observer you will slow down and redden, while you yourself will see the entire history of the universe flash before you.
