Quantum Superposition

📊 2
A falling raindrop can land at any point in a puddle. While it's falling, its position is a superposition of all points in the puddle. Only the impact with the water determines the specific spot.

History

Erwin Schrödinger established the concept of superposition in 1926 in his wave mechanics. Louis de Broglie had earlier suggested that particles behave as waves, paving the way.

How it works

If you throw a stone into water, ripples spread in all directions. A quantum particle behaves similarly: its wave function is a collection of ripples, and superposition allows it to interfere with itself, like intersecting waves.

Quantum superposition is a fundamental principle of quantum mechanics, according to which a physical system can exist in a linear combination of several quantum states. Mathematically, this is described as a sum of basis states with complex weights. The state of the system before measurement is a vector in Hilbert space, and its evolution follows the Schrödinger equation. Upon measurement, it collapses into one of the basis states with a probability equal to the square of the modulus of the corresponding coefficient.

History

Louis de Broglie proposed the hypothesis of wave properties of matter in 1924, which became a prerequisite. Erwin Schrödinger in 1926 developed wave mechanics and introduced the equation named after him, where superposition of solutions is natural. Max Born proposed the probabilistic interpretation of the wave function, explaining the physical meaning of superposition. Later, John Stewart Bell showed that superposition leads to nonlocal correlations, experimentally verified by Alain Aspect.

How it works

The state of the system is described by the vector |ψ⟩ = Σ c_i |u_i⟩ in Hilbert space. The time evolution is given by the Schrödinger equation: iħ ∂|ψ⟩/∂t = H|ψ⟩. When measuring a physical quantity corresponding to the operator A with eigenvectors |a_i⟩, the probability of obtaining the value a_i is |⟨a_i|ψ⟩|². Superposition manifests in interference effects, for example in the double-slit experiment, where a particle passes through both slits simultaneously.
