Quantum superposition

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
2

A quantum particle can simultaneously exist in multiple states, like a spinning coin on its edge — until it falls, it's both heads and tails.

In practice: Quantum computers build superposition into the basis of computation: a qubit in a superposition of zero and one processes both values at once. This speeds up solving problems, such as modeling drugs or optimizing large systems.
Quantum superposition is not just a mix of pre-existing states, but a fundamental ability of nature to form their coherent combinations. Formally, such a state is written as a sum of basis vectors with complex weights: |ψ⟩ = c₁|φ₁⟩ + c₂|φ₂⟩ + ... . The probability of finding the system in a particular state after measurement is equal to the square of the modulus of the corresponding weight. That is why in the double-slit experiment, the particle demonstrates interference, as if passing through both slits at once. Superposition is preserved only with complete isolation from the environment; interaction with the surroundings destroys coherence, causing it to quickly vanish in macroscopic objects.

History

Formally, the principle of superposition of states was introduced in 1926 by Austrian physicist Erwin Schrödinger as a consequence of his famous wave equation. Before that, the phenomenon of superposition was well known for classical waves, but Schrödinger was the first to suggest that particles could behave similarly. Later, he proposed a thought experiment with a cat that is simultaneously alive and dead to demonstrate the strangeness of this idea applied to a large object.

How it works

When ripples from two thrown stones spread across water, they overlay each other, creating an alternation of reinforced and weakened wavelets. Similarly, a quantum state can be a mixture of several wave 'images' at once. In isolation, a particle remains in such a blurred form. However, upon measurement, for example, of its energy or position, the mixture instantaneously turns into one definite value — this is called the collapse of superposition.

💡 A quantum bit, or qubit, in a superposition of 0 and 1 is capable of processing both states in parallel. This gives quantum computers an exponential advantage over classical ones in certain computational tasks.
Quantum superposition can be compared to a coin in flight: while it is spinning, you cannot say whether it will land heads or tails — both outcomes coexist. Similarly, a microparticle before measurement can combine several different states. After measurement, this uncertainty disappears — the state 'collapses' to one specific result. This behavior is called quantum superposition (from Latin superpositio — placing over). It follows from the basic equation of quantum mechanics — the Schrödinger equation: if two states are possible, then any combination of them is also possible.

Quantum superposition is a central concept of quantum mechanics, stating that any linear combination of allowed states of a system is also an allowed state. In the mathematical apparatus of the theory, this is expressed through vectors in Hilbert space: if |φ₁⟩ and |φ₂⟩ are possible states, then the state α|φ₁⟩ + β|φ₂⟩ with complex α, β is physically realizable up to the moment of measurement. The principle of superposition underlies practically all non-classical effects: quantum interference, entanglement, tunneling, and the operation of quantum algorithms. After measurement, the system irreversibly transitions to one of the basis states with a probability equal to the square of the modulus of the corresponding amplitude; this process is called wave function reduction or collapse. Decoherence, caused by inevitable interaction with the environment, quickly suppresses the coherence of macroscopic superpositions, explaining why we do not observe them in everyday life.

History

The idea of superposition was born from attempts to reconcile the wave behavior of matter, predicted by Louis de Broglie in 1924, with the particle picture. Erwin Schrödinger in 1926 constructed a wave equation for which, as with any linear differential equations, the sum of solutions is also a solution — thus the formal principle of superposition of states arose. In 1935, he also devised the paradox of 'Schrödinger's cat', demonstrating the problem of the transition from the micro-world to the macro-world. John Stewart Bell in 1964 showed that superposition leads to statistical correlations that cannot be explained classically (Bell's theorem); later, laboratory tests, initiated by Alain Aspect, brilliantly confirmed this prediction. Since the end of the 20th century, superposition has been actively used in quantum computing; the pioneer of this direction was David Deutsch, who described the universal quantum computer in 1985.

How it works

The state of a system is encoded as a vector |ψ⟩ in Hilbert space. According to the superposition principle, any linear combination of the form Σ c_i |φ_i⟩, where {|φ_i⟩} is a basis, and c_i are complex amplitudes, describes a physically realizable state. Time evolution obeys the Schrödinger equation, which preserves superposition: if the initial state was a superposition, then the subsequent state will remain a superposition as long as the system is isolated. Measurement, on the contrary, irreversibly projects the vector onto one of the eigenstates of the measured quantity; the probabilities are determined by the squared moduli of the amplitudes. In real conditions, the inevitable coupling to the heat bath entangles the system with a huge number of environmental degrees of freedom, quickly destroying coherence (decoherence) — that is why macroscopic superpositions are extremely difficult to create and maintain.

|\psi\rangle = \sum_i c_i |\phi_i\rangle
|ψ⟩ — state vector of the system; c_i — complex amplitudes, the squared modulus |c_i|² gives the probability of finding the system in the basis state |φ_i⟩; |φ_i⟩ — orthonormal basis in Hilbert space.
