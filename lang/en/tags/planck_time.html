Planck Time

📊 0 articles
Planck time is the shortest possible interval of time, about 5.4×10⁻⁴⁴ seconds, below which time loses meaning.
💡 In Planck time, light travels a distance equal to the Planck length.
Planck time is the time it takes for light to travel the Planck length. It is the smallest time interval that can be measured within known physics.

History

Like the Planck length, Planck time was introduced by Max Planck in 1899.

How it works

On times shorter than Planck time, quantum effects of gravity become important, and the concept of continuous time may be replaced by discrete 'quanta' of time.

💡 Planck time is 10⁴³ times smaller than the time it takes light to cross a proton.
Planck time t_P = √(ħG/c⁵) ≈ 5.391×10⁻⁴⁴ s is a fundamental unit of time associated with the Planck length. It is believed that processes shorter than this time cannot be described by classical physics and require quantum gravity.

History

Max Planck in 1899 defined Planck time as a natural unit of time. In modern cosmology, the Universe is thought to have originated at the Planck time after the Big Bang.

How it works

Planck time is the time it takes for light to travel the Planck length. It is also the characteristic time for quantum gravity effects. For example, the age of the Universe in Planck units is about 8×10⁶⁰ t_P.

t_P = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar G}{c^5}}
Planck time is expressed in terms of Planck's constant, the gravitational constant, and the speed of light.
