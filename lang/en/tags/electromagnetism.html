electromagnetism

📊 12 articles

Electricity and magnetism are two sides of one force: moving charges create magnetism, and a changing magnetic field generates electricity. This is the basis of generators, motors and wireless communication.

In practice: All modern communications, from Wi-Fi to satellites, use electromagnetic waves, and without electromagnetism, electric motors, dynamos and MRI scanners would not work. From electric lighting to radio, television and the internet—all this is embodied electromagnetism.
Maxwell's equations describe how electric and magnetic fields are created by charges and currents and how they mutually generate each other. A changing electric field creates a vortex magnetic field, and a changing magnetic field creates a vortex electric field. This tandem propagates in a vacuum as an electromagnetic wave at speed c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀), where ε₀ and μ₀ are the electric and magnetic constants, measured in experiments with charges and currents. The spectrum of these waves ranges from low-frequency radio waves to gamma rays. Quantum electrodynamics complements the picture, describing the interaction as an exchange of photons—massless particles of light.
💡 The speed of light c is not just a speed, but a limit embedded in the very laws of electromagnetism; the sizes of atoms and chemistry depend on it, and hence the conditions for life in the Universe.
Electricity (movement of charges) and magnetism (forces that attract iron and turn a compass needle) are two sides of one fundamental interaction—electromagnetism. A flow of electrons (tiny negatively charged particles) through a wire creates a magnetic field around it (the region of magnetic forces). And vice versa: if you quickly move a magnet inside a wire coil, an electric current appears in it. This 'dance' generates waves that spread through space at the speed of light: from radio waves and microwaves to visible light and X-rays.

History

Until the beginning of the 19th century, electricity and magnetism were considered independent. In 1820, Hans Christian Ørsted discovered that a current deflects a magnetic needle, and Michael Faraday in 1831 showed that a moving magnet creates a current in a coil. In the 1860s, the Scottish physicist James Clerk Maxwell generalized these laws into a system of equations that not only described all known phenomena but also predicted electromagnetic waves.

How it works

When electrons in an antenna oscillate, they create changing electric and magnetic fields around them, which, like ripples on water, spread through space as an electromagnetic wave. The eye perceives high-frequency waves as light, and an antenna picks up radio frequencies as a Wi-Fi signal.

The electromagnetic interaction is one of the four fundamental interactions of nature, responsible for the forces between electrically charged particles and for all classical electromagnetic phenomena. Classical electrodynamics is unified by four Maxwell equations: Gauss's law for electricity, Gauss's law for magnetism, Faraday's law of induction, Ampère's law with Maxwell's correction. Their solution yields electromagnetic waves traveling at speed c. In quantum electrodynamics, the interaction is represented as the exchange of virtual photons—massless gauge bosons. The absence of mass for the photon explains the infinite range and the inverse-square law. The U(1) gauge symmetry guarantees the conservation of electric charge and underlies the Standard Model.

History

The recognition of the connection between electricity and magnetism began with the experiment of H.C. Ørsted (1820), showing that a current causes a magnetic needle to deflect. M. Faraday in 1831 discovered electromagnetic induction—the emergence of current in a circuit when the magnetic flux changes. A brilliant synthesis was accomplished by James Clerk Maxwell: in 1861–64 he formulated a system of equations, adding to Ampère's law the 'displacement current'—a term with the time derivative of the electric field—which ensured consistency and predicted waves. The existence of waves was experimentally confirmed by H. Hertz in 1887. Hendrik Lorentz in 1895 introduced the Lorentz force and created the electron theory, explaining the behavior of charges at the microlevel. The quantum stage was completed in the mid-20th century by the works of R. Feynman, J. Schwinger and S. Tomonaga, who built renormalizable quantum electrodynamics.

How it works

Electrodynamics obeys the principle of relativity and is invariant under Lorentz transformations. From Maxwell's equations follows the wave equation for the fields. In quantum electrodynamics, the state of a system is described by a vector in Fock space, creation and annihilation operators for photons are built from the potentials, and the interaction with matter (fermions) is given by the interaction Lagrangian eψ̄γ^μψA_μ (where e is the charge, ψ is the Dirac field, A_μ is the electromagnetic potential). Scattering processes are calculated perturbatively in the fine-structure constant α ≈ 1/137 using Feynman diagrams. At small distances, the effective charge increases (vacuum polarization effect), which has been verified with high accuracy. The classical limit gives the Lorentz force F = q(E + v × B), describing the motion of charged bodies in fields.
