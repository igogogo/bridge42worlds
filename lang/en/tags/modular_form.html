modular form

📊 0 articles

Symmetry of a pattern on a tile: you rotate it — the pattern is unchanged. Only the tile is the upper half-plane, and the transformations are fractional-linear.

In practice: Proof of Fermat's Last Theorem, error-correcting codes, foundation for string theory and quantum invariants.
A modular form is a holomorphic function on the upper complex half-plane satisfying the functional equation f((az+b)/(cz+d)) = (cz+d)^k f(z) for all matrices in the modular group SL(2,Z) and possessing certain growth conditions. The weight k is an integer. Modular forms constitute finite-dimensional vector spaces, and their Fourier series contain deep arithmetic information.
💡 The Riemann zeta function, a mysterious function about the distribution of prime numbers, is closely related to modular forms through a modular form called the theta function.
A modular form is a function (a rule mapping a number to a number) defined on the upper half-plane (all points with positive imaginary part) and possessing a special symmetry: its value does not change if the argument is subjected to certain fractional-linear transformations (e.g., replacing z with (az+b)/(cz+d) for integers a,b,c,d with ad-bc=1). This is similar to periodic functions that repeat upon a shift, but here the 'shifts' are more cunning.

History

In the 19th century, mathematicians such as Eisenstein and Poincaré began studying these functions in connection with elliptic curves and number theory. Henri Poincaré made significant contributions to the theory of automorphic forms, which generalizes modular forms.

How it works

Imagine you are walking on a floor with non-Euclidean geometry, and each step transforms your coordinates, but some functions of the coordinates remain unchanged. Modular forms are like such invariants, remembering the global shape of the space.

💡 Pierre Deligne, who proved the Ramanujan conjecture about the growth of the τ-function, used deep methods of algebraic geometry and received the Fields Medal. The τ-function itself is defined through the expansion of the modular discriminant function Δ(z) = q Σ (1-q^n)^{24}.
A modular form of weight k for the group Γ (usually Γ = SL(2,ℤ)) is a holomorphic function f: ℍ → ℂ such that: f(γ(z)) = (cz+d)^k f(z) for all γ = (a b; c d) ∈ Γ, where γ(z) = (az+b)/(cz+d); and f is holomorphic at infinity (f remains bounded as y→∞). The modular group is generated by the translation T(z)=z+1 and the inversion S(z)=-1/z. The invariance condition under T yields periodicity with period 1, hence a Fourier series expansion: f(z) = Σ a_n e^{2πi n z}, where a_n are the Fourier coefficients. A key example: the Eisenstein series. The space of modular forms is finite-dimensional, and its basis consists of cusp forms (vanishing at the boundary) and Eisenstein series. Deep connections with elliptic curves through the Taniyama–Shimura conjecture (proved by Wiles) played a role in the proof of Fermat's Last Theorem. Modular forms are also related to representations, number theory, algebraic geometry, and physics.

History

The origins of the theory of modular forms go back to the works of Gauss and Eisenstein, but systematic study began in the late 19th century with Poincaré, who introduced the general concept of automorphic forms. Felix Klein studied modular functions in connection with the icosahedron. In the 20th century, Hecke developed the theory of operators, Ramanujan discovered deep properties of the τ-function, and later the Taniyama–Shimura conjecture about the modularity of elliptic curves led to Andrew Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem. Modern development includes the Langlands program and the modularity of all elliptic curves over ℚ.

How it works

A modular form is a section of a certain line bundle over the modular curve. The functional equation ensures that the function transforms well under the action of the modular group, while holomorphicity and the growth condition mean smoothness and finiteness at the cusps. Fourier series allow expressing the form through coefficients a_n, which often carry arithmetic meaning (e.g., the number of points on an elliptic curve over a finite field). The connection to elliptic curves is through modularity: every elliptic curve corresponds to a modular form whose L-series coincide. This is used in the proof of Fermat's theorem. Modular forms also generate representations of the group GL(2) over the adeles.

f\left(\frac{az+b}{cz+d}\right) = (cz+d)^k f(z)
f is a modular form of weight k, z is a point in the upper half-plane (Im(z) > 0), a,b,c,d are integers, ad−bc = 1.
