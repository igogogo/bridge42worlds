Renormalization Group

📊 0 articles
The renormalization group is a way to look at physics at different scales, like adjusting the focus of a microscope.
💡 Thanks to the renormalization group, we know that the strong interaction becomes weak at short distances (asymptotic freedom).
The renormalization group is a mathematical framework describing how physical constants (e.g., charge) change with energy scale. Imagine looking at a magnet: at short distances you see individual atoms, at large distances a uniform field. RG equations show how interactions 'smear out'.

History

The concept of the renormalization group was introduced in the 1950s by Murray Gell-Mann and Francis Low, and later developed by Kenneth Wilson in the 1970s for critical phenomena. Wilson received the Nobel Prize in 1982.

How it works

RG equations show how the coupling constant g changes with energy scale μ: dg/d(ln μ) = β(g). If β(g) < 0, the coupling decreases with energy (asymptotic freedom); if >0, it increases.

💡 The renormalization group predicted the existence of the 'Landau pole'—a scale where the electromagnetic interaction becomes infinite.
The renormalization group (RG) is a semigroup of transformations describing how parameters of a theory (coupling constants, masses) change with energy scale. RG equations (β-functions) determine how 'running' constants depend on scale. RG allows understanding critical behavior in statistical physics and asymptotic freedom in quantum chromodynamics. It is also used to analyze renormalizability and find fixed points.

History

The idea of RG arose in quantum field theory (Gell-Mann and Low, 1954). In the 1970s, Kenneth Wilson applied RG to critical phenomena, creating the theory of scale invariance. In 1973, Gross, Wilczek, and Politzer discovered asymptotic freedom in QCD by computing the β-function. Later, RG became a key tool in condensed matter physics and cosmology.

How it works

Consider the β-function for a gauge theory: β(g) = μ dg/dμ = -β_0 g^3/(16π^2) + ... For QCD, β_0 > 0, so β(g) < 0 at small g—asymptotic freedom. For QED, β_0 < 0, so the charge increases with energy. RG equations are solved: g(μ) = g(μ_0)/√(1 + β_0 g(μ_0)^2 ln(μ/μ_0)/(8π^2)).

\beta(g) = \mu \frac{dg}{d\mu} = -\frac{\beta_0}{16\pi^2} g^3 + \mathcal{O}(g^5)
The change of the coupling constant g with scale μ is determined by the β-function; the sign of β_0 indicates whether the coupling grows or decreases.
