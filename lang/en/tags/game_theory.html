Game theory

📊 0 articles
Game theory studies strategies in situations where the outcome depends on the actions of several participants — like in chess or negotiations.
💡 The famous 'Prisoner's Dilemma' shows that rational players can arrive at a result that is suboptimal for everyone.
Game theory is the mathematical analysis of conflict and cooperation situations. A game consists of players, their strategies, and payoffs. A solution of a game is a set of strategies that is an equilibrium (e.g., Nash equilibrium). It is applied in economics, politics, biology.

History

Foundations were laid by John von Neumann in 1928, and in 1944 the book 'Theory of Games and Economic Behavior' was published with Oskar Morgenstern.

How it works

In a game, each player chooses a strategy. Each player's payoff depends on the combination of strategies. A Nash equilibrium is a situation where no player can benefit by unilaterally changing their strategy.

💡 Game theory was used to analyze the Cold War and the arms race (Hawk-Dove model).
Game theory is a branch of mathematics that studies decision-making in the context of interaction among multiple agents. Formally, a game is defined by a set of players, sets of strategies, and payoff functions. Main types: cooperative and non-cooperative games, games with complete and incomplete information, static and dynamic games. Important concepts: Nash equilibrium, maximin, mixed strategies, core (for cooperative games). Nash's theorem on the existence of equilibrium in mixed strategies.

History

John von Neumann proved the minimax theorem in 1928. In 1944, the book by von Neumann and Morgenstern was published. John Nash introduced Nash equilibrium in 1950. In the 1960s, Thomas Schelling applied game theory to conflicts. In the 1970s, Reinhard Selten and John Harsanyi developed theory for dynamic and incomplete games. In 1994, Nash, Selten, and Harsanyi received the Nobel Prize.

How it works

Normal-form game: players simultaneously choose strategies, receiving payoffs. Nash equilibrium is a strategy profile where each player maximizes their payoff given the strategies of others. For games with complete information, backward induction methods exist (dynamic games). Cooperative games are solved via characteristic function and payoff distribution (Shapley value).

Open problems & fun facts

Computational complexity of finding Nash equilibrium (PPAD-completeness); Design of mechanisms that incentivize truthful behavior (mechanism design theory)
