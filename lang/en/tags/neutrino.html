Neutrinos are fundamental particles without electric charge, almost imperceptible. They are produced in nuclear transformations in the Sun's interior, in supernova explosions, and in nuclear reactors. Their amazing feature is neutrino oscillations: one type of neutrino (called flavors) can turn into another in flight, which indicates they have mass.

History

The neutrino was predicted by Wolfgang Pauli in 1930 to explain the mysterious energy loss in beta decay (when a nucleus emits an electron). They were first detected in 1956 in the 'Poltergeist' experiment at a nuclear reactor.

How it works

Occasionally a neutrino collides with an atomic nucleus, turning it into another element. Detectors are built on this: huge tanks of water where such collisions produce weak light flashes, which are registered by sensitive instruments.

Neutrinos are fundamental fermions (half-integer spin 1/2), belonging to the lepton family. They participate only in weak and gravitational interactions, making their interaction cross-section with matter extremely small — on the order of 10⁻⁴⁴ cm². There are three neutrino flavors: electron (νₑ), muon (ν_μ), and tau (ν_τ), each with a corresponding antineutrino. Neutrino masses do not fit into the minimal Standard Model and require an extension (seesaw mechanism, etc.). Neutrino oscillations are described by the Pontecorvo–Maki–Nakagawa–Sakata (PMNS) matrix, parameterized by mixing angles and squared mass differences. Measurements show that the sum of the masses of all three states does not exceed 0.12 eV. Neutrinos play a key role in astrophysics: they carry away 99% of the energy in a supernova core collapse, enable cooling of neutron stars, and influence nucleosynthesis in the early Universe.

History

In 1930, Wolfgang Pauli in a letter to 'radioactive ladies and gentlemen' postulated the existence of a light neutral particle to save the law of energy conservation in beta decay. Enrico Fermi in 1934 built the theory of weak interaction and introduced the term 'neutrino'. The first direct detection (electron antineutrinos from a reactor) was achieved by Clyde Cowan and Frederick Reines in 1956 in the 'Poltergeist' experiment. Later, the discovery of muon (1962) and tau (2000) neutrinos completed the picture. Experiments at Super-Kamiokande (1998) and SNO (2001) proved neutrino oscillations, which became the first direct indication of physics beyond the Standard Model.

How it works

Neutrinos are emitted in weak interactions, where W- or Z-bosons transform one particle into another. For example, in beta decay of a neutron (n → p + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ), an antineutrino is born. Due to the extremely small probability of interaction, neutrinos are registered in massive detectors: a neutrino strikes a nucleus or an electron, creating a charged lepton that emits Cherenkov or scintillation light. Oscillations arise because flavor states are superpositions of mass states. As the wave function propagates, it accumulates different phases for different mass components, leading to periodic changes in the probability of detecting a particular flavor — this effect is described by the oscillation formula.

P_{\nu_\alpha \to \nu_\beta} = \sin^2(2\theta) \sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta m^2 L}{4 E}\right)
P is the transition probability; θ is the mixing angle; Δm² is the squared mass difference of neutrino states (eV²); L is the distance traveled by the neutrino; E is the neutrino energy.
