The neutrino is a tiny ghost particle that barely interacts with matter. Imagine billions of invisible bullets piercing you every second. They are emitted by the Sun and other stars.

History

The existence of the neutrino was first postulated by Wolfgang Pauli in 1930 to explain the missing energy in beta decay. The particle was experimentally detected by Clyde Cowan and Frederick Reines in 1956.

How it works

Neutrinos are born in nuclear reactions, for example, in the center of the Sun. Like moths flying through a dense forest, they easily bypass atoms and even entire planets because they participate only in the weak interaction.

💡 About 100 billion neutrinos from the Sun pass through every square centimeter of your body each second, and you don't notice it.
The neutrino is the lightest known particle, having no electric charge. Like elusive spirits, it passes through planets and stars, leaving almost no trace. Its mass is so small that for a long time it was thought to be zero.

The neutrino is a fundamental particle of the lepton family, having three generations: electron, muon, and tau neutrinos. It is described in the Standard Model as massless, but the discovery of oscillations requires nonzero mass, making neutrinos an important window into physics beyond the Standard Model. Extremely weak interaction is due to participation only in the weak and gravitational interactions, making it an ideal probe for astrophysical processes.

History

In 1930, Wolfgang Pauli postulated the neutrino to conserve energy in beta decay. Frederick Reines and Clyde Cowan in 1956 first detected neutrinos from a nuclear reactor. Ralph Alpher and others predicted the cosmic neutrino background after the Big Bang. The phenomenon of neutrino oscillations was established at the end of the 20th century, leading to Nobel Prizes for Arthur McDonald and Takaaki Kajita in 2015, confirming nonzero rest mass.

How it works

The neutrino participates only in the weak interaction through the exchange of W and Z bosons. The general theory is described by the local gauge symmetry SU(2)_L × U(1)_Y. Oscillations arise due to flavor mixing via the Pontecorvo–Maki–Nakagawa–Sakata matrix, where flavor quantum states are superpositions of mass states. This violates lepton number conservation separately. The IceCube neutrino telescope detects Cherenkov radiation in Antarctic ice from high-energy neutrinos. The Pauli principle plays a key role for neutrinos as fermions.
