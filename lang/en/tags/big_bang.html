The Big Bang is a cosmological model describing the development of the Universe from an extremely high-density and high-temperature state. According to this model, about 13.8 billion years ago, space-time and matter emerged from a singularity, after which a phase of inflationary expansion began, leading to the formation of the modern large-scale structure. Over time, the Universe cooled, allowing quarks, protons, neutrons, and then atoms to form. The recombination of hydrogen made the Universe transparent to radiation, which we now detect as the cosmic microwave background — the 'echo' of the Big Bang.

History

The concept of an expanding Universe was born from the equations of Einstein's general relativity, which Alexander Friedmann solved in 1922–1924 without the cosmological constant, predicting non-stationarity. In 1927, Georges Lemaître independently proposed the 'primeval atom' model and linked expansion to the redshift of galaxies. Edwin Hubble in 1929, based on observations, confirmed the proportionality between the recession velocity of galaxies and their distance, providing direct evidence for expansion. In 1948, George Gamow, Ralph Alpher, and Robert Herman developed the theory of Big Bang nucleosynthesis, and in 1965, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson discovered the cosmic microwave background, firmly establishing the Big Bang theory.

How it works

The key principle is the expansion of space-time itself, described by the Friedmann equations derived from general relativity. According to Hubble's law, the recession velocity of galaxies is v = H₀ r, where H₀ is the Hubble constant. In the early stages, radiation dominated, followed by matter, and now dark energy, which influences the expansion rate. Big Bang nucleosynthesis explains the abundance of light elements: hydrogen (≈75%), helium (≈25%), and traces of lithium. The cosmic microwave background is the cooled radiation that reached us from the epoch of recombination; its spectrum perfectly matches Planck's law for black-body radiation.
