partition function

📊 0
The partition function is like a catalog of tickets in an energy lottery: it lists all possible energy states of a system and indicates the chance of each occurring.

History

The idea was born with the statistical mechanics of Ludwig Boltzmann in the 1870s, but in its modern form, the canonical sum was introduced by Josiah Willard Gibbs at the turn of the 20th century. Later, the quantum version accounting for particle indistinguishability was developed by Bose, Einstein, Fermi, and Dirac.

How it works

The summation is done with an exponential factor: the higher the energy of a state, the smaller its contribution—just as a steep climb on a mountain is encountered less often than a gentle slope on a walk. At low temperatures, only the lowest steps are 'populated'.

💡 Knowing just this sum, you can extract from it temperature, pressure, and even how a kettle on the stove will behave—everything that can be measured macroscopically.
Imagine a universal handbook of all microstates of a gas, where each is given a weight depending on energy and temperature. This handbook—the partition function—is the key that opens the door from atoms to thermodynamics: heat capacities, phase transitions.

The partition function Z is a central quantity in statistical physics, defined as the sum over all possible microstates of a system, weighted by the Boltzmann factor exp(-βE_i) in the canonical ensemble, where β = 1/(k_B T). It connects microscopic properties to thermodynamic potentials: for example, the Helmholtz free energy F = -k_B T ln Z. For quantum systems, the sum is replaced by a trace.

History

Boltzmann introduced the concept of statistical weight and entropy in the 1870s, but the explicit formula Z = Σ e^{-βE_i} for the canonical ensemble was introduced by Gibbs in 1902 in his book 'Elementary Principles in Statistical Mechanics'. Einstein and Bose in 1924 generalized it to quantum gases with integer spin, and Fermi and Dirac to half-integer spin, leading to Bose–Einstein and Fermi–Dirac statistics.

How it works

All thermodynamic quantities are expressed through Z: internal energy U = -∂ ln Z/∂β, heat capacity C_v = ∂U/∂T, pressure P = k_B T (∂ ln Z/∂V)_T. In the quantum case, Z = Tr(e^{-βĤ}), where Ĥ is the Hamiltonian. For non-interacting systems, it factorizes over single-particle states, but in general requires approximate methods.

Z = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i}
Z is the partition function; index i runs over all microstates of the system; β = 1/(k_B T), where k_B is the Boltzmann constant (k_B ≈ 1.38×10⁻²³ J/K), T is the absolute temperature in kelvins; E_i is the energy of the i-th microstate.
F = -k_B T \ln Z
F is the Helmholtz free energy; k_B is the Boltzmann constant; T is the temperature; Z is the partition function.

Open problems & fun facts

Calculation of Z for a strongly interacting system: no general analytical methods exist, and numerical calculations are exponentially complex.; Thermalization of isolated quantum systems: the applicability of the canonical ensemble to closed systems via the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis (ETH) remains unproven in general.; The role of long-range correlations: in systems with long-range interactions (gravity, plasma), the extensivity of entropy breaks down, and the usual Z does not yield correct thermodynamics.
