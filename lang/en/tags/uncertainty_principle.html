uncertainty principle

📊 11 articles
The uncertainty principle states that in the world of tiny particles, you cannot simultaneously know exactly where a particle is and where it is going. Imagine a blurry photograph of a bee: if you see its position, its wings are smeared, and if you capture the movement of the wings, the bee itself becomes blurry. So with quanta — the more precisely you know one thing, the fuzzier the other becomes.
💡 Because of this principle, subatomic particles don't have a definite location like a billiard ball; they are more like a smeared cloud of probabilities. Even in absolute vacuum, pairs of particles are constantly being born and dying — this is quantum jitter.
Heisenberg's uncertainty principle states that it is impossible to simultaneously measure with high precision the position and momentum of a particle. It's like trying to measure the length of a spring that is constantly vibrating: the measurement itself disturbs the system. This principle fundamentally limits our knowledge of the microworld.

History

Discovered by Werner Heisenberg in 1927. It arose from the mathematical formalism of quantum mechanics developed by scientists such as Erwin Schrödinger, Paul Dirac, and others.

How it works

The wave describing a particle has a width in space and a frequency in momentum. Just as a musical chord cannot be compressed into a single instant and simultaneously have a pure tone, so a quantum particle cannot be perfectly localized without uncertainty in its speed.

💡 The uncertainty principle is not related to instrument error — it is a property of nature itself. Moreover, it ensures the stability of atoms: without it, the electron would fall into the nucleus, and the Universe would be a compressed point.
The uncertainty principle is a fundamental relation in quantum mechanics that sets a limit on the joint precision of determining canonically conjugate observables, such as position and momentum. Mathematically, it is expressed by an inequality relating the variances of operators. This principle stems not from imperfections of measurement but from the wave-like nature of matter and the non-commutativity of operators.

History

Formulated by Werner Heisenberg in 1927 based on his matrix mechanics. Later that year, Jordan generalized it for arbitrary non-commuting observables. Robertson in 1929 derived the general inequality. Erwin Schrödinger introduced the concept of coherent states that minimize uncertainty. The principle became one of the cornerstones of quantum theory, sparking deep philosophical discussions (Einstein-Bohr).

How it works

The wave function of a particle in the position representation is related to the wave function in the momentum representation by a Fourier transform. The product of the widths of these distributions is bounded from below. The position and momentum operators do not commute: [x̂, p̂] = iħ, leading to the inequality Δx Δp ≥ ħ/2. Similar relations exist for energy and time, phase and particle number. The principle manifests in quantum tunneling, zero-point oscillations of oscillators, and the absence of particle trajectories.

\Delta x \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}
Δx — standard deviation of position, Δp — standard deviation of momentum, ħ = h/(2π) — reduced Planck constant, h ≈ 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s.
