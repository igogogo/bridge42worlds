History

Stars have been studied since antiquity, but their nature became clear only in the 19th-20th centuries. In 1838, Friedrich Bessel measured the parallax of the star 61 Cygni, determining the distance. In the 1920s, Arthur Eddington realized that the energy source of stars is thermonuclear fusion. In the 1930s, Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar and others developed the theory of stellar evolution.

How it works

A star is born from a cloud of gas and dust that collapses under gravity. In the core, thermonuclear reactions begin, creating pressure that balances gravity. The star lives as long as there is hydrogen in the core, then evolves into a red giant and ends its life as a white dwarf, neutron star, or black hole. Analogy: a star is like a campfire: as long as there is wood (hydrogen), it burns; when the wood runs out, the fire dies and embers remain.
