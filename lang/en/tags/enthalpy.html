enthalpy

📊 0 articles
Energy that a substance stores as heat and the ability to push or expand. By heating a pot of water, you increase its enthalpy: part goes to heating, part to expansion. It's like a battery: it's not just hot, but also exerts pressure on the pipes to heat the room.
💡 Melting ice absorbs 334 J per gram without raising the temperature — its enthalpy jumps. That's why a handful of snow thrown into a fire hisses and cools down faster than it seems.
The system's total 'heat plus work'. Imagine a traveler: the ticket is the internal energy, and the surcharge for luggage and visas is the work against the external environment. The sum of these costs is enthalpy. At constant pressure, heating or cooling equals the change in this sum.

History

The term 'enthalpy' was proposed by the Dutch physicist Heike Kamerlingh Onnes in 1909, but the idea of latent heat and heat content was developed earlier by Rudolf Clausius in the mid-19th century.

How it works

In an ordinary chemical reaction in air, pressure doesn't change, so the released heat equals the change in enthalpy. Methane combustion: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O releases 890 kJ/mol — that's the reaction enthalpy. In our bodies, the 'burning' of glucose with oxygen is also measured by enthalpy.

💡 The most powerful known chemical reaction — combustion of dicyanoacetylene in fluorine — releases about 10,000 kJ per gram, heating the flame to 5000 °C. The enthalpy of such a flame is comparable to the surface of a small star.
Enthalpy H = U + PV is a thermodynamic potential most convenient for processes at constant pressure. Its change dH = dU + P dV = δQ at P=const, i.e., equals the heat received. In statistical mechanics, enthalpy is computed through the isothermal-isobaric ensemble. It is widely used in chemistry (Hess's law) and engineering (calculation of steam turbines, air conditioners).

History

The roots of the concept go back to the work of Hermann Helmholtz and Rudolf Clausius (1850s), who called it 'heat content'. In 1909, Heike Kamerlingh Onnes introduced the modern name from the Greek 'to heat'. Later, with the development of statistical physics, enthalpy received a microscopic foundation as the average energy of a system at fixed pressure and temperature.

How it works

From the first law of thermodynamics: dU = δQ - P dV. At constant pressure, dH = dU + P dV = δQ. Therefore, the heat effect of a reaction at P=const equals ΔH. The second law of thermodynamics, through the Gibbs free energy G = H - TS, determines the direction of spontaneous processes. In engineering, the enthalpy of steam or refrigerant allows calculation of cycle efficiencies.

H = U + PV
H is enthalpy, U is internal energy, P is pressure, V is volume
\Delta H = Q_p
ΔH is the change in enthalpy, Q_p is the heat transferred to the system at constant pressure
