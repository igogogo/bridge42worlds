quantum tunneling

📊 3
Imagine you're rolling a ball over a hill, but it miraculously appears on the other side without even climbing over the top. For microscopic particles, this is routine – it's called quantum tunneling.

History

The phenomenon was first described in 1928 when George Gamow explained alpha decay of radioactive nuclei. Later, tunneling became key to semiconductor electronics (tunnel diodes) and explaining nuclear fusion in stars.

How it works

An electron in a transistor encounters an energy barrier, but due to its wave nature, it can 'seep' through, creating a tunnel current. This resembles water seeping through sand, though the sand seems like a solid barrier.

Quantum tunneling is a phenomenon where a particle overcomes a potential barrier whose height exceeds its total energy. From a classical standpoint, this is impossible, but in quantum mechanics, the particle's wavefunction decays exponentially inside the barrier and has a nonzero amplitude beyond it. This leads to a finite probability of passage. The effect is widespread: from radioactive decay and thermonuclear fusion to modern transistors and scanning tunneling microscopy (STM). In quantum cosmology, tunneling is considered a possible mechanism for the birth of the Universe from 'nothing'.

History

The first theoretical explanation of tunneling was given in 1928 independently by George Gamow, and also by Ronald Gurney and Edward Condon for alpha decay of nuclei. Subsequently, the phenomenon was applied in solid-state physics: Leo Esaki in 1958 created the tunnel diode, for which he received the Nobel Prize. In astrophysics, tunneling explained the mechanism of stellar nucleosynthesis (Hans Bethe, 1938). Later, Stephen Hawking used the concept of tunneling to describe black hole radiation.

How it works

Within nonrelativistic quantum mechanics, tunneling is described by solving the stationary Schrödinger equation for a potential with a barrier. The wavefunction inside the barrier (E < V₀) has the form ψ(x) ∝ e^{-κx}, where κ = √(2m(V₀−E))/ħ. The transmission coefficient (tunneling probability) for a rectangular barrier of width L: T ≈ e^{-2κL}. The Heisenberg uncertainty principle allows a temporary violation of energy conservation, making tunneling possible. This effect is also used to explain the cosmological birth of the Universe and Hawking radiation.

T \approx e^{-2\kappa L}, \quad \kappa = \frac{\sqrt{2m(V_0 - E)}}{\hbar}
T is the transmission coefficient (tunneling probability); κ is the decay constant of the wavefunction in the barrier; m is the particle mass; V₀ is the height of the potential barrier; E is the particle energy; L is the barrier width; ħ is the reduced Planck constant, ħ ≈ 1.054571817×10⁻³⁴ J·s.
