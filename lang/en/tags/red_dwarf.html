Red Dwarf

📊 0 articles
The most common type of star in the galaxy: small, cool, and dim, but incredibly long-lived. Red dwarfs live for trillions of years — longer than the current age of the universe. Imagine a glowing ember that will burn almost forever, unlike a bright bonfire.
💡 The closest star to the Sun, Proxima Centauri, is a red dwarf, and it has an Earth-like planet in the habitable zone.
The most common type of star in the galaxy: small, cool, and dim, but incredibly long-lived. Red dwarfs live for trillions of years — longer than the current age of the universe. Imagine a glowing ember that will burn almost forever, unlike a bright bonfire.

History

Red dwarfs were first classified in the early 20th century by spectral types. For a long time, it was thought they could not have planets due to low luminosity, but the discovery of exoplanets around the red dwarf Gliese 581 in 2007 changed that view. Now red dwarfs are a prime target in the search for habitable worlds.

How it works

Red dwarfs have masses from 0.08 to 0.5 solar masses. In their interiors, a slow thermonuclear reaction occurs, and convection fully mixes the material, allowing all fuel to be used. Due to low temperature, they emit mainly in the red and infrared range. Analogy: it's like a candle that burns very dimly but lasts for thousands of years.
