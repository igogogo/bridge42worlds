red dwarf

14

A cosmic candle burning so frugally that it will last for trillions of years — longer than the Universe has existed.

In practice: The most numerous stars in the Universe. Most known exoplanets, including those in the habitable zone where liquid water is possible, orbit them.
Red dwarfs are M-class stars (mass from 0.075 to 0.5 solar masses) with surface temperatures of 2000–3500 K. Their luminosity ranges from hundredths to a few percent of the Sun's. Thanks to full convection in their interiors, hydrogen is consumed extremely sparingly, so their lifetime exceeds the age of the Universe, and no red dwarf has yet left the main sequence. Many have planets, and terrestrial-type planets often lie in the habitable zone.

History

The first red dwarfs were observed as early as the 19th century. In the early 20th century, astronomers identified them as a separate class of low-mass stars. For a long time, it was believed that they could not have planets with life, but modern discoveries have shown otherwise.

How it works

Inside a red dwarf, hydrogen turns into helium, releasing energy, but the reactions proceed slowly due to low core temperature and pressure. Unlike the Sun, the star's material is fully mixed: hydrogen from outer layers constantly flows into the core, and helium does not accumulate. Hence, the star shines stably for trillions of years.

💡 The closest red dwarf, Proxima Centauri, has an Earth-sized planet in the habitable zone, despite frequent powerful flares from the star.
A red dwarf is a small and relatively cool star. Like a slowly smoldering campfire, it consumes fuel extremely frugally, so it lives very long. The red color comes from the low surface temperature — 2000–3500 °C (the Sun is about 5500 °C). Red dwarfs are lighter and dimmer than the Sun, but for every star like ours, there are several such dwarfs.

Red dwarfs are low-mass stars (0.075–0.5 solar masses) of spectral class M on the main sequence. Due to low core temperatures (a few million kelvin), thermonuclear fusion occurs only via the proton-proton chain (the CNO cycle does not operate). Full convection mixes all stellar material, ensuring a homogeneous chemical composition and preventing the accumulation of a helium core. Surface temperature (2000–3500 K) shifts the emission maximum to the infrared. Luminosity ranges from 0.0001 to 0.05 solar. The rate of thermonuclear reactions in such stars is extremely low, so their main-sequence lifetime reaches 10¹²–10¹³ years. Many red dwarfs exhibit flare activity due to strong magnetic fields generated by convection and rotation. During flares, ultraviolet and X-ray radiation can increase hundreds or thousands of times, creating extreme conditions for the atmospheres of close exoplanets.

History

Class M was identified in the Harvard spectral classification in the early 20th century. Henri Poincaré laid the foundations of the theory of convective stars, and later Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar described the structure of low-mass stars in detail. In the 1990s, surveys showed that red dwarfs are the most numerous type of stars in the Galaxy. Interest in them grew with the search for exoplanets: the first Earth-like planets in the habitable zone were found around red dwarfs.

How it works

Thermonuclear fusion in red dwarfs proceeds via the proton-proton chain: the core temperature is insufficient for the CNO cycle. The key evolutionary parameter is mass. At masses less than 0.35 M☉, the star remains fully convective throughout its life; at higher masses, a radiative core eventually forms. Energy generation is described by the equation of state for degenerate or ideal gas depending on mass. Due to low luminosity, the main-sequence stage lasts trillions of years. After exhausting hydrogen, the star (theoretically) becomes a blue dwarf and then a white dwarf. Full convection, through the dynamo mechanism, generates strong magnetic fields that cause powerful flares and coronal mass ejections. The emission spectrum follows Planck's law with a peak in the near-infrared; class M is characterized by molecular bands of titanium and vanadium oxides (TiO, VO).
