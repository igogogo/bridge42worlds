Dark Matter

📊 2 articles
A hypothetical form of matter that does not interact with electromagnetic radiation but reveals itself through gravitational influence on visible matter. It constitutes about 27% of the mass-energy of the universe. Imagine an invisible mass that holds galaxies together like invisible glue.
💡 Dark matter is not just 'dark', it is completely transparent: if you could see it, you would see stars on the other side of a galaxy as if through a ghost.
A hypothetical form of matter that does not interact with electromagnetic radiation but reveals itself through gravitational influence on visible matter. It constitutes about 27% of the mass-energy of the universe. Imagine an invisible mass that holds galaxies together like invisible glue.

History

First evidence was obtained by Fritz Zwicky (1933) while studying the Coma Cluster: he found that visible mass was insufficient to hold the galaxies together. Vera Rubin (1970s) showed that galaxy rotation curves are flat, indicating additional mass. Modern searches include experiments with WIMPs and axions.

How it works

Dark matter does not emit or absorb light, so it cannot be seen. It participates only in gravitational interaction. Its presence is detected by the motion of stars and galaxies, gravitational lensing, and the structure of the cosmic microwave background. It is thought to consist of slow massive particles (WIMPs) or light axions.
