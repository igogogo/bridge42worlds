oxygen

📊 12 articles
Oxygen is an invisible gas that fills our lungs with every breath. It is like a magical elixir of life: without it, fire goes out and fish in water stop breathing. Oxygen is born in the green leaves of plants under the sun, as a gift to us from generous nature.
💡 Oxygen is the most abundant element on Earth: it makes up almost half the mass of the Earth's crust and two-thirds of your body's mass. And it is oxygen that colors blood red by combining with iron.
Oxygen is a chemical element without which combustion and respiration are inconceivable. Just as a mill uses the flow of water to grind grain, the cells of our body use oxygen to extract energy from food. This gas makes up 21% of the atmosphere, and its constant circulation in nature connects the world of animals and plants.

History

The honor of discovering oxygen is shared by Swedish apothecary Carl Scheele (1771) and English clergyman Joseph Priestley (1774). However, the real revolution was made by Frenchman Antoine Lavoisier: he gave the new gas the name 'oxygène' (acid-former) and proved that it is oxygen that supports combustion and respiration.

How it works

Oxygen reacts with nutrients, like wood in a campfire: slowly, without flame, but with energy release. In the lungs, it enters the blood and, like on a conveyor belt, is distributed by red blood cells to all organs, where it helps 'burn' glucose, giving us heat and strength.

💡 Pure oxygen is toxic at depth: divers breathe helium mixtures to avoid convulsions. Also, liquid oxygen is a pale blue liquid that boils at -183°C and makes even iron burn.
Oxygen (O) is a chemical element with atomic number 8, the most abundant on Earth. In free form it exists as a diatomic gas O₂, making up 21% of the atmosphere. It is a strong oxidizing agent, forming oxides with almost all elements. Oxygen is a component of water, all organic compounds, and many minerals. Its high electronegativity (3.44 on the Pauling scale) makes it a key agent in biochemical energy synthesis.

History

Oxygen was first isolated by Scheele (1771) and Priestley (1774), but the decisive role was played by Lavoisier (1777), who refuted the phlogiston theory and showed that air consists of two gases: 'vital' (oxygen) and 'asphyxiating' (nitrogen). In 1781, Cavendish synthesized water from hydrogen and oxygen. In the 20th century, the Nobel Prize was awarded to Otto Warburg (1931) for his work on the respiratory enzyme using oxygen.

How it works

In mitochondria, oxygen serves as the final electron acceptor in the electron transport chain, being reduced to water. This process, coupled with ATP synthesis, obeys the second law of thermodynamics: the energy of chemical bonds in food is converted into the energy of high-energy phosphate bonds in ATP with heat dissipation. Without oxygen, the cell switches to anaerobic glycolysis, which is 18 times less efficient.
