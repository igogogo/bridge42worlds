nucleosynthesis

📊 1 articles

Nature is an alchemist: hydrogen and helium in stellar furnaces transform into the entire diversity of chemical elements that make up the world.

In practice: Nucleosynthesis explains the origin of all elements of the periodic table, from carbon in our cells to gold in jewelry, and is used in models of stellar evolution and cosmology.
Nucleosynthesis is the set of nuclear reactions leading to the formation of chemical elements. Big Bang nucleosynthesis (in the first minutes after the Big Bang, producing hydrogen, helium, and trace lithium), stellar nucleosynthesis (in stellar interiors, e.g., CNO cycle, triple-alpha process), and explosive nucleosynthesis (in supernovae) are distinguished. Through nucleosynthesis, the Universe evolves from simple nuclei to complex ones, providing chemical diversity.
💡 Iron is the end of the road for stellar fusion: its nucleus does not release energy when fused, so massive stars that accumulate an iron core collapse and explode.
In the beginning, the Universe had only the lightest elements — hydrogen and helium. But stars, like giant furnaces, inside themselves glue light nuclei into heavier ones (called nuclear fusion). This creates carbon, oxygen, iron. The heaviest elements, like gold and uranium, are born during supernova explosions or neutron star collisions.

History

The idea that elements are created in stars was proposed by Fred Hoyle in the mid-20th century. He showed how carbon could form in stars, and later, with other scientists, created a complete picture of element origin.

How it works

At the center of a star, the temperature is so high that atomic nuclei overcome repulsion and merge. It's like gluing balls of clay if you throw them together with enormous force. Energy is released, making the star shine.

💡 Iodine-127, essential for the thyroid gland, is primarily produced in the r-process — meaning your ability to regulate metabolism is thanks to a neutron star collision billions of years ago.
Nucleosynthesis occurs in various astrophysical environments. Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) in the epoch 3–20 minutes after the Big Bang fixed the ratio of light isotopes. Stellar nucleosynthesis includes hydrogen burning (pp chain, CNO cycle), helium burning (triple-alpha), carbon, neon, oxygen, and silicon burning. Rapid neutron capture (r-process) requires extreme neutron fluxes, likely in neutron star mergers (kilonovae). Slow neutron capture (s-process) occurs in AGB stars. Heavy elements are ejected into the interstellar medium, enriching subsequent generations of stars and planets.

History

Fred Hoyle in 1954 predicted a resonant state of carbon-12 necessary for synthesis from helium; experiments confirmed this soon after. Ralph Alpher and George Gamow earlier laid the foundations of BBN, calculating helium formation. Margaret Burbidge, Geoffrey Burbidge, William Fowler, and Fred Hoyle (B²FH) published a classic review of element synthesis in stars in 1957. The discovery of a kilonova in 2017 confirmed the r-process in neutron star mergers.

How it works

Nuclear reactions obey conservation of charge, energy, and nucleon number. In stars, temperatures reach 10^7–10^9 K, overcoming the Coulomb barrier. The tunnel effect allows reactions to proceed at energies below the barrier. Triple-alpha process: 3^4He → ^12C + γ, and then alpha capture creates oxygen, neon, etc. The iron peak is explained by the maximum binding energy per nucleon. For the r-process, densities >10^22 neutrons/cm³ are required, achieved during de-neutronization in neutron star ejecta.
