nucleosynthesis

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
18

All elements heavier than hydrogen and helium were forged in stellar furnaces and supernova explosions: from the carbon in muscles to the gold in jewelry — our body and the world around us literally consist of stardust.

In practice: Understanding the origin of elements and their abundance; dating the age of stellar populations; technologies using element synthesis (nuclear energy, medical isotopes).
Nucleosynthesis is the set of nuclear processes leading to the formation of chemical elements. Primordial nucleosynthesis (the first few minutes after the Big Bang) produced hydrogen (~75% by mass), helium (~25%), and traces of lithium. Stellar nucleosynthesis occurs in two stages: burning of light elements (up to iron) in stellar cores and explosive synthesis (heavier than iron) during supernovae and neutron star mergers. Main reactions: pp-chain, CNO cycle, triple-alpha process, s-process (slow neutron capture), r-process (rapid neutron capture).

History

In the 1940s–1950s, scientists George Gamow, Ralph Alpher and others proposed that the elements originated in the hot early universe. But it later turned out that most elements were created in stars. In 1957, the famous B²FH paper was published, which described in detail the processes of stellar nucleosynthesis.

How it works

In the center of a star, temperatures of millions of degrees cause nuclei to collide and fuse, releasing energy, like compressed springs releasing their stored force when joined together. The star successively burns hydrogen, then helium, carbon and so on, until iron is formed. Iron is 'nuclear ash', further fusion yields no energy. Then the core collapses, and the star explodes, creating elements heavier than iron.

💡 The element technetium, which has no stable isotopes and is almost absent on Earth, has been detected in the spectra of giant stars — this is direct evidence that stars continuously produce new nuclei.
Stars are giant thermonuclear reactors. In their interiors, from hydrogen nuclei (protons) heavier and heavier elements are assembled: helium, carbon, oxygen, and so on up to iron. This process is like stringing beads onto a thread. When a star ends its life in an explosion, it scatters the created elements into space, from which new stars, planets, and even living organisms eventually form.

Nucleosynthesis encompasses the entire history of element formation in the universe. Cosmological nucleosynthesis (BBN) took place at a temperature of ~10^9 K and a baryon density of ~10^-5 g/cm³ during the first 3 minutes, synthesizing mainly ^4He, ^2D, ^3He, ^7Li. The relative abundance of elements is determined by the expansion rate and the baryon-to-photon ratio. Stellar nucleosynthesis begins on the main sequence with hydrogen burning via pp-chains (energy release ~26.73 MeV) or the CNO cycle (at higher temperatures). Then follow the stages of helium burning (triple-alpha process: 3 ^4He → ^12C + γ, then ^12C + ^4He → ^16O), carbon, neon, oxygen, silicon burning, up to the formation of an iron core. All reactions proceed under gravitational confinement, and energy is released due to the mass defect (E=mc^2). Explosive nucleosynthesis during core collapse or thermonuclear explosion of white dwarfs produces iron-peak elements and rapid neutron capture (r-process), explaining the origin of elements heavier than iron (gold, uranium, thorium). Slow neutron capture (s-process) occurs in the envelopes of asymptotic giant branch stars.

History

In the 1920s, Aston and Eddington proposed that the energy source of stars is nuclear reactions. In the 1940s, Bethe and von Weizsäcker discovered the CNO cycle. In 1948, Gamow and Alpher developed the hot universe model, predicting the cosmic microwave background and primordial nucleosynthesis. The key B²FH paper (Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, Hoyle) in 1957 systematized the processes of stellar nucleosynthesis, including s- and r-processes. For this research Fowler received the Nobel Prize in 1983.

How it works

Nuclear reactions are initiated by the tunnel effect (quantum penetration through the Coulomb barrier). In stars, the weak interaction is essential, converting protons into neutrons, enabling the synthesis chains. Reaction rates are computed through cross-sections averaged over the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution (Gamow peak). The released energy counteracts gravity, creating hydrostatic equilibrium. As fuel is exhausted, the core contracts and heats up, triggering the next stage. For the synthesis of elements heavier than iron, an external source of neutrons is required; the s-process occurs at low neutron density (~10^8 cm⁻³), the r-process during an explosion, when the neutron flux is enormous (~10^20–10^30 cm⁻³). The equivalence of mass and energy E=mc^2 determines the energetics.

3 ^4 \mathrm{He} \rightarrow ^{12}\mathrm{C} + \gamma + 7.275 \, \mathrm{MeV}
Three helium-4 nuclei (^4He) fuse to form a carbon-12 nucleus (^{12}C) and a gamma ray (γ), releasing energy of 7.275 MeV.

Open problems & fun facts

The cosmological lithium problem: the predicted abundance of ^7Li is 3–4 times higher than observed in old stars.; Precise rates of key reactions: for example, ^12C(α,γ)^16O, which determines the ratio of carbon to oxygen in the universe.; Astrophysical sites of the r-process: the contribution of supernovae and neutron star mergers is not yet fully clear.; Nucleosynthesis in the first generation of stars (Population III): how elements formed in the absence of metals.
