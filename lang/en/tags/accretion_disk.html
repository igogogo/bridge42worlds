Accretion disk

42

A swirling disk of scorching gas around a black hole—a cosmic funnel in which friction heats matter to millions of degrees, emitting light. These disks power quasars and reveal the locations of invisible black holes.

In practice: Accretion disks are the main energy sources of quasars and X-ray radiation from binary systems with black holes and neutron stars. Studying them allows weighing black holes and investigating strong gravity.
An accretion disk is a flattened rotating structure of gas and plasma that forms when matter flows toward a massive compact object: a black hole, neutron star, or white dwarf. Due to conservation of angular momentum, the gas does not fall straight in but enters a circular orbit and slowly drifts inward while viscous forces (e.g., magnetorotational instability) redistribute angular momentum outward. The inner edge of the disk is determined by the last stable circular orbit, which for a non-rotating black hole is three gravitational radii. Closer to the center, temperatures soar to tens of millions of degrees, and the disk emits hard X-ray and gamma radiation.

History

The foundations of disk theory were laid in the mid-20th century. The key 'α-disk' model was proposed by N. I. Shakura and R. A. Sunyaev in 1973. Even earlier, in 1916, Karl Schwarzschild found the solution to Einstein's equations for a non-rotating black hole, and Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar in the 1930s determined the limiting mass of white dwarfs. In the 1960s, the discovery of quasars showed that accretion onto supermassive black holes explains their enormous luminosity.

How it works

Any cloud of gas has at least a slight intrinsic rotation. Falling toward the center, it conserves angular momentum (rotational impulse) and thus flattens into a disk. Then viscous friction plays a key role: it transfers angular momentum from the inner layers to the outer ones, allowing gas to move inward. The gravitational energy released in the infall is converted into heat and radiation, which we observe.

💡 The accretion disk of a supermassive black hole can outshine all the stars of its host galaxy, while the hole itself remains completely dark.
When swirling gas falls onto a compact object like a black hole, it flattens into a thin rotating disk—a sort of cosmic 'pancake'. The inner regions move faster than the outer ones: just as runners on the inner lane of a stadium outpace those on the outer lane. Friction between adjacent layers heats the gas to millions of degrees, making the disk glow brightly.

An accretion disk is a thin gas-dynamic structure in which matter rotates around a central gravitating body in nearly circular orbits. Equilibrium condition: radially, centrifugal force balances gravity; vertically, the pressure gradient. Viscosity, often sourced by magnetorotational instability, ensures outward transport of angular momentum and heating of the gas. In the classical model (α-disk by Shakura and Sunyaev), the viscous stress is parameterized as α times the pressure. The radiation spectrum is close to a multicolor blackbody with an effective temperature increasing toward the center. In strong gravitational fields, the inner edge corresponds to the innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO): about 6GM/c² for a non-rotating black hole and as low as 1.2GM/c² for an extremely rotating one.

History

The theory of disk accretion actively developed from the 1960s. The α-disk model, which became a standard, was published by N. I. Shakura and R. A. Sunyaev in 1973. The discovery of quasars (Maarten Schmidt, 1963) pointed to accretion as the source of their incredible luminosity. X-ray binary systems (e.g., Cygnus X-1) became natural laboratories for testing the theory. Modern instruments—Hubble, Chandra, Spektr-RG—continuously refine the picture, and the Event Horizon Telescope in 2019 directly imaged for the first time the disk around the supermassive black hole in the center of M87.

How it works

In a steady state, the disk structure is determined by the balance of gravity, pressure, and rotation. For a Keplerian disk, the angular velocity Ω = √(GM/r³). The radiation flux at radius r: F(r) = (3GMṀ / 8πr³) [1 − √(r_in/r)], where Ṁ is the accretion rate, r_in is the inner radius (ISCO). The total luminosity L = GMṀ / (2r_in). Angular momentum transport is provided by magnetorotational instability (MRI), generating turbulent viscosity. In a strong gravitational field, general relativistic corrections are important: ISCO moves closer to the horizon for rotating holes, increasing energy output. Part of the energy can be extracted from the hole's rotation via the Blandford–Znajek mechanism and focused into relativistic jets.
