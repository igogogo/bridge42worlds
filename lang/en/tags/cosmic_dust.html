cosmic dust

141

Cosmic dust is the smoky veil of the Universe, hiding the light of distant stars while simultaneously serving as raw material for new worlds.

In practice: Cosmic dust forms planets and even living organisms; moreover, dust clouds scatter radio waves, creating interference for satellite communications and navigation.
Cosmic dust consists of solid microparticles of carbon, silicates, and ices, born in the atmospheres of cool giant stars or during supernova explosions. It absorbs ultraviolet and visible light, re-emitting energy in the infrared range. Dense dust clouds can completely hide objects in the optical spectrum, so astronomers use infrared and radio telescopes to study them.

History

The existence of cosmic dust was hypothesized as early as the 19th century, but serious study began with the development of astrophotography in the 20th century, when dark spots blocking stars were noticed on photographs.

How it works

Just as dust in the atmosphere gives sunsets red hues, cosmic dust makes the light from distant stars redder. Small particles scatter blue light more strongly than red, so the red part of the spectrum predominantly reaches the observer — this effect is called interstellar reddening.

Cosmic dust consists of solid microscopic particles of irregular shape, ranging in size from a few nanometers to fractions of a millimeter. The chemical composition includes silicates (amorphous and crystalline), carbon compounds (graphite, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons), ices (water, methane, ammonia), and refractory metals. Dust forms in stellar atmospheres (especially the asymptotic giant branch) and is ejected into the interstellar medium via stellar winds, or condenses in expanding shells of supernovae and novae. In dense molecular clouds, dust catalyzes molecule formation and plays a key role in gas cooling, promoting star formation. Optical properties are determined by Mie scattering theory for particles comparable to the wavelength of light, leading to interstellar absorption and reddening. Polarization of light passing through dust clouds aligned by magnetic fields allows the study of the galactic magnetic field structure.

History

The existence of dark regions in the Milky Way has been known since antiquity, but their nature remained a mystery for a long time. In the 18th century, William Herschel suggested they were 'holes in the heavens'. Only in the early 20th century did Edward Barnard and other astronomers prove that they are clouds of light-absorbing matter. A decisive contribution was made by Robert Trumpler in 1930, who showed that distant star clusters appear redder and dimmer due to interstellar absorption, revealing the existence of scattering dust particles. Later, spectroscopic and infrared observations allowed detailed study of the composition and distribution of dust.

How it works

Cosmic dust interacts with electromagnetic radiation through absorption (photons turn into thermal energy) and scattering (photons change direction). Mie theory is used to describe scattering on spherical particles, and the Rayleigh approximation for very small particles. Dust also emits in the infrared range according to Planck's radiation law, modified for emissivity depending on composition. Chemical reactions on the surface of dust grains, including the formation of molecular hydrogen, are catalyzed by low temperatures and a large surface area. Elongated particles, aligned by the galactic magnetic field, cause partial linear polarization of transmitted light (polarimetry).
