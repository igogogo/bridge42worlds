cosmic dust

📊 91 articles
Cosmic dust is the invisible dirt of the Universe. Microscopic grains of carbon and silicates float between stars, obscuring their light. Without it, there would be no planets and us: rocks stick together from dust and worlds are born.
💡 Every day, about 100 tons of cosmic dust fall to Earth.
Interstellar dust is solid particles smaller than a micron, consisting mainly of carbon, silicates and ices. It makes up only 1% of the mass of the interstellar medium, but absorbs and scatters almost half of starlight, making it redder.

History

Herschel already noticed dark gaps in the Milky Way, but the nature of interstellar absorption was explained in the early 20th century by Edward Barnard. In the 1930s, astronomers proved that the dimming of light is caused by dust, not gas. Later, Fred Hoyle showed that heavy elements synthesized in stellar interiors later condense into dust grains.

How it works

Dust grains act like light filters: blue light is scattered more strongly, so through a layer of dust stars appear red — an effect familiar from the setting Sun. The grains themselves stick together in dense clouds, forming planetesimals — the embryos of planets.

💡 Some dust clouds contain complex organic molecules, including amino acid precursors.
Cosmic dust is solid microparticles scattered in the interstellar, interplanetary, and intergalactic medium. Typical size ranges from several nanometers to a micron. They consist of amorphous silicates (olivine, pyroxene), carbon compounds (graphite, amorphous carbon, PAHs), ices (H₂O, CO, CO₂, NH₃, CH₄), and refractory minerals. Dust is responsible for interstellar extinction, polarization, and infrared emission. It plays a key role in the thermal balance of clouds and chemical evolution: catalytic reactions occur on the surface of dust grains, forming molecular hydrogen. Dust is the raw material for planet formation in protoplanetary disks. It is observed in all types of galaxies and is actively studied in the infrared, submillimeter, and microwave ranges.

History

The existence of interstellar light absorption was suspected since the 19th century (discovery of dark nebulae). In 1909, Edward Barnard catalogued dark regions, hypothesizing that they were dense clouds of 'absorbing matter'. In the 1930s, Robert Trumpler quantitatively showed that interstellar reddening is caused by scattering, not a true color change of stars. Fred Hoyle and Wickramasinghe in the 1960s developed a model of dust as a mixture of silicates and carbon. Margaret Burbidge contributed to understanding the formation of heavy elements that condense into dust. The Infrared Astronomical Satellite IRAS (1983) and subsequent missions (Spitzer, Herschel) discovered giant dust reservoirs in galaxies.

How it works

The interaction of dust with radiation is described by Mie theory for spherical particles. The refractive index is complex: m = n + i k. The extinction cross-section Q_ext = Q_abs + Q_sca depends on the size parameter x = 2πa/λ. For x << 1 (Rayleigh scattering) Q_sca ∝ λ⁻⁴, causing reddening. Polarization arises with elongated particles aligned by the Galactic magnetic field. Dust also reprocesses ultraviolet stellar radiation into infrared: absorption of UV photons heats the grain to an equilibrium temperature of 10–100 K, and it re-emits in the far-IR. Emission features at 3.3, 6.2, 7.7, 8.6, 11.3 μm are attributed to polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs). In dynamics, dust is subject to radiation pressure, gas drag, and gravitational settling in disks. The Smoluchowski coagulation equation describes particle growth. A crucial process is H₂ formation on surfaces: hydrogen atoms adsorb, recombine, and desorb, heating the grain. This mechanism explains the high efficiency of molecular clouds.
