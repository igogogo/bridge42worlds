solar wind

📊 0 articles
Imagine the Sun is a giant dandelion that constantly blows away its fluff. But it's not fluff, it's tiny invisible particles—electrically charged particles that fly in all directions. This is the solar wind.
💡 The solar wind is so powerful that it can 'blow out' comet tails, making them glow far from the Sun.
Just as the wind carries plant seeds across a field, the solar wind carries energy and matter throughout the Solar System.

History

The existence of a particle stream was predicted by Eugene Parker in 1958, and it was proven by the Soviet Luna-1 and Luna-2 spacecraft.

How it works

The hot corona of the Sun is so heated that its particles start moving at enormous speeds and overcome gravity, escaping into space, similar to how steam escapes from a boiling kettle.

💡 Winds from other stars can be hundreds of thousands of times stronger than the solar wind, literally blowing away the atmospheres of planets.
The solar wind is a stream of charged particles (electrons, protons, and helium nuclei) flowing from the upper layers of the solar atmosphere at speeds of 300–800 km/s. It forms the heliosphere—the region that protects us from galactic cosmic rays.

History

The idea of the solar wind emerged in the 1950s based on observations of comet tails and geomagnetic storms. Eugene Parker mathematically described it in 1958, and confirmation came with the Luna and Mariner space missions. Later, the properties of the wind were studied by Voyager and Ulysses spacecraft.

How it works

The mechanism of solar wind acceleration is still under study, but a key role is played by magnetic reconnection in the corona, releasing energy. Particles move along magnetic field lines, and at Earth's orbit, the wind density is about 5–10 particles per cubic centimeter.
