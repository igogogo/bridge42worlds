Solar wind

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

The solar wind is an invisible shower of charged particles that the Sun continuously sprays in all directions, like a giant garden sprinkler.

In practice: The solar wind causes polar auroras, but it can also damage satellites and power grids; forecasting its gusts protects technological infrastructure.
The solar wind is a stream of plasma (a mixture of free electrons and ions) flowing from the solar corona at speeds from 300 to 800 km/s. It carries the Sun's magnetic field frozen into it, which, due to the star's rotation, twists into a Parker spiral. Slow wind comes from quiet regions, fast wind from coronal holes. At Earth, the solar wind shapes the magnetosphere and causes geomagnetic storms.

History

In the 1950s, physicist Eugene Parker predicted that the hot solar corona could not be held by gravity and must expand as a supersonic flow (faster than the speed of sound). In the early 1960s, the first spacecraft confirmed his theory.

How it works

This can be compared to steam over a boiling pot: the outer atmosphere of the Sun (the corona) is heated to millions of degrees, causing particles to accelerate and fly off into space, dragging the magnetic field with them.

💡 The solar wind creates a giant protective bubble around the Solar System — the heliosphere, which deflects most harmful galactic cosmic rays.
The Sun constantly emits a stream of tiny charged particles: electrons (light negative) and protons (heavier positive). This wind stretches comet tails and, when it hits Earth's magnetic field, gives birth to polar auroras — shimmering curtains of light in polar regions.

The solar wind is a continuous supersonic outflow of fully ionized hydrogen plasma from the solar corona. Acceleration is provided by the high pressure of the hot corona (temperature ~10⁶ K) and, presumably, wave processes (Alfvén waves). At a distance of several solar radii, the plasma speed exceeds the local Alfvén speed; this boundary is called the Alfvén surface. The wind then expands and cools, and its magnetic field twists into a Parker spiral due to the Sun's rotation. Interaction with planetary magnetospheres generates electric currents, auroras, and radiation belts. Direct measurements are made by the Parker Solar Probe and Solar Orbiter spacecraft.

History

The idea of particle outflow from the Sun was suggested as early as the early 20th century (Eddington assumed influence on comets), but the physical theory was developed by Eugene Parker in 1958. His work was met with skepticism until the Mariner 2 satellite registered a constant plasma flow in 1962. Further missions (Helios, Ulysses) revealed the three-dimensional structure of the wind.

How it works

The outflow is described by magnetohydrodynamic equations taking into account thermal conductivity and wave heating. In Parker's simplest model, the plasma pressure in the corona exceeds the pressure of the interplanetary medium, leading to continuous acceleration to supersonic speeds. The analytical solution of the equation of motion gives a transonic transition. The magnetic field frozen into the plasma creates the heliospheric current sheet and spiral structure. Modern models include kinetic effects and heating by coronal holes. Observations confirm predictions of the existence of slow and fast wind.
