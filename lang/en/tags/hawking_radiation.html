Hawking Radiation

📊 0 articles
Hawking radiation is a process by which black holes evaporate, emitting particles like a hot object cools down.
💡 Hawking radiation predicts that black holes are not eternal—they slowly lose mass and eventually disappear.
Hawking radiation is a quantum effect in which black holes emit particles due to quantum vacuum fluctuations near the event horizon. This leads to a loss of black hole mass.

History

Hawking radiation was predicted by Stephen Hawking in 1974 based on quantum field theory in curved spacetime.

How it works

Near the event horizon, virtual particle-antiparticle pairs are created. One particle falls into the black hole, the other escapes, carrying away energy, which reduces the black hole's mass.

💡 Hawking radiation is extremely weak: for a black hole with the mass of the Sun, the temperature is only 60 nanokelvins.
Hawking radiation is a predicted quantum effect in which black holes emit thermal radiation. It arises from quantum vacuum fluctuations in a strong gravitational field. The radiation spectrum is blackbody with a temperature inversely proportional to the black hole's mass.

History

Stephen Hawking in 1974 published a paper showing that black holes can radiate. This discovery united quantum mechanics, general relativity, and thermodynamics. Hawking radiation has not yet been observed experimentally due to its weakness.

How it works

In quantum field theory, the vacuum is not empty but contains virtual particle pairs. Near the event horizon, one particle may fall into the black hole, and the other escapes outward. The escaping particle has positive energy, while the infalling one has negative energy, reducing the black hole's mass. The process is analogous to evaporation.

The temperature of a black hole is inversely proportional to its mass M.
