quantum measurement

To measure a particle is to force it to decide, like asking a wave where its crest is and getting a point-like splash.

In practice: Quantum sensors that detect gravitational waves and devices that see through walls by changes in quantum states are built on this.
Quantum measurement is the process of obtaining classical information about a quantum system, radically changing its state. According to quantum mechanics, before measurement the system is in a superposition of possible values of a physical quantity; measurement irreversibly projects it onto one of these values. The probability of the outcome is determined by the squared modulus of the projection of the initial state onto the corresponding eigenstate. A key feature is that the result is probabilistic even with full knowledge of the wave function, and the act of measurement itself influences the system (observer effect).

History

In the 1920s, Werner Heisenberg and Niels Bohr formulated that measurement does not simply reveal pre-existing values but creates them. Erwin Schrödinger devised the paradox of a cat that is simultaneously alive and dead until the box is opened, to illustrate the strangeness of this rule.

How it works

Suppose we have a photon that is simultaneously polarized vertically and horizontally. Measuring polarization means passing it through a polarizing filter. If the photon passes, its polarization becomes strictly vertical, and any information about the horizontal component is lost. The outcome is random, but its probability can be precisely calculated.

💡 In the delayed-choice quantum eraser experiment, it appears that the decision of what to measure now can change the particle's behavior in the past.
In everyday life, measurement happens unnoticed: we look at a thermometer and see the temperature. In the quantum world, before measurement a particle has no definite position or velocity — it's like a cloud of probabilities. The act of measurement 'collapses' this cloud into a concrete point. It's like reading a book with uncut pages: until you slit them with a knife, you might think you've read any page simultaneously, but once cut, only one remains.

Quantum measurement is a central problem in interpretations of quantum mechanics. In the standard formalism, it is described by the von Neumann reduction postulate: upon measurement of observable O, the wave function collapses into one of the eigenstates of the operator Ô with a probability given by the Born rule. The observable is realized as a Hermitian operator. Measurement can be discussed in terms of projective measurements (projection operators) and more general POVMs (positive operator-valued measures). In modern approaches, decoherence explains the transition from quantum superpositions to classical probabilities via interaction with the environment, but does not solve the 'hard problem' — why one outcome is observed. The many-worlds interpretation avoids collapse at the cost of branching universes.

History

The problem was recognized by the founders of quantum mechanics: Bohr and Heisenberg in 1927–28 introduced the concept of wave function reduction. The Einstein–Bohr debate (1927–1935) revealed the paradoxical nature. Von Neumann in 1932 formalized two types of processes: unitary evolution and collapse. In 1964, Bell proved that any hidden-variable theory reproducing quantum predictions must be nonlocal. Aspect's experiments in the 1980s confirmed quantum nonlocality and emphasized the role of measurement. Decoherence theory (Zeh, 1970s) provided an understanding of the loss of interference, but the measurement problem remains open.

How it works

Consider measuring the spin of an electron. Spin is described as a two-level system: |ψ⟩ = a|↑⟩ + b|↓⟩. When passing through a Stern–Gerlach apparatus (a magnet with an inhomogeneous field), the electron's trajectory is deflected, entangling the spin state with spatial position. As a result, entanglement with the environment occurs, effectively implementing the measurement. The spin operator Ŝ_z with eigenstates |↑⟩ and |↓⟩ yields values +ħ/2 and –ħ/2 with probabilities |a|² and |b|². After measurement, the state collapses into the corresponding one. The Heisenberg uncertainty principle limits the simultaneous knowledge of non-commuting observables, such as spin components.
