An exoplanet is a planet that orbits another star, not our Sun. Imagine our Solar System as one apartment in a huge apartment building, and each apartment has its own planet tenants. Some exoplanets are similar to Earth, others to Jupiter, and there are truly strange worlds.
💡 The first exoplanet was discovered in 1992 — but not around an ordinary star, but a pulsar! That is a dead star that spins rapidly and sends out pulses like a cosmic lighthouse.
An exoplanet is a celestial body orbiting a star outside the Solar System. Just as fireflies betray their presence by glowing, exoplanets reveal themselves when they pass in front of their star (transit), slightly dimming it, or by their gravitational influence, causing the star to wobble.

History

The first confirmed discovery of exoplanets took place in 1992 around the pulsar PSR B1257+12. In 1995, Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz discovered the first exoplanet around a solar-type star — 51 Pegasi b, for which they received the Nobel Prize. Since 2009, the Kepler space telescope (with William Borucki involved in its development) has discovered thousands of planets by the transit method.

How it works

Exoplanets are found by observing the star's light: when a planet passes between us and the star, the star dims by a fraction of a percent—as if a fly flew in front of a light bulb. Another method: the planet, by its gravity, makes the star wobble slightly, and from the spectrum shift we determine its mass.

An exoplanet is a planet belonging to a planetary system of a star other than the Sun. Observational methods are divided into indirect (transit, radial velocity method, microlensing) and direct (observation in the visible or infrared range). The transit method detects periodic dips in stellar brightness as the planet crosses its disk, allowing measurement of the planet's radius. The radial velocity (Doppler) method is based on registering periodic shifts in the star's spectral lines due to its orbital motion around the common center of mass with the planet; it provides an estimate of the minimum mass. Combining methods yields density and composition. Astrometry, pulsar timing, and gravitational microlensing complement the arsenal. Key parameters: orbital period, semi-major axis, eccentricity. Atmospheric characteristics are studied with spectroscopy during transits. Exoplanets are classified by mass and size: terrestrial, super-Earths, Neptunes, Jupiters.

History

In 1992, Alexander Wolszczan and Dale Frail discovered two planets around the pulsar PSR B1257+12. In 1995, Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz discovered 51 Pegasi b — the first exoplanet around a main-sequence star, sparking intense interest. With the launch of the Kepler mission (2009) under William Borucki's leadership, an era of statistical discoveries began, confirming thousands of transit candidates. In 2014, the Kepler spacecraft discovered Kepler-186f — the first Earth-sized exoplanet in the habitable zone. The TESS telescope continues to search for nearby systems. Atmospheric spectroscopy has become possible with the Hubble and James Webb telescopes.

How it works

Exoplanet detection is based on physical principles: according to the law of universal gravitation, the star and planet revolve around a common center of mass, so the star exhibits periodic Doppler shift of spectral lines (Doppler effect), defined as Δλ/λ = v_r/c. From the radial velocity curve, the planet's mass is determined. The transit method relies on measuring the eclipse depth δ = (R_p/R_s)^2, where R_p and R_s are the radii of the planet and star. The orbital period is related to the semi-major axis by Kepler's third law: P^2 = (4π^2/G(M_s+m_p)) a^3. Atmospheric parameters are derived from the spectral dependence of transmission. Microlensing uses gravitational lensing to detect planets around distant stars.
