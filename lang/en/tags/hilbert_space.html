Hilbert space

📊 0 articles
Imagine an infinite room where each point is a possible state of a particle. Hilbert space is a mathematical home for quantum states, like an album for all possible photographs of Schrödinger's cat: both alive and dead at the same time.
💡 In Hilbert space, you can "rotate" quantum states like a magic kaleidoscope, and get superpositions - mixtures of different realities.
Hilbert space is a generalization of our ordinary three-dimensional space to the case of many dimensions, where each dimension corresponds to a certain quantum state. Like in a musical score, where each note sets the pitch, here each coordinate sets the probability of a particular measurement outcome.

History

The concept was introduced by the German mathematician David Hilbert in the early 20th century as part of research into integral equations. Soon Erwin Schrödinger used it to formulate quantum mechanics, representing wave functions as vectors in Hilbert space. John Stewart Bell in the 1960s applied it to prove the nonlocality of quantum entanglement.

How it works

In quantum physics, the state of any system, for example, an electron, is described by a vector in Hilbert space. Measurement is like projecting this vector onto one of the axes. The inner product of vectors gives the probability of transition between states. The evolution of the state over time is a "rotation" of the vector, determined by the Schrödinger equation.

💡 Hilbert space can be not only infinite-dimensional but also "rigged" to account for states like a perfectly localized particle, reminiscent of mathematical tricks with taming infinity.
Hilbert space is a complete vector space with an inner product, a generalization of Euclidean space to the infinite-dimensional case. In quantum mechanics, pure states of a system are represented by rays in a complex separable Hilbert space. Observables correspond to self-adjoint operators. The inner product of state vectors defines probability amplitudes. The state space of a composite system is constructed as a tensor product of subsystem spaces, giving rise to quantum entanglement phenomena.

History

The concept of Hilbert space was developed by David Hilbert in 1904-1910 while studying integral equations. In the 1920s, Werner Heisenberg and Erwin Schrödinger independently formulated quantum mechanics: Heisenberg used matrix mechanics, and Schrödinger used wave mechanics, but both approaches were unified within the framework of Hilbert space. In 1932, John von Neumann gave a rigorous mathematical axiomatization of quantum mechanics based on Hilbert spaces. Bell's work (1964) and experimental tests by Aspect (1980s) confirmed the fundamental role of Hilbert space in describing entangled states.
