Category theory is a formal mathematical structure that studies abstract properties of mathematical objects and their relations. A category consists of a class of objects and a class of morphisms (arrows) between them, satisfying axioms of associativity and existence of identity morphisms. It allows unifying different mathematical disciplines by revealing common patterns. For example, categories of sets, groups, topological spaces have similar constructions such as products and colimits.

History

Category theory was proposed by Samuel Eilenberg and Saunders Mac Lane in 1945 in the paper 'General Theory of Natural Equivalences'. Initially it served as a tool for algebraic topology, but later spread to algebra, logic, and computer science. Important contributions were made by Alexander Grothendieck, who developed sheaf theory and topoi, and William Lawvere, who applied categories to the foundations of mathematics.

How it works

A category is defined by a collection of objects and morphisms, where each morphism has a domain and codomain. Composition of morphisms is associative, and for each object there exists an identity morphism. Functors are mappings between categories that preserve structure, and natural transformations are morphisms between functors. These concepts allow formulating universal properties such as limits and colimits.
