Supernova

📊 1 articles
A powerful stellar explosion that can temporarily outshine an entire galaxy. This is the final stage of evolution for massive stars or a thermonuclear explosion of a white dwarf. Imagine a star as a balloon being inflated to the limit, then it bursts, scattering pieces in all directions.

A powerful stellar explosion that can temporarily outshine an entire galaxy. This is the final stage of evolution for massive stars or a thermonuclear explosion of a white dwarf. Imagine a star as a balloon being inflated to the limit, then it bursts, scattering pieces in all directions.

History

Supernovae have been observed since ancient times: for example, the supernova of 1054 created the Crab Nebula. In the 1930s, Walter Baade and Fritz Zwicky first linked supernovae to the formation of neutron stars. In 1987, supernova SN 1987A exploded in the Large Magellanic Cloud — the first visible to the naked eye in 400 years.

How it works

For massive stars: when the core runs out of fuel, pressure drops, and the core collapses, forming a neutron star or black hole, while the outer layers are ejected in an explosion. For white dwarfs: accretion of matter from a companion star exceeds the Chandrasekhar limit, triggering a thermonuclear explosion. Analogy: it's like the lower floors of a skyscraper collapsing, causing the entire building to fold inward and then explode.
