A supernova is a stellar explosion that makes the star billions of times brighter than the Sun. It happens when a massive star runs out of fuel and its core collapses, or when a white dwarf accretes too much matter.

History

Supernovae were first observed by Chinese astronomers as early as 185 AD. In the 20th century, Fritz Zwicky defined them as a separate class and predicted that they leave behind neutron stars.

How it works

In a massive star, nuclear fusion creates iron. But iron does not burn, and the core stops pushing outward. Gravity crushes the core into a neutron star or black hole, while the outer layers rebound and are flung into space.

💡 Without supernovae, there would be no life: they scatter carbon, oxygen, and iron across the cosmos – the very elements we are made of!
A supernova is the catastrophic finale of a star's evolution, accompanied by a huge energy release (~10^44 J) and synthesis of heavy elements. Types are distinguished: I (no hydrogen in spectrum) and II (with hydrogen lines). Type II supernovae occur from the iron-core collapse of a massive star (>8 solar masses), type Ia from the thermonuclear explosion of a white dwarf in a binary system upon reaching the Chandrasekhar limit. The explosion creates neutron stars or black holes and scatters matter, enriching the galaxy with elements.

History

The term 'supernova' was coined by Fritz Zwicky and Walter Baade in 1934, suggesting that core collapse leads to the formation of a neutron star. Previously observed 'guest stars', such as in 1054 (the Crab Nebula), were interpreted as novae. A type classification was developed by Rudolph Minkowski in 1941 based on spectra. In 1987, supernova 1987A in the Large Magellanic Cloud provided an opportunity to test theories and record a neutrino burst from the collapse.

How it works

For core-collapse supernovae (Type II): when the core exhausts its nuclear fuel by fusing iron, degenerate electron pressure can no longer withstand gravity. The core collapses at up to 70,000 km/s, reaching nuclear density. Neutronization occurs: p + e- → n + ν_e. A neutron star is born, and the shock wave powered by neutrino heating tears apart the outer layers. For Type Ia: a white dwarf accretes matter, reaches the Chandrasekhar limit (~1.4 M☉), triggering runaway carbon fusion that completely destroys the dwarf.

M_{Ch} \approx 1.4 M_{\odot}
M_{Ch} — Chandrasekhar limit, M_{\odot} — solar mass (≈ 2×10^{30} kg). Exceeding this mass makes the white dwarf unstable and it explodes as a Type Ia supernova.
