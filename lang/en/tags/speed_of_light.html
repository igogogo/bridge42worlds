Speed of Light

📊 0 articles
PopularSimpleAdvanced
A fundamental constant equal to approximately 299,792,458 m/s, the maximum speed at which any signals and interactions can propagate in nature. Nothing with mass can reach this speed, and massless particles (photons) travel only at this speed. It's like a universal speed limit in the Universe that cannot be exceeded.
💡 The speed of light is exactly defined as 299,792,458 m/s, and the meter is defined as the distance light travels in 1/299,792,458 seconds.

History

The first measurement of the speed of light was made by Ole Rømer (1676) by observing eclipses of Jupiter's moons. James Bradley (1728) used stellar aberration. In 1849, Hippolyte Fizeau measured the speed of light on Earth using a rotating cogwheel. Albert Einstein (1905) postulated the constancy of the speed of light in special relativity.

How it works

The speed of light in vacuum is the same in all inertial reference frames. This leads to time dilation and length contraction at speeds close to light. Light propagates as an electromagnetic wave; its speed depends on the medium (slower in water). The constancy of c is the foundation of modern physics.
