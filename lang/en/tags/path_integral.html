Path Integral

📊 0 articles
The path integral is a method in quantum physics that sums over all possible paths of a particle from point A to point B, as if the particle 'tries' all routes simultaneously.
💡 Richard Feynman came up with this method while watching ripples on water: waves propagate along all possible paths.
The path integral is a way to compute the transition probability of a quantum system from one state to another by summing over all possible paths (trajectories) with a weight determined by the action. Each path contributes exp(iS/ħ), where S is the classical action. In the classical limit ħ→0, the path that minimizes the action dominates (principle of least action).

History

The method was developed by Richard Feynman in 1948 based on ideas of Paul Dirac. Feynman used it to reformulate quantum mechanics and quantum electrodynamics, for which he received the Nobel Prize in 1965.

How it works

Imagine you want to go from one city to another. The path integral says: you need to consider all possible routes — straight, with detours, loops — and sum their contributions, taking into account the phase. The result is the probability amplitude. In practice, summing an infinite number of paths is done analytically using methods of functional analysis.

💡 The path integral is used not only in quantum mechanics but also in financial mathematics for option pricing (Black-Scholes model).
The path integral (Feynman integral) is a functional integral representing the transition amplitude as a sum over all paths in configuration or phase space. Formally: ⟨x_f|e^{-iHt/ħ}|x_i⟩ = ∫ Dx(t) exp(iS[x]/ħ), where S[x] is the action. The path integral is the foundation of quantum field theory, where it is generalized to fields. It allows computing correlation functions and scattering amplitudes. The method requires regularization and renormalization to remove divergences.

History

The idea of the path integral goes back to Paul Dirac (1933), who noted that the quantum amplitude is related to the classical action. Richard Feynman in 1948 developed the full formulation, publishing the paper 'Space-Time Approach to Quantum Electrodynamics'. Feynman used diagrams to visualize contributions. The method became key in quantum field theory and statistical mechanics. For developing quantum electrodynamics, Feynman received the Nobel Prize in 1965.

How it works

The path integral is defined as a limit of time discretization: splitting the time interval into N steps, inserting completeness at each step, then taking the continuous limit. For a free particle, the integral is Gaussian and can be computed exactly. For interacting fields, perturbation theory methods (Feynman diagrams) are used. The path integral is also applied in Euclidean formulation (imaginary time) to compute the partition function.

\langle x_f | e^{-iHt/\hbar} | x_i \rangle = \int \mathcal{D}x(t) \, e^{iS[x]/\hbar}
The probability amplitude for transition from x_i to x_f in time t equals the integral over all paths x(t) with weight exp(iS/ħ).

Open problems & fun facts

Rigorous mathematical definition of the path integral (the functional integral is not a measure in the usual sense).; The problem of non-perturbative methods: how to compute integrals for strongly interacting theories (e.g., QCD).
