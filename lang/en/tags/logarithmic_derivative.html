Logarithmic derivative

📊 0 articles
The logarithmic derivative shows how fast a function changes relative to itself — like the percentage growth of a bank deposit.
💡 The logarithmic derivative is used in physics to describe processes with exponential growth, such as radioactive decay.
The logarithmic derivative of a function f(x) is the derivative of ln|f(x)|, i.e., f'(x)/f(x). It measures the relative rate of change of the function. If f(x) grows exponentially, the logarithmic derivative is constant.

History

The concept dates back to the works of Leibniz and Newton, but is systematically used in differential equations and probability theory.

How it works

For a function f(x), the logarithmic derivative is computed as f'(x)/f(x). For example, for f(x)=e^{kx} it equals k. This is convenient when working with products and quotients, since the logarithm turns them into sums.

💡 The logarithmic derivative is used in mathematical physics to solve the Schrödinger equation.
The logarithmic derivative is the derivative of the natural logarithm of a function: (ln f)' = f'/f. It is defined where f ≠ 0. Properties: the logarithmic derivative of a product equals the sum of the logarithmic derivatives of the factors; for a quotient, the difference. It is invariant under scaling: (cf)'/(cf) = f'/f. Applications in differential equations, probability theory (likelihood function), complex analysis (logarithmic residue).

History

The concept of logarithmic derivative was implicitly used in the 17th century when solving differential equations. In the 19th century, it became a standard tool in analysis. In probability theory, the logarithmic derivative is related to the likelihood function. In complex analysis, it is used to count zeros and poles of meromorphic functions.

How it works

For a differentiable function f(x), the logarithmic derivative equals f'(x)/f(x). If f(x) = ∏ g_i(x), then (ln f)' = ∑ (ln g_i)'. This property simplifies differentiation of complex expressions. In complex analysis, the integral of the logarithmic derivative over a closed contour gives the difference between the number of zeros and poles inside the contour.

\frac{d}{dx} \ln f(x) = \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}
Relative rate of change of the function f(x).
