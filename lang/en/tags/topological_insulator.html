topological insulator

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A topological insulator is a crystal that is an insulator inside but conducts electricity on its surface like a metal, due to the 'twist' of electron waves.

In practice: Topological insulators could become the basis for ultra-fast and noise-resistant quantum computers, and are also used in thermoelectric converters.
A topological insulator is a material that is a dielectric with a band gap in the bulk but possesses conducting states on its surface (or edges) protected by topological invariants. The electronic surface states are described by the two-dimensional Dirac equation for massless particles and have a linear dispersion law. The most important property is insensitivity to disorder and impurities as long as time-reversal symmetry (or another protective symmetry) is preserved. Topological protection means that the state cannot be destroyed by small perturbations without closing the bulk band gap.

History

In 2005, scientists Charles Kane and Eugene Mele theoretically predicted that graphene could be a topological insulator. However, due to the weak effect, this did not happen. In 2007, Laurens Molenkamp and his group first created a topological insulator based on mercury telluride. Since then, many such materials have been discovered.

How it works

The secret lies in topology — a branch of mathematics that studies properties that do not change under smooth deformations. The quantum states of electrons in such a crystal are 'twisted,' and this twisting is preserved. Therefore, on the surface, electrons are forced to move in a certain direction, not changing course even when colliding with irregularities of the crystal. This is similar to how water in a river flows around a rock without stopping.

💡 If a layer of superconductor is deposited on a topological insulator, special particles — Majorana fermions, which are their own antiparticles — can emerge at the interface. Their existence was predicted by Italian physicist Ettore Majorana in 1937.
A topological insulator works like a sponge: its bulk does not let water pass, but the surface slides without friction. Similarly with electricity: the bulk of the crystal does not conduct current (it is an insulator), while the surface conducts it better than a metal because electrons move along it without scattering on defects. This happens due to a special property of electronic states called topology.

A topological insulator is a phase of quantum matter characterized by a nontrivial topological invariant (Z₂ invariant for systems with time-reversal symmetry). Unlike ordinary band insulators, the valence band wavefunctions have a nonzero Kane-Mele index. This leads to the emergence of topologically protected edge states crossing the band gap. The surface states form a Dirac cone and possess spin polarization rigidly tied to the direction of momentum, which prevents backscattering. Realization is possible in narrow-gap semiconductors with strong spin-orbit coupling (e.g., HgTe/CdTe, Bi₂Se₃). There are also topological crystalline insulators where the protective symmetry is crystalline rather than time-reversal.

History

The foundations were laid by work on the quantum Hall effect (1980s), when David Thouless, F. Duncan Haldane, and Michael Kosterlitz used topology to explain the robustness of Hall conductivity. In 2005, Charles Kane and Eugene Mele proposed that graphene, when spin-orbit coupling is considered, could be a two-dimensional topological insulator (quantum spin Hall phase), but the effect in graphene turned out to be negligibly small. In 2006, Bernevig, Hughes, and Zhang predicted the quantum spin Hall effect in HgTe/CdTe quantum wells. Experimentally, a two-dimensional topological insulator was realized by Laurens Molenkamp's group in 2007. Almost simultaneously (2008-2009), three-dimensional topological insulators — tetradymites (Bi₂Se₃, Bi₂Te₃) — with surface Dirac fermions were discovered.

How it works

A topological insulator forms due to band inversion driven by strong spin-orbit coupling. The bulk Hamiltonian can be described in the k·p approximation, and under certain parameters, a condition for a nonzero Z₂ invariant arises. Surface states are solutions of the two-dimensional Dirac equation H = v_F (σ_x p_x + σ_y p_y), where v_F is the Fermi velocity. Owing to spin polarization and chirality (spin direction is rigidly tied to momentum), backscattering is suppressed, and conductivity is quantized. Topological protection is destroyed only by perturbations capable of closing the bulk band gap, such as a strong magnetic field or breaking of time-reversal symmetry.

H = v_F (\sigma_x p_x + \sigma_y p_y)
H — energy operator (Hamiltonian), v_F — Fermi velocity (characteristic velocity of electrons on the surface), σ_x and σ_y — Pauli matrices (describe spin), p_x and p_y — momentum components of the electron in the surface plane.
