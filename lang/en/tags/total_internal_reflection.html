total internal reflection

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

Light is trapped inside a dense medium at a steep angle of incidence - like water not letting rays out when viewed from below. Basis of fiber optics and diamond sparkle.

In practice: Fiber-optic communication (internet), prisms in binoculars, medical endoscopes, touch sensors in screens.
Total internal reflection (TIR) occurs when light tries to go from a denser optical medium (with higher refractive index n₁) to a less dense one (n₂ < n₁) at an angle greater than the critical one. In this case, it is completely reflected back, and only an evanescent field remains beyond the boundary. This effect is indispensable in waveguides.

History

The phenomenon has been noticed since antiquity: when looking from underwater, the surface can appear mirror-like. In 1611, Kepler first described it mathematically, and later Snellius discovered the law of refraction, allowing the calculation of the critical angle.

How it works

Light travels at different speeds in different media - this is described by the refractive index. At the boundary between a 'slow' and a 'fast' medium, the beam bends. If the angle of incidence is too large, the bending becomes so strong that the light does not exit but returns. This happens only when transitioning from an optically denser medium to a less dense one.

💡 The brilliance of diamonds is a result of total internal reflection: due to the high refractive index (2.42), light repeatedly reflects off facets and exits at various angles, creating a play of highlights.
When transitioning from a dense medium (water) to a less dense one (air), light incident at a too shallow angle is not refracted but completely reflected. Imagine a ball rolling up a hill: not having gained enough speed, it rolls back down. The critical angle is the boundary beyond which total reflection begins.

Total internal reflection is an optical effect at the boundary of two dielectrics when the angle of incidence exceeds the critical one. From Snell's law n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂, when sin θ₂ > 1 the refracted ray disappears. Instead, an evanescent wave, exponentially decaying with depth, appears in the second medium: the penetration depth is on the order of the wavelength. Energy flux across the boundary is zero, but closely spaced objects can interact with this field (frustrated total internal reflection). According to Fresnel formulas, the magnitude of the reflection coefficient is 1, but the phase is shifted; the shift depends on polarization.

History

The first description of total internal reflection was given by Kepler in 'Dioptrice' (1611). In 1621, Snellius discovered the law of refraction, from which the critical angle is directly derived. In the 1820s, Fresnel developed the theory, including evanescent waves. In 1854, Tyndall demonstrated a light guide using a water jet - a prototype of optical fiber. In the 1960s, practical application of fiber optics began.

How it works

Condition for TIR: n₁ > n₂ and angle of incidence θ₁ > θ_c. Conservation of the tangential component of the wave vector gives k_x = (n₁ ω/c) sin θ₁. When θ₁ > θ_c, k_x exceeds n₂ ω/c, so the normal component k_z₂ becomes imaginary: k_z₂ = iκ. The field decays exponentially: exp(−κ z), penetration depth δ = 1/κ = λ₀/(2π n₁ √(sin²θ₁ − (n₂/n₁)²)). The evanescent wave carries energy along the boundary but not into the volume. The phase shift upon reflection differs for s- and p-polarizations. Frustrated TIR is analogous to quantum tunneling: when two prisms are brought within a distance of about δ, light partially penetrates into the second.
