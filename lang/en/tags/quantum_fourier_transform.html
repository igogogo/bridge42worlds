Quantum Fourier transform

📊 0

A quantum way to decompose a signal into pure tones—just as the ear distinguishes notes in a chord, but with unimaginable speed for giant 'chords' of data.

In practice: The heart of Shor's algorithm, threatening modern RSA encryption systems, as well as a key phase estimation step in quantum computing—from finding eigenvalues to estimating the periodicity of hidden patterns.
The quantum Fourier transform is a unitary mapping that transforms the computational basis |j⟩ into a uniform superposition with phases exp(2π i j k / N), where N = 2^n. It is the quantum analogue of the discrete Fourier transform and can be implemented with O(n^2) gates, providing an exponential speedup over the classical FFT, which requires O(N log N) operations on an N-element vector. QFT underlies algorithms for hidden periodicity and phase estimation.

History

The quantum Fourier transform appeared in 1994 as the central block of Peter Shor's factoring algorithm. It generalized the classical fast Fourier transform, discovered in 1965, to quantum states.

How it works

Imagine a row of tops with numbers. Each top can be given its own rotation (phase shift). QFT triggers a chain of rotations such that the rotations interfere (add up or cancel each other), and in the end, there remains one 'signal' with a pattern corresponding to the frequency. In a quantum register, this is implemented by a cascade of simple operations—Hadamard gates and controlled phase rotations.

💡 The quantum Fourier transform cannot be directly used to compress photos or videos because the result remains inside the quantum chip and is destroyed upon readout; however, for teasing out global periodicities it saves time by factors of thousands.
The ordinary Fourier transform takes a sound recording and shows what frequencies it consists of: bass, violin, whisper. The quantum Fourier transform (QFT) does the same with a quantum state, which is a 'soup of probabilities' (superposition of many possibilities). QFT redistributes the probability amplitudes so that after measurement, the number of the harmonic associated with the periodicity of the original function emerges with high probability. It works with avalanche speed: for a thousand options, an ordinary computer needs to go through a thousand numbers, but a quantum one manages by juggling the phases of a dozen qubits.

The quantum Fourier transform is a unitary transformation acting on an n-qubit register as QFT |j⟩ = (1/√N) Σ_k ω^{j k} |k⟩, where ω = exp(2π i / N), N = 2^n. An efficient implementation decomposes into a sequence of gates: a Hadamard gate H is applied to the first qubit, then controlled phase shifts R_k = diag(1, ω^{1/2^{k}}) with subsequent qubits, after which the procedure is recursively repeated for the remaining qubits. The inverse transform QFT^† is used to map the estimated phase to a bit result in algorithms like Shor's and Harrow-Hassidim's.

History

First described in the context of Peter Shor's factoring algorithm (1994). An efficient construction with a polynomial number of gates was developed by Don Coppersmith (1994) and simultaneously by Deutsch's group. Earlier, in 1985, David Deutsch showed the fundamental possibility of speedup through quantum parallelism. Mathematically, QFT is a realization of the Wigner transform over a finite field and is closely related to group representation theory.

How it works

The quantum circuit begins with the state |x_1 x_2 ... x_n⟩. H is applied to the first qubit, creating a superposition (|0⟩ + e^{2π i 0.x_1}|1⟩). Then a controlled R_2 from the second qubit is applied, adding to the phase the contribution exp(2π i 0.x_1 x_2), and so on up to R_n. The same operations are repeated on the second qubit with rotations R_2...R_{n-1}, and so on. The final amplitude for state |k⟩ equals (1/√N) Σ_j exp(2π i j k / N) x_j, where x_j is the input amplitude. Upon measurement, the output probability distribution is concentrated near a frequency that is a multiple of 1/T, if the input data contained a hidden periodicity T.

\langle k | QFT | j \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \exp\left(\frac{2\pi i j k}{N}\right)
|j⟩ is the input basis state (j in decimal representation), |k⟩ is the output computational basis state, N = 2^n is the dimension of the Hilbert space (n is the number of qubits), i is the imaginary unit, exp is the exponential function.

Open problems & fun facts

Implementation accuracy suffers from decoherence and noise in multi-qubit gates, limiting the register size to tens of qubits in modern experiments.; QFT provides a speedup only for problems with hidden periodicity; for an arbitrary Fourier transform of a data array, the quantum method offers no advantage.; The need for an inverse QFT in most algorithms doubles the required sequence length.
