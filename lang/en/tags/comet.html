Comet

📊 0 articles
A small icy body in the Solar System that, when approaching the Sun, forms a tail of gas and dust. Comets are 'dirty snowballs' of ice, dust, and organics. Imagine taking a piece of ice out into the sun — it will start to melt and evaporate, forming a cloud of vapor.
💡 Halley's Comet returns every 75-76 years, and in 2061 it will be visible to the naked eye again.

History

Comets have been known since antiquity, often seen as omens of disaster. Edmund Halley in 1705 predicted the return of a comet (now Halley's Comet) based on his calculations. In 1950, Jan Oort proposed the existence of a comet cloud at the edge of the Solar System (the Oort Cloud). The Rosetta mission in 2014 landed a probe on comet 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko for the first time.

How it works

When a comet approaches the Sun, ice sublimates, ejecting gas and dust. The solar wind and light pressure form two tails: a gas (ion) tail pointing away from the Sun, and a dust tail curved along the orbit. Analogy: it's like a snowball you throw into a fire — it hisses, emits steam, and leaves a trail.
