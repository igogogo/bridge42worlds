Connection

📊 0 articles
A connection is a rule that tells how to transport vectors from one point to another along a curve without changing their direction, as if you were carrying a compass needle along a curved road.
💡 The connection is used in general relativity: it determines how particles move in curved spacetime.
A connection is an additional structure on a manifold that allows comparing vectors at different points, i.e., it defines parallel transport. It determines how a vector changes when moving along a curve so that it remains 'parallel to itself'. A connection also defines the covariant derivative — a way to differentiate vector fields.

History

The concept of a connection was introduced by Tullio Levi-Civita in 1917 for Riemannian manifolds. Then Élie Cartan generalized it to arbitrary bundles, introducing the concept of a connection on a principal bundle. In physics, the connection became key in gauge theories.

How it works

Imagine you are walking on a curved surface carrying a spear, trying to keep it pointing in the same direction. The connection is the rule that tells how to rotate the spear so that it remains 'parallel' relative to the surface. If the surface is curved, after going around a closed loop the spear may end up rotated — this is the effect of curvature.

💡 The connection in gauge theories (e.g., electrodynamics) is the same as the vector potential A_μ, which describes the electromagnetic field.
A connection is a mathematical object that defines a rule for parallel transport of vectors along curves on a manifold. On a Riemannian manifold, there is a unique metric connection without torsion — the Levi-Civita connection. In general, a connection on a vector bundle is a linear operator ∇: Γ(E) → Γ(E⊗T^*M) satisfying the Leibniz rule. A connection on a principal bundle is given by a 1-form with values in the Lie algebra of the structure group. The curvature of a connection is a 2-form measuring the non-commutativity of covariant derivatives.

History

Tullio Levi-Civita in 1917 introduced parallel transport on Riemannian manifolds. Élie Cartan in the 1920s developed a general theory of connections, including connections on frame bundles. In the 1950s, Shiing-Shen Chern and others applied connections to gauge theories. In physics, the connection became the foundation for describing fundamental interactions: electromagnetic, weak, and strong.

How it works

A connection ∇ defines a covariant derivative: for a vector field X and a vector field Y, ∇_X Y is the derivative of Y in the direction of X. In coordinates, the connection is given by Christoffel symbols Γ^k_{ij}: ∇_{∂_i} ∂_j = Γ^k_{ij} ∂_k. Parallel transport of a vector v along a curve γ(t) is the solution to the equation ∇_{γ'(t)} v(t) = 0. Curvature R(X,Y) = [∇_X, ∇_Y] - ∇_{[X,Y]} measures the non-commutativity of covariant derivatives.

\nabla_{\partial_i} \partial_j = \Gamma^k_{ij} \partial_k
The covariant derivative of a basis vector field ∂_j in the direction ∂_i is expressed through Christoffel symbols Γ^k_{ij}.
R(X,Y)Z = \nabla_X \nabla_Y Z - \nabla_Y \nabla_X Z - \nabla_{[X,Y]} Z
Curvature measures the non-commutativity of covariant derivatives.
