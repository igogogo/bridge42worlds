Cosmological Constant

📊 0 articles
The cosmological constant is a mysterious force that causes the Universe to expand faster and faster, like an invisible engine.
💡 Einstein introduced it to make the Universe static, then called it his 'biggest blunder,' but it turned out to exist after all.
The cosmological constant (Λ) is a parameter in the equations of general relativity that describes dark energy, causing the accelerated expansion of the Universe. It manifests as negative pressure that stretches space.

History

Einstein introduced Λ in 1917 for a static Universe, but after the discovery of expansion (Hubble, 1929), he abandoned it. In 1998, the discovery of accelerated expansion revived Λ.

How it works

The cosmological constant acts as antigravity: it repels galaxies from each other, causing the Universe to expand faster.

💡 The value of the cosmological constant is extremely small (≈10⁻⁵² m⁻²), but its influence is noticeable on cosmological scales.
The cosmological constant Λ is a term in Einstein's equations representing the energy density of the vacuum. It leads to the accelerated expansion of the Universe. In modern cosmology, Λ is the main component of dark energy, responsible for the observed acceleration.

History

Einstein introduced Λ in 1917 for a static model of the Universe. After Hubble's discovery of expansion in 1929, he abandoned it. In 1998, observations of Type Ia supernovae showed that the expansion is accelerating, leading to the revival of Λ as a model for dark energy.

How it works

The cosmological constant enters Einstein's equations as an additional term: R_μν - 1/2 g_μν R + Λ g_μν = 8πG T_μν. It creates negative pressure p = -ρc², which counteracts gravity and accelerates expansion. Λ is time-independent and homogeneous.

R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}g_{\mu\nu}R + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}
