A neutron star is a compact object representing a degenerate state of matter where the pressure of degenerate neutron gas counteracts gravitational collapse. It forms from the cores of stars with masses of 8–25 M☉ as a result of gravitational collapse during a Type II or Ib/c supernova explosion. Typical mass is 1.4–2.2 M☉, radius 10–13 km. It consists of an outer crust of iron, a neutron-rich inner crust, and outer and inner cores where exotic states may exist: hyperons, quark-gluon plasma. Characterized by extreme magnetic fields (10^8–10^15 G) and rapid rotation (periods from milliseconds to seconds), which produces pulsar emission.

History

The neutron star hypothesis was proposed by Walter Baade and Fritz Zwicky in 1934, who suggested that supernovae create superdense bodies of neutrons. In 1967, Jocelyn Bell Burnell, a graduate student of Anthony Hewish, discovered the first pulsar (CP 1919) by detecting strictly periodic radio pulses with a period of 1.337 s, confirming the existence of neutron stars. In 1974, Russell Hulse and Joseph Taylor discovered the binary pulsar PSR B1913+16, whose study proved the existence of gravitational waves. Fritz Zwicky also played a key role in early theory, and although Jocelyn Bell Burnell co-authored the landmark discovery, her supervisor received the Nobel Prize.

How it works

The structure of a neutron star is sustained by the equation of state of supranuclear density. The outer layers are a crystalline lattice of iron nuclei; below is the 'nuclear pasta' phase (complex nuclear shapes: gnocchi, spaghetti, lasagna, Swiss cheese). The core is dominated by neutron superfluid. Gravitational equilibrium is described by the Einstein equations of general relativity, as the compactness (GM/Rc²) approaches 0.2–0.3. The magnetic field arises from conservation of magnetic flux of the progenitor star. Acceleration of relativistic particles in the magnetosphere, especially in the light cylinder, explains the coherent emission of pulsars. Planck's radiation law describes the thermal spectrum, and the Doppler effect shapes the pulses as the star rotates. The mass is limited by the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit (about 2.2 M☉), beyond which collapse into a black hole occurs, related to the Pauli exclusion principle for neutrons.
