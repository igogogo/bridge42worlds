gauge invariance

📊 0 articles
Gauge invariance is a rule by which certain changes in the description of particles do not alter the predictions of physics. A direct analogy: if you measure the height of a mountain from sea level or from its base, the mountain itself does not change—similarly in physics, shifting the "reference point" of a field does not affect real effects.
💡 Thanks to gauge invariance, electric charge is always conserved—nothing can appear out of nothing.
Gauge invariance is a fundamental property of physical theories whereby certain transformations of fields do not change observable quantities. It is akin to how rotating a coordinate grid does not change the distance between cities: the mathematical description may look different, but the physical reality is the same.

History

The idea of gauge symmetry arose in classical electrodynamics: Maxwell's equations are invariant with respect to changes in potentials. In 1918, Hermann Weyl proposed the concept of gauge invariance to unify gravity and electromagnetism; later it became the foundation of modern field theories.

How it works

In electrodynamics, gauge invariance means we can add an arbitrary function to the electric potential, and Maxwell's equations remain unchanged. Analogy: when riding an elevator in a skyscraper, you feel only the acceleration, not the constant velocity—only the change in potential matters, not its absolute value.

💡 All fundamental interactions (except gravity) are described by gauge theories, and their carriers are gauge bosons: photons for electromagnetism, gluons for the strong interaction, W and Z for the weak.
Gauge invariance is the property of a physical system's Lagrangian to remain unchanged under local field transformations that depend on coordinates. In quantum field theory, it requires the introduction of compensating vector fields, which become the carriers of fundamental forces. For example, electrodynamics possesses U(1) gauge symmetry: the transformation ψ → e^{iα(x)}ψ is compensated by adding the electromagnetic field A_μ. Non-Abelian gauge theories (SU(2), SU(3)) underlie the Standard Model and describe the weak and strong interactions. Gauge invariance guarantees the renormalizability of the theory and the conservation of charges.

History

The concept of gauge invariance arose from the work of Hermann Weyl in 1918, who attempted to unify gravity and electromagnetism through scale stretching ("gauge"). The modern form of gauge symmetry as local phase invariance was recognized after the advent of quantum mechanics; Vladimir Fock and Fritz London showed that electromagnetic interaction is introduced through the requirement of gauge invariance of the wave function. The development of non-Abelian gauge theories by Chen-Ning Yang and Robert Mills in 1954 led to the creation of quantum chromodynamics. Emmy Noether's 1918 theorem established the connection between symmetries and conservation laws, fundamental for understanding gauge theories.

How it works

Gauge invariance is implemented by replacing the ordinary derivative with a covariant derivative: ∂_μ → D_μ = ∂_μ - i g A_μ, where A_μ is the gauge field, g is the coupling constant. Under a local phase transformation of the wave function ψ → e^{iα(x)}ψ, the gauge field transforms as A_μ → A_μ - (1/g) ∂_μ α(x), which preserves the invariance of the Lagrangian. In non-Abelian symmetry groups, the fields themselves interact with each other, leading to complex dynamics such as confinement in quantum chromodynamics. According to Noether's theorem, gauge symmetry ensures current conservation ∂_μ J^μ = 0 and the corresponding charge.

D_\mu = \partial_\mu - i \frac{e}{\hbar c} A_\mu
D_μ — covariant derivative, \partial_μ — partial derivative with respect to coordinate μ, i — imaginary unit, e — elementary charge (e ≈ 1.602×10⁻¹⁹ C), \hbar — reduced Planck constant (\hbar ≈ 1.055×10⁻³⁴ J·s), c — speed of light (c ≈ 2.998×10⁸ m/s), A_μ — component of the electromagnetic potential.
\partial_\mu J^\mu = 0
J^μ — conserved current, \partial_μ — 4-divergence; from gauge invariance follows the conservation of electric charge.
A_\mu \to A_\mu - \frac{1}{g} \partial_\mu \alpha(x)
A_μ — gauge field, g — coupling constant, α(x) — arbitrary function of coordinates.
