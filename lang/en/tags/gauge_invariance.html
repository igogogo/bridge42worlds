Gauge Invariance

📊 0 articles
Gauge invariance means that physics does not change if we 'color' the fields differently, but the essence remains the same.
💡 Gauge invariance is the reason for the existence of photons, gluons, and other force-carrying particles.
Gauge invariance is a symmetry where physical laws do not change under local transformations of fields. For example, in electrodynamics, one can change the potential by a constant, and the electric field remains the same. This requires the introduction of gauge fields (photons) that mediate the interaction.

History

The idea of gauge invariance arose in electrodynamics (Maxwell, 1860s). In 1954, Chen Ning Yang and Robert Mills generalized it to non-Abelian groups, creating the foundation for theories of weak and strong interactions.

How it works

Imagine you can change the phase of an electron's wave function differently at each point in space, but physics should not change. To make this work, you need to add a field (photon) that 'adjusts' to these changes.

💡 Gauge invariance is the only way to describe particle interactions in quantum field theory without contradictions.
Gauge invariance is a local symmetry under which the Lagrangian of a theory does not change under transformations that depend on spacetime points. To preserve invariance, gauge fields (e.g., the electromagnetic field A_μ) must be introduced. These fields are carriers of interactions. The Standard Model is based on the gauge group SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1).

History

Gauge invariance in electrodynamics was recognized by Maxwell and developed by Hermann Weyl in 1918 (the term 'gauge'). In 1954, Yang and Mills constructed a non-Abelian gauge theory. In the 1960s, Glashow, Weinberg, and Salam unified electromagnetic and weak interactions into a SU(2)×U(1) gauge theory. Quantum chromodynamics (strong interaction) is based on the group SU(3).

How it works

Consider the Lagrangian of a free fermion: L = iψ̄γ^μ∂_μψ. It is invariant under global phase transformations ψ → e^{iα}ψ. To make the symmetry local (α → α(x)), we replace the derivative with a covariant one: D_μ = ∂_μ - igA_μ, where A_μ is the gauge field. The field A_μ transforms as A_μ → A_μ + ∂_μα, and the Lagrangian becomes invariant. The kinetic term for A_μ yields Maxwell's equations.

D_\mu = \partial_\mu - i g A_\mu
Replacing the ordinary derivative with a covariant one compensates for the local gauge transformation.

Open problems & fun facts

The confinement problem in quantum chromodynamics: why are quarks not observed freely?; Does a gauge theory of gravity exist (e.g., supergravity)?
