A nebula is a giant cloud of gas and dust in space. It looks like smoke from a campfire, but hundreds of light-years across. New stars are born inside such clouds.
💡 The most beautiful nebula is perhaps the Horsehead Nebula. It resembles a foal's head floating in space, though it is actually dust obscuring the light of a distant star.
A nebula is an interstellar cloud of gas and dust, illuminated by nearby stars or stirred up by shock waves. In dark nebulae, like in cocoons, stellar embryos hide, and planetary nebulae are the ejected shells of dying stars, resembling soap bubbles.

History

Charles Messier first cataloged nebulae in the 18th century, mistaking them for comets. Later, William Herschel divided them into star clusters and true clouds. Photographic spectra in the 19th century revealed their gaseous composition — the work of William Huggins.

How it works

Gas in the nebula is ionized by ultraviolet light from young hot stars. Electrons, falling back onto nuclei, emit light of very specific colors — a palette by which chemists recognize hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen. Radiation pressure inflates the material, and supernova shock waves stir the layers like a spoon in a pot.

A nebula is a vast interstellar cloud of gas and dust, observed either in emission lines of ionized hydrogen (H II region), as reflection glow (starlight reflected by dust), or as a dark silhouette against bright backgrounds. Planetary nebulae are outflowing shells of stars on the asymptotic giant branch, while supernova remnants are shock-compressed filaments after the explosion of a massive star.

History

The first telescopic observations of nebulae were recorded by Galileo, but the first systematization belongs to Charles Messier, who published a catalog of 110 objects to avoid confusing them with comets. In the 19th century, William and John Herschel compiled a general catalog, later becoming the New General Catalogue (NGC). Spectral studies by William Huggins (1864) proved the gaseous nature of many nebulae. Modern theory of star formation in nebulae builds on the work of James Jeans, while emission mechanisms rely on quantum mechanics (the Schrödinger equation for energy levels).

How it works

The main emission source of emission nebulae is recombination and cascade transitions in hydrogen atoms, described by the Schrödinger equation and selection rules. The spectral shape is explained by Maxwell's equations for moving charges and Planck's radiation law for the thermal background of dust. The balance between gravitational contraction and gas pressure is regulated by Noether's theorem (energy conservation) and the second law of thermodynamics during radiation. In reflection nebulae, light polarization is described by Mie scattering theory, and the particle size distribution follows power laws.
