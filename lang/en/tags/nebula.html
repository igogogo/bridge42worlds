Nebula

📊 0 articles
A giant cloud of gas and dust in space where stars are born or which remains after their death. Nebulae can be emission, reflection, or dark. Imagine a cosmic watercolor painting: bright colors are glowing gas, and dark patches are dust clouds.
💡 The Orion Nebula is one of the brightest and closest star-forming regions, visible to the naked eye as a fuzzy patch in Orion's sword.

History

The term 'nebula' was used for any fuzzy object in the sky until the 1920s when Edwin Hubble proved that many are separate galaxies. In the 18th century, Charles Messier compiled a catalog of nebulae to avoid confusing them with comets. Modern telescopes like Hubble have revealed the incredible beauty of nebulae.

How it works

Emission nebulae glow because ionized gas (usually hydrogen) emits light upon recombination. Reflection nebulae scatter light from nearby stars. Dark nebulae are dense dust clouds that block light. Analogy: a nebula is like a cloud on Earth: some clouds glow from the sun, others are dark and rainy.
