Curvature is a measure of how much a geometric object (curve, surface, manifold) deviates from flat (Euclidean) space. For a surface, curvature at a point can be positive (sphere), negative (saddle), or zero (plane). In differential geometry, curvature is described by the Riemann tensor, which shows how the direction of a vector changes when parallel transported around a closed loop.

History

Gauss in 1827 introduced Gaussian curvature for surfaces. Riemann generalized the concept to multidimensional manifolds. Einstein in 1915 linked the curvature of spacetime to the distribution of matter through field equations.

How it works

Imagine you are walking on the surface of a sphere, holding an arrow pointing forward. If you go around a closed loop, the arrow will be rotated relative to its original direction. The angle of rotation is proportional to the curvature inside the loop. On a plane there will be no rotation, on a sphere there will be, on a saddle in the opposite direction.

History

Gauss in 1827 introduced Gaussian curvature and proved the Theorema Egregium (curvature is an intrinsic property of a surface). Riemann in 1854 generalized curvature to multidimensional manifolds. Einstein in 1915 used curvature to describe gravity. In the mid-20th century, the theory of curvature in complex geometry and gauge theories developed.
