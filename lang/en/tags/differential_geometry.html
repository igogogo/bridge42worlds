Differential Geometry

📊 0 articles
This is the science of curved surfaces and spaces, for example, how a sheet of paper bends when twisted, or how space curves around the Earth.
💡 Differential geometry explains why light rays bend around massive objects — this is called gravitational lensing.
Differential geometry studies smooth curves and surfaces, as well as their generalizations — manifolds. It allows measuring curvature, distances, and angles on curved objects. For example, it can be used to calculate the shortest path between two points on the Earth's surface (a geodesic line).

History

The foundations were laid by Carl Friedrich Gauss in 1827 in his work 'General Investigations of Curved Surfaces'. Then Bernhard Riemann in 1854 generalized the ideas to multidimensional spaces, creating Riemannian geometry, which later became the mathematical foundation of Einstein's general theory of relativity.

How it works

Imagine you are crawling on the surface of an apple. Differential geometry gives you a ruler and a protractor to measure distances and angles, but these tools adjust to the bends of the surface. If the surface is curved, parallel lines may intersect (as on a sphere) or diverge (as on a saddle).

💡 Differential geometry is used in computer graphics to create realistic 3D models, for example, in movies and video games.
Differential geometry is a branch of mathematics that studies smooth manifolds, their geometric properties (curvature, metric) and structures (connections, bundles). It uses methods of mathematical analysis and linear algebra to describe local and global properties of spaces. Key concepts: metric tensor, which defines distances; connection, which defines parallel transport; Riemann curvature tensor, which measures deviation from Euclidean space. Differential geometry is the language of general relativity, where gravity is interpreted as curvature of spacetime.

History

Differential geometry began with Gauss's work on the theory of surfaces (1827), where he introduced the concept of Gaussian curvature. Riemann in 1854 generalized it to multidimensional manifolds, introducing the Riemannian metric. In the early 20th century, Einstein used Riemannian geometry to create general relativity (1915), where gravity is the curvature of spacetime. Later, in the mid-20th century, complex differential geometry and gauge theories in particle physics developed.

How it works

Differential geometry works with manifolds — spaces that locally resemble Euclidean space. On a manifold, a metric tensor g_{ij} is introduced, defining an inner product at each point. A connection (e.g., Levi-Civita connection) determines how vectors are parallel transported along curves. Curvature is measured by the Riemann tensor R^i_{jkl}, which shows how the direction of a vector changes when parallel transported around a closed loop. Geodesics are curves that minimize distance (or extremize length).

ds^2 = g_{ij} dx^i dx^j
The square of the line element ds^2 is expressed through the metric tensor g_{ij} and coordinate differentials dx^i.
R^i_{jkl} = \partial_k \Gamma^i_{jl} - \partial_l \Gamma^i_{jk} + \Gamma^i_{km} \Gamma^m_{jl} - \Gamma^i_{lm} \Gamma^m_{jk}
The Riemann tensor is expressed in terms of Christoffel symbols Γ and their derivatives, describing the curvature of the manifold.
