laser

A laser is a device that creates light of incredible purity and directionality. Its waves move perfectly in sync, like soldiers marching in step, so the beam does not spread and can be focused into a tiny spot.

In practice: Lasers are used everywhere: they cut and weld metal, read barcodes, transmit terabytes of information over fiber optic cables, and allow measuring the distance from Earth to the Moon with millimeter precision.
Laser (Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation) creates coherent monochromatic radiation. The main components: an active medium (gas, liquid, crystal, or semiconductor), a pumping system to supply energy, and an optical resonator—two mirrors between which light is repeatedly reflected. The operating principle is based on stimulated emission: a passing photon 'nudges' an excited atom to emit an exactly identical photon, doubling the radiation. An avalanche of identical photons arises, which after amplification forms a laser beam.

History

Although the theoretical possibility of stimulated emission was substantiated by Einstein back in 1917, the first device to use this principle for microwaves—the maser—was created by Charles Townes and his group in 1954. Then in 1960, Theodore Maiman built the first laser that emitted visible light, using a ruby crystal.

How it works

First, the laser's active medium (e.g., a crystal or gas) is pumped with energy from an external source. Atoms go into an excited state, and then, 'shedding' excess energy, emit photons. These initial photons stimulate other excited atoms to emit exactly identical photons—thus an avalanche of identical light particles is born. Two parallel mirrors repeatedly pass light through the medium, amplifying it until part of it exits through one of the mirrors, forming a directed beam.

💡 A femtosecond laser produces flashes lasting only a few quadrillionths of a second (femtoseconds). Such ultrashort pulses allow 'filming' the motion of electrons in atoms and molecules.
In a regular light bulb, atoms emit randomly: waves of different colors and unsynchronized are generated. A laser, on the other hand, synchronizes the atoms: they all emit light of the same wavelength (monochromatic) and exactly in phase (coherent). If a light bulb is the murmur of a crowd, a laser is the harmonious singing of a choir.

A laser generates coherent monochromatic radiation through stimulated emission of photons. For stable generation, it is necessary to create population inversion—a state where there are more excited atoms in the working medium than in the ground state. Then a passing photon with energy hν equal to the energy level difference can induce an avalanche of identical photons. The optical resonator (a pair of mirrors) provides positive feedback: it selects photons of a certain frequency and direction, narrowing the emission spectrum to values determined by the resonator's quality factor and the gain linewidth of the active medium.

History

In 1917, Einstein theoretically predicted stimulated emission. In 1954, Charles Townes, James Gordon, and Herbert Zeiger created the first maser, operating on ammonia. Six years later, Theodore Maiman demonstrated the first optical laser using ruby. Soon Ali Javan designed a helium-neon laser (1961), and Robert Hall the first semiconductor laser (1962). These discoveries transformed science, telecommunications, medicine, and manufacturing.

How it works

The active medium (for example, neodymium in a YAG crystal or a helium-neon mixture) is excited by external pumping, transferring atoms to a metastable energy level. When the upper-level population exceeds the lower-level population (population inversion), a spontaneously emitted photon stimulates emission from neighboring excited atoms, generating a cascade of coherent photons. Inside the optical resonator (e.g., a Fabry–Pérot etalon), light is repeatedly reflected by mirrors and amplified on each pass. Part of the radiation exits through a semi-transparent mirror. Photons, being bosons, accumulate in a single quantum state, ensuring identical parameters of the laser beam. In semiconductor lasers, feedback is achieved through Bragg diffraction on periodic structures (distributed feedback) with the condition mλ = 2nΛ.

E = h \nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}
E is the photon energy, h is Planck's constant (h ≈ 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s), ν is the radiation frequency, c is the speed of light (c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s), λ is the wavelength
