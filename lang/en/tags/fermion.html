Fermion

📊 1 articles
A particle with half-integer spin, obeying the Pauli exclusion principle: two fermions cannot occupy the same quantum state. Fermions include quarks and leptons, as well as composite particles (protons, neutrons). Imagine fermions as people on a bus: each takes a separate seat, and two people cannot sit in one.
💡 White dwarfs and neutron stars are prevented from collapsing by the pressure of degenerate fermions—electrons or neutrons.

History

The exclusion principle was formulated by Wolfgang Pauli in 1925. Fermi-Dirac statistics were developed by Enrico Fermi and Paul Dirac in 1926. Fermions constitute matter, while bosons are force carriers.

How it works

Due to the Pauli principle, fermions form the structure of atoms: electrons fill shells, preventing collapse. Analogy: parking—only one car can occupy a spot. This property explains the stability of matter and the diversity of chemical elements.
