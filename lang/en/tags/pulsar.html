Pulsar

📊 0 articles
A rapidly rotating neutron star that emits narrow beams of radio radiation, which we perceive as periodic pulses. Imagine a lighthouse that rotates and sends out a beam of light — if you stand in its path, you see flashes at regular intervals.
💡 The fastest pulsar, PSR J1748-2446ad, rotates at 716 revolutions per second — faster than the blades of a kitchen blender.
A rapidly rotating neutron star that emits narrow beams of radio radiation, which we perceive as periodic pulses. Imagine a lighthouse that rotates and sends out a beam of light — if you stand in its path, you see flashes at regular intervals.

History

Pulsars were discovered in 1967 by Jocelyn Bell Burnell, then a graduate student, who noticed regular radio pulses. Initially they were jokingly called LGM-1 (Little Green Men), but soon it was understood they were natural objects. For this discovery, her supervisor Antony Hewish received the Nobel Prize.

How it works

A neutron star rotates at tremendous speed (up to hundreds of revolutions per second) and has a strong magnetic field. Particles are accelerated along the magnetic poles, creating narrow beams of radiation. If the beam hits Earth, we see a pulse. Analogy: if you spin a flashlight, an observer sees flashes only when the beam is pointed at them.
