History

Gravitational lensing is the deflection of light in a gravitational field, predicted by Einstein's general theory of relativity. A massive object (lens) curves spacetime, causing light rays from a source to follow geodesics. An observer can see multiple images, rings (Einstein rings), arcs, or magnified images. Different types include strong lensing (multiple images, arcs), weak lensing (distortion of background galaxy shapes), and microlensing (brightness changes of stars when a compact object passes). It is used to map dark matter and study cosmology.

History

The idea of light bending by a massive body had been discussed since the 18th century, but within Newtonian theory. In 1915 Einstein derived the correct deflection angle (twice the Newtonian value). In 1919 Eddington's expedition confirmed the deflection during a solar eclipse. In the 1930s Fritz Zwicky proposed using lensing by galaxy clusters as 'telescopes'. The first multiple system (double quasar) was found in 1979 by Dennis Walsh and colleagues. Today it is a key tool for cosmology, e.g., surveys by the Vera Rubin Observatory.

How it works

According to general relativity, Einstein's equations describe spacetime curvature. Light follows null geodesics, and its trajectory is described by an effective refractive index n = 1 - 2Φ/c², where Φ is the gravitational potential. The deflection angle for a point mass is α = 4GM/(c²ξ), where ξ is the impact parameter. When source, lens, and observer align, an Einstein ring forms with angular radius θ_E = sqrt((4GM)/(c²) * (D_ls)/(D_l D_s)), where D are distances. For distributed mass, lensing yields distortions from which a density map of matter, including dark matter, is constructed.

\theta_E = \sqrt{\frac{4GM}{c^2} \frac{D_{ls}}{D_l D_s}}
θ_E — angular radius of Einstein ring (radians), G — gravitational constant (≈ 6.674×10⁻¹¹ m³/(kg·s²)), M — lens mass (kg), c — speed of light (≈ 3×10⁸ m/s), D_ls — angular distance from lens to source (m), D_l — distance to lens (m), D_s — distance to source (m).
