isotope

📊 0 articles
Isotopes are 'twin brothers' of chemical elements. They have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons, so they behave almost identically in chemistry but may have different masses. For example, all carbon atoms have 6 protons, but carbon-12 and carbon-14 are isotopes with 6 and 8 neutrons, respectively.
💡 Carbon-14 helps archaeologists determine the age of ancient finds. It is radioactive and gradually decays, so by measuring how much remains, one can find out when the organism died.
Isotopes are varieties of atoms of the same chemical element with the same nuclear charge but different mass. Analogy: like different sorts of apples: all apples are similar and equally useful, but some are larger, others smaller. So are hydrogen isotopes: protium is light, deuterium is twice as heavy, and tritium is even heavier.

History

The term 'isotope' was proposed by Frederick Soddy in 1913. He noticed that radioactive elements could occupy the same place in the periodic table but have different atomic masses.

How it works

The properties of isotopes are determined by the number of neutrons. In nature, most elements are a mixture of stable isotopes, but there are also radioactive ones. Analogy: like in a family, children may be of the same height but different weights—they have similar character (chemical properties) but different physical parameters.

💡 Deuterium and tritium—isotopes of hydrogen—are used for thermonuclear fusion. If we mastered controlled fusion, ocean water would become an inexhaustible energy source, as it contains plenty of deuterium.
Isotopes are atoms with the same atomic number Z (number of protons) but different mass number A (sum of protons and neutrons). Nuclides are denoted as ᴬZX. Isotopes of the same element have identical electron shells, which accounts for their chemical identity, but differ in nuclear properties: stability, spin, magnetic moment. The isotopic composition of the Earth reflects nucleosynthesis processes in stars and subsequent evolution.

History

After the discovery of radioactivity by Becquerel in 1896, Ernest Rutherford and Frederick Soddy showed in 1902 that radioactive decay is accompanied by the transmutation of elements. In 1913, Soddy introduced the term 'isotope' for chemically indistinguishable radioelements with different masses. That same year, J.J. Thomson discovered neon isotopes of masses 20 and 22. The discovery of the neutron by James Chadwick in 1932 explained the existence of isotopes. Today, the study of isotopes underlies a multitude of methods: from radiocarbon dating to nuclear medicine.

How it works

The difference in neutron number leads to a difference in nuclear binding energy, which determines stability. According to the liquid drop model and the Weizsäcker formula, the binding energy depends on Z and A. Radioactive isotopes decay according to the radioactive decay law N(t)=N₀e^{-λt}. Mass spectroscopy allows separating isotopes by mass-to-charge ratio. Quantum effects such as tunneling explain α-decay. The Pauli principle governs the filling of nuclear shells, and neutron resonances play a role in neutron capture.
