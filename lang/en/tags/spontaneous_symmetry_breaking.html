Spontaneous Symmetry Breaking

📊 0 articles
Spontaneous symmetry breaking occurs when symmetric rules lead to an asymmetric outcome, like a ball on top of a hill rolling down one side.
💡 Thanks to spontaneous symmetry breaking, elementary particles acquire mass, and the Universe became what it is.
Spontaneous symmetry breaking happens when a system has symmetric laws, but its ground state (vacuum) does not possess that symmetry. For example, a magnet: the laws of physics are symmetric with respect to the direction of magnetization, but the magnet itself chooses one direction. This phenomenon underlies the Higgs mechanism.

History

The concept was introduced into particle physics by Yoichiro Nambu in 1960. Later, Peter Higgs and others applied it to explain the masses of W and Z bosons.

How it works

Imagine a potential shaped like a 'Mexican hat': a bump in the center is the symmetric state, but it is unstable. A particle rolls into one of the valleys at the rim, breaking the symmetry. In the Higgs field, this gives mass to particles.

💡 Spontaneous symmetry breaking in cosmology could have led to inflation—the rapid expansion of the Universe.
Spontaneous symmetry breaking is a phenomenon where the Lagrangian of a theory has a symmetry, but the vacuum state (ground state) does not. As a result, massless Goldstone bosons arise (if the symmetry is global) or massive gauge bosons (if the symmetry is local, the Higgs mechanism). Examples: ferromagnet (breaking of rotational symmetry), superconductivity, electroweak interaction.

History

The idea of spontaneous symmetry breaking was first formulated in condensed matter physics (Landau, 1937). In 1960, Yoichiro Nambu applied it to elementary particle theory to explain nucleon masses. In 1964, Peter Higgs, Robert Brout, and François Englert independently proposed a mechanism giving mass to gauge bosons. This became a key element of the Standard Model.

How it works

Consider the Higgs potential: V(φ) = μ^2|φ|^2 + λ|φ|^4, with μ^2 < 0. The minimum is at |φ| = v/√2 ≠ 0. The vacuum chooses a specific phase, breaking the symmetry. In the case of local (gauge) symmetry, the Goldstone bosons are 'eaten' by the gauge fields, which acquire mass. A massive particle remains—the Higgs boson.

V(\phi) = \mu^2 |\phi|^2 + \lambda |\phi|^4, \quad \mu^2 < 0
The potential has a minimum at a nonzero field value, leading to spontaneous symmetry breaking.

Open problems & fun facts

Why does the Higgs potential have this particular form? It is not explained by the Standard Model.; The hierarchy problem: why is the Higgs mass so small compared to the Planck mass?

💡 Spontaneous symmetry breaking in cosmology could have led to inflation—the rapid expansion of the Universe.
