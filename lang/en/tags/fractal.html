Fractal

📊 0 articles
A fractal is a shape that repeats itself at different scales: like tree branches resembling the whole tree.
💡 The Koch snowflake is a classic fractal: its perimeter is infinite, although its area is finite.
A fractal is a set that has the property of self-similarity: its parts resemble the whole. Fractals often have fractional dimension (e.g., dimension 1.26 for the Koch snowflake). They occur in nature: coastline, clouds, blood vessels.

History

The term 'fractal' was introduced by Benoit Mandelbrot in 1975, although examples (Cantor set, Sierpinski carpet) were known earlier.

How it works

Fractals are built iteratively: take an initial shape and apply a rule that replaces each element with a reduced copy of the whole shape. Repetition yields a complex structure.

💡 The Mandelbrot set is one of the most famous fractals; its image is obtained from the simple recurrence formula z = z^2 + c.
A fractal is a geometric figure that possesses self-similarity and fractional Hausdorff dimension. Formally, a fractal is a set whose Hausdorff dimension strictly exceeds its topological dimension. Fractals can be deterministic (Koch snowflake) or random (Brownian tree). They are described by iterated function systems (IFS). Fractal dimension measures the degree of space filling. Applications: computer graphics, image compression, modeling natural objects.

History

Georg Cantor in 1883 constructed the Cantor set. Wacław Sierpiński in 1915 created the triangle and carpet. Benoit Mandelbrot in 1975 introduced the term 'fractal' and popularized them, showing the connection with natural forms. In 1980, the Mandelbrot set appeared. Fractals became important in chaos theory and dynamical systems.

How it works

Fractals are constructed recursively. For example, the Koch snowflake: start with an equilateral triangle, divide each side into three parts and replace the middle part with an equilateral triangle. Repeat infinitely. Hausdorff dimension D = log(4)/log(3) ≈ 1.26. For random fractals (e.g., Brownian motion), the dimension is 2 for the trajectory.

D = \frac{\log 4}{\log 3} \approx 1.26
Fractal dimension shows how the number of details grows as the scale decreases.

Open problems & fun facts

Determination of exact fractal dimension for natural objects; Connection between fractals and renormalization group theory in physics
