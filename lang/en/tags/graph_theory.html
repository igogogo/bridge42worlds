Graph theory

📊 0 articles
Graph theory studies networks: how people are connected in social networks, metro stations, or web pages.
💡 The problem of the seven bridges of Königsberg, solved by Euler, is considered the beginning of graph theory.
A graph is a set of vertices (points) and edges (lines) connecting some pairs of vertices. Graph theory studies properties of such structures: connectivity, cycles, colorings, shortest paths. It is applied in computer science, logistics, sociology.

History

The foundations were laid by Leonhard Euler in 1736, solving the bridge problem. Then it developed in the 19th-20th centuries (Kirchhoff, Cayley, Erdős).

How it works

A graph is given by a set of vertices V and edges E. For example, in the bridge problem, vertices are banks and islands, edges are bridges. Euler proved that traversing all bridges exactly once is possible only if the graph has 0 or 2 vertices of odd degree.

💡 The four-color theorem, proved with a computer, states that any map can be colored with four colors so that neighboring countries have different colors.
Graph theory is a branch of discrete mathematics studying graphs as mathematical structures. A graph G = (V, E) consists of a set of vertices V and a set of edges E ⊆ {{u,v} | u,v ∈ V}. There are undirected and directed graphs, weighted and unweighted. Basic concepts: vertex degree, path, cycle, connectivity, tree, planarity. Important results: Euler's theorem on planar graphs, the four-color theorem, Ramsey's theorem.

History

Leonhard Euler in 1736 published a solution to the Königsberg bridge problem, considered the birth of graph theory. Gustav Kirchhoff in 1847 applied graphs to electrical circuits. Arthur Cayley in 1857 studied trees for chemical isomers. In the 20th century, Paul Erdős and Alfréd Rényi developed random graph theory. In 1976, Appel and Haken proved the four-color theorem using a computer.

How it works

Graphs are represented by an adjacency matrix or adjacency lists. Traversal algorithms (BFS, DFS) find connected components. Shortest paths — Dijkstra's algorithm. Vertex coloring is an NP-hard problem. Graph theory is closely related to combinatorics and optimization.

Open problems & fun facts

P vs NP: clique problem and Hamiltonian cycle problem; Hadwiger's conjecture on graph coloring
