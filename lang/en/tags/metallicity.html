metallicity

📊 0 articles

The chemical shade of a star: how many 'heavy' elements (in astronomy, all elements heavier than helium, from lithium to uranium, are called metals) it contains compared to the original hydrogen and helium. The higher the metallicity, the more the star is saturated with the products of the life cycles of previous generations of stars.

In practice: Metallicity determines a star's color, temperature, lifetime, and the probability of rocky planet formation around it; for galaxies, it is a chronicle of chemical evolution, allowing the reconstruction of the history of star formation and mergers.
Metallicity (usually denoted as Z) is a quantitative characteristic that determines what fraction of the mass of a star or interstellar gas consists of elements heavier than helium. Although astrophysicists call them 'metals', this group includes carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, and even uranium and gold. The index [Fe/H] is widely used — the difference of the decimal logarithms of the ratio of the number of iron atoms to the number of hydrogen atoms in the star and in the Sun: [Fe/H] = log₁₀(N_Fe/N_H) – log₁₀(N_Fe/N_H)_⊙. Iron was chosen because its lines are easy to measure in spectra. Solar metallicity by mass Z_⊙ ≈ 0.016 (according to modern data ~0.012). Stars with high Z have stronger winds due to many spectral lines that absorb radiation, and they are cooler at the same mass. The presence of giant planets is statistically correlated with increased metallicity of the parent stars.

History

In the first half of the 20th century, while studying stellar spectra, astronomers noticed that some stars had stronger absorption lines of metals than others. In 1925, Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin convincingly showed in her dissertation that hydrogen is the main element of stars, and differences in line intensities are explained by conditions in atmospheres. Later, Walter Baade introduced the concepts of stellar populations I and II, linking them to the abundance of heavy elements.

How it works

Heavy elements are born inside massive stars through thermonuclear reactions and are scattered into interstellar space by supernova explosions or stellar winds. New stars condense from this enriched gas, so each subsequent generation gets a little more metals. Thus, the oldest Population II stars are very metal-poor ([Fe/H] < –1), while young ones like our Sun (Population I) are relatively rich. It’s like a family heirloom: each generation adds new 'jewels' (elements) to the common treasury.

💡 The record low-metallicity star SMSS 0313-6708 has [Fe/H] ≈ –7.4 — meaning it has almost 25 million times less iron than the Sun. It formed from gas enriched probably by just one supernova explosion in the early Universe.
In astrophysics, all elements heavier than helium — from carbon to iron and uranium — are called metals. Metallicity indicates what fraction of a star’s or gas cloud’s mass consists of these heavy atoms. It’s like a recipe: the Sun has about 1.6% metals, while the oldest stars in the galactic halo have only 0.001–0.01%. The rest is hydrogen (about 70%) and helium (about 28%), left over from the Big Bang. Metallicity is often expressed through the iron-to-hydrogen ratio [Fe/H] on a logarithmic scale relative to the Sun: 0 is solar value, –1 means 10 times less, +1 means 10 times more.

Metallicity Z is the mass fraction of all chemical elements except hydrogen and helium. Since most metals are synthesized in stars, their abundance serves as a cosmic clock: low Z indicates a primitive, early age of the object. The logarithmic index [Fe/H] is used as a proxy because iron lines are abundant and easy to measure in the optical range. Sometimes the ratio of α-elements (O, Mg, Si, …) to iron — [α/Fe] — is analyzed separately, reflecting the relative contribution of supernovae of different types. Metallicity critically affects stellar evolution: it sets the opacity (the absorption coefficient is determined by bound-bound and bound-free transitions), and hence the energy transport regime (radiative or convective), effective temperature, and main-sequence lifetime. In the interstellar medium, the amount of dust increases with Z, enhancing cooling and facilitating molecule formation. In galaxies, a radial metallicity gradient is observed — a decline from the center to the periphery, caused by the history of star formation 'from the inside out'.

History

At the beginning of the 20th century, spectroscopists noticed that stars differ in the intensity of metal lines. The key breakthrough was made by Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin in 1925: applying Saha’s ionization theory, she proved that stars consist mainly of hydrogen, and differences in spectra are explained by temperature, not composition. In the 1940s, Walter Baade, studying galaxies resolved into individual stars, identified Population I (metal-rich, disk) and Population II (metal-poor, halo). With the advent of high-sensitivity spectroscopy and large-scale surveys (SDSS, Gaia), the metallicities of millions of stars have been measured, allowing the reconstruction of the chemical evolution of the Milky Way.

How it works

Elements heavier than helium, except for a small amount of lithium and beryllium, are synthesized inside stars. On the main sequence, hydrogen is converted into helium, then in later stages helium is converted into carbon, oxygen, and so on up to iron (the peak of the binding energy curve). Heavy nuclei (copper, gold, uranium) are created by neutron capture processes: the slow s-process in the shells of asymptotic giant branch stars and the rapid r-process during supernova explosions or neutron star mergers. The products of nucleosynthesis are ejected into the interstellar medium through winds, planetary nebulae, and explosions. Subsequent generations of stars form from this enriched material, increasing the average metallicity with time. The observed distribution of [Fe/H] in different components of the Galaxy (thin disk, thick disk, halo) reflects their age and migration. Thus, massive elliptical galaxies retain metals in a deep potential well and exhibit high Z. In stellar models, metallicity enters into the equation of state, opacity (calculated using OPAL or OP grids), and nuclear reaction rates. For spectroscopic determination of [Fe/H], the measured equivalent widths of Fe I and Fe II lines are compared with synthetic spectra, solving the transfer equations assuming local thermodynamic equilibrium (LTE) or taking into account non-LTE deviations.

[\text{Fe/H}] = \log_{10} \left( \frac{N_{\text{Fe}}}{N_{\text{H}}} \right)_{\text{star}} - \log_{10} \left( \frac{N_{\text{Fe}}}{N_{\text{H}}} \right)_{\odot}
N_Fe — number of iron atoms, N_H — number of hydrogen atoms, the ⊙ symbol denotes solar values (for the Sun, log₁₀(N_Fe/N_H)⊙ ≈ –4.5)
Z \approx Z_{\odot} \cdot 10^{[\text{Fe/H}]}
Z — mass fraction of metals, Z_⊙ — solar metallicity (Z_⊙ ≈ 0.0122 according to Asplund et al. 2009)
