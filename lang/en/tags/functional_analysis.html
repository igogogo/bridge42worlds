Functional analysis is a branch of mathematics where functions are viewed as points in infinite-dimensional spaces (e.g., Hilbert and Banach spaces). In these spaces, analogs of distance (norm) and angle (inner product) are defined, and linear operators (mappings that transform one function into another) play the role of matrices from linear algebra. The central idea is the study of the properties of operators and their spectra (generalization of eigenvalues).
💡 Quantum mechanics was mathematically formulated in the language of functional analysis: a particle's state is a vector in Hilbert space, and physical quantities are operators.
Functional analysis studies functions (dependencies of one quantity on another) as elements of infinite-dimensional spaces (a space with an infinite number of dimensions, such as the set of all possible melodies), similarly to how geometry studies points in a three-dimensional world. It provides tools for working with such spaces, including the concepts of distance (metric) and angle (inner product).

History

In the early 20th century, mathematicians including David Hilbert and Stefan Banach laid the foundations of functional analysis to solve integral and differential equations.

How it works

Just as a vector (having length and direction) represents a force in physics, functional analysis describes functions as vectors in an abstract space, allowing measurement of their 'length' and 'closeness'.

Functional analysis is a field of mathematics studying topological vector spaces and their mappings. Main objects: Banach and Hilbert spaces, normed and metric spaces, operators (linear, compact, self-adjoint), spectral theory, dual spaces. Key theorems: Hahn–Banach, uniform boundedness principle (Banach–Steinhaus theorem), open mapping theorem, closed graph theorem. The spectral theorem for self-adjoint operators generalizes matrix diagonalization to the infinite-dimensional case, which is critical for quantum mechanics. C*-algebras, distribution theory (generalized functions), and ergodic theory are also studied. Functional analysis finds applications in differential equations, quantum physics, optimization theory, and machine learning.

History

The roots of functional analysis lie in the works of Volterra, Fredholm, and Hilbert on integral equations in the late 19th century. A decisive contribution was made by Stefan Banach, who in the 1920s systematized the theory of normed spaces (Banach spaces) and proved fundamental theorems. John von Neumann in the 1930s introduced the concept of Hilbert space for a rigorous formulation of quantum mechanics. Development continued in the works of Gelfand, Naimark, Pontryagin, and others.

How it works

Functional analysis investigates the structure of infinite-dimensional spaces by introducing topologies (notion of closeness) and algebraic operations. Just as in the finite-dimensional case linear operators are represented by matrices, in functional analysis operators on functions can be integral, differential, or more general. The spectral theorem (for self-adjoint operators in Hilbert space) asserts that an operator can be diagonalized in a suitable basis, meaning decomposition into eigenfunctions. The Schrödinger equation from quantum mechanics is naturally viewed as an eigenvalue problem for the Hamiltonian operator in the Hilbert space of states.

|\langle x, y \rangle| \leq \|x\| \|y\|
⟨x,y⟩ is the inner product of vectors x and y, ‖x‖ is the norm of vector x (square root of the inner product with itself).

Open problems & fun facts

The invariant subspace problem: does every bounded linear operator on a Hilbert space have a non-trivial closed invariant subspace?; The approximation problem: can every compact operator be approximated uniformly by finite-dimensional operators?; The conjecture that every C*-algebra is isometrically embeddable into a von Neumann factor.
