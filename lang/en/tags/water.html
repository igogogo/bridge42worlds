Water is what we drink and splash in. It has no color or smell, but without it there would be no trees or animals. Water can be liquid, like in a river, solid, like ice on a rink, and invisible vapor in clouds.

Water is the most anomalous substance on Earth. It is the only one that occurs in three states at once: liquid, solid, and gaseous. As a universal solvent, it dissolves almost everything, like a caring hostess, and transports heat around the planet, moderating the climate.

History

Ancient philosophers considered water an element, but in 1781 Henry Cavendish proved that water is a compound of hydrogen and oxygen. Antoine Lavoisier determined its formula as H₂O.

How it works

At the micro level, water is like tiny magnets: the molecules stick together, creating surface tension on which water striders run. Ice floats because its molecules are spaced more widely than in the liquid — like dancers keeping a distance.

Water (H₂O) is a binary inorganic compound, the foundation of the hydrosphere, climatic processes, and biochemistry. Its anomalous physical properties — high heat capacity, maximum density at 4°C, expansion upon freezing — are due to hydrogen bonds and the tetrahedral structure of molecules. As a universal polar solvent, water dissociates and hydrates ions, providing a medium for metabolism. In space, water has been found in the form of ice on comets, planetary moons, and in the atmospheres of exoplanets, making it a key marker for the search for life.

History

The idea of water as an element prevailed until the experiments of Henry Cavendish in 1781, who synthesized water from 'inflammable air' (hydrogen) and 'dephlogisticated air' (oxygen). Antoine Lavoisier then determined its composition and gave names to hydrogen and oxygen. In the 20th century, water research in space became groundbreaking: missions to Mars and Jupiter's moons found water ice, and the Kepler telescope (mission leader William Borucki) discovered thousands of exoplanets, many of which potentially have water in the habitable zone.

How it works

Hydrogen bonds are the key to water's anomalies: they provide strong intermolecular attraction, but upon freezing they lock into an open crystal lattice of ice, which is less dense than the liquid. Quantum mechanical effects in water, such as proton tunneling in hydrogen bonds, are described by the Schrödinger equation. Macroscopic thermodynamic properties, including phase transitions and heat transfer, follow the second law of thermodynamics.
