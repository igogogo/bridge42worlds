Photon

📊 1 articles
An elementary particle, the quantum of electromagnetic radiation. Photons have no mass, always move at the speed of light, and carry energy proportional to frequency. Imagine light as a stream of tiny bullets, each carrying a certain amount of energy—these are photons.
💡 A photon has no mass but has momentum: sunlight exerts a pressure on Earth's surface of about 10 micronewtons per square meter.
An elementary particle, the quantum of electromagnetic radiation. Photons have no mass, always move at the speed of light, and carry energy proportional to frequency. Imagine light as a stream of tiny bullets, each carrying a certain amount of energy—these are photons.

History

The idea of light quanta was introduced by Max Planck in 1900 to explain blackbody radiation. Albert Einstein in 1905 explained the photoelectric effect, assuming light consists of photons. For this he won the Nobel Prize. Later, quantum electrodynamics described the interaction of photons with matter.

How it works

Photons are emitted and absorbed by atoms during electron transitions between energy levels. Analogy: if you throw a stone into water, waves form—so an electron transitioning to a lower level emits a photon. Photons exhibit wave-particle duality.
