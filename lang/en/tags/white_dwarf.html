A white dwarf is a compact object consisting of degenerate electron gas. It has no thermonuclear energy sources; it shines due to thermal reserves. Typical mass ~0.6 M☉, radius ~10^4 km. It cools over billions of years.

History

In 1910, Henry Norris Russell discovered the white dwarf 40 Eridani B. In 1926, Ralph Fowler explained its structure using quantum mechanics. Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar in 1931 theoretically derived the maximum mass of a white dwarf — the Chandrasekhar limit.

How it works

The pressure of degenerate electrons counteracts gravity. The state is described by Fermi-Dirac statistics. There is the Chandrasekhar mass limit (~1.4 M☉), above which collapse to a neutron star or a Type Ia supernova explosion occurs. Cooling is governed by the thermal conductivity of degenerate gas and crystallization.
