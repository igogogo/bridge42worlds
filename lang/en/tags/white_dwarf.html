White dwarf

📊 1 articles
A dense remnant of a star like the Sun after it has shed its outer layers. A white dwarf consists of degenerate electron gas and gradually cools over billions of years. Imagine a cooling ember in a fire — it no longer burns but remains hot for a long time.
💡 A diamond is crystalline carbon, and white dwarfs often consist of carbon and oxygen, so they can be considered giant Earth-sized diamonds.

History

The first discovered white dwarf was Sirius B in 1862. In 1926, Ralph Fowler explained its structure using quantum mechanics and the Pauli principle. Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar in 1930 calculated the maximum mass of a white dwarf (the Chandrasekhar limit), for which he received the Nobel Prize.

How it works

When a Sun-like star exhausts its nuclear fuel, it sheds its outer layers, forming a planetary nebula, and the core collapses into a white dwarf. The pressure of degenerate electrons prevents further collapse. The white dwarf is initially very hot but gradually cools, becoming a black dwarf. Analogy: it is like ash after a fire — still hot but no longer burning.
