Black Body

📊 0 articles
A black body is an ideal object that absorbs all incident radiation and re-emits it depending on its temperature.
💡 Stars, including the Sun, behave almost like black bodies, so their color depends on temperature.
A black body is a physical model that absorbs all electromagnetic radiation of any frequency. When heated, it emits a continuous spectrum whose shape depends only on temperature.

History

The concept of a black body was introduced by Gustav Kirchhoff in 1860. The problem of black body radiation led to the creation of quantum mechanics by Planck in 1900.

How it works

A black body emits energy according to Planck's law: the higher the temperature, the more energy and the shorter the wavelength of the peak (like hot metal changing color from red to white).

💡 Wien's displacement law states that the wavelength of the peak emission is inversely proportional to temperature.
A black body is an idealized body that completely absorbs all incident radiation of all frequencies. Its emissivity is described by Planck's law: the spectral energy density depends only on temperature. Real objects, such as stars, approximate a black body.

History

In 1860, Kirchhoff introduced the concept of an absolute black body. In 1896, Wilhelm Wien derived the displacement law. In 1900, Max Planck, trying to explain the black body spectrum, proposed energy quantization, which marked the beginning of quantum physics.

How it works

A black body emits electromagnetic waves due to the thermal motion of charged particles. The emission spectrum is continuous and described by Planck's formula. As temperature increases, the peak shifts to shorter wavelengths (Wien's law), and the total power increases as T⁴ (Stefan-Boltzmann law).

B_\nu(T) = \frac{2h\nu^3}{c^2} \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/kT} - 1}

Open problems & fun facts

An ideal black body does not exist, but good approximations are created in the laboratory.
