Action

📊 0 articles
Action is a number that nature 'tries' to minimize when something moves.
💡 The principle of least action works not only for balls, but also for light, electricity, and even particles in quantum mechanics.
Action is the integral of the difference between kinetic and potential energy over time. Imagine that nature 'chooses' the path with the least action, like a skier choosing the fastest descent. This principle underlies all of physics.

History

The concept of action was introduced by Pierre de Maupertuis in 1744, and the principle of least action was formulated by William Hamilton. Later, Richard Feynman used it in quantum mechanics.

How it works

Action is computed as S = ∫(T - V) dt. From the principle of least action, all equations of motion are derived—from Newtonian to Einsteinian.

💡 In quantum mechanics, a particle 'tries' all possible paths, and the action determines how they add up (Feynman path integral).
Action is a scalar quantity equal to the time integral of the Lagrangian (difference between kinetic and potential energy). The principle of least action states that the true trajectory of a system is the one for which the action is stationary (usually minimal). This variational principle is fundamental to classical mechanics, field theory, and general relativity.

History

The concept of action dates back to Pierre de Maupertuis (1744), who proposed the principle of least action for light. Leonhard Euler and Joseph-Louis Lagrange developed the mathematical formalism. In 1833, William Hamilton gave the modern formulation. In 1948, Richard Feynman reinterpreted action in quantum mechanics, creating the path integral method.

How it works

Action S = ∫ L dt, where L = T - V. Variation δS = 0 gives the Euler-Lagrange equations. In quantum mechanics, the probability amplitude for a transition between states is proportional to exp(iS/ħ), summed over all paths (Feynman integral). In field theory, action is constructed from the Lagrangian density.

S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(q, \dot{q}, t) \, dt
Action is the integral of the Lagrangian over time between two moments.
