A natural satellite of a planet. Earth's Moon is the fifth largest moon in the Solar System and the only one visited by humans. Imagine a rocky sphere orbiting Earth, reflecting sunlight and creating tides.

History

The Moon has always been an object of fascination. In 1609, Galileo first pointed a telescope at the Moon and saw mountains and craters. In 1969, Apollo 11 landed the first humans on the Moon. Soviet Luna missions returned soil samples. New missions are planned, including the establishment of a lunar base.

How it works

The Moon orbits Earth in an elliptical path, always showing the same face (tidal locking). Its gravity causes tides on Earth. The Moon has no atmosphere, so its surface is covered with impact craters. Analogy: the Moon is like a stone tied to a string that you swing overhead — it always faces you with one side.

💡 The Moon is gradually moving away from Earth at a rate of about 3.8 cm per year — eventually it will appear smaller in the sky.
