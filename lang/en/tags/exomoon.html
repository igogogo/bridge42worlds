Exomoon

📊 0 articles
A moon orbiting an exoplanet outside the Solar System. Exomoons could be potential sites for life, as they may receive energy from the planet and star. Imagine our Moon orbiting Earth, but Earth is around another star — that is an exomoon.
💡 Some exomoons may be Earth-sized and have their own atmosphere, making them among the most interesting targets for the search for extraterrestrial life.

History

The first exomoon candidates were discovered in 2018: Kepler-1625b I, found using transit data from the Kepler telescope. However, confirmation requires additional observations as the signal is weak. The search for exomoons is a new field of astronomy, actively developing with the launch of JWST.

How it works

Exomoons are detected via the transit method: when a moon passes in front of its star, it causes an additional dip in brightness, and may also cause variations in the planet's transit timing. Analogy: it is like watching a streetlamp, with a fly (planet) flying in front, and then a mosquito (moon) — you notice a double flicker.
