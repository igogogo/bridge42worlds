A distant moon, circling not Earth but a planet around an alien star—a satellite in another planetary system.

In practice: Such satellites help us understand how common moons are in the Universe and whether they have conditions for life.
An exomoon is a natural satellite of an exoplanet. Their detection is difficult due to small sizes. Main methods: analysis of transit timing variations (TTV), where the satellite's gravity shifts the moments of eclipses; search for an additional dip in brightness from the satellite itself; microlensing—gravitational distortion of light from a distant star by a planet-satellite system. Candidates have been found, e.g., Kepler-1625b I, but reliable confirmations are still lacking.
💡 Some exomoon candidates could be the size of Neptune—more like binary planets than familiar moons.
An exomoon is a satellite of a planet orbiting another star. Just as our Moon orbits Earth, an exomoon circles its planet, only the light from its star takes many years to reach us. No exomoon has been definitively confirmed yet, but the search is actively underway.

History

The first serious attempts to find exomoons began in the 2000s, when telescopes learned to capture tiny brightness fluctuations of distant stars.

How it works

A massive satellite causes its planet to wobble slightly in its orbit. As a result, the time when the planet blocks the star's light (transit) shifts a little. Astronomers measure these shifts and infer the presence of a moon.

💡 If the satellite of Kepler-1625b is real, its mass could be about 10% of Neptune's—a kind of super-Moon.
An exomoon is a natural satellite orbiting an exoplanet. They are probably as common as moons in the Solar System and are extremely important for astrobiology, as they could have habitable conditions. Their detection is one of the most challenging tasks in observational astronomy due to the extremely small signal.

History

Systematic search began with the launch of the Kepler telescope (2009), whose high photometric precision allowed tracking transit timing variations. The foundation was laid by William Borucki, who led Kepler's science team. In 2017, using data from Kepler and Hubble, the first promising candidate—Kepler-1625b I—was identified. As of 2025, there are no confirmed discoveries.

How it works

Detection relies on high-precision photometry. The main method is transit timing variations (TTV): the satellite's gravity shifts the barycenter, disrupting the strict periodicity of transits. If the satellite is far from the planet, its own transit can be recorded as an additional micro-dip. Additionally, the transit duration variation (TDV) method is used, which accounts for changes in duration due to the planet's motion around the common center of mass. Joint analysis of TTV and TDV yields the satellite's mass and orbital parameters. The TTV amplitude is proportional to the satellite mass and the semi-major axis of its orbit.

Open problems & fun facts

Absence of confirmed detections; Difficulty in separating the weak signal from noise, stellar activity, and atmospheric inhomogeneities; Modeling the orbital stability of exomoons in dynamically complex systems
