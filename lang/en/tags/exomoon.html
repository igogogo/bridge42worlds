exomoon

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A moon of a planet in another star system—like a distant Jupiter's moon, which for now we can only suspect.

In practice: Understanding the formation of moons helps refine models of planetary evolution; some exomoons may turn out to be more habitable than their planets.
Exomoons are natural satellites of exoplanets. Their detection is the cutting edge of observational astronomy. Two main methods are used for the search. During the transit of a planet across the star's disk, a moon can cause an additional slight dimming (photometric method). Additionally, the moon's gravity makes the planet wobble, which leads to uneven timing of transits—transit timing variations (TTV). Detection requires ultra-precise measurements, so the main hopes are pinned on telescopes like Kepler, TESS, and future missions. It is thought that exomoons can be born from a circumplanetary disk or be captured by the planet's gravity. Their presence expands the possible habitable zones, as a moon can maintain a stable climate thanks to tidal heating and the presence of an atmosphere.

History

The idea of moons around exoplanets arose simultaneously with the idea of exoplanets themselves. The first serious attempts to detect an exomoon began in the 2000s with the launch of the Kepler telescope. There is no universally recognized discovery yet, but there are several candidates requiring confirmation.

How it works

When a planet with a moon passes in front of a star, the moon adds a small dip to the light curve. The effect is barely perceptible, like the shadow of a small bird against the Sun, but precise instruments can detect it. In addition, the moon's gravity causes the planet to wobble, causing the transit timings to shift slightly.

💡 The most famous candidate is the moon of the planet Kepler-1625 b, which could be the size of Neptune. If its existence is confirmed, it will be the first discovered 'mega-moon'.
An exomoon is a natural satellite orbiting a planet in another star system. Our Moon is an example of a moon in the Solar System. Scientists search for exomoons by observing changes in the star's brightness as the planet passes across its disk. Tiny additional dimmings may indicate the presence of a moon. This is an extremely difficult task because the signal from the moon is very weak.

Exomoons are searched for using transit photometry and timing methods. During the transit of the 'star–planet–moon' system, the light curve may contain additional dips or asymmetry caused by the moon. Transit timing variations (TTV) arise because the planet and moon orbit a common center of mass; the amplitude of TTV is proportional to the product of the moon-to-planet mass ratio and the distance between them. Other possible signatures include transit duration variations (TDV) and gravitational microlensing. Detection requires photometric precision better than 10⁻⁵ and careful filtering of false signals: star spots, instrumental noise, and the influence of undiscovered planets. Exomoons can form as a result of a giant impact (like the Moon), accretion from a circumplanetary disk (like the Galilean moons), or gravitational capture. Their orbital stability is investigated within the restricted three-body problem taking into account tidal forces. Habitability depends on radiation levels, tidal heating, and the presence of a magnetic field.

History

The term 'exomoon' appeared in the 1990s with the discovery of the first exoplanets. Systematic searches began after the launch of the Kepler telescope: the 'Hunt for Exomoons with Kepler' (HEK) project was led by David Kipping. In 2017, the candidate Kepler-1625 b I was announced, but subsequent Hubble observations did not confirm its existence unambiguously. The search for exomoons remains a collective task.

How it works

The detection of exomoons relies on high-precision photometry. During a planet's transit, starlight dims according to a known law; a moon adds a secondary signal. If the moon is far from the planet, its transit may be observed as a separate event before or after the planetary transit. The TTV method is based on the fact that the planet and moon move around a common barycenter: this shifts the planet's position and causes deviations of transit times from strict periodicity. The TTV amplitude can reach minutes and depends on the moon's mass and orbital size. Additionally, dynamical stability is assessed: the orbit must lie within the planet's Hill sphere and be stable against perturbations from the star and other planets. Tidal heating of the moon is described by the quality factor Q. The light curve is modeled using the Mandel–Agol algorithm for the planet plus the moon's contribution.
