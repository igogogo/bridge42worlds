dark energy

📊 157
Dark energy is a mysterious force that pushes galaxies in the Universe apart faster and faster, like wind blowing balloons apart. We don't see it, but we know it exists because the Universe is expanding at an accelerating rate.

History

In 1998, two independent groups of astronomers, observing type Ia supernovae, discovered that the Universe is expanding with acceleration. This came as a shock: it was expected that gravity slows down expansion. The discovery brought the Nobel Prize, and the phenomenon itself was called dark energy — in honor of its invisible and mysterious nature.

Dark energy is the dominant component (about 68%) of the total energy density of the modern Universe, responsible for the observed accelerated expansion. In general relativity, it is described by the cosmological constant Λ in Einstein's equations, or by more general dynamical fields (quintessence). The cosmological constant corresponds to vacuum energy with the equation of state w = -1, where w is the ratio of pressure to energy density. Observational evidence includes data from type Ia supernovae, baryon acoustic oscillations, the cosmic microwave background, and large-scale structure.

History

In 1917, Albert Einstein introduced the cosmological constant for a static Universe, but abandoned it after Edwin Hubble's discovery of expansion in 1929. Georges Lemaître also linked the cosmological constant with vacuum energy. In 1998, the Supernova Cosmology Project and High-Z Supernova Search Team discovered accelerated expansion, independently confirming the need for dark energy. Subsequent experiments, such as WMAP and Planck, refined its parameters.

How it works

In the framework of GR, dark energy manifests as a source in Einstein's equations, creating a repulsive gravitational effect under negative pressure. The Friedmann equation describing the evolution of the scale factor a(t) contains a term with the cosmological constant: (ȧ/a)² = (8πG/3)ρ - k/a² + Λ/3. Under the dominance of Λ, the scale factor grows exponentially. Hubble's law v = H₀ d is modified: the Hubble parameter changes with time, and data from distant supernovae show deviation from linearity, indicating acceleration.

\left(\frac{\dot{a}}{a}\right)^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho - \frac{k c^2}{a^2} + \frac{\Lambda c^2}{3}
a — scale factor; ȧ — its time derivative; G — gravitational constant, G ≈ 6.674×10⁻¹¹ m³/(kg·s²); ρ — density of matter and radiation; k — spatial curvature (0, ±1); c — speed of light, c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s; Λ — cosmological constant
w = \frac{p}{\rho c^2}
w — parameter of the equation of state; p — pressure; ρ — energy density; c — speed of light. For the cosmological constant, w = -1.
