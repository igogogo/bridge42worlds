Feynman diagram

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
Particles at the quantum level constantly transform into each other. A Feynman diagram is a visual scheme where lines are particles and the points where lines meet are moments of their interaction. Like a traffic diagram for the microworld.

History

Proposed by Richard Feynman in 1948 to simplify calculations in quantum electrodynamics.

How it works

Each line corresponds to a particle: photons are wavy lines, electrons are straight lines. Vertices where lines meet show interaction. Analogy: like a culinary recipe: arrows show what mixes with what, so a diagram shows which particles collide and what results.

💡 Feynman himself drew these diagrams on napkins in cafes. Now, no research in particle physics is complete without them.

A Feynman diagram is a graphical representation of terms in the perturbation series of quantum field theory. Each line and vertex corresponds to a mathematical propagator or interaction vertex. Summing all possible diagrams yields the process amplitude. They elegantly combine special relativity and quantum mechanics in intuitively understandable images.

History

Introduced by Richard Feynman in 1948 as an alternative to cumbersome S-matrix calculations. The idea grew out of his work on quantum electrodynamics, where he interpreted positrons as electrons moving backward in time. Later, Julian Schwinger and Sin-Itiro Tomonaga independently developed similar methods, but Feynman's approach became the standard. Feynman, Schwinger, and Tomonaga shared the 1965 Nobel Prize. Over time, diagrams spread to all gauge theories, including quantum chromodynamics and electroweak theory.

How it works

In quantum electrodynamics, the electron line is represented by the propagator i(γ^μ p_μ + m)/(p^2 - m^2 + iε), the photon line by -i g_μν/(k^2 + iε). The interaction vertex gives a factor -i e γ^μ. Integration over all internal momenta yields the diagram's contribution to the amplitude. Feynman rules allow automatically accounting for Fermi statistics through diagram signs. The superposition principle and gauge invariance ensure the renormalizability of the theory, where infinities cancel when defining physical parameters.

S(p) = \frac{i(\gamma^\mu p_\mu + m)}{p^2 - m^2 + i\epsilon}
S(p) is the electron propagator; i is the imaginary unit; γ^μ are the Dirac gamma matrices; p_μ is the electron's four-momentum; m is the electron mass (m ≈ 9.109×10⁻³¹ kg); p^2 is the square of the four-momentum; ε is an infinitesimal positive quantity ensuring causality.

Open problems & fun facts

Exact summation of an infinite number of diagrams in the non-perturbative regime; Inclusion of gravity without breaking renormalizability; Application in strongly coupled systems, such as quantum chromodynamics at low energies
