Dust in astronomy refers to small solid particles ranging in size from nanometers to micrometers, composed of silicates, carbon, and ice. It scatters and absorbs light, making cosmic objects appear dim. Imagine looking through a dusty window: the light becomes blurred and reddish.

History

Cosmic dust was first detected by its effect on starlight. In the 19th century, Wilhelm Tempel noticed dark nebulae. In the 20th century, dust became an object of study in infrared astronomy. It is now known that dust plays a key role in the formation of stars and planets.

How it works

Dust forms in the atmospheres of cool stars and in supernova ejecta. It condenses into solid particles, which then disperse into the interstellar medium. Dust absorbs ultraviolet and visible radiation, re-emitting it in the infrared, allowing the study of cold regions.
