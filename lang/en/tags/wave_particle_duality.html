wave-particle duality

📊 0
Imagine that light is an ocean that behaves like waves, but when you look at it very, very closely, it looks like many tiny droplets-particles. So light, and even tiny particles (like electrons) can manifest themselves now as a wave, now as a particle, depending on how we look at them.

History

The idea sparked at the beginning of the 20th century: first Einstein showed that light consists of particles (photons), then de Broglie suggested that electrons also have wave properties. The decisive experiment with electrons was performed by Davisson and Germer in 1927, observing electron diffraction on a crystal — a typically wave phenomenon.

How it works

Duality manifests when we try to measure the properties of a particle. While we are not looking at an electron, it exists as a blurred wave of probability that can interfere with itself. But as soon as we place a detector to determine which slit it passed through, the wave 'collapses', and the electron exhibits corpuscular properties, leaving a clear trace at a single point.

Wave-particle duality is the amazing property of microparticles (light, electrons) to behave both as waves and as particles. Imagine the sea surf: from a distance it seems like a continuous wave, but up close you can distinguish individual splashes-droplets. So light: in some experiments it creates an interference pattern (like waves), in others it knocks electrons out of metal (like a stream of particles-photons).

Wave-particle duality is a fundamental principle of quantum mechanics stating that any object (photon, electron, atom) possesses both wave and corpuscular properties. In Young's experiment on interference of individual photons or electrons, a pattern characteristic of waves is observed even if the particles are emitted one by one, confirming that each particle interferes with itself, passing through both slits simultaneously. Mathematically, this is described by a wave function that sets the probability amplitude of detecting the particle. Upon measurement, the wave function collapses, and the particle manifests corpuscular properties, being registered at a certain point. Duality underlies Heisenberg's uncertainty principle: the more precisely we know the position, the less defined the momentum, and vice versa.

History

In the 17th century, Newton adhered to the corpuscular theory of light, and Huygens to the wave theory. In 1801, Thomas Young experimentally proved the wave nature of light by observing interference. In 1905, Einstein, explaining the photoelectric effect, introduced the concept of photons — quanta of light, confirming their corpuscular properties. In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed the hypothesis of wave properties of matter, which was soon confirmed by electron diffraction experiments. Erwin Schrödinger in 1926 created the wave equation, mathematically uniting the wave and corpuscular approaches.

How it works

At the heart of duality lies the description of particles using a wave function, whose evolution is determined by the Schrödinger equation. The wave function represents a superposition of all possible states, and the square of its modulus gives the probability density of detecting the particle. Upon measurement, the wave function collapses into one of the eigenstates of the operator of the measured quantity. Thus, in the double-slit experiment, the electron's wave function passes through both slits, creating an interference pattern. If a detector is placed, the coherence of the superposition is disrupted, and interference disappears. This principle is also closely related to Heisenberg's uncertainty principle, which forbids simultaneous precise measurement of conjugate variables.
