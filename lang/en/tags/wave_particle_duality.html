wave-particle duality

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

Light and matter behave as waves when we don't track their path, and as particles when we do — like a chameleon that changes its appearance depending on the environment.

In practice: The wave nature of electrons allows electron microscopes to distinguish individual atoms through diffraction. The particle nature of photons underlies the photoelectric effect used in solar cells and light-sensitive detectors.
Wave-particle duality means that quantum objects are neither exclusively particles nor exclusively waves — they exhibit properties of both depending on how they are observed. The classic double-slit experiment demonstrates this: single photons or electrons, accumulating on a detector, form an interference pattern typical of waves. This is explained by the superposition of alternative paths: the particle seems to pass through both slits simultaneously. However, any attempt to determine which slit it went through destroys the superposition, and the pattern becomes particle-like. Mathematically, duality is described by the wave function, and the Heisenberg uncertainty principle (the more precisely the position is measured, the less defined the momentum) quantitatively expresses the connection between wave and particle manifestations.

History

The debate about the nature of light dates back to the 17th century: Newton thought light was a stream of particles, Huygens — a wave. By the 19th century, the wave theory prevailed. However, in 1905, Einstein, explaining the photoelectric effect, introduced the concept of photons — particles of light. In 1924, de Broglie hypothesized that if light (a wave) shows particle properties, then electrons (particles) should also display wave properties. Already in 1927, experiments by Davisson and Germer on electron scattering from crystals confirmed their diffraction. Since then, duality has been recognized as a universal property of matter.

How it works

A moving electron can be compared to a stone thrown into a pond: the stone is a particle, and the spreading ripples are a wave. In the quantum world, these two aspects are inseparable: the more precisely the electron's position is determined (particle property), the less defined its momentum (wave property), and vice versa. The double-slit experiment illustrates this: without a detector, the electron goes through both slits simultaneously, creating an interference pattern; but if a detector is placed, the electron chooses one slit and interference disappears.

💡 The de Broglie wavelength of a baseball flying at 40 m/s is approximately 10⁻³⁴ m — negligibly small compared to its size. That's why in everyday life we don't notice that a baseball is also a wave.
If you throw a handful of sand through two slits, two separate piles form behind them — that's particle behavior. If you send water, a pattern of alternating peaks and troughs emerges — a wave property. When single photons or electrons are sent through the same slits, a wave pattern gradually appears on the screen, even though each particle leaves a dot. This shows that micro-objects behave as waves (passing through both slits at once) and as particles (being detected at a specific point). This duality is called wave-particle duality. It was proposed by French physicist Louis de Broglie in 1924, who suggested that every moving particle has an associated wavelength.

Wave-particle duality is a fundamental principle of quantum physics, stating that all micro-objects (photons, electrons, atoms, and even large molecules) possess both wave properties (interference, diffraction) and particle properties (discrete interactions with transfer of energy and momentum). In quantum mechanics, duality is explained by the probabilistic interpretation of the wave function: its squared modulus gives the probability density of finding the particle at a given point, and interference arises from the addition of complex amplitudes. The contradiction is resolved in quantum field theory, where particles are quantized excitations of corresponding fields; depending on the energy and experimental conditions, wave or particle features manifest. Bohr's complementarity principle emphasizes that wave and particle descriptions are complementary: one cannot simultaneously measure parameters belonging to both pictures with arbitrary precision.

History

Historically, duality goes back to the dispute over the nature of light: Newton advocated the corpuscular theory, Huygens — the wave theory. Diffraction experiments by Young and Fresnel in the early 19th century strengthened the wave theory, and Maxwell's equations (1864) described light as an electromagnetic wave. A turning point came in 1905 when Einstein explained the photoelectric effect by introducing the concept of photons. In 1923, Compton observed the scattering of X-rays off electrons, proving that the photon behaves like a particle with momentum. In 1924, de Broglie generalized duality, associating a wave of length λ = h/p with every particle. The hypothesis was confirmed in 1927 by electron diffraction by Davisson and Germer. Experiments with fullerene C₆₀ interference (1999) demonstrated duality at mesoscopic scales.

How it works

In the framework of quantum mechanics, the state of a particle is described by a wave function ψ(x,t), which obeys the Schrödinger equation or, for relativistic particles, the Dirac equation. In the double-slit experiment, the wave function passes through both slits, forming a superposition of states 'particle went through slit 1' and 'particle went through slit 2'. Interference between these states yields a probability distribution on the screen with maxima and minima. Measurement of the particle's position collapses the wave function to a single point, so individual particles are registered as dots, and the interference pattern builds up statistically. The Heisenberg uncertainty principle Δx·Δp ≥ ħ/2 links the precision of position and momentum measurement, consistently expressing the complementarity of wave and particle manifestations.

\lambda = \frac{h}{p}
λ is the de Broglie wavelength, h is Planck's constant, h ≈ 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s; p is the particle's momentum, p = mv (for a non-relativistic particle).

Open problems & fun facts

The interpretation problem of quantum mechanics: does a real trajectory of the particle exist before measurement?; The quantum-classical transition: at what size or mass does an object stop exhibiting interference?; Experimental demonstration of duality for increasingly massive particles (the decoherence boundary).
