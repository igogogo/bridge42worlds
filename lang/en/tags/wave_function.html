wave function

📊 0 articles
The wave function is a mathematical description of a cloud of probabilities. Imagine you throw a pebble into a pond: the ripples on the water show where the particle might be. It's like a musical chord that sounds all notes at once, until you press one key.

History

Erwin Schrödinger introduced the wave function in 1926, trying to describe the behavior of electrons in atoms.

How it works

When we don't observe, the electron is in all possible places simultaneously, like a smeared ripple on a pond. Measurement forces it to take a specific position - just as when you touch water, the circles disappear and we see the point of impact.

💡 If you measure the wave function, it "collapses" into one value - as if from a whole ocean of possibilities, one fish is picked.

The wave function is a mathematical representation of the quantum state of a system as a complex probability amplitude. It provides a complete description of the system in Hilbert space, and its time evolution is determined by the Schrödinger equation. The square of the modulus of the wave function gives the probability density of finding the particle at a given point, which is the basis of the probabilistic interpretation of quantum mechanics.

History

Erwin Schrödinger derived his famous equation in 1925-1926, generalizing de Broglie's ideas about matter waves. Max Born proposed the probabilistic interpretation, and John von Neumann introduced a rigorous mathematical formalism. John Stewart Bell in 1964 showed that local hidden variables do not agree with the predictions of quantum mechanics, which was later confirmed by Alain Aspect in experiments with entangled photons.

How it works

The wave function ψ(x,t) is a solution to the Schrödinger equation: iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = H ψ, where H is the Hamiltonian of the system. The superposition principle allows any combination of states to be admissible. Measurement causes wave function collapse according to the projection postulate. Entanglement is described by the non-separability of the overall wave function, confirmed by the violation of Bell's inequalities. Noether's theorem connects symmetries of the wave function with conserved quantities.
