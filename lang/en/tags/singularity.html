singularity

4

A singularity is a point where the familiar laws of physics break down, like a crack in the fabric of spacetime, from which infinite density and temperature erupt.

In practice: Understanding singularities will lead to a theory of quantum gravity that describes the birth of the universe and the interiors of black holes.
A singularity is a region of spacetime where curvature and density become infinite, and known physical theories (general relativity and quantum mechanics) lose meaning. Such singularities are predicted at the centers of black holes and at the point of the Big Bang. Einstein's equations cease to work there, and a theory of quantum gravity is required.

History

The first mathematical description of a singularity appeared in Karl Schwarzschild's solution in 1916. Later, Roger Penrose and Stephen Hawking proved that singularities inevitably arise during the collapse of massive stars.

How it works

A rubber sheet bends under the weight of a ball—this is how gravity works; if the ball is compressed into a point, the sheet ruptures, forming an infinitely sharp funnel. Similarly, spacetime 'tears' at a singularity, creating infinite curvature.

💡 When falling into a black hole, an astronaut reaches the singularity in finite proper time, but for an external observer, he never crosses the horizon—this contradiction illustrates the conflict between theories.
At the center of a black hole (a region from which light cannot escape), all its mass is compressed into a single point with zero volume—this is a singularity. Here, gravity becomes infinite, and time stops. It is like a funnel with infinitely steep walls, into which everything falls and cannot escape.

A singularity in general relativity is a place where the curvature tensor diverges and geodesics terminate, making spacetime incomplete. In the cosmological singularity of the Big Bang, the scale factor goes to zero at infinitely large density and temperature. In black holes, the singularity is hidden by an event horizon according to the cosmic censorship hypothesis, although naked singularities are also possible. Mathematically, singularities indicate the boundary of applicability of classical GR and motivate the search for a quantum theory of gravity, where they may be replaced by Planck-scale structures.

History

In 1916, Karl Schwarzschild found the first exact solution to Einstein's equations for a spherically symmetric body, containing a singularity at the center. For a long time, the singularity was considered a mathematical artifact. In the 1960s, Roger Penrose and Stephen Hawking proved singularity theorems, showing the inevitability of singularities in gravitational collapse and cosmology within classical GR. John Archibald Wheeler popularized the term 'black hole' and, together with his students, developed approaches to quantizing gravity to eliminate singularities; this problem remains unsolved.

How it works

A singularity occurs when the spacetime curvature and the stress-energy tensor in Einstein's equations tend to infinity. In the case of the collapse of a star with a mass exceeding the Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit, gravity overcomes the pressure of degenerate gas, and matter is compressed without limit down to the Planck scale (10^-35 m), where quantum corrections must come into play. In quantum cosmology, the Big Bang singularity may be replaced by quantum tunneling from 'nothing' or a cyclic bounce. Modern theories such as loop quantum gravity and string theory propose mechanisms for resolving singularities through discretization of spacetime.
