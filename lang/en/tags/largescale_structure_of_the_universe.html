large-scale structure of the universe

📊 0 articles

The cosmic web — filaments of galaxies surrounding voids, like soap foam stretched to the size of the Universe.

In practice: Maps of large-scale structure are used to test cosmological models and constrain the properties of dark matter and dark energy.
Large-scale structure is the cellular distribution of galaxies on scales of hundreds of millions of light-years. It arose from primordial quantum fluctuations stretched by inflation. Under gravity, dark matter collapsed into halos, and gas cooled to form galaxies. Characteristic elements: filaments (threads up to 150 Mpc long), voids (empty regions 20–50 Mpc across), superclusters. The structure resembles a void-filament network.
💡 The Hercules–Corona Borealis Great Wall is one of the largest known structures, spanning 10 billion light-years.
The Universe is not uniform: galaxies gather into long filaments and walls, and between them are huge voids. It's like a sponge with holes. Gravity created this picture, pulling together dark matter (invisible substance) and gas.

History

Early hints were found in the 1970s, and 3D maps were built in the 1980s with the CfA survey.

How it works

After the Big Bang, tiny clumps of dark matter attracted material, gradually forming lumps that stretched into fibers. Where filaments intersected, galaxy clusters arose.

The large-scale structure of the Universe is a complex web-like configuration of matter distribution on scales >10 Mpc. It consists of filaments, walls, and nodes where galaxy clusters are concentrated, as well as vast low-density voids. The structure was formed by primordial density perturbations that left an imprint on the cosmic microwave background at recombination. Subsequent growth via gravitational instability led to the observed network. Dark matter, forming the scaffolding, and dark energy, governing global expansion dynamics, play crucial roles. It is studied through redshift surveys (SDSS, 2dF). Statistical characteristics: correlation function, power spectrum.

History

The idea of a hierarchical structure was first voiced by I. Kant and P. Laplace. In the 20th century, E. Hubble confirmed that galaxies lie outside the Milky Way. The first galaxy distribution maps began in the 1950s (Shapley, Ames). In the 1970s, Ya. Zeldovich developed the theory of structure formation (Zeldovich pancakes). Modern understanding began with the CfA survey (1980s), which discovered the Great Wall. Contributions were also made by F. Zwicky and V. Rubin, who discovered dark matter in clusters and galaxies.

How it works

Primordial adiabatic density perturbations, arising during inflation, begin growing under gravity after recombination. Dark matter, not interacting electromagnetically, collapses first, creating potential wells. Baryonic matter falls into these wells, heats, cools, and forms galaxies. On scales of ~100 Mpc, a characteristic cellular pattern emerges, explained within the ΛCDM model. According to the theory, small-scale perturbations collapse earlier, forming substructure; larger ones form later — hierarchical clustering. Evolution is described by the Friedmann equations for the background and Newtonian theory for perturbations. Baryon acoustic oscillations — sound waves in the primordial plasma that left an imprint on the distribution statistics at ~150 Mpc — are important.
