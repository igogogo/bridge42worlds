phonon

📊 0
In solids, atoms are not frozen in place but constantly oscillate. A phonon is the quantum of these vibrations, like a drop of water in a wave.

History

The concept of quantized lattice vibrations was introduced by Soviet physicist Yakov Frenkel in 1932; the term "phonon" was proposed by Igor Tamm.

How it works

Phonons carry heat and sound in solids. When you heat one end of a metal spoon, heat spreads via phonons—they pass energy from atom to atom like a relay baton.

💡 Phonons behave like particles: they can collide, reflect, and transfer energy, like tiny invisible balls.
A phonon is a collective excitation of atoms in a crystal lattice, manifesting as a quantum of sound wave. It resembles a wave traveling across a field of grain: each stalk tilts only slightly, but the wave is transmitted over long distances.

A phonon is a quasiparticle, representing a quantum of vibrational excitation of the crystal lattice. In quantum mechanics, elastic vibrations of atoms in a solid are quantized, analogous to how a photon is a quantum of the electromagnetic field. Phonons obey Bose–Einstein statistics because they have integer spin 0 or 1. Acoustic and optical phonons are distinguished depending on the type of vibration: in the former, atoms move in phase; in the latter, in antiphase. Phonons play a key role in the heat capacity of solids, thermal conductivity, and electron scattering, determining the electrical resistance of materials.

History

The quantization of lattice vibrations dates back to the work of Albert Einstein (1907) and Peter Debye (1912) on the heat capacity of solids. The term "phonon" was coined by Igor Tamm in 1932, and the theoretical foundation was laid by Yakov Frenkel. Since phonons obey Bose–Einstein statistics, an important contribution belongs to Satyendra Nath Bose, who developed quantum statistics for identical particles.

How it works

In the harmonic approximation, the crystal lattice is considered as a system of coupled oscillators. After quantization, the normal modes of vibration are represented by independent harmonic oscillators with quantized energy: E_n = \hbar \omega (n + 1/2). An excitation with energy \hbar \omega is interpreted as a phonon. Bose–Einstein statistics determine the average number of phonons in a mode at a given temperature: ⟨n⟩ = 1/(e^{\hbar \omega / k_B T} - 1). Phonons interact with other quasiparticles, such as electrons, causing scattering.
