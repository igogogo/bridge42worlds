void

📊 0 articles

A void is a cosmic emptiness with a diameter of hundreds of millions of light-years, almost devoid of galaxies. It resembles a gigantic soap bubble from which matter has drained into surrounding walls and filaments.

In practice: Studying voids helps to refine the properties of dark energy and test alternative theories of gravity without resorting to complex ground-based experiments.
Voids are empty spaces with diameters from 30 to 500 megaparsecs, forming, together with galactic filaments, the large-scale structure of the Universe. The matter density in them is orders of magnitude below average, and the typical volume of voids accounts for up to 90% of the observed space. They naturally arise in cold dark matter models due to gravitational instability of primordial fluctuations.

History

Astronomers first saw large voids on maps of galaxy distribution in the late 1970s. In 1981, the Boötes Void was discovered—one of the most prominent 'holes' with a size of over 300 million light-years.

How it works

After the Big Bang, matter was distributed unevenly. In regions with slightly lower density, gravity does not attract matter as strongly, and it 'flows out' into denser regions. Dark energy additionally accelerates the expansion of these voids.

💡 At the center of the largest known void, the Boötes Void, only one dwarf galaxy has been discovered—an absolute record of isolation.
A void is a vast region in the Universe where there are very few galaxies. Such empty spaces resemble holes in the cosmic web. At the center of a void, the sky would be absolutely black to the naked eye. Gravity over billions of years has pulled matter into clumps, leaving almost empty spaces.

A cosmic void is a region with density contrast δ = (ρ – ρ̄)/ρ̄ < –0.8, where ρ is the local and ρ̄ is the average density of the Universe. The hierarchy of voids extends from minivoids (~10 Mpc) to supervoids like the Eridanus Supervoid. The spatial distribution of voids follows the cosmological foam model, and their evolution is described by the solution of supercritical expansion in the ΛCDM model. The internal dynamics gives an effective negative sign of gravitation, pushing out residual matter.

History

The first voids were identified in samples of bright galaxies (Boötes Void, Kirshner et al., 1981). Yakov Zeldovich predicted the formation of voids through 'pancake' dynamics in 1970. The Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS) has cataloged thousands of voids since 2000. The Millennium Simulation (2005) confirmed that in the standard cosmological model, voids are inevitable and reproduce the observed statistics.

How it works

Voids form from negative density perturbations in the cosmic microwave background. Expanding faster than the Hubble flow, they push matter to the boundaries, where walls form. Dark energy (cosmological constant Λ ≈ 10⁻³⁵ s⁻²) ensures the accelerated divergence of walls. In the spherical model, void growth ends when the internal density drops to ~0.2ρ̄; then Λ dominates.

Open problems & fun facts
