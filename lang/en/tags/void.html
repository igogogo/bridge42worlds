void

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A cosmic void is a giant cavity in the distribution of galaxies, where matter is so sparse that light-years of space contain only a few stars.

In practice: Studying voids reveals the mechanisms of large-scale structure formation in the Universe and helps understand how dark energy influences its expansion.
Voids are extensive regions with a reduced density of galaxies, occupying about 80% of the Universe's volume but containing only a few percent of all galaxies. Their diameters range from 20 to 200 megaparsecs (65–650 million light-years). Due to their low density, voids expand faster than the surrounding space. They are not completely empty: inside, there are dwarf galaxies and clouds of cold gas, noticeable as absorption lines in the spectra of distant quasars (Lyman-alpha forest). The edges of voids are delineated by galactic filaments and clusters.

History

Voids were discovered in 1978 when compiling three-dimensional maps of galaxy distributions. It turned out that the large-scale structure of the Universe resembles a network with cells, rather than a homogeneous sea.

How it works

After the Big Bang, dark matter under gravity began to clump together, dragging ordinary matter with it. Where dark matter was slightly less abundant, matter flowed away to denser regions, leaving behind rarefied bubbles — akin to water on a bumpy surface draining into hollows, leaving the high points dry.

💡 The recently discovered KBC void is so large that its size is difficult to explain within the standard cosmological model — perhaps it points to unknown forces or special properties of dark energy.
Galaxies in the Universe are not distributed evenly, but are gathered into thick filaments and walls, between which yawn almost empty regions — voids, resembling bubbles in cosmic foam. For example, the Boötes void is about 330 million light-years across, and it contains so few galaxies that there are only a few per billion cubic light-years.

A void is a large-scale region with below-average matter density (underdensity), identified by algorithms like VoidFinder from galaxy catalogs. Voids arise from primordial negative density fluctuations and evolve nonlinearly: they expand faster than the Hubble flow, and their boundaries become denser. The radial density profile is often described by a universal formula, close to a power law. Internal dynamics lead to a systematic blueshift of galaxies relative to the void center (void expansion effect). Voids serve as laboratories for testing gravitational theories: in modified models (f(R), MOND) different population and profiles are predicted. Voids are used to measure the integrated Sachs-Wolfe effect (delay in structure growth), which is sensitive to dark energy parameters and neutrino mass.

History

The idea of voids in the distribution of galaxies dates back to pioneering works on mapping the Universe in the 1970s (Chincarini, Gregory, Thompson). The term 'void' became established after analysis of the CfA Redshift Survey data in the 1980s, when the Great Wall and adjacent voids were discovered. In 1981, Kirshner and colleagues identified the Boötes void. Theoretical understanding was provided by the works of Sheth and van de Weygaert (2004), establishing statistical properties of voids in hierarchical models. Modern surveys SDSS (2000–) and DESI (2021–) have cataloged tens of thousands of voids.

How it works

Formation and dynamics of a void within the ΛCDM model. From linear theory, the growth of a density perturbation δ(t) ∝ D(t) depends on the cosmological growth factor. A void corresponds to δ < 0. Nonlinear evolution is described by the spherical model (analogous to collapse for overdensities). The equation of motion for a shell of radius R: \ddot{R}/R = -(4πG/3)(ρ_m + 3P_total/c²), where P_total includes the pressure of dark energy P_Λ = -ρ_Λ c². In the era of Λ dominance, the right-hand side becomes positive, which accelerates the void's expansion. Compensation: the excess density on the walls equals the deficit within some radius. Voids also allow testing the model through redshift-space distortions (Alcock-Paczynski test) and through weak lensing.

\frac{\ddot{R}}{R} = -\frac{4\pi G}{3}\sum_i \left(\rho_i + \frac{3p_i}{c^2}\right)
R — radius of the shell; G — gravitational constant (≈6.674×10⁻¹¹ m³/(kg·s²)); the sum is over all components of the Universe (matter, radiation, dark energy) with density ρ_i and pressure p_i; c — speed of light (≈3×10⁸ m/s). For Λ (dark energy) p_Λ = -ρ_Λ c², so the term in brackets becomes -2ρ_Λ, leading to accelerated expansion of the void.
