spacetime

📊 0 articles
Spacetime is like a fabric that unifies space and time into a single whole. Imagine the Universe as a giant trampoline. If you place a heavy ball (a star) on it, it will sag, and everything around will start moving toward that depression. Time also slows down near massive objects—as if clocks run slower when they are near the ball.
💡 If you could travel at a speed close to the speed of light, time would flow slower for you, and you could "travel into the future" faster than people on Earth!
Spacetime is a single arena where all events unfold. It can be imagined as a river, where space is the channel and time is the current. Massive objects, like boulders in the channel, curve spacetime, altering the flow of events. Gravity is not a force of attraction but a consequence of this curvature: as if a boat sailed along a curved channel, skirting around a boulder.

History

The idea of spacetime was introduced by Hermann Minkowski in 1908, unifying space and time into a four-dimensional continuum. Later, Albert Einstein developed it into the general theory of relativity (1915), showing that gravity is the curvature of spacetime. Scientists such as Henri Poincaré and Karl Schwarzschild contributed to understanding its properties.

How it works

Curved spacetime manifests as gravity: planets revolve around the Sun not because something attracts them, but because the Sun warps spacetime, and they move along the straightest possible lines in this curved world—geodesics. It is as if an ant crawled on a crumpled sheet of paper: to it, the path seems straight, but in fact it goes around a dent.

💡 GPS navigation accounts for time dilation due to Earth's gravity: clocks on satellites run faster than on the surface, and without general relativity corrections, position error would accumulate by 10 km per day.
Spacetime is a fundamental physical concept that unifies three spatial dimensions and one temporal dimension into a single four-dimensional continuum. In special relativity, space and time are linked by Lorentz transformations such that the speed of light is constant in all inertial reference frames. In general relativity, gravity is described as a geometric property of spacetime, whose curvature is produced by mass and energy according to Einstein's equations. The spacetime metric defines the intervals between events, and the motion of test particles occurs along geodesic lines. Cosmological solutions, such as the Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric, describe the expansion of the Universe. Quantum gravity attempts to unify it with quantum mechanics.

History

In 1905, Henri Poincaré and Albert Einstein independently laid the foundations of special relativity, where space and time ceased to be absolute. In 1908, Hermann Minkowski formulated four-dimensional spacetime. In 1915, Einstein completed the general theory of relativity, where the field equations (Einstein equations) linked curvature with energy-momentum. In 1916, Karl Schwarzschild found the first exact solution for a spherical mass, predicting black holes. In 1927, Georges Lemaître applied the equations to cosmology, showing the expansion of the Universe, later confirmed by Edwin Hubble.

How it works

The properties of spacetime are described by the metric tensor g_{\mu\nu}, which defines distances and causal structure. Mass curves spacetime, and curvature dictates motion, as expressed in Einstein's equations: G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}. Special relativity is based on the principle of the constancy of the speed of light, which states that the speed of light in vacuum is the same for all inertial observers. Motion along geodesics explains gravity without forces. In cosmology, the Friedmann equations, derived from Einstein's equations, together with Hubble's law describe the expansion of spacetime on large scales.
