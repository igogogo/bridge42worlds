differential equation

📊 0 articles

A differential equation is like a recipe that tells us how fast something is changing, and from that we find out what will happen next. Knowing a car's speed at each moment, we can recover its path.

In practice: They are needed everywhere: from weather forecasting and calculating satellite orbits to modeling population growth and the operation of electronic circuits.
A differential equation describes the relationship between a function and its derivatives — the rates of change. Such equations underlie physical laws: from planetary motion to quantum mechanics. Newton's second law (acceleration proportional to force) is a differential equation: force gives the acceleration (second derivative of position), and the equation allows finding the trajectory.
💡 The Navier-Stokes equations, describing fluid motion, are differential equations for which a million dollars is offered for a solution (one of the Millennium Prize problems).
A differential equation relates an unknown quantity (e.g., position) with how fast it changes (rate of change). It’s like knowing the steepness of the slope at each point of a mountain and from that finding the height. The derivative shows the slope (how fast the height changes), and the equation is the rule for how to obtain the height from the slope.

History

People began solving simple differential equations in the 17th century. Isaac Newton and Gottfried Leibniz independently created the mathematics of change (differential and integral calculus), which allowed writing such equations.

How it works

You walk along a trail, and you have a device that shows the slope at each step. A differential equation is the rule for how to reconstruct the shape of the trail from the slope. Solving the equation gives the height for any position.

💡 The Korteweg-de Vries equation, describing waves in shallow water, has amazing solutions — solitons that behave like particles upon collisions. It can be solved using methods of quantum scattering theory.
A differential equation is an equation containing an unknown function and its derivatives. If the function depends on one variable, it is an ordinary differential equation; if on several — a partial differential equation. The order is determined by the highest derivative. Classes: linear and nonlinear, homogeneous and nonhomogeneous. The Cauchy problem includes initial conditions; a boundary value problem — conditions on the boundary. Solution methods: separation of variables, integrating factor, variation of parameters, series, numerical methods (Runge-Kutta).

History

The concept of differential equation emerged simultaneously with differential and integral calculus at the end of the 17th century. Isaac Newton introduced differential equations to describe motion; Gottfried Leibniz developed the formalism of derivatives and integrals. In the 18th century, Leonhard Euler and Joseph-Louis Lagrange developed methods for solving ODEs, and Jean le Rond d'Alembert — for partial differential equations (vibrating string). In the 19th century, Augustin-Louis Cauchy gave a rigorous theory of existence and uniqueness of solutions. Today the theory includes qualitative analysis, stability, and numerical methods.

How it works

A differential equation expresses a local rule: the rate of change of a quantity depends on the quantity itself. The solution recovers the global behavior. Newton's law: \( m\ddot{x} = F(x, \dot{x}, t) \) is a second-order ordinary differential equation. Integrating with initial conditions yields the trajectory. Linear ODEs with constant coefficients reduce to the characteristic equation. Nonlinear ones require linearization or analysis of phase portraits. Partial differential equations (heat conduction \( \partial_t u = \alpha \nabla^2 u \)) are solved taking into account boundary conditions. Methods: separation of variables, spectral decomposition, finite differences.
