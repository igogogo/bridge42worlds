An asteroid is a rocky boulder racing around the Sun. There are millions of such cosmic cobblestones between Mars and Jupiter in the asteroid belt.

History

The first asteroid, Ceres, was discovered by Giuseppe Piazzi in 1801. At first it was considered a planet, but soon Pallas, Juno, and Vesta were found in the same region of the sky — it became clear that this was a new class of objects. The term 'asteroids' (star-like) was proposed by William Herschel.

How it works

Asteroids are leftover construction debris from the time of planet formation. Jupiter's gravity prevented them from sticking together into a single body, and now they circle like a swarm of gnats around a lantern, occasionally colliding and breaking into pieces.

An asteroid is a solid body of the Solar System, ranging from a few meters to hundreds of kilometers in size, orbiting the Sun primarily between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter. Chemically, they are divided into carbonaceous (C-class), silicate (S-class), and metallic (M-class) asteroids. Their shape is often irregular due to insufficient gravity to achieve sphericity.

History

After the discovery of Ceres in 1801 by Giuseppe Piazzi as part of the search for a supposed planet between Mars and Jupiter, several more large bodies were quickly found. By the mid-19th century, their number exceeded a hundred. Photographic methods of the 20th century, and later space surveys (LINEAR, Pan-STARRS), increased the catalog to hundreds of thousands. Today, specialized missions study them: NEAR Shoemaker, OSIRIS-REx, Hayabusa.

How it works

The motion of asteroids is described by Kepler's third law and perturbations from planets, especially Jupiter. Their collisional evolution is governed by the conservation laws of angular momentum and energy. The equilibrium temperature is determined by the Stefan–Boltzmann law, taking into account albedo and distance from the Sun. Spectral classification (by absorption lines in the infrared range) relies on Maxwell's equations to interpret radiative properties.

T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{G M_\odot} a^3
T — orbital period, G — gravitational constant (6.67430×10⁻¹¹ m³ kg⁻¹ s⁻²), M_⊙ — solar mass (1.989×10³⁰ kg), a — semi-major axis of the asteroid's orbit
