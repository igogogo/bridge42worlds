Lie Algebra

📊 0 articles
A Lie algebra is a mathematical 'multiplication table' for infinitesimal symmetries, for example, how to describe a rotation by a very small angle.
💡 Lie algebras are used in robotics to control robot movements: they describe how a robot can move smoothly.
A Lie algebra is a vector space with a bilinear operation — the Lie bracket — that satisfies properties of antisymmetry and the Jacobi identity. It is a linearization of a Lie group in the neighborhood of the identity. For example, the Lie algebra of the rotation group SO(3) consists of 3×3 skew-symmetric matrices, and the bracket is the matrix commutator.

History

The concept of a Lie algebra was introduced by Sophus Lie simultaneously with Lie groups in the late 19th century. Élie Cartan classified all simple Lie algebras over complex numbers, which was an important step for physics.

How it works

Imagine you want to describe a very small rotation of a cube. Instead of using the rotation angle, you take an 'infinitesimal' generator — an element of the Lie algebra. Combining two such small rotations gives a third, and the Lie bracket shows how much the result differs from the sum.

💡 Lie algebras underlie quantum mechanics: the commutation relations of position and momentum operators form the Heisenberg algebra.
A Lie algebra is a vector space g over a field (usually ℝ or ℂ) with a bilinear operation [,]: g×g→g, called the Lie bracket, which is antisymmetric ([X,Y] = -[Y,X]) and satisfies the Jacobi identity ([X,[Y,Z]] + [Y,[Z,X]] + [Z,[X,Y]] = 0). Lie algebras are classified into semisimple, solvable, and nilpotent. Semisimple Lie algebras are completely classified by Dynkin diagrams. They are tangent spaces to Lie groups and describe infinitesimal symmetries.

History

Sophus Lie introduced Lie algebras as infinitesimal groups of transformations. Élie Cartan in his dissertation (1894) classified all complex simple Lie algebras. Hermann Weyl and Eugene Wigner applied representation theory of Lie algebras to quantum mechanics. In the mid-20th century, Lie algebras became the foundation of gauge theories and string theory.

How it works

A Lie algebra g is associated with a Lie group G as the tangent space at the identity. The Lie bracket comes from the commutator of left-invariant vector fields. A representation of a Lie algebra is a homomorphism g→gl(V) preserving the bracket. The classification of simple Lie algebras gives series A_n, B_n, C_n, D_n and exceptional E6, E7, E8, F4, G2. Structure constants f^c_{ab} are defined as [e_a, e_b] = f^c_{ab} e_c.

The Jacobi identity is a necessary condition for a Lie bracket, ensuring consistency.
