Large Hadron Collider

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A giant ring that accelerates protons to near-light speeds and smashes them head-on to peer deep into matter.

In practice: The LHC discovered the Higgs boson — the last missing piece of the Standard Model. The superconducting magnets and vacuum technologies developed for it are now used in proton cancer therapy and for sterilizing medical equipment.
The Large Hadron Collider is a 27-kilometer ring, buried underground at depths of up to 175 meters. In it, two counter-rotating beams of protons are accelerated to an energy of 6.8 TeV each (speed — 0.99999999 of the speed of light) and collide head-on with a total energy of 13.6 TeV. The beams are kept in orbit by 1232 superconducting magnets, cooled by liquid helium to 1.9 K (colder than outer space). At the collision points, gigantic detectors — ATLAS, CMS, LHCb, and ALICE — record the hundreds of particles born in each collision.

History

Construction began in 1998 at CERN. The first launch was on September 10, 2008, but a few days later a serious accident disabled a sector, and repairs took more than a year. Full high-energy collisions began in 2010, and in 2012 the ATLAS and CMS collaborations announced the discovery of the Higgs boson — a particle predicted 48 years earlier.

How it works

Protons, like two trains rushing along opposite tracks, are accelerated by electric fields and held by magnets. When they collide head-on, new particles are born, like fragments from a crash. Detectors work like giant microscopes, examining these fragments.

💡 In one second, protons in the LHC circle the ring 11,245 times, and the total energy of each beam is comparable to that of a high-speed train.
The LHC is the most powerful particle accelerator in the world. It is built in an underground tunnel on the border between Switzerland and France. Two beams of protons (or lead nuclei) are accelerated to near-light speeds and collide head-on in four locations. There, detectors the size of a multi-story building record what is born in these micro-explosions.

The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) is a proton-proton and nucleus-nucleus collider, designed for research at center-of-mass energies up to 14 TeV (protons) and 2.76 TeV per nucleon (Pb-Pb). Acceleration occurs in several stages: Linac4 linear accelerator → PSB booster synchrotron → PS proton synchrotron → SPS super proton synchrotron, then injection into the main LHC ring. To keep the beams on track, dipole magnets based on NbTi superconductor are used, operating at superfluid helium temperature. Focusing is provided by quadrupole and higher-order magnets. The luminosity (collision density) reaches 2×10³⁴ cm⁻²s⁻¹, enabling gigahertz event rates. Major discoveries: Higgs boson (2012), pentaquarks, study of quark-gluon plasma.

History

The LHC project emerged in the early 1980s as a successor to the Large Electron-Positron Collider (LEP). After approval in 1994, construction proceeded from 1998 to 2008. On September 10, 2008, a beam was successfully circulated, but 9 days later an electrical fault caused a helium explosion and took a sector out of commission. A restart after repairs and upgrades occurred in November 2009, and the first 7 TeV collisions took place in March 2010. Thousands of CERN engineers and scientists played a key role in creating the accelerator, and the ATLAS and CMS collaborations were instrumental in discovering the Higgs boson.

How it works

Protons are accelerated by high-frequency resonators (400 MHz), and the trajectory is maintained by superconducting magnets. When counter-rotating bunches collide at the intersection points, thousands of particles are born, which are registered by multi-layered detectors: tracking systems (silicon pixels), calorimeters (electromagnetic made of PbWO₄ crystals, hadronic — iron/scintillator), muon chambers. Magnetic fields in the detectors (solenoids or toroidal magnets) enable momentum measurement. A huge challenge is extracting rare events from the background at high luminosity — this is accomplished using online trigger systems and offline analysis on computer farms worldwide (GRID).

\sqrt{s} = 2E_{\mathrm{beam}}
\sqrt{s} is the total energy in the center-of-mass system (effective collision energy), E_beam is the energy of one proton in the beam. At E_beam = 6.8 TeV, \sqrt{s} = 13.6 TeV.

Open problems & fun facts

The hierarchy problem: why is the mass of the Higgs boson many orders of magnitude smaller than the Planck mass, although quantum corrections should make it huge?; Dark matter: despite extensive searches, the LHC has not found candidates (WIMP particles, superpartners), challenging theories.; Incompleteness of the Standard Model: it does not include gravity, does not explain dark energy, and does not account for the observed predominance of matter over antimatter.
