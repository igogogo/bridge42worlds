Large Hadron Collider

📊 0 articles
The Large Hadron Collider is a giant underground racetrack for the tiniest particles. Imagine two streams that are accelerated to nearly the speed of light and collide, like two boats at full speed. At the moment of impact, new particles are born like splashes. Scientists study these 'splashes' to understand what everything is made of.
💡 The collider's ring is 27 kilometers long, like the circumference of a city, and the magnets inside are so powerful that it would take the electricity of an entire nuclear power plant to power them. The temperature inside is lower than in outer space.
The LHC is the world's most powerful particle accelerator, speeding up protons and ions to record energies. Just as the collision of two bullets releases energy and fragments, here new particles are created at collision points, including the elusive Higgs boson, discovered in 2012.

History

Built at the European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN) under the border between Switzerland and France. Construction began in 1998, the first run in 2008. In 2012 the discovery of the Higgs boson was announced.

How it works

Particle beams are accelerated by electric fields in a circular tunnel, while powerful superconducting magnets guide them around. At four detector points, the beams collide, and sensors record the debris of these micro-catastrophes, recreating conditions that existed shortly after the Big Bang.

💡 Inside the LHC, protons travel at almost the speed of light, making over 11,000 laps per second. And the vacuum in the tube is so profound that the pressure is ten trillion times lower than atmospheric pressure – like in interstellar space.
The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) is a proton-proton (and ion) collider located at CERN, with a ring circumference of 26.7 km, operating at a center-of-mass energy up to 13.6 TeV (as of 2023). It is designed to study fundamental particles and interactions of the Standard Model and to search for new physics, including dark matter, extra dimensions, and supersymmetry. The collider uses superconducting niobium-titanium magnets, cooled to 1.9 K, producing fields up to 8.3 T.

History

The idea for the Large Hadron Collider dates back to the 1980s. The project was officially approved in 1994. Construction took place in the existing LEP (Large Electron-Positron Collider) tunnel. In 2008, the startup was postponed due to a helium cooling incident. After repairs and upgrades, the LHC reached design parameters and in 2012 made the historic discovery of the Higgs boson, predicted by Peter Higgs and others in 1964. During 2015–2018 (Run 2), the collider operated at 13 TeV, and in Run 3 (from 2022) the energy was increased to 13.6 TeV.

How it works

Protons are accelerated in several stages: a linear accelerator, the Proton Synchrotron (PS), the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS), and finally the LHC ring. Radiofrequency cavities create an electric field that accelerates the particles. The magnetic system, based on the Meissner effect in superconductors, keeps the beams in a circular orbit. At the beam crossing points (ATLAS, CMS, ALICE, LHCb) detectors record the products of collisions. Particle creation is described by equations of quantum field theory, and the analysis is based on conservation of energy and momentum (relativistic kinematics, E=mc²).

E = mc^2
E — energy, m — rest mass, c — speed of light in vacuum (c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s). In the collider, the kinetic energy of colliding particles converts into the mass of new particles.
