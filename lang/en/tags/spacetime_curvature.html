spacetime curvature

📊 233 articles
Spacetime bends like a rubber sheet when a heavy ball is placed on it. Massive objects — stars and planets — dent it, forcing other bodies to move along curved trajectories, which we call orbits.
💡 The Sun curves space so much that light from distant stars passing nearby is deflected by a tiny angle — this was proven by Eddington during the 1919 eclipse.
General relativity describes gravity not as a force, but as geometry. Just as a river's current flows around stones, changing direction, so mass curves spacetime, prescribing the paths of everything, including light.

History

The idea of curved space dates back to Bernhard Riemann in 1854, but the physical meaning was given by Albert Einstein in 1915, completing the general theory of relativity. The first exact model of curved spacetime for a point mass was found by Karl Schwarzschild in 1916.

How it works

If you place a watermelon on a trampoline, it creates a dent. An orange thrown nearby will roll along a curve — similarly, the Earth moves in the well created by the Sun's mass. The more massive the object, the deeper the 'pit', and even light cannot escape from a black hole.

💡 Near the Earth's surface, time runs slightly slower than in orbit — due to spacetime curvature. GPS satellites constantly adjust their clocks, otherwise navigation would drift by kilometers.
Spacetime curvature is the key concept of general relativity, replacing Newton's notion of gravitational force with a geometric property of the manifold. In the presence of mass and energy, the spacetime metric ceases to be flat, and geodesics — the shortest paths — curve. Curvature is quantitatively described by the Riemann tensor, which, via Einstein's equations, is linked to the stress-energy tensor. Along with local effects like gravitational redshift and Shapiro delay, global curvature determines cosmological evolution and can lead to exotic objects such as black holes and wormholes.

History

The concept of non-Euclidean geometry was developed by mathematicians Gauss, Lobachevsky, and Bolyai, but Riemann in 1854 introduced the notion of a curved manifold of arbitrary dimension. Gregorio Ricci and Tullio Levi-Civita developed tensor calculus, which became the language of the theory. In 1915, Einstein, using these tools and the equivalence principle, formulated the field equations linking curvature to matter. Karl Schwarzschild in 1916 obtained the first exact solution for a spherically symmetric body, predicting black holes. Subsequently, Friedmann and Lemaître applied the theory to the entire universe, showing its expansion.

How it works

Mathematically, curvature is described by the metric tensor g_{μν}, which defines the invariant interval ds^2 = g_{μν} dx^μ dx^ν. Curvature is determined through Christoffel symbols and the Riemann tensor R^ρ_{σμν}. Einstein's equations: R_{μν} - (1/2) g_{μν} R + Λ g_{μν} = (8πG/c^4) T_{μν}, where R_{μν} is the Ricci tensor, R is the scalar curvature, Λ is the cosmological constant, T_{μν} is the stress-energy tensor. In the weak gravitational limit, the equations yield Newton's law of universal gravitation. Curvature has been experimentally verified through effects: light deflection, signal delay, perihelion precession of Mercury, gravitational waves.
