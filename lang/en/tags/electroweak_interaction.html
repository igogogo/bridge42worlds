Electroweak Interaction

📊 1 articles
The electroweak interaction is the unification of two forces of nature: electromagnetism and the weak interaction, like the unification of electricity and magnetism.
💡 At high energies, electromagnetism and the weak interaction become indistinguishable, like water at different temperatures.
The electroweak interaction is a single force that unifies the electromagnetic and weak interactions. It manifests at high energies, while at low energies it separates into two different interactions.

History

The theory of the electroweak interaction was developed by Sheldon Glashow, Abdus Salam, and Steven Weinberg in the 1960s.

How it works

At high energies, photons and W/Z bosons behave identically, but at low energies, the symmetry is broken and they become different particles.

💡 The prediction of W and Z bosons was confirmed in 1983, for which Glashow, Salam, and Weinberg received the Nobel Prize.
The electroweak interaction is a unified fundamental interaction that combines electromagnetism and the weak interaction. It is described by a gauge theory with the group SU(2)×U(1). The Higgs mechanism breaks the electroweak symmetry, giving mass to the W and Z bosons.

History

The theory of the electroweak interaction was created in the 1960s by Glashow, Salam, and Weinberg. They predicted the existence of neutral currents (1973) and W/Z bosons (1983). Experimental confirmation earned them the Nobel Prize in 1979.

How it works

At energies above 100 GeV, the electroweak symmetry is restored, and all gauge bosons are massless. At low energies, the Higgs mechanism breaks the symmetry, giving mass to the W and Z, while the photon remains massless. This explains the differences in the range of interactions.
