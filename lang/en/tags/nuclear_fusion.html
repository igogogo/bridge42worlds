nuclear fusion

📊 0 articles
Just as two droplets of oil merge into one larger one and release heat, the tiny hydrogen nuclei in the heart of the Sun fuse into helium, giving us light and life.
💡 Every second, about 4 million tons of mass disappear in the depths of the Sun, turning into energy that would last humanity for billions of years.
Nuclear fusion is the merging of light nuclei into heavier ones, like the kiss of two drops of mercury that merge into one and release heat. This process powers stars and creates chemical elements.

History

In the 1920s, astronomers realized that stars could not shine for billions of years if they were simply contracting. Arthur Eddington suggested that energy comes from the conversion of hydrogen into helium, and by 1939, Hans Bethe described the specific chains of nuclear reactions.

How it works

For nuclei to fuse, they must overcome mutual electric repulsion — like two magnets with the same poles. Inside the Sun, colossal pressure and a temperature of 15 million degrees accelerate the nuclei so much that they 'punch through' into each other, releasing gigantic energy.

💡 The hydrogen bomb is an example of uncontrolled fusion, while scientists worldwide are trying to tame this process in tokamaks and laser facilities to obtain an inexhaustible source of clean energy.
Nuclear fusion is the process of merging two atomic nuclei to form a heavier nucleus and, often, free neutrons or protons. The reaction proceeds with energy release when the total rest mass of the products is less than the mass of the initial nuclei; the mass defect transforms into kinetic energy of particles and radiation according to E=Δmc². In astrophysics, fusion is the source of stellar energy and the main mechanism of nucleosynthesis of elements up to iron.

History

Spectroscopic studies by Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin in 1925 showed that stars consist mainly of hydrogen and helium, laying the observational foundation. Theoretical understanding of thermonuclear reactions developed through the efforts of Eddington, Atkinson, Houtermans, and particularly Hans Bethe, who in 1939 detailed the proton-proton chain and CNO cycle. Modern experiments on controlled fusion began in the mid-20th century and continue on facilities like ITER and NIF.

How it works

To overcome the Coulomb barrier, nuclei need high kinetic energy, which in stars is achieved by thermal motion at temperatures of millions of kelvins. Quantum tunneling allows reactions to occur at energies below the barrier height. The released energy creates radiation pressure that balances gravitational contraction. On Earth, magnetic confinement of plasma (tokamak) or inertial confinement (laser compression of targets) are used to initiate fusion.

4p \rightarrow {}^4\mathrm{He} + 2e^+ + 2\nu_e + 26.7\,\mathrm{MeV}
p — proton (hydrogen nucleus); ^4He — helium-4 nucleus; e^+ — positron; ν_e — electron neutrino; 26.7 MeV — total energy released per cycle of fusion of four protons
