Nuclear Fusion

📊 2 articles
A process in which light atomic nuclei merge to form a heavier nucleus, releasing enormous amounts of energy. It is the energy source of the Sun and stars. Imagine two ping-pong balls sticking together into one larger ball, releasing energy — similarly, nuclei overcome repulsion and fuse.
💡 One gram of hydrogen fuel in a fusion reaction yields as much energy as burning 11 tons of coal.
A process in which light atomic nuclei merge to form a heavier nucleus, releasing enormous amounts of energy. It is the energy source of the Sun and stars. Imagine two ping-pong balls sticking together into one larger ball, releasing energy — similarly, nuclei overcome repulsion and fuse.

History

The idea of nuclear fusion as the source of stellar energy was proposed by Arthur Eddington in the 1920s. Hans Bethe described the proton-proton chain in 1939. The hydrogen bomb (1952) demonstrated uncontrolled fusion. Currently, work is underway on controlled thermonuclear fusion (ITER).

How it works

Fusion requires extreme temperatures (millions of degrees) and pressure to overcome the Coulomb repulsion of nuclei. Analogy: to bring two strong magnets together with like poles, you need to apply force. In stars, fusion occurs in the core, converting hydrogen into helium.
