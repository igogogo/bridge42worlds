nuclear fusion

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
1

Thermonuclear fusion is the stellar source of energy: light nuclei merge into heavier ones, converting a tiny fraction of mass into colossal power.

In practice: Drives the Sun and stars; Earth-based prototypes like tokamaks promise inexhaustible and clean energy of the future.
Thermonuclear fusion is a reaction of merging light nuclei, occurring with energy release due to the increase in the binding energy per nucleon. The most promising is the deuterium-tritium reaction: D + T → He-4 + n + 17.6 MeV. To initiate it, a temperature of about 100 million °C and high pressure are required. In stars, pressure is created by gravity; on Earth, by magnetic traps (tokamaks) or inertial confinement with lasers.

History

Arthur Eddington in the 1920s suggested the nuclear nature of stellar heat, and in the 1930s Hans Bethe described the reaction chains that power the stars. On Earth, controlled fusion has been researched since the mid-20th century, but a net energy gain has not yet been achieved.

How it works

Nuclei are positive and repel each other. To fuse them, matter is heated to millions of degrees, turning it into plasma — a gas of free electrons and ions. In the plasma, nuclei collide and, if they have enough energy, overcome the electric repulsion and stick together under the action of the strong nuclear force (the most powerful but short-range force of nature).

💡 The hydrogen bomb realizes uncontrolled fusion with a giant energy release, but the peaceful version has long remained a 'plasma trap': keeping the incandescent gas without touching the walls is extremely difficult.
Nuclear fusion is the merging of two light nuclei (for example, hydrogen isotopes — deuterium and tritium) into one larger one (helium). The total mass of the products is slightly less than the original — this mass defect is converted into energy according to E=mc². In the Sun's core, every second 600 million tons of hydrogen turn into helium, releasing the energy that warms the Earth.

Nuclear fusion is an exoenergetic reaction of nuclei merging, possible for elements lighter than iron (the peak of the binding energy curve). The most easily achievable Earthly cycle is D–T with a Coulomb barrier of ~0.1 MeV. The probability of tunneling through the barrier is determined by the Gamow factor. The Lawson criterion (nTτ_E) sets the conditions for ignition. Stellar nucleosynthesis: the pp-chain and CNO cycle produce He from H; in massive stars, carbon, oxygen, etc., burn up to iron. Heavy elements are formed in s- and r-processes during supernova explosions or neutron star mergers.

History

Eddington (1920) proposed the hypothesis of nuclear energy in stars. Quantitative models were proposed by Atkinson and Houtermans (1929), and Bethe and von Weizsäcker (1938) developed detailed cycles. The discovery of deuterium (Urey, 1932) and tritium facilitated laboratory research. The first thermonuclear bomb — Ivy Mike (1952). The idea of the tokamak (Sakharov and Tamm, 1950s) gave rise to the JET and ITER projects. Q ~ 0.67 was achieved (JET, 1997); ITER's goal is Q > 10.

How it works

Fusion is enabled by the strong interaction at a scale of ~1 fm, overcoming Coulomb repulsion thanks to quantum tunneling. In stars, gravity confines the plasma; thermonuclear reactions are self-sustaining at a temperature of ~1.5×10⁷ K (the Sun's core). In tokamaks, magnetic fields of a helical configuration isolate the plasma. The energy release is described by the mass-energy equivalence: E = Δm c², where Δm is the mass defect. For D–T, Δm = 0.0189 amu per reaction, yielding 17.6 MeV, distributed between an α-particle and a neutron.

E = m c^2
E is the released energy (J), m is the mass defect (difference in mass of the initial nuclei and products, kg), c is the speed of light in vacuum (c ≈ 2.99792458×10⁸ m/s)
