Argon

📊 0 articles
Argon (Ar) is an inert, colorless, odorless gas, the third most abundant in Earth's atmosphere (0.93%). It is like a 'silent guardian': it does not react with anything, so it is used to create a protective atmosphere in welding or for storing historical documents. In an electrical discharge, argon glows bluish.
💡 Argon makes up about 1% of the Martian atmosphere, making it suitable for use as a shielding gas in future colonizations.

History

Argon was discovered in 1894 by Lord Rayleigh and William Ramsay. Rayleigh noticed that nitrogen from air was slightly heavier than nitrogen obtained chemically. This discrepancy led to the isolation of a new gas — argon (from Greek 'argos' — lazy, inactive). The discovery of argon initiated the study of noble gases.

How it works

Argon is chemically inert because its outer electron shell is completely filled. Therefore, it does not form compounds (except exotic ones under extremely high pressures). In everyday life, argon is used in incandescent lamps to prevent oxidation of the tungsten filament, as well as in fire extinguishers and in the production of titanium.
