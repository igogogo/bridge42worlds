primordial black hole

📊 0

Primordial black holes are hypothetical tiny objects that arose in the first moments of the Universe, when the compression of superdense matter could create "seeds" of darkness the size of an atom.

In practice: They can explain the nature of dark matter and serve as sources of gravitational waves, opening a window into the physics of the birth of the cosmos.
Primordial black holes are hypothetical black holes formed not as a result of stellar collapse, but due to gravitational compression of extremely dense regions in the first moments after the Big Bang. Their masses can range from micrograms to thousands of solar masses. Due to their small size, they are almost invisible, but can reveal themselves through gravitational lensing or bursts of radiation during evaporation.

History

The idea was proposed by Soviet physicists Yakov Zeldovich and Igor Novikov in 1966. Later, Stephen Hawking showed that they must radiate and evaporate.

How it works

In the infancy of the Universe, density and temperature were enormous. If in some region the density turned out to be slightly higher than average, it could collapse under its own gravity, forming a black hole. Ordinary black holes are born from dying stars, while PBHs arose from the very "dough" of the Universe.

💡 If a primordial black hole with the mass of an asteroid were to pass through Earth, it would leave a microscopic tunnel and cause weak seismic waves — this signal is being sought in seismograph data.
In the early Universe (immediately after the Big Bang — the start of expansion from a super-hot point), random clumps of superdense energy could collapse into black holes (regions of space with such strong gravity that even light cannot escape). These primordial black holes are much smaller than ordinary ones: with the mass of an asteroid or a mountain, but incredibly dense.

Primordial black holes (PBHs) are a population of black holes thought to have originated in the prestellar era of the Universe, before the formation of the first stars. Their formation requires the presence of superdense regions in the primordial matter, which collapse under their own gravity when the Hubble scale is sufficiently small. The mass spectrum of PBHs depends on the mechanism of generation of primordial density perturbations, for example, during inflation or phase transitions. PBHs could constitute part of dark matter if their masses fall within certain ranges that avoid observational constraints from microlensing, cosmic gamma-ray background, and gravitational wave events.

History

The concept of primordial black holes was first proposed by Yakov Zeldovich and Igor Novikov in 1966. Stephen Hawking in 1974 showed that quantum effects cause black holes to emit radiation (Hawking radiation) and evaporate; this meant that low-mass PBHs should have disappeared by the present time, while more massive ones survived to this day. In the following decades, PBHs were actively studied as candidates for dark matter, and with the onset of gravitational wave observations (LIGO/Virgo), they are considered as a possible source of compact mergers.

How it works

The formation of PBHs requires extreme density fluctuations in the early Universe. According to Einstein's equations of general relativity, if the density in a region exceeds the critical value at the particle horizon, that region collapses into a black hole. Depending on the moment of collapse, the mass of the PBH is approximately equal to the mass inside the horizon at that time. Hawking radiation leads to the evaporation of PBHs whose lifetime is less than the age of the Universe: PBHs with mass less than ~5×10^14 grams have already evaporated. More massive ones survived and can manifest themselves through gravitational microlensing of stars, dynamical influence on star clusters, or mergers detected by gravitational wave observatories.
