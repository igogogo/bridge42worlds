Fiber Bundle

📊 0 articles
A fiber bundle is a mathematical construction where to each point of a space a separate space is 'attached', for example, as to each point of the Earth a plumb line is attached showing the downward direction.
💡 Fiber bundles are used in string theory: each point of spacetime has a 'curled up' internal Calabi-Yau space.
A fiber bundle is a triple (E, B, π), where E is the total space, B is the base, π: E→B is a projection, such that locally E looks like B×F (F is the fiber). For example, the tangent bundle of a manifold: at each point a tangent space is attached to the manifold. Bundles can be vector bundles, principal bundles, and associated bundles.

History

The concept of a fiber bundle arose in the 1930s in the works of Hassler Whitney and Eduard Stiefel. In the 1940s, Norman Steenrod developed the theory of fiber bundles. In physics, fiber bundles became important after the works of Shiing-Shen Chern and in Yang-Mills gauge theories.

How it works

Imagine you have a book (base) and on each page you draw a circle (fiber). Together, all circles form the total space — a cylinder. A fiber bundle is a way to 'glue' these circles into a single whole, and locally (on one page) it looks like page×circle, but globally it may be twisted (like a Möbius strip).

💡 Principal bundles with group U(1) describe the electromagnetic field in quantum mechanics: the wave function of a particle is a section of such a bundle.
A fiber bundle is a structure (E, B, π, F, G), where E is the total space, B is the base, π: E→B is a continuous surjection, F is the typical fiber, G is the structure group acting on F. Locally, E is homeomorphic to U×F for open U⊂B, and the gluing functions are given by elements of G. Examples: tangent bundle TM (fiber is a vector space), principal bundle (fiber is a Lie group), sphere bundle. A section of a bundle is a map s: B→E such that π∘s = id_B.

History

The concept of a fiber bundle was formalized by Hassler Whitney in 1935. Eduard Stiefel introduced characteristic classes. Norman Steenrod published the book 'The Topology of Fiber Bundles' in 1951. In physics, gauge theories (Yang, Mills, 1954) use principal bundles: the gauge field is a connection on a principal bundle. String theory uses Calabi-Yau bundles.

How it works

A fiber bundle is given by an atlas of trivializations: for each U_i ⊂ B there is a homeomorphism φ_i: π^{-1}(U_i) → U_i × F. On overlaps U_i ∩ U_j, transition functions t_{ij}: U_i∩U_j → G act on F: φ_j∘φ_i^{-1}(x, f) = (x, t_{ij}(x)·f). A connection on a bundle is a distribution of horizontal subspaces, allowing parallel transport of fibers. The curvature of a connection measures the non-integrability of the horizontal distribution.

\pi^{-1}(U) \cong U \times F
Over a sufficiently small neighborhood U, the total space is homeomorphic to the direct product of the base and the fiber.
