Mathematics is like a spiderweb: objects are nodes, and arrows are threads connecting them. Category theory studies these connections, forgetting what is inside the nodes. The key is how the threads intertwine, not the material they are made of.
💡 Categories can even be found in a cookbook: recipes are objects, and arrows are ways to go from one to another (e.g., adding an ingredient). This idea is used by programmers to create reliable software.
A coral reef ecosystem is a living category: fish, algae, corals are objects; relationships such as "who eats whom" or "who hides where" are arrows. Category theory studies how these interactions form a whole picture without delving into the internal structure of species.

History

Category theory was invented in the 1940s by mathematicians Samuel Eilenberg and Saunders Mac Lane to bring order to topology. They realized: many formulas in different branches look the same if you look at mappings between objects.

How it works

A category is defined by objects and arrows that can be composed sequentially. It's like a food chain: if a tiger eats an antelope, and an antelope eats grass, then the tiger indirectly depends on grass. In category theory, such chains help prove theorems.

💡 Some philosophers consider category theory an alternative to set theory—it perceives the world not as a collection of elements, but as a network of relations. In physics, it is applied to describe quantum computing and topological states.
Category theory is a branch of mathematics that abstracts structures through objects and morphisms (arrows) between them. Basic axioms: composition of morphisms is associative, and each object has an identity morphism. Categories can be small (when objects form a set) and large (class). Functorial transformations between categories are called functors, and mappings between functors are natural transformations. Key constructions include limits, colimits, adjoint functors, and universal properties. The theory provides a unified language for many fields, from algebraic geometry to computer science.

History

Formally, the theory originated in 1945 in an article by Eilenberg and Mac Lane, where the concepts of category, functor, and natural transformation were introduced for the needs of algebraic topology. In the 1950s, Alexander Grothendieck applied abelian categories to rethink algebraic geometry. In the 1960s, William Lawvere proposed using category theory as a foundation for mathematics, introducing elementary topoi. Later, in the 1980s, Joachim Lambek and others connected cartesian closed categories with typed lambda calculus, laying the foundations of theoretical computer science.

How it works

A category C consists of a class of objects Ob(C) and a class of morphisms Hom(C); for each pair of objects A, B, a class Hom(A,B) is defined. Composition ∘: Hom(B,C) × Hom(A,B) → Hom(A,C) is associative, and for each object X there exists an identity morphism id_X ∈ Hom(X,X). A functor F: C → D maps objects and morphisms, preserving composition and identity. A natural transformation η: F ⇒ G assigns to each object X a morphism η_X: F(X) → G(X) such that diagrams commute. A central result is the Yoneda lemma: each object is fully determined by its relationships with other objects, allowing any category to be embedded in a category of presheaves. The principle of universality (e.g., product A×B) is defined through the property of mapping into it. In physics, categories are applied via monoidal structures to describe entanglement and topological quantum fields.

Open problems & fun facts

Construction of a complete formal homotopy theory in the language of higher categories (∞,1)-topoi.; Creation of a physical theory in which categorification explains quantum gravity.; Development of efficient computational methods for working with large categories in the context of machine learning and databases.
