category

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A category is a map of relations, where what matters is not the objects but the transformations between them: as if instead of cities, you studied the network of roads and the rules of their connection.

In practice: This approach facilitates the transfer of ideas between different branches of mathematics. In computer science, categories are used for strict type checking of programs, and in physics, for modeling quantum computations.
Formally, a category is defined by a class of objects and, for each pair of objects, a set of morphisms (arrows representing admissible transformations) with a composition operation. Composition is associative: if you apply arrows f, then g, then h, it does not matter whether you group them as h after (g after f) or as (h after g) after f. For each object, there is an identity arrow that leaves it unchanged. The essence of the theory is to ignore the internal structure of objects and focus on the connections between them, providing a universal language for constructing such things as the product of objects (a way to combine two objects) or the colimit (a way to merge information from several objects).

History

Category theory was created in the 1940s by American mathematicians Saunders Mac Lane and Samuel Eilenberg. They needed a language to compare very different areas — algebra and topology.

How it works

The basic rule is the ability to combine arrows without additional conditions. If you go from A to B, then from B to C, and then from C to D, the final route does not depend on how you group the segments: first go A→B→C and then C→D, or A→B and then B→C→D. Thanks to this, routes can be safely combined into long chains.

💡 Category theory is sometimes jokingly called 'abstract nonsense', emphasizing its extreme generality, which nonetheless has provided a rigorous foundation for many areas of mathematics.
In a category, there are objects (e.g., numbers or geometric figures) and arrows showing how one object transforms into another. Arrows can be connected: if there is an arrow from A to B and from B to C, then you get a composite arrow from A to C. It is like a road system: from city A to city C you can travel via city B; the whole route is like a composite arrow.

Category theory is a branch of mathematics in which properties of objects are described through the connections between them rather than their internal structure. A category C consists of: 1) a class of objects Ob(C); 2) for each ordered pair of objects (A, B), a set of morphisms Hom_C(A, B); 3) an associative binary operation of composition ∘: Hom(B, C) × Hom(A, B) → Hom(A, C); 4) for each object A, an identity morphism id_A satisfying f ∘ id_A = f = id_B ∘ f for every morphism f: A → B. Functors (mappings between categories preserving structure), natural transformations, and adjoint functors provide the basis for universal properties. The categorical language has penetrated algebraic topology, algebraic geometry, logic (toposes as models of set theory), and computer science (categorical semantics of programming languages).

History

Categories emerged in the work of Samuel Eilenberg and Saunders Mac Lane (1942–1945) on the axiomatization of natural transformations in algebraic topology. In the 1950s–60s, Alexander Grothendieck rebuilt algebraic geometry in categorical language and introduced the notion of a topos. William Lawvere developed categorical logic. Since the 1980s, categories have been actively applied in computer science (e.g., monads in functional programming). In the 21st century, applications appeared in quantum physics (categorical quantum mechanics) and type theory.

How it works

The work is based on the axioms of composition and universal properties. For example, the product of objects A and B in a category is defined not by construction but by a property: it is an object P with a pair of projections p₁: P→A, p₂: P→B, such that for any object X with morphisms f: X→A and g: X→B, there exists a unique morphism h: X→P making the diagram commute. This allows proving theorems without referring to the elements of objects. The principle of duality (reversing all arrows) doubles the number of provable statements.

h \circ (g \circ f) = (h \circ g) \circ f
f: A → B, g: B → C, h: C → D are morphisms in the category; ∘ is the composition operation. Associativity means that the result of sequential application does not depend on the parentheses ordering.

Open problems & fun facts
