tensor

📊 0 articles
Imagine you want to describe the tension inside a piece of rubber: you pull it along, across, or diagonally – the force responds differently. A tensor is like a 'summary table' of all such interactions at once. It stores information about how a material responds to stress in any direction. Simply put, if a number is a point, a vector is an arrow, then a tensor is a whole system of interconnected arrows.
💡 Without tensors, we wouldn’t be able to accurately predict how a bridge deforms under the weight of a train. And it was tensors that helped Einstein describe how massive bodies warp spacetime – like a heavy ball on a stretched sheet.
A tensor generalizes the concepts of vectors and matrices to multidimensional arrays of numbers. If a scalar is a zero-dimensional quantity (just a number), a vector is a one-dimensional sequence, then a tensor is a multidimensional lattice describing physical properties that depend on several directions. For example, to describe the stress inside a solid body, we need to know the forces acting on each face in all directions – this is the stress tensor.

History

The idea of tensor calculus originated in the late 19th century. Italian mathematicians Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro and Tullio Levi-Civita created the formal apparatus that later became the language of general relativity. James Clerk Maxwell, without knowing it, had already used tensors in his equations of electromagnetism, and Hendrik Lorentz applied them for transformations of space and time.

How it works

Tensors manifest themselves when we rotate the coordinate system. Ordinary numbers (scalars) do not change upon rotation, while vector components change according to a simple rule. A tensor, however, transforms more complexly, preserving internal geometry, much like the shadow of a rotating cube changes shape but always remains a projection of the same object.

💡 In machine learning, tensors are the basic data type. Neural networks process huge multidimensional tensors: for instance, a color image is a three-dimensional tensor (width, height, color channels), and video is four-dimensional, adding time.
A tensor is a geometric object whose components in a given basis transform according to a certain linear law under coordinate changes. Tensors of rank (p,q) are invariant under the group of basis transformations. They are ubiquitous in differential geometry, continuum mechanics, and field theory. For example, the metric tensor g_{μν} defines distance in curved spacetime, the stress-energy tensor T_{μν} describes the density and flux of energy, and the electromagnetic field tensor F_{μν} unites electric and magnetic fields into a single relativistic structure.

History

The history of tensors begins with works on differential geometry and mechanics. Gauss and Riemann laid the foundations, but the systematic calculus was developed by Ricci-Curbastro and Levi-Civita in 1884–1900. In 1915, Albert Einstein used their apparatus to formulate general relativity, where gravity manifests as the curvature of spacetime, described by the Ricci tensor and the Einstein tensor. James Clerk Maxwell had already in 1865 essentially written his equations in tensor form, though he didn't use the term. Hendrik Lorentz in 1904 derived transformations that became the basis for understanding the tensor nature of physical laws in special relativity.

How it works

The transformation law for tensor components when transitioning to new coordinates x'^μ = x'^μ(x^ν) is given by the product of Jacobian matrices. For example, for a tensor of rank (1,1): T'^μ_ν = (∂x'^μ/∂x^α) (∂x^β/∂x'^ν) T^α_β. Maxwell's equations in tensor form: ∂_ν F^{μν} = μ_0 J^μ, where F^{μν} is the electromagnetic field tensor, J^μ is the 4-current. Einstein field equations: G_{μν} + Λ g_{μν} = (8πG/c^4) T_{μν}, where G_{μν} is the Einstein tensor expressing spacetime curvature through the metric tensor and its derivatives. Tensors also describe stress and strain in mechanics: σ_{ij} = C_{ijkl} ε^{kl}, where σ is the stress tensor, ε is the strain tensor, and C is the fourth-rank elasticity tensor.

G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}
G_{μν} is the Einstein tensor (spacetime curvature), Λ is the cosmological constant, g_{μν} is the metric tensor, G is the gravitational constant, G ≈ 6.67430×10⁻¹¹ m³ kg⁻¹ s⁻², c is the speed of light, c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s, T_{μν} is the stress-energy tensor of matter
F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu
F_{μν} is the electromagnetic field tensor, ∂_μ is the partial derivative with respect to coordinate x^μ, A_μ is the electromagnetic four-potential
