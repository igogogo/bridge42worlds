Tensor

📊 0 articles
A tensor is a mathematical object that can store several numbers and transform according to certain rules, for example, like an Excel table that changes when you rotate the sheet.
💡 Tensors are used in artificial intelligence: neural networks work with multidimensional tensors of data, such as color images.
A tensor is a generalization of vectors and matrices to multidimensional arrays of numbers that transform according to specific laws under coordinate changes. For example, the stress tensor in physics describes how forces are distributed inside a solid body. Tensors come in different ranks: scalar (rank 0), vector (rank 1), matrix (rank 2), etc.

History

The concept of a tensor was introduced by Italian mathematician Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro in the late 19th century, and his student Tullio Levi-Civita developed tensor calculus. Einstein used tensors to formulate general relativity (1915), where the stress-energy tensor describes the distribution of matter.

How it works

Imagine you have a box with forces acting in different directions. The stress tensor is like a 3×3 table, where each cell shows the force in one direction on one face. If you rotate the box, the numbers in the table change according to strict rules to describe the same physical situation in the new reference frame.

💡 Tensors are at the core of machine learning: libraries TensorFlow and PyTorch are named after the tensors they work with.
A tensor is a geometric object that linearly maps vectors and covectors to numbers, invariant under coordinate changes. The components of a tensor transform according to multilinear laws: covariant components transform like basis vectors, contravariant components like coordinates. Tensors of rank (r,s) have r upper and s lower indices. Examples: metric tensor (0,2), Riemann curvature tensor (1,3). Tensor calculus is the foundation of general relativity and differential geometry.

History

Tensor calculus was developed by Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro and his student Tullio Levi-Civita in the 1890s. Einstein, working on general relativity, turned to Ricci for help and in 1915 presented the field equations using the stress-energy tensor and the Ricci tensor. Later, tensors became key in continuum mechanics, electrodynamics, and quantum field theory.

How it works

A tensor T of type (r,s) at a point of a manifold is a multilinear map T: (T^*M)^r × (TM)^s → ℝ. In coordinates, its components T^{i_1...i_r}_{j_1...j_s} transform under coordinate changes using Jacobian matrices and their inverses. Contraction of tensors is summation over a repeated upper and lower index. A tensor field is a smooth assignment of a tensor at each point of the manifold.

T'^{i}_{j} = \frac{\partial x'^i}{\partial x^k} \frac{\partial x^l}{\partial x'^j} T^{k}_{l}
The components of a (1,1) tensor transform using the Jacobian matrix of the forward and inverse coordinate transformation.
T^{i_1...i_{r-1}k}_{j_1...j_{s-1}k} = \sum_k T^{i_1...i_{r-1}k}_{j_1...j_{s-1}k}
Contraction over a repeated index k yields a tensor of rank (r-1, s-1).

Open problems & fun facts

💡 Tensors are at the core of machine learning: libraries TensorFlow and PyTorch are named after the tensors they work with.
