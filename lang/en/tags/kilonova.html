kilonova

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A kilonova is a cosmic explosion a thousand times brighter than a nova, creating gold and platinum during the merger of neutron stars.

In practice: Studying kilonovae reveals the origin of heavy elements — from gold in jewelry to rare-earth metals in electronics.
A kilonova flares up when two neutron stars or a neutron star and a black hole collide. The ejected matter, supersaturated with neutrons, transforms into heavy nuclei via rapid neutron capture (r-process). The brightness of the flare is powered by the radioactive decay of newborn isotopes such as nickel-56 and even californium.

History

Theory predicted kilonovae in 1998, but they were first observed in 2017, simultaneously recording gravitational waves from the same event.

How it works

The impact generates a cloud of superheavy atomic nuclei, which quickly decay, glowing due to radioactivity — like the flickering embers of a campfire. This glow is what astronomers observe.

💡 A single kilonova can synthesize an amount of gold comparable to the mass of several Earth-like planets.
The collision of two neutron stars — superdense remnants of dead stars the size of a city — produces an explosion called a kilonova. Like the merging of two drops of water, but instead of splashes, jets of matter rich in precious metals fly out. The glow lasts several days.

A kilonova is an astronomical transient (variable event) with a peak luminosity on the order of 10⁴¹ erg/s, roughly a thousand times brighter than a classical nova but fainter than a supernova. The energy source is the radioactive decay of unstable nuclei synthesized via the r-process (rapid neutron capture) in the ejected material. Neutron star mergers are accompanied by a gravitational-wave signal, a short gamma-ray burst, and subsequent afterglow in optical, infrared, and radio bands. The kilonova spectrum features broad absorption lines formed in the expanding shell, rich in lanthanides and actinides. Expansion velocities reach 0.1–0.3 times the speed of light. Modeling shows multiple components of the ejecta: a dynamical (tidal) tail, wind from the accretion disk, and a jet outflow.

History

The kilonova concept was proposed in 1998 by Li-Xin Li and Bohdan Paczyński, who predicted bright radioactive glow from matter ejected during neutron star mergers, calling it a 'macronova' or 'r-process supernova'. The term 'kilonova' later stuck by analogy with nova and supernova. The first observation occurred on August 17, 2017, when the LIGO and Virgo detectors registered gravitational waves from the merger of two neutron stars (GW170817), and over the following hours and days, observatories worldwide detected the electromagnetic source AT2017gfo, unambiguously identified as a kilonova.

How it works

During the merger of neutron stars, tidal forces eject part of the material at high speed. This material is neutron-rich (neutron density reaches 10²⁰ cm⁻³) and undergoes rapid neutron capture, forming elements up to uranium and thorium. Unstable isotopes then decay, releasing energy: beta decay and nuclear fission heat the shell to temperatures around 10,000 K, causing thermal emission in the optical range. The light curve (brightness over time) has a characteristic hump with a rise time of about a day and subsequent decline following a power law. Gravitational waves from the merger are described by Einstein's equations of general relativity, and accretion disk dynamics by magnetohydrodynamic equations.
