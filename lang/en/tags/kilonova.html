kilonova

📊 0 articles
The gold in our jewelry was once created during the merger of two dead stars. A kilonova is a cosmic furnace where heavy elements are forged in just a few days, shining brighter than a million suns.
💡 The light of a kilonova is initially blue due to the incredible temperature, and after a few days it turns red—by its color we can tell how much gold and platinum was forged.
Just as a campfire illuminates a forest clearing but quickly dies out, a kilonova illuminates a galaxy after a neutron star merger, releasing energy and scattering heavy elements across space like golden sparks.

History

The term 'kilonova' was proposed by Brian Metzger in 2010, but the process itself had been discussed since the 1970s as a possible source of gamma-ray bursts.

How it works

Two neutron stars, dancing in orbit, lose energy through gravitational waves and eventually collide. Shock heating and the synthesis of heavy element nuclei create an expanding cloud that emits visible light—a flash that outshines the entire host galaxy.

💡 The only confidently observed kilonova, AT 2017gfo, which accompanied the gravitational-wave event GW170817, provided the first direct confirmation that neutron star collisions produce gold and platinum.
A kilonova is a transient astrophysical source resulting from the merger of two neutron stars or a neutron star with a black hole. The emission of a kilonova is thermal in nature and is powered by the decay of radioactive isotopes synthesized via rapid neutron capture (the r-process). The typical luminosity is about 10⁴¹ erg/s at peak, a thousand times brighter than a classical nova, and the spectrum evolves from blue to infrared over a few days.

History

The possibility of nucleosynthesis in mergers of compact objects was discussed by Lattimer and Schramm as early as the 1970s. The term 'kilonova' was coined in 2010 to describe the optical afterglow of short gamma-ray bursts. Decisive observational confirmation came on August 17, 2017, when the LIGO and Virgo gravitational-wave detectors registered the signal GW170817, and telescopes worldwide detected the electromagnetic transient AT 2017gfo in the galaxy NGC 4993. This event united gravitational-wave and electromagnetic astronomy for the first time.

How it works

As neutron stars approach each other during the final milliseconds of their orbit, a portion of neutron-rich matter is ejected by tidal forces. The ejected material is neutron-rich and undergoes the r-process—the rapid capture of neutrons by nuclei, forming stable heavy elements from strontium to uranium. The radioactive decay of these nuclei heats the expanding shell to temperatures of tens of thousands of kelvins, producing the optical flash. The equation of state of neutron matter and the dynamics of the ejection are described by relativistic hydrodynamics including a nuclear reaction network.
