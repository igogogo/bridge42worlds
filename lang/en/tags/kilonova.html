kilonova

📊 0

A cosmic explosion from the merger of two neutron stars, producing gravitational waves and a full spectrum of radiation – from radio waves to gamma-ray bursts. Within days, it forges heavy elements, including gold and platinum.

In practice: Kilonovae are the main source of heavy elements in the Universe, explaining the origin of gold and other precious metals on Earth.
A kilonova is an optical and infrared transient (temporary glow) accompanying the merger of two neutron stars. Its peak luminosity can reach 10⁴¹ erg/s, which is a thousand times brighter than classical novae but fainter than supernovae. The spectrum shows lines of heavy elements produced via the r-process (rapid neutron-capture nucleosynthesis). The blue component of emission is due to lanthanides (a group of 15 metals, e.g., neodymium), while the red component is from lighter elements. Event GW170817 for the first time combined gravitational-wave and electromagnetic astronomy.

History

The idea of a kilonova was proposed in 1974 by Soviet astrophysicist V.M. Lipunov, but the first real observation occurred in 2017 when LIGO and Virgo detectors registered gravitational waves (ripples in spacetime) from a neutron star merger. Dozens of telescopes worldwide captured the flash in the galaxy NGC 4993.

How it works

During the merger, some of the neutron star material is ejected at enormous speed. In this material, atomic nuclei capture neutrons (tiny nuclear particles) and quickly transform into heavy elements – gold, uranium, and others. The decay of these radioactive atoms makes the cloud glow, and we see the kilonova.

💡 The 2017 kilonova showed that one such merger creates enough gold to make several Earth-sized planets.
A kilonova is an explosion when two neutron stars (superdense remnants of dead stars, the size of a city but heavier than the Sun) collide and merge. This releases energy comparable to that of a hundred suns over their entire lifetimes. After the merger, a rapidly expanding cloud of matter forms, glowing from the radioactive decay of unstable atoms. It is in this cloud, like a cosmic forge, that precious metals are born.

A kilonova is an astrophysical transient with a characteristic light curve lasting from a few days to weeks, and a spectrum whose peak shifts from optical to infrared due to high absorption and re-emission of photons in the ejected material with opacity dominated by lanthanides (so-called 'lanthanide opacity'). Models predict two ejecta components: dynamical ejecta during the merger and ejecta from the accretion disk afterwards. r-process nucleosynthesis produces elements with atomic mass A > 130, enriching the interstellar medium and explaining the observed abundance of elements in the Universe. The explosion energy (~10⁵¹ erg) is partially converted into kinetic energy of the ejecta, and the glow is sustained by radioactive decay of unstable isotopes.

History

The concept of a kilonova was predicted in 1974 by V.M. Lipunov and later detailed in works on r-process nucleosynthesis from neutron star mergers (e.g., Li & Paczyński, 1998). The key observational confirmation came with the registration of gravitational waves GW170817 by the LIGO-Virgo collaboration on August 17, 2017, which triggered an unprecedented electromagnetic follow-up campaign from gamma-ray burst GRB 170817A to the optical/infrared transient AT 2017gfo. Subsequent ultraviolet, optical, and infrared observations confirmed the synthesis of heavy elements. Theoretical modeling of such events is actively developed, taking into account the equation of state of neutron stars and merger hydrodynamics.

How it works

The emission mechanism of a kilonova is based on the radioactive decay of heavy nuclei synthesized in the r-process. The ejected material expands adiabatically, cooling, but continuous heating from decays (primarily isotopes with half-lives on the order of a day) maintains a photosphere at ~10⁴ K. The radiative flux is described by solving the radiative transfer equation in a strongly absorbing medium, where the peak shifts over time due to changing opacity. This process also explains the observed connection with short gamma-ray bursts: relativistic jets from the resulting black hole can punch through the kilonova material, producing collimated emission.
