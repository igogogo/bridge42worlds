Hamiltonian

📊 0 articles
The Hamiltonian is like the total energy of a system, but written in a special way to predict its future.
💡 The Hamiltonian is named after William Rowan Hamilton, who in 1833 devised this formalism to describe planetary motion.
The Hamiltonian is a mathematical expression equal to the sum of the kinetic and potential energy of a system. It allows, like a map, to determine how the system will evolve over time. For example, for a pendulum, the Hamiltonian describes its total energy and predicts oscillations.

History

The concept was introduced by William Hamilton in 1833 to reformulate classical mechanics. Later it became the foundation of quantum mechanics through the work of Erwin Schrödinger.

How it works

The Hamiltonian acts as a 'conductor': knowing it, one can derive the equations of motion of the system. In quantum mechanics, it determines how the wave function changes over time.

💡 In quantum mechanics, the Hamiltonian is an operator, not just a number, and its eigenvalues correspond to the possible energies of the system.
The Hamiltonian is a function or operator describing the total energy of a physical system (kinetic + potential). In classical mechanics, it gives the canonical equations of motion, and in quantum mechanics, it is an operator whose eigenvalues are energy levels. It plays a central role in the Hamiltonian formalism, which is equivalent to the Lagrangian one but more convenient for some problems, especially in statistical and quantum mechanics.

History

The concept of the Hamiltonian was introduced by William Rowan Hamilton in 1833 as part of his reformulation of Newtonian mechanics. In 1925, Werner Heisenberg and Erwin Schrödinger independently applied it to quantum mechanics: Schrödinger used the Hamiltonian in his famous equation. Later, Paul Dirac developed the formalism, linking the Hamiltonian to the evolution of quantum systems.

How it works

In classical mechanics, the Hamiltonian H(q,p) = T + V, where q are coordinates and p are momenta. Hamilton's equations: dq/dt = ∂H/∂p, dp/dt = -∂H/∂q. In quantum mechanics, the Hamiltonian becomes an operator Ĥ, and the Schrödinger equation iħ d|ψ⟩/dt = Ĥ|ψ⟩ describes the evolution of the state. The eigenvalues of Ĥ are the energy levels of the system.

i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial t}|\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{H}|\psi(t)\rangle
The rate of change of a quantum state is proportional to the action of the Hamiltonian on that state.

Open problems & fun facts

Exact solution of the Hamiltonian for many-particle systems (e.g., in quantum chemistry) is often impossible due to exponential growth in complexity.; The problem of quantum gravity: how to construct a Hamiltonian for general relativity?
