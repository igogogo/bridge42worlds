VLBI

📊 1

VLBI turns a network of radio telescopes into one virtual instrument the size of Earth — detail like a microscope looking at the sky. A cosmic magnifying glass that sees a coin on the Moon.

In practice: Thanks to VLBI, the first image of a black hole's shadow was obtained, and the movement of lithospheric plates is measured with millimeter precision.
VLBI is a technique in radio astronomy where signals from distant radio telescopes are combined taking into account the delay in wave arrival. Resolving power (ability to distinguish fine details) depends on the distance between antennas, not their size. With baselines of thousands of kilometers, an angular resolution of microarcseconds is achieved — one can see a lighter on the Moon. To combat atmospheric distortions, the method of closure phases is used, combining signals from three or more antennas.

History

The idea of combining widely separated antennas emerged in the 1960s. The first successful observations were made in 1967 by groups of scientists in the USA and Canada.

How it works

Imagine two people looking at the same star and precisely noting when its light reaches each of them. If the distance between them is known, from the time difference one can reconstruct details inaccessible to a single observer. VLBI radio telescopes work the same way, only they record radio waves and synchronize by atomic clocks.

💡 The largest virtual telescope — the Event Horizon Telescope — combined stations from Antarctica to Europe to show the silhouette of a black hole in 2019.
VLBI (Very Long Baseline Interferometry) is a method where several radio telescopes (instruments that capture radio waves from space), scattered across continents, simultaneously observe one object. Signals are recorded with time stamps from atomic clocks (very precise clocks); later computers process them together, as if a single telescope with an antenna thousands of kilometers in size was operating.

Very Long Baseline Interferometry (VLBI) uses independent recording of radiation at antennas separated by thousands of kilometers, with subsequent correlation of signals. Each station is equipped with a highly stable local oscillator and an atomic frequency standard (maser), allowing time tagging with femtosecond precision. Due to atmospheric and ionospheric phase distortions, the method of closure phases is used — combinations of phase differences of three or more antennas, robust against errors and not requiring full calibration. This allows reconstruction of the true structure of sources. VLBI operates in centimeter and millimeter bands; for observing black holes, 1-mm VLBI is used, placing telescopes in dry highlands (Chile, South Pole).

History

The principle of interferometry in radio astronomy was developed by Martin Ryle and others in the 1940s–50s. A scheme with independent local oscillators and magnetic tape recording was proposed in the early 1960s by Soviet and American scientists. The first successful observations in 1967 were performed by groups in the USA and Canada, using rubidium frequency standards. Subsequently, the transition to digital recording and the advent of hydrogen masers increased accuracy; today, real-time data transmission (e-VLBI) via fiber optics is being introduced.

How it works

Radio telescopes, operating as a single interferometer, record the signal along with precise time stamps from atomic clocks. The correlator later aligns the data, compensating for geometric delays due to Earth's rotation and antenna positions. Resolution is determined by the ratio of wavelength to baseline projection: θ ≈ λ / B. For wavelength 1.3 mm and baseline 8000 km, θ ≈ 20 microarcseconds. For millimeter VLBI, suppression of water vapor is critical, so telescopes are built at altitudes above 3000 m.

\theta \approx \frac{\lambda}{B}
θ — angular resolution (radians), λ — wavelength of radiation, B — baseline length (distance between antennas, perpendicular to the direction to the source)
