Hot Jupiter

📊 0 articles
This is a planet the size of Jupiter, but located very close to its star, causing its atmosphere to be heated to thousands of degrees.
💡 The first hot Jupiter, 51 Pegasi b, was discovered in 1995 and launched the era of exoplanet discovery.
A hot Jupiter is a gas giant that orbits its star at a very close distance, often less than 0.1 AU (for comparison, Mercury is 0.4 AU from the Sun). Due to its proximity to the star, its atmosphere is heated to 1000–3000 K. Such planets usually have elongated orbits and may be tidally locked.

History

The discovery of 51 Pegasi b in 1995 by Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz was a sensation, as such a planet did not fit existing formation theories.

How it works

Hot Jupiters likely form farther from the star and then migrate inward due to interactions with the protoplanetary disk or other planets. Proximity to the star causes strong heating, atmospheric expansion, and even evaporation.

💡 Some hot Jupiters have a 'tail' of evaporating gas, like a comet.
A hot Jupiter is an exoplanet of the gas giant class with a mass on the order of Jupiter's mass, but with an orbital period of less than 10 days. Due to proximity to the star, their atmospheres are highly heated, leading to expansion and mass loss. They are often on tidally locked orbits, with one side always facing the star. Studying hot Jupiters provides information on planet migration and atmospheric evolution.

History

The first hot Jupiter, 51 Pegasi b, was discovered in 1995 by Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz (Nobel Prize 2019). This discovery prompted a revision of planet formation theories. Since then, hundreds of hot Jupiters have been detected, including HD 209458b, whose atmosphere was first measured.

How it works

Hot Jupiters form beyond the snow line, where there is plenty of ice, and then migrate inward due to gravitational interactions with the disk or other planets. Approaching the star, they experience strong heating, causing atmospheric expansion and mass loss. Some hot Jupiters may be destroyed by tidal forces.
