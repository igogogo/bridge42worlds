Spiral Galaxy

📊 0 articles
This is a galaxy resembling a giant whirlpool with twisted arms of stars and gas.
💡 Our Milky Way Galaxy is a spiral galaxy with two main arms and several smaller ones.
A spiral galaxy is a disk of stars, gas, and dust that rotates around a center, forming spiral arms. Imagine a fireworks display frozen in the shape of a rotating spiral. At the center there is usually a bright bulge, and sometimes a bar of stars.

History

In 1845, Lord Ross first noticed the spiral structure in the Whirlpool Galaxy. Edwin Hubble in 1926 classified spiral galaxies as a separate type.

How it works

The arms are not solid structures but regions of higher density where stars and gas accumulate, like traffic jams on a highway. Gravity and rotation create density waves that form the spiral pattern.

💡 Spiral galaxies make up about 60% of all galaxies in the nearby universe.
A spiral galaxy is a type of galaxy characterized by a flat rotating disk with a central bulge and spiral arms. The arms contain many young hot stars, gas, and dust, giving them a bluish tint. Spiral galaxies are divided into ordinary (S) and barred (SB), where the bar is a strip of stars passing through the center. Examples: Milky Way (SBbc), Andromeda Galaxy (Sb), Whirlpool (Sc).

History

The first observation of a spiral structure was made by William Parsons (Lord Rosse) in 1845. In 1926, Edwin Hubble developed the Hubble sequence, classifying galaxies by shape. Modern research using telescopes such as Hubble and JWST studies the evolution of spiral galaxies.

How it works

Spiral arms form due to density waves that propagate through the galactic disk. Gravity compresses gas in the arms, triggering star formation. Young stars shine brightly, making the arms visible. Over time, stars leave the arms, but the density wave persists, creating the illusion of a rotating pattern.

Open problems & fun facts
