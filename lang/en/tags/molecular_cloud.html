molecular cloud

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A molecular cloud is a cosmic incubator of stars: a giant accumulation of icy gas and dust where gravity compresses matter until thermonuclear reactions ignite.

In practice: Knowledge of these clouds is necessary for predicting star formation and galactic evolution. Spectral analysis of clouds allows searching for complex organic molecules—possible precursors of life.
Molecular clouds are the main reservoir of cold interstellar gas. Their diameters reach 600 light-years, and masses—millions of solar masses. They are opaque to visible light but transparent to radio waves, which allows radio telescopes to study their structure. More than 200 types of molecules have been found in them, including organic ones (ethanol, glycolaldehyde). The clouds live for tens of millions of years, after which the radiation of young hot stars disperses the remaining gas.

History

In the mid-20th century, astronomers assumed that interstellar matter accumulates in dense clumps that give birth to stars. In the 1970s, radio observations of carbon monoxide (CO) for the first time directly detected giant molecular clouds in our Galaxy.

How it works

Gravity slowly pulls the cloud's matter inward. If enough mass accumulates in some region, gravity overcomes the internal pressure, and the clump begins to contract faster and faster. In the end, at its center, the temperature becomes so high that nuclear reactions ignite—a star is born.

💡 In the molecular cloud Sagittarius B2, enormous quantities of ethyl alcohol have been found—enough to theoretically 'fill' an uncountable number of bottles, but in reality it is extremely cold and ionized gas.
A molecular cloud resembles a huge cold dark fog stretched over hundreds of light-years. The temperature inside is about –260 °C. It consists mainly of molecular hydrogen (H₂) and microscopic dust grains. Such a cloud absorbs visible light, so astronomers observe it using radio telescopes (instruments that catch radio waves).

Giant molecular clouds (GMCs) are the most massive objects in galaxies, extending 50–200 parsecs and with masses of 10⁴–10⁶ M☉. They consist of molecular hydrogen H₂ (99% by mass), helium, and dust (~1%). Dust screens ultraviolet radiation, allowing molecules to survive, and serves as a catalyst for the formation of complex compounds. The average density is 10²–10³ particles/cm³, in dense cores up to 10⁵–10⁶. Temperature is 10–30 K, in cold cores a few Kelvins. The structure is hierarchical: filaments (fibers), on which prestellar cores concentrate, giving rise to protostars. They are observed in rotational transition lines of molecules CO, CS, NH₃, HCN with instruments like ALMA, NOEMA.

History

The first indication of the existence of molecules in the interstellar medium: detection of CH and CN in optical spectra (1937). In 1970, with the discovery of CO emission at a wavelength of 2.6 mm, systematic mapping of molecular clouds began. In 1975, Fred Hoyle and colleagues published a model of chemical evolution of clouds. Large surveys of the 1980s–2000s (FCRAO, Bell Labs 7-m) revealed thousands of clouds in the Milky Way. Modern interferometers (ALMA) with high resolution showed a complex filamentary structure and disks around protostars.

How it works

Dynamics are controlled by the balance of gravity, thermal pressure, magnetic field, and turbulence. Stability is determined by the Jeans criterion: if the mass of a gas clump is greater than the Jeans mass M_J ∝ T^{3/2} ρ^{-1/2}, it collapses. Compression accelerates, the cloud fragments into many clumps—a star cluster forms. During compression, gravitational energy is released, part of it turns into heat, part into magnetic field energy and kinetic energy of outflows. When the temperature in the center of the protostar reaches ~10⁷ K, nuclear burning of hydrogen begins. The newborn stars ionize the surrounding gas (creating H II regions) and through stellar wind and radiation disperse the remnants of the cloud.

M_J \approx \left(\frac{5kT}{G\mu m_H}\right)^{3/2} \left(\frac{3}{4\pi\rho}\right)^{1/2}
M_J is the Jeans mass; k is the Boltzmann constant (≈1.38×10⁻²³ J/K); T is the cloud temperature; G is the gravitational constant (≈6.674×10⁻¹¹ m³/(kg·s²)); μ is the mean molecular weight (≈2.33); m_H is the mass of a hydrogen atom (≈1.674×10⁻²⁷ kg); ρ is the cloud density.
