molecular cloud

📊 0 articles

A molecular cloud is a giant cold agglomeration of gas and dust where new stars are being born right now. Inside it, it is so dark and cold that light from neighboring stars does not penetrate through the thickness of the material.

In practice: Analysis of molecules in such clouds allows tracing chemical evolution from simple compounds to organics from which life could arise.
Molecular clouds are among the coldest (10–30 K) and densest parts of the interstellar medium, composed primarily of H₂. Their masses range from tens to millions of solar masses, and sizes reach hundreds of light-years. Dust blocks ultraviolet radiation, allowing complex molecules like alcohols and amino acids to exist.
💡 In the Sagittarius B2 cloud, ethyl alcohol has been detected in amounts exceeding everything humanity has produced throughout history.
A molecular cloud is a huge cloud of very cold gas (mostly hydrogen) and cosmic dust. It resembles a giant opaque fog that can stretch over tens of light-years. Gravity compresses the densest clumps, and stars ignite there, like lanterns in the fog.

History

The first dark nebula was described by William Herschel as early as the 18th century, but proving that they are real clouds of molecules was only possible in the 1960s with radio telescopes that detected carbon monoxide radiation.

How it works

Gas and dust are attracted by gravity, forming dense cores. When the temperature and pressure in the core reach millions of degrees, a thermonuclear reaction begins—and a star is born. The remaining material can become planets.

💡 If a molecular cloud of average density were compressed to the size of the Earth's atmosphere, its material would be billions of times more tenuous than air.
A molecular cloud is a gravitationally bound region of interstellar gas with a temperature of 10–30 K and a number density of 10²–10⁶ particles/cm³. The main component is molecular hydrogen H₂ (not directly observable due to symmetry), mapped via CO lines. The internal structure is hierarchical: filaments, Jeans cores, protostellar disks. Dust (silicates, carbonaceous particles) serves as a catalyst for chemical reactions and provides cooling through infrared radiation.

History

The cataloging of dark nebulae began with Edward Barnard in 1919. In 1937, Bengt Strömgren theoretically justified ionization zones around stars. Radio observations of CO lines by Wilson, Jefferts, and Penzias in 1970 confirmed the molecular composition. Fred Hoyle developed the theory of star formation in clouds in the 1950s. The Herschel Space Observatory (2009–2013) mapped clouds in detail in the far infrared.

How it works

The gravitational contraction of cloud fragments is described by the Jeans criterion: when the critical mass is exceeded, the gas collapses. Cooling through rotational transitions of CO and thermal emission of dust allows heat to be shed. Magnetic fields and turbulence slow down the collapse. When the central temperature reaches ~10⁷ K, the hydrogen cycle starts, and the protostar enters the main sequence.

Open problems & fun facts

Why is the observed star formation efficiency (the fraction of gas turned into stars) only a few percent?; What is the role of magnetic fields in maintaining turbulence and preventing rapid collapse?; Are complex organic molecules formed in molecular clouds in quantities sufficient for panspermia?
