Standard Model

📊 75 articles
The Standard Model is the recipe by which nature "bakes" everything around us. It explains which tiny "bricks" matter is made of and how these bricks exchange "letters"—special force-carrying particles.
💡 The Standard Model predicted the existence of particles long before their discovery. For example, the Higgs boson was only found in 2012, 48 years after it was conceived in theory!
The Standard Model unifies everything we know about the subatomic world. It is a mathematical framework describing 17 elementary particles: quarks (which build protons and neutrons), leptons (such as electrons), and bosons (which carry forces). Analogy: if the Universe is a complex orchestra, the Standard Model is the score that explains which instruments play and how they interact.

History

The Standard Model was gradually formed throughout the 20th century. In the late 1800s, Maxwell unified electricity and magnetism. In the 1960s, Glashow, Weinberg, and Salam created the theory of the electroweak interaction. The model took its final form in the 1970s after the confirmation of quark structure and the discovery of W and Z bosons.

How it works

The Standard Model says that forces arise from the exchange of bosons. For example, two magnets attract each other because they "toss" photons—particles of the electromagnetic field. In the atomic nucleus, protons are held together by gluons, which, like threads, stitch the quarks together. And the weak interaction, similar to radioactive decay, is caused by W and Z bosons.

💡 In the Standard Model, there is a particle called the neutrino, which is so elusive that every second trillions of these particles pass through your fingernail, and almost none are stopped. And more: the mass of particles is explained by the Higgs field, which can be imagined as a snow field—the more a particle sinks into it, the heavier it becomes.
The Standard Model (SM) is a relativistic quantum field theory describing the electroweak and strong interactions of elementary particles. It is based on the gauge symmetry group SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1), spontaneously broken to SU(3)×U(1) by the Higgs mechanism. It includes: 6 quarks (u, d, c, s, t, b), 6 leptons (e, μ, τ and corresponding neutrinos), 12 gauge bosons (photon, W⁺, W⁻, Z, 8 gluons), and the scalar Higgs boson. The theory is renormalizable and consistent with the Copenhagen interpretation of quantum mechanics. The SM predicts the anomalous magnetic moment of the electron to an accuracy of 10⁻¹², which is a triumph of quantum electrodynamics.

History

The development of the SM began with Maxwell's unification of electricity and magnetism (1864), followed by the quantization of the electromagnetic field (Planck, Einstein). In the 1920s, Schrödinger and Heisenberg built quantum mechanics, and Dirac created the relativistic equation for the electron. In 1930, Pauli formulated the exclusion principle, explaining the structure of atoms. The theory of strong interactions took shape in the 1960s–70s with the quark hypothesis (Gell-Mann, Zweig) and gluons, confirmed in collider experiments. The electroweak model was proposed by Weinberg, Salam, and Glashow. The discovery of the W and Z bosons (1983), the top quark (1995), and the Higgs boson (2012) at the LHC completed the experimental verification of the SM.

How it works

The foundation of the SM is the principle of gauge invariance. The Lagrangian of the SM contains kinetic terms for matter fields (fermions), gauge fields, and the Higgs sector, including Yukawa interactions that give masses to fermions. Quantum electrodynamics (part of the SM) is based on Maxwell's equations and is described by photon exchange, obeying Coulomb's law at macroscopic distances. The strong interaction is described by quantum chromodynamics (QCD) with color charge and confinement. The Schrödinger equation and its relativistic generalizations (Klein–Gordon, Dirac) underlie the description of free fields. The Pauli principle determines the filling of fermion states. Fermi–Dirac and Bose–Einstein statistics govern the behavior of particles at low temperatures. Noether's theorem connects symmetries with conservation laws.
