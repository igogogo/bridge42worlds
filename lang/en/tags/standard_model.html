Standard Model

📊 1 articles
A theory describing three of the four fundamental interactions (electromagnetic, weak, and strong) and all known elementary particles. It is like the periodic table but for the subatomic world: it systematizes quarks, leptons, bosons, and their interactions.
💡 The Standard Model predicted the existence of many particles, including W and Z bosons, gluons, and the top quark, long before their experimental discovery.
A theory describing three of the four fundamental interactions (electromagnetic, weak, and strong) and all known elementary particles. It is like the periodic table but for the subatomic world: it systematizes quarks, leptons, bosons, and their interactions.

History

The Standard Model was formed in the 1960s-1970s thanks to the work of Glashow, Weinberg, and Salam (electroweak unification). The discovery of the Higgs boson in 2012 completed its particle content. However, it does not include gravity or dark matter.

How it works

Particles are divided into fermions (matter) and bosons (force carriers). Quarks form protons and neutrons, leptons form electrons and neutrinos. Analogy: it is like the rules of chess — the pieces (particles) and their moves (interactions) are precisely described.
