Fermi Paradox

📊 0 articles
The Fermi paradox is the question: if there are many civilizations in the Universe, why don't we see them?
💡 Enrico Fermi asked this question over lunch in 1950, and there is still no definitive answer.
The Fermi paradox is the contradiction between the high probability of the existence of extraterrestrial civilizations and the lack of observed evidence for their existence. The Universe should be full of life, but we see no traces.

History

The paradox was formulated by physicist Enrico Fermi in 1950 during an informal conversation.

How it works

If an advanced civilization existed, its traces (radio signals, probes) would be noticeable, but we have detected nothing, which makes us ponder the reasons for the silence of space.

💡 One possible explanation is the 'Great Filter': some catastrophe destroys civilizations before they become visible.
The Fermi paradox is the apparent contradiction between high estimates of the number of intelligent civilizations in the galaxy (Drake equation) and the lack of any observed evidence of their existence. It raises questions about the prevalence of life, technological civilizations, and their longevity.

History

In 1950, Enrico Fermi during lunch at Los Alamos asked the question: 'Where is everybody?' Later, Frank Drake in 1961 derived an equation to estimate the number of civilizations. The paradox is actively discussed in astrobiology and SETI.

How it works

The paradox is based on estimates: the Milky Way has billions of stars, many with planets, the Universe is old. If at least one civilization began colonization, its traces would be noticeable. The absence of signals and artifacts leads to conclusions: either civilizations are rare, or short-lived, or avoid contact.
