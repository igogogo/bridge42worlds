Asteroid belt

📊 0 articles
A region of the Solar System between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter, where most asteroids are concentrated. The belt contains millions of objects, from dust grains to dwarf planets. Imagine a ring of rocks orbiting the Sun between two planets.
💡 The dwarf planet Ceres, the largest object in the asteroid belt, contains more fresh water than all of Earth.

History

The asteroid belt was discovered in the early 19th century when astronomers were searching for a 'missing' planet between Mars and Jupiter. The first discovered was Ceres (1801), followed by Pallas, Juno, and Vesta. By mid-century, it became clear that this was not a planet but a collection of bodies. Modern missions like Dawn have studied Vesta and Ceres.

How it works

Asteroids in the belt orbit the Sun; their total mass is small (about 4% of the Moon's mass). Jupiter's gravity prevents them from forming a planet, causing resonances and perturbations. Collisions fragment asteroids, creating dust and meteoroids. Analogy: it is like a construction site with many leftover stones, but the foreman (Jupiter) constantly interferes with their assembly.
