string theory

📊 8 articles

In the depths of matter, not points but vibrating strings; their vibrations, like musical notes, determine the properties of all particles and forces.

In practice: String theory is not yet proven, but it is the main candidate for unifying quantum mechanics and gravity. This is necessary to describe black holes and the first moments of the Universe.
String theory replaces point particles with one-dimensional objects—strings—of the size of the Planck length (about 10⁻³⁵ m, the scale where quantum effects and gravity are comparable). Different vibrational modes of the string correspond to different elementary particles with specific masses and charges. For the theory to be mathematically consistent, spacetime must have 10 dimensions: the familiar three spatial and one temporal, as well as 6 additional ones curled into microscopic loops; thus we do not notice them. Strings can be closed (rings) or open (segments). Particle interactions are described as the merging and breaking of strings, and this approach eliminates the mathematical infinities that arose in previous point-particle models.
💡 Early versions of the theory predicted tachyons—hypothetical particles moving faster than light. In more perfect versions with supersymmetry, they disappear, giving way to stable states.
All matter is made of molecules, molecules—of atoms, atoms—of protons, neutrons, and electrons. For a long time these particles were considered pointlike. String theory offers a different view: all fundamental particles are actually not points, but one-dimensional 'threads' (strings). Their length is unimaginably small—billions of billions times smaller than an atomic nucleus. Just as a violin string can produce different notes when vibrating differently, a fundamental string vibrates in various ways, creating particles with different masses and electric charge (a property that allows electromagnetic interaction). Thus, all forces of nature—electromagnetism, gravity, and nuclear forces—may be manifestations of a single 'string orchestra'.

History

In the late 1960s, physicists were studying strong nuclear interactions (forces holding protons together in the atomic nucleus). Unexpectedly, they found mathematical expressions resembling the behavior of vibrating strings. In 1968, Gabriele Veneziano showed that these formulas describe interactions as string-like. Later, in the 1970s, Leonard Susskind and John Schwarz developed the idea into a full-fledged theory. Since then, string theory has undergone several 'revolutions' and remains an active area of research, although experimental confirmation is still lacking.

How it works

Plucking a guitar string sets it vibrating, and depending on the frequency, it produces different sounds. Similarly, a fundamental string can vibrate at different frequencies—these vibrational modes manifest as various elementary particles. For example, one mode is an electron, another is a photon (a light particle), a third is a graviton (the carrier of gravity). The strings themselves are so small that they appear as points, just as a rope looks like a thin line from a distance.

💡 In some models, the graviton is a closed string, while all other particles are open strings whose ends are attached to a brane. This can explain the weakness of gravity: it propagates into all extra dimensions, and we only feel the part that remains on the brane.
String theory is the leading candidate for a unified theory of everything, combining quantum mechanics and general relativity. It is based on replacing pointlike elementary particles with one-dimensional extended objects (strings) and multidimensional membranes (branes). The typical size of strings is the Planck length ℓₚ ≈ 1.6×10⁻³⁵ m. The mass and spin of a particle are determined by the string's vibrational mode: the lowest mode, for example, corresponds to a photon (spin 1) or a graviton (spin 2). Mathematical consistency is achieved only in 10-dimensional spacetime for superstrings (or 11-dimensional for M-theory). The extra dimensions are compactified to sizes of the order of the Planck length and govern gauge symmetries, which manifest as fundamental interactions. The theory automatically contains quantum gravity: closed strings describe the graviton, which allows avoiding ultraviolet divergences in calculations at the Planck scale. Five consistent superstring theories (type I, IIA, IIB, heterotic E₈×E₈ and SO(32)) are unified by dualities within M-theory.

History

The origins of the theory go back to 1968, when Gabriele Veneziano proposed a scattering amplitude that satisfied the duality hypothesis, which was later interpreted as string interactions. In the early 1970s, Yoichiro Nambu and Leonard Susskind independently identified the string nature of these amplitudes. The bosonic string, operating only with bosons, suffered from tachyon instability and required 26 dimensions. In 1984, the 'first superstring revolution', initiated by the work of Michael Green and John Schwarz, incorporated supersymmetry and reduced the dimension to 10, eliminating the tachyon. The second revolution in 1995, associated with the work of Edward Witten, led to dualities and M-theory, unifying the five string theories and introducing branes. Contemporary research focuses on the landscape approach (studying a multitude of possible vacuum states with different physical laws) and the search for low-energy phenomenological implications.

How it works

The dynamics of strings is described by the Nambu-Goto or Polyakov action, which generalizes the action of a relativistic point particle. Quantization of the string in conformal gauge generates an infinite set of harmonic oscillators: creation and annihilation operators for each mode produce the mass spectrum. The selection conditions (super-conformal symmetry) fix the critical dimension. Interactions are determined by the topology of the worldsheet—a two-dimensional surface swept by the string in spacetime; scattering amplitudes are computed by summing over all possible worldsheets of a given topology, leading to a perturbative series without ultraviolet divergences. The theory predicts that each particle has a supersymmetric partner, and all coupling constants are determined by the expectation values of scalar fields (moduli) associated with the sizes and shapes of extra dimensions.

Open problems & fun facts

Lack of experimental predictions testable at current energies.; The landscape problem: about 10^500 possible vacuum states giving different physical laws, without a selection criterion for our own.; Experimental verification is difficult because strings have Planck-scale size, unreachable for modern accelerators.

💡 In some models, the graviton is a closed string, while all other particles are open strings whose ends are attached to a brane. This can explain the weakness of gravity: it propagates into all extra dimensions, and we only feel the part that remains on the brane.
