string theory

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
15

The Universe consists not of point particles, but of microscopic vibrating strings. Like a guitar string gives rise to different notes, so the vibrations of fundamental strings set the entire diversity of particles and forces.

In practice: There are no direct applications yet, but the mathematical apparatus of string theory is used for studying black holes and quantum entanglement, and in the future may become the basis of a unified 'theory of everything'.
String theory is a model in which elementary particles are represented not as points, but as one-dimensional objects—strings. Different modes of string vibration correspond to each known particle: quarks, electrons, and even the hypothetical graviton. For internal consistency, the theory requires additional dimensions: superstrings exist in 10-dimensional spacetime. These dimensions are compactified (curled up) to scales of the Planck length—about 10⁻³⁵ m, which is why we do not observe them. There are variants: bosonic string, supersymmetric strings, and M-theory with membranes.

History

The idea arose in the late 1960s when physicists were looking for a way to describe the strong interaction—the force that holds atomic nuclei together. They noticed that a formula proposed by Gabriele Veneziano described interactions similar to the vibration of strings. In the 1980s–1990s, the theory turned into a candidate for a 'theory of everything,' promising to unite all fundamental forces.

How it works

Under normal conditions, strings are invisible: their size is about 10⁻³⁵ meters, unimaginably small. The theory predicts that at extreme energies, comparable to the conditions of the Big Bang, strings stretch and their effects become noticeable. Analogy: if you strongly heat a guitar string, it will sound at a new, previously inaudible frequency.

💡 If a string were the size of an atom, then the atom itself would have a diameter spanning an entire galaxy.
String theory attempts to unite quantum mechanics (the rules of the microworld) and general relativity (the description of gravity). Instead of point particles, it introduces tiny one-dimensional threads—strings. Just as different notes on a guitar string produce different sounds, each mode of vibration of a string creates a specific particle: an electron, a quark, or even a graviton—the hypothetical carrier particle of gravity. For the model to be consistent, additional dimensions are needed beyond the three spatial and one time dimension—10 or 11 in total. In everyday life, they are not noticeable because they are curled up into microscopic loops or spheres the size of the Planck length.

String theory is an approach to quantum gravity based on replacing point particles with one-dimensional extended objects (strings) with a characteristic length on the order of the Planck length. Vibrational excitations of the string produce a spectrum of states with increasing masses, including the graviton—the carrier of gravity. For the consistency of fermion quantization, supersymmetry is required, and to eliminate anomalies, a critical dimension of 10 (superstrings) or 26 (bosonic string). In the low-energy limit, the theory reproduces general relativity and gauge fields. The extra dimensions are compactified on six-dimensional Calabi-Yau manifolds, dictating the physics of our four-dimensional world.

History

In 1968, Gabriele Veneziano discovered that Euler's beta function describes the scattering amplitude of mesons. This led to the dual resonance model, which Yoichiro Nambu, Holger Nielsen, and Leonard Susskind interpreted as a quantum theory of relativistic strings. In the 1970s, the bosonic string suffered from tachyons, but the addition of supersymmetry and fermions led to superstring theory (John Schwarz, Michael Green). In 1984–86, the 'first superstring revolution' showed that 10-dimensional superstrings are free of anomalies and can incorporate the Standard Model. The mid-1990s—the 'second superstring revolution'—revealed dualities and a unifying M-theory (Edward Witten).

How it works

The string action generalizes the action of a point relativistic particle: instead of a worldline—a worldsheet (two-dimensional surface) in spacetime. The quantum behavior of the string leads to the requirement of a conformal field theory on the worldsheet. The mass spectrum is discrete, and for a closed string, the massless spin-2 excitation corresponds to the graviton, automatically incorporating gravity. Interactions are described by Feynman diagrams, but with the topology of worldsheets—without ultraviolet divergences, yielding a finite quantum theory of gravity. Upon compactification, the extra dimensions determine the set of massless fields and symmetry groups of the effective theory. The superposition principle applies to string states.

T = \frac{1}{2\pi\alpha'}
T is the string tension (energy per unit length), α' is the Regge slope parameter, related to the characteristic string length ℓ_s as ℓ_s = √(α') in Planck units (where ℓ_s ≈ Planck length ≈ 1.6×10⁻³⁵ m).
