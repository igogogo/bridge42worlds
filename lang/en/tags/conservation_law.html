Conservation law

📊 0 articles
A conservation law is a rule by which something important (energy, momentum) cannot disappear or appear out of nowhere.
💡 Thanks to the law of conservation of energy, you will never get a perpetual motion machine—energy does not come from nothing.
A conservation law states that a certain physical quantity (energy, momentum, charge) remains constant over time in an isolated system. For example, energy is neither created nor destroyed, but only transformed from one form to another.

History

The first conservation laws (momentum) were formulated by René Descartes in the 17th century. The law of conservation of energy was established by Julius Mayer, James Joule, and Hermann Helmholtz in the 1840s. Emmy Noether in 1918 showed the connection with symmetries.

How it works

Conservation laws follow from symmetries of spacetime: homogeneity of time gives conservation of energy, homogeneity of space gives conservation of momentum, isotropy gives conservation of angular momentum. In an isolated system, these quantities are unchanged.

💡 The law of conservation of lepton number predicted that neutrinos must be very light—this was confirmed by experiments.
A conservation law is a physical principle according to which a certain measurable quantity (energy, momentum, electric charge, baryon number, etc.) of an isolated system remains constant over time. Conservation laws are consequences of spacetime symmetries (Noether's theorem) or internal symmetries. They play a fundamental role in all branches of physics, from mechanics to quantum field theory.

History

The idea of conservation dates back to ancient philosophers, but scientific justification came in the 17th century (Descartes—momentum, Leibniz—vis viva). In the 19th century, the law of conservation of energy was experimentally confirmed by Mayer, Joule, and Helmholtz. In 1918, Emmy Noether gave a mathematical justification for the connection between symmetries and conservation laws. In the 20th century, conservation of baryon and lepton numbers were discovered.

How it works

For each continuous symmetry of the Lagrangian, there exists a conserved Noether current. For example, invariance under time shifts gives conservation of energy: dE/dt = 0. In quantum mechanics, conserved quantities correspond to operators that commute with the Hamiltonian. Conservation laws impose restrictions on possible processes (e.g., forbid the creation of a particle from nothing).
