critical point

📊 0
Imagine a kettle with boiling water. Normally, when water turns into steam, you can see bubbles and bubbling. But if you heat water under high pressure, you can reach a state where water and steam become completely indistinguishable — this is the critical point. At this point, liquid and gas merge into a single state, like a thick fog, but without a surface boundary. It's similar to how, in the morning, the air is so saturated with moisture that dew droplets and air merge into one whole.

History

Thomas Andrews first discovered the critical point in 1861 while studying carbon dioxide. He noticed that above 31°C, the gas would not liquefy under any pressure. Later, Van der Waals developed the theory, linking it to molecular forces.

How it works

Under normal conditions, liquid molecules attract each other and stay together, while in a gas they fly apart. With increasing temperature and pressure, the kinetic energy of molecules rises, and intermolecular forces can no longer hold them in an ordered state. At the critical point, these forces become equal: the liquid ceases to be dense, and the gas ceases to be rarefied. It's like a crowd at a concert: when there are too many people and everyone is moving, you can't tell who is standing still and who is dancing—everyone blends into a homogeneous mass.

💡 Did you know that at the critical point, water can simultaneously wet and not wet the walls of the container? The difference between liquid and steam disappears, and surface tension vanishes, so the liquid stops forming droplets.

The critical point is a thermodynamic state in which the distinctions between liquid and gas phases vanish. It is characterized by critical parameters: temperature T_c, pressure P_c, and volume V_c. Above the critical point, the substance exists as a supercritical fluid, possessing properties of both liquids and gases. The second-order phase transition at the critical point is accompanied by anomalies in heat capacity, compressibility, and correlation length, which are described by critical exponents and scaling theory. Near the critical point, critical opalescence and density fluctuations are observed, which are related to the universality of critical behavior of different systems.

History

The critical point was discovered by Charles Cagniard de la Tour in 1822, but systematically studied by Thomas Andrews in 1869. Andrews showed that for each gas there is a temperature above which it cannot be liquefied. In 1873, Johannes van der Waals proposed an equation of state for real gas that for the first time explained the existence of a critical point based on molecular forces. In the 20th century, Landau developed the theory of second-order phase transitions, and Wilson applied renormalization group to describe universal critical phenomena.

How it works

From a microscopic perspective, the critical point is the result of a balance between intermolecular attraction, which tends to condense the substance, and thermal motion, which tends to expand it. At the critical temperature, the attraction energy equals the thermal energy, and the phase boundary disappears. This is described by the van der Waals equation, which includes parameters a and b. At the critical point, compressibility tends to infinity, and the correlation length of fluctuations becomes macroscopic, leading to critical opalescence. In modern understanding, critical phenomena are universal and do not depend on system details, but are determined only by the dimensionality of space and the symmetry of the order parameter, as described by renormalization group theory.

T_c = \frac{8a}{27Rb}
T_c is the critical temperature; a is the van der Waals constant accounting for intermolecular attraction; b is the van der Waals constant accounting for the excluded volume of molecules; R is the universal gas constant (R ≈ 8.314 J/(mol·K))
P_c = \frac{a}{27b^2}
P_c is the critical pressure; a and b are the van der Waals constants (a is a measure of attraction, b is a measure of repulsion/volume)
