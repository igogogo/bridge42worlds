Phase transition

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A phase transition is a sudden transformation of a substance, like water freezing into ice or turning into steam at a certain temperature.

In practice: The basis of many technologies: metal quenching, semiconductor production, climate modeling, and creation of superconductors.
A phase transition is an abrupt change in the state of a thermodynamic system when external conditions, such as temperature or pressure, change. First-order transitions, like ice melting or water boiling, involve the release or absorption of latent heat and a jump in density. Second-order transitions occur without latent heat but with a sharp change in heat capacity; examples include the disappearance of iron's magnetization upon heating (the Curie point) or the transition to a superconducting state. Near the critical point, where differences between phases vanish, critical phenomena are observed, described by renormalization group theory.

History

Research began in the 19th century with the work of James Joule and William Thomson. Lev Landau created a general theory of phase transitions in 1937, and later Kenneth Wilson applied the renormalization group to explain critical phenomena.

How it works

The molecules of a substance are in constant motion. Heating increases their energy, and the bonds holding molecules in an ordered structure break—solid melts or liquid evaporates. Cooling, on the contrary, slows motion, and molecules arrange into a regular lattice.

💡 At the critical point, a liquid and its vapor become indistinguishable: one can observe opalescence—a cloudiness due to intense density fluctuations.
A phase transition is the transformation of a substance from one state to another, for example, from liquid to gas or liquid to solid, when temperature or pressure changes. At the transition point, properties change abruptly: water freezes at 0°C or boils at 100°C.

A phase transition is the transformation of a thermodynamic system from one phase to another, occurring when a control parameter (temperature, pressure, magnetic field) changes. According to Ehrenfest classification, first-order transitions are characterized by a jump in the first derivative of the thermodynamic potential (entropy, volume) and nonzero latent heat; second-order transitions have a discontinuity in the second derivative (heat capacity, compressibility) without latent heat. In modern theory, continuous transitions are described by introducing an order parameter (e.g., magnetization or superconducting gap) and expanding the free energy à la Landau. Fluctuations near the critical point diverge, and universal behavior is described by the renormalization group method. Examples: melting, ferromagnetism, superconductivity, Bose-Einstein condensation.

History

Early experimental research was conducted by James Joule and William Thomson. Thomas Andrews discovered the critical point in 1869. Paul Ehrenfest proposed the classification of transitions in 1933. Lev Landau created a phenomenological theory based on the order parameter in 1937. Lars Onsager provided an exact solution of the two-dimensional Ising model in 1944. Kenneth Wilson developed the renormalization group approach to critical phenomena in 1971. Boltzmann's statistical mechanics laid the foundations for describing phase transitions.

How it works

The thermodynamic potential (e.g., Gibbs free energy) determines the stable phase as the global minimum. When parameters change, the minimum point may shift abruptly (first-order transition) or continuously give way to another when stability is lost (second-order). Near the critical point, large-scale fluctuations develop, and mean-field theory breaks down. The renormalization group allows one to compute critical exponents. In quantum systems, phase transitions can occur even at zero temperature due to quantum fluctuations.
