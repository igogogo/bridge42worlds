hadron

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

Hadrons are particles assembled from quarks, like a mosaic of pieces. Protons and neutrons inside the atomic nucleus are the most well-known hadrons.

In practice: Understanding hadrons explains the structure of matter and allows studying the forces inside atomic nuclei, which is applied in nuclear energy and medical imaging (PET scanning).
Hadrons are a class of subatomic particles that participate in the strong interaction. They are not fundamental but consist of quarks held together by gluons (carriers of the strong interaction). The name comes from the Greek 'hadros' meaning massive, reflecting their relatively large mass compared to leptons (e.g., electrons). Hadrons include protons and neutrons (baryons, fermions with half-integer spin), as well as mesons (bosons with integer spin). Inside a hadron, quarks are bound so tightly that free quarks are not observed—this is the property of confinement.

History

The idea that hadrons consist of more fundamental particles arose in the 1960s. Physicists Murray Gell-Mann and George Zweig independently proposed the existence of quarks. Later experiments at accelerators confirmed their reality.

How it works

Quarks inside a hadron are never alone—they are always confined within. Attempting to tear a hadron apart requires enormous energy and leads to the creation of new quarks, which immediately form new hadrons. It's like trying to break a magnet: instead of two halves, you get two whole magnets.

💡 Although the proton appears stable, some theories predict that it could decay over an unimaginably long time—more than 10³⁴ years. Such decay has not yet been observed.
Hadrons are tiny particles that make up almost everything around us: they form atomic nuclei. Their peculiarity is that they are not elementary but assembled from even smaller parts—quarks, glued together by a 'glue'—a special strong interaction (the strongest interaction in nature, holding quarks together). Imagine quarks as glowing beads, and the strong interaction as a thread that keeps them from flying apart. Hadrons come in two types: baryons—made of three quarks (like the proton and neutron), and mesons—made of a quark and an antiquark (unstable particles).

A hadron is a composite particle formed from quarks and gluons bound by the strong interaction, described by quantum chromodynamics (QCD). Quarks possess color charge—an analogue of electric charge for the strong interaction—and gluons are the carriers of this interaction and themselves carry color charge, leading to self-interaction and confinement. Hadrons are divided into baryons (three quarks, fermions) and mesons (quark-antiquark, bosons). There are also exotic hadrons: tetraquarks, pentaquarks, and glueballs. The mass of a hadron significantly exceeds the sum of the masses of its constituent quarks due to the energy of gluon fields (the Higgs mechanism provides only a small part). The internal structure of hadrons is studied in deep inelastic scattering experiments. At high energies, QCD exhibits asymptotic freedom: quarks behave as nearly free particles.

History

The concept of hadrons became established by the mid-20th century as the number of discovered particles grew rapidly. In 1961, Murray Gell-Mann and Yuval Ne'eman proposed a classification of hadrons based on the SU(3) group, leading to the prediction of the Ω⁻ baryon. In 1964, Gell-Mann and, independently, George Zweig introduced the quark hypothesis. Experimental confirmation of quarks was obtained in the late 1960s in electron-proton scattering experiments at SLAC. The development of QCD in the 1970s (Gross, Wilczek, Politzer) explained the dynamics of the strong interaction.

How it works

Hadrons arise due to confinement: the color charges of quarks are attracted by gluons so strongly that the potential energy grows linearly with distance. When attempting to separate quarks, the gluon field string breaks, producing a quark-antiquark pair from the vacuum, leading to hadronization—the formation of jets of hadrons. This is described by the laws of QCD, in which asymptotic freedom and infrared slavery are important. Hadron masses are calculated using lattice QCD. The Pauli principle explains why baryons have an antisymmetric color wave function: the three quarks must be of different colors.
