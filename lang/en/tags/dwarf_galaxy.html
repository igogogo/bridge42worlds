dwarf galaxy

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A dwarf galaxy is a cosmic bubble in which there are hundreds of times fewer stars than in our Milky Way, but so much dark matter that it keeps it from bursting.

In practice: Studying dwarf galaxies helps understand what dark matter is made of and how all galaxies are born.
A dwarf galaxy is a type of galaxy with a mass less than 10⁹ solar masses and sizes up to several thousand light-years. They dominate the local Universe in number, but are difficult to observe due to low surface brightness. They contain an anomalously high proportion of dark matter, making them ideal laboratories for cosmology.

History

The first dwarf galaxies were discovered in the mid-20th century, but their ubiquity became clear thanks to telescopes like Hubble. Astronomer Vera Rubin, studying their rotation, found evidence of dark matter.

How it works

Stars in a dwarf galaxy move faster than they should if only visible mass is considered. This means there is invisible 'dark' matter inside, creating additional attraction.

💡 The dwarf galaxy in the constellation Fornax is so filled with dark matter that the ratio of dark matter to ordinary matter can reach 100 to 1.
A dwarf galaxy is a small star system, typically containing no more than a few billion stars (the Milky Way has hundreds of billions). They often orbit larger galaxies as satellites.

Dwarf galaxies are low-mass or evolved stellar systems with masses of 10⁶–10⁹ M☉ and effective radii of 0.1–10 kpc. They are subdivided into dwarf elliptical, spheroidal, irregular, and ultra-compact galaxies. They are predominantly dark: the mass-to-light ratio reaches up to 1000 M☉/L☉, indicating the dominance of non-baryonic dark matter. Their population is a key test of the ΛCDM model, which predicts an excess of such objects compared to observations (the missing satellites problem).

History

Dwarf satellites of the Milky Way began to be discovered in the 1930s–1940s (the Sculptor Dwarf Galaxy, 1937). In the 1970s, Vera Rubin used rotation curves to confirm the high fraction of dark matter in dwarf irregular galaxies. Fritz Zwicky had already hypothesized hidden mass in clusters in the 1930s, but it was in dwarfs that the connection became obvious. Since the 1990s, the Sloan Digital Sky Survey has revealed many ultra-faint dwarf satellites.

How it works

Dwarf galaxies form in mini-halos of dark matter, where primordial gas cools and forms stars. Their evolution is determined by star formation, suppressed by supernova outbursts and tidal interactions with massive neighbors. The rotation of stars and gas is described by Newtonian dynamics with the inclusion of hidden mass: the law of universal gravitation is used to estimate the gravitational potential. Dark matter dominates, and rotation curves remain flat far beyond the stellar disk. The virial theorem allows estimating the total mass from the velocity dispersion: M ≈ 5Rσ²/G.

M \approx \frac{5 R \sigma^2}{G}
M is the mass of the galaxy, R is the radius, σ is the velocity dispersion of stars, G is the gravitational constant, G ≈ 6.674×10⁻¹¹ m³kg⁻¹s⁻²

Open problems & fun facts

The missing satellites problem (discrepancy between the predicted and observed numbers of dwarf galaxies); The nature of dark matter (WIMPs, axions, or modified gravity); How dwarf galaxies lose gas and cease star formation
