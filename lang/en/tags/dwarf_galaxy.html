Dwarf Galaxy

📊 0 articles
This is a small galaxy that contains only a few million or billion stars, unlike giants with trillions.
💡 Dwarf galaxies are the most common galaxies in the universe; there may be tens of times more of them than large ones.
A dwarf galaxy is a small galaxy, ranging from a few hundred to a few thousand light-years in size. Imagine a village compared to a metropolis. They often orbit larger galaxies as satellites. Examples: the Magellanic Clouds, satellites of the Milky Way.

History

The first dwarf galaxies were discovered in the early 20th century, but systematic study began only in the 1970s. Many dwarf galaxies are now known in the Local Group.

How it works

Due to their low mass, dwarf galaxies easily lose gas when interacting with large galaxies, halting star formation. They can also be completely absorbed by larger neighbors.

💡 The dwarf galaxy Segue 2 contains only about 1000 stars—one of the smallest known galaxies.
Dwarf galaxies are small galaxies with low luminosity, containing from a few million to a few billion stars. They are subdivided into dwarf elliptical, dwarf spheroidal, dwarf irregular, and dwarf spiral. Dwarf galaxies are often satellites of larger galaxies. They play an important role in the study of dark matter, as their dynamics strongly depend on its presence.

History

The first dwarf galaxies (Magellanic Clouds) have been known since antiquity, but their nature as separate galaxies was established in the 20th century. In 1938, Harlow Shapley discovered the Sculptor Dwarf Galaxy. Modern surveys such as SDSS have detected many ultra-faint dwarf galaxies.

How it works

Dwarf galaxies are held together by gravity, with dark matter often dominating. Due to low gravity, they easily lose gas during tidal interactions or starbursts. Many are gradually disrupted and merge with larger galaxies. Their study helps understand the formation of structures in the universe.

Open problems & fun facts

The 'missing satellites' problem: many more dwarf galaxies are predicted than observed.; The nature of dark matter in dwarf galaxies remains a mystery.
