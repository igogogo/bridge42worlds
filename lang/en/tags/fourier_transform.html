Fourier transform

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A mathematical 'translator' from the language of time to the language of frequency: it decomposes a complex signal into simple sine waves, like breaking down a musical chord into individual notes. Any intricate dance becomes the sum of smooth swayings.

In practice: Image compression (JPEG), noise filtering in audio, signal analysis in MRI and radar, digitization of music — everywhere where it is necessary to isolate frequencies or remove interference.
The Fourier transform is an integral transform that maps a function of a real variable (often time) to a function of a complex variable (frequency). It decomposes the signal into an orthogonal basis of harmonic oscillations. The result (spectrum) shows the amplitude and phase of each frequency component. The inverse transform allows reconstructing the original signal. Thanks to the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), the algorithm works very quickly and is used everywhere in signal processing.

History

French mathematician Joseph Fourier proposed this idea in 1807 while studying how heat propagates in solids. He realized that any temperature distribution could be represented as a sum of simple waves. Later, the method began to be applied everywhere — from music to quantum physics.

How it works

Imagine that from a footprint in the sand you need to guess which waves left it. The Fourier transform acts like a detective: it alternately tries on waves of different lengths and strengths to the footprint. Where the match is good, a big 'clue' appears. In a computer, this is done by multiplying the signal by test sinusoids and summing the results. The result is a spectrum — a graph showing which frequencies are present.

💡 In quantum mechanics, the momentum representation of the wave function is the Fourier transform of its coordinate representation. The Heisenberg uncertainty principle arises from the properties of the Fourier transform: the narrower the signal in time, the wider its spectrum.
The Fourier transform decomposes a complex wave into simple components. Imagine that your voice is a soup of different sounds: a low hum and a high whistle. The Fourier transform takes this 'soup' and tells how much hum, how much whistle, and how much hiss there is. It does this by comparing the original signal with pure sine waves (smooth waves) of all possible frequencies (frequency — number of oscillations per second). Thus, from an audio recording, you can find out the notes, and from a photograph — smooth transitions and sharp contours.

For an absolutely integrable function f(t), the direct Fourier transform is defined as f̂(ω) = ∫₋∞∞ f(t) e^{-iωt} dt, where t is time, ω is angular frequency. Inverse: f(t) = (1/2π) ∫₋∞∞ f̂(ω) e^{iωt} dω. There are variants for periodic functions (Fourier series) and discrete sequences (Discrete Fourier Transform, DFT). The Fast Fourier Transform algorithm (Cooley–Tukey, 1965) allows computing the DFT in O(n log n) operations. Properties: linearity, shift gives a phase factor, differentiation ↔ multiplication by iω, convolution ↔ product. Generalizations: multidimensional FT, windowed FT, wavelets, Fourier analysis on groups. In physics, the FT connects coordinate and momentum spaces, ensuring the uncertainty principle.

History

Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier in 1807 (published in 1822) in a memoir on heat propagation showed that an arbitrary function can be expanded into a trigonometric series. The idea met resistance from Lagrange and Laplace, who doubted the convergence of the series. Later, Dirichlet (1829) clarified the conditions. In the 20th century, Laurent Schwartz extended the transform to generalized functions (Dirac delta function). Today it is the basis of harmonic analysis.

How it works

Mathematically, the kernel e^{-iωt} acts as a 'probe': for each frequency ω, we compute the correlation of the signal with a harmonic oscillation of that frequency. The integral measures the 'similarity'. Thanks to the orthogonality of sinusoids, the decomposition is invertible without loss. The Fast Fourier Transform uses the symmetry of complex roots of unity, reducing the computation of a large matrix to a series of small ones. In quantum mechanics, the wave function in momentum representation is the Fourier transform of the coordinate wave function, and the relation Δx Δp ≥ ħ/2 follows directly from the properties of the FT.

\hat{f}(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(t) e^{-i\omega t} dt
f(t) — original function (usually time), ω — angular frequency, i — imaginary unit, e — base of the natural logarithm.
f(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\omega) e^{i\omega t} d\omega
From the spectrum f̂(ω), the original function f(t) is reconstructed. The factor 1/(2π) normalizes the transform.
