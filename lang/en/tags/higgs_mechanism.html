Higgs Mechanism

📊 0 articles
The Higgs mechanism is the way particles acquire mass by moving through an invisible field, like people walking through a crowd.
💡 Without the Higgs mechanism, all particles would be massless and travel at the speed of light, like photons.
The Higgs mechanism explains why W and Z bosons (carriers of the weak force) have mass, while the photon does not. The Higgs field fills all space, and particles 'catch' on it, acquiring mass—as if they were moving through molasses. The stronger the interaction, the larger the mass.

History

Proposed by Peter Higgs, Robert Brout, and François Englert in 1964. Experimentally confirmed in 2012 at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) with the discovery of the Higgs boson.

How it works

The Higgs field has a nonzero value in the vacuum. When a particle moves through this field, it interacts with it, which manifests as mass. Gauge bosons, interacting with the field, 'eat' the massless Goldstone bosons and become massive.

💡 The Higgs boson is the only elementary particle with zero spin discovered to date.
The Higgs mechanism is the process by which gauge bosons acquire mass through spontaneous breaking of local symmetry. A scalar field (the Higgs field) is introduced with a nonzero vacuum expectation value. As a result, gauge fields absorb massless Goldstone bosons and become massive, leaving one scalar particle—the Higgs boson. This mechanism explains the masses of W and Z bosons in the electroweak theory.

History

In 1964, three groups (Higgs; Brout and Englert; Guralnik, Hagen, and Kibble) independently proposed the mechanism. The idea was developed in the context of electroweak unification by Glashow, Weinberg, and Salam. In 2012, the Higgs boson was discovered at the LHC with a mass of about 125 GeV, confirming the mechanism. The 2013 Nobel Prize was awarded to Higgs and Englert.

How it works

Introduce a complex scalar field φ with potential V = μ^2|φ|^2 + λ|φ|^4, μ^2<0. The minimum is at |φ| = v/√2. Choose the vacuum in the real part: φ = (v+h)/√2. Substitute into the Lagrangian with covariant derivative. The term (D_μφ)^2 gives mass terms for gauge fields: M_W = gv/2, M_Z = √(g^2+g'^2) v/2. The field h is the Higgs boson with mass m_h = √(2λ) v.

M_W = \frac{g v}{2}
The W boson mass is proportional to the coupling constant g and the vacuum expectation value v of the Higgs field.
