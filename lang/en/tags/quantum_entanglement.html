quantum entanglement

Imagine two twins who always dress alike, even if they are in different cities. If one puts on a red shirt, the other instantly puts on a blue one, and vice versa. Quantum entanglement is like such an invisible bond between particles: if one particle changes its state, the other changes immediately, no matter how far apart they are. This is not information transfer, but simply a surprising property of the quantum world.

History

The concept of quantum entanglement was first described in 1935 in the famous paper by Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen (EPR paradox), which questioned the completeness of quantum mechanics. Later, in 1964, physicist John Bell proposed inequalities that allow experimentally distinguishing entanglement from classical correlations. Decisive experiments conducted by Alain Aspect in the 1980s confirmed the existence of entanglement and the violation of Bell's inequalities.

How it works

Quantum entanglement occurs when two particles interact and form a single system with a shared state. Imagine two coins that always land opposite sides: if one is heads, the other is tails, but before the toss both are in a superposition of heads and tails. The moment one coin is measured, the state of the other is instantly fixed, regardless of distance. This non-local correlation is the basis of quantum information and teleportation.

In nature, an analogy can be made with two spinning tops that always spin in opposite directions. If you look at one and see it is turning clockwise, you instantly know the other is turning counterclockwise, without even looking. In quantum entanglement, particles behave similarly, but with quantum states such as spin or polarization. The most striking thing is that this correlation persists over any distance and apparently violates our usual notion of locality.

Quantum entanglement is a phenomenon of quantum mechanics in which two or more particles become correlated in such a way that the state of one particle cannot be described independently of the other, even if they are spatially separated. Mathematically, it is described by a non-separable state vector in Hilbert space. Entanglement underlies effects such as the violation of Bell's inequalities, quantum teleportation, superdense coding, and quantum computing. It does not allow information to be transmitted faster than light, but demonstrates the non-locality of the quantum world.

History

In 1935, Albert Einstein, Boris Podolsky, and Nathan Rosen formulated a paradox pointing to the incompleteness of quantum mechanics. Erwin Schrödinger introduced the term 'entanglement' that same year and emphasized the fundamental importance of this phenomenon. In 1964, John Stewart Bell derived a theorem showing that any local hidden variables cannot reproduce all predictions of quantum mechanics; Bell's inequalities became a testable criterion. Experiments by Alain Aspect in 1981–1982, using polarized photons, for the first time unequivocally demonstrated the violation of Bell's inequalities and confirmed the non-locality of entangled states. Subsequent work by Zeilinger, Clauser, and others closed possible loopholes, strengthening the understanding of quantum non-locality.

How it works

Entanglement arises from particle interactions if their joint wave function does not factorize. The Schrödinger equation describes the unitary evolution of such a superposition, and measurement leads to collapse and instantaneous correlation of states — this is consistent with Heisenberg's uncertainty principle. Bell's inequalities mathematically constrain correlations for local realistic theories; their violation proves non-locality. Entanglement measures such as von Neumann entropy quantify it, and the no-communication theorem is consistent with the constant speed of light. Entanglement is a resource for quantum information science, but decoherence limits its application.
