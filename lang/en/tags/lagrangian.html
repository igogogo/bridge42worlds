lagrangian

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

The Lagrangian is a 'recipe' for motion: the difference between the energy of speed and the energy of position sets the system on the path of least resistance.

In practice: Used for calculating the orbits of planets, trajectories of rockets, optimizing mechanical devices, and in the foundations of quantum theory.
The Lagrangian is a function depending on the coordinates and velocities of all parts of a system. Instead of considering forces, as in Newtonian mechanics, one operates with energies: kinetic (energy of motion) and potential (energy of position). From the principle of least action it follows that the actual motion of a system occurs such that the total difference of these energies along the trajectory is minimal. This approach is particularly convenient for systems with constraints, e.g., a pendulum on an inextensible string, and allows one to derive the equations of motion (Euler-Lagrange equations).

History

Developed by Joseph-Louis Lagrange in 1788 as a universal method for describing motion, alternative to Newton's laws.

How it works

Just as water flows downhill, choosing the path of least resistance, so any mechanical system evolves by minimizing the accumulated difference of kinetic and potential energies, given by the Lagrangian.

💡 Lagrange's method made it possible to solve problems that were too complex for the direct application of Newton's laws, such as the motion of many-body systems with constraints.

Knowing the Lagrangian, one can predict how the system will change over time: nature always chooses the trajectory for which the total difference of these energies over the entire path is the smallest.

The Lagrangian L(q, q̇, t) is a fundamental function in analytical mechanics, defined as the difference between the kinetic energy T and the potential energy V: L = T − V. Hamilton's principle (the principle of stationary action) states that the true trajectory of a system between two moments in time makes the action integral S = ∫ L dt stationary. Variation of S leads to the Euler-Lagrange equations: d/dt (∂L/∂q̇_i) − ∂L/∂q_i = 0, which are equivalent to Newton's second law but applicable in arbitrary coordinates. The Lagrangian formalism extends to field theory (via Lagrangian density), quantum mechanics (Feynman path integrals), and elementary particle physics. Symmetries of the Lagrangian lead to conservation laws via Noether's theorem.

History

First presented by Joseph-Louis Lagrange in 'Analytical Mechanics' (1788) with the goal of constructing mechanics without geometric constructions. In the 19th century, William Rowan Hamilton reformulated it into Hamiltonian formalism. Henri Poincaré developed the theory of dynamical systems. Emmy Noether in 1918 proved the theorem linking continuous symmetries of the Lagrangian to conserved quantities. In quantum field theory, the Lagrangian became the basis of the Standard Model.

How it works

For a system with generalized coordinates q_i, the Lagrangian is written as L(q, q̇, t) = T − V. The Euler-Lagrange equations are obtained by requiring the variation of the action to be zero. For example, for a particle in a potential field, L = ½ m ẋ² − V(x). Noether's theorem states: to each continuous symmetry of the Lagrangian there corresponds a conserved integral of motion (energy, momentum, etc.).
