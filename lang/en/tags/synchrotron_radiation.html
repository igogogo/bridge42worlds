synchrotron radiation

📊 0 articles

Light born on a turn: as if a car skidded and sparks flew from under the wheels — only these are particles of light from electrons flying in a circle.

In practice: Used in X-ray microscopy to study the structure of materials, helping to see details as small as an atom.
Relativistic electrons entering a magnetic field experience acceleration perpendicular to their velocity and emit electromagnetic waves. Due to the high speed, the radiation is concentrated in a narrow cone along the direction of motion. The spectrum of synchrotron radiation is broad — from radio to X-rays. The radiation power increases with the particle energy and field strength.
💡 Synchrotron radiation from giant jets of black holes is so powerful that it is visible through telescopes across billions of light-years.
A charged particle (for example, an electron), moving rapidly and turning in a magnetic field, emits light. This is similar to water flying off a wet rag if you spin it quickly. The faster the particle, the brighter and more energetic the radiation.

History

First noticed in 1947 by engineers at a synchrotron accelerator when they saw a bright glow.

How it works

The magnetic field forces electrons to move in a circle, their path is bent, and they lose energy by emitting photons (light particles). This light is emitted in a narrow beam forward, like from a searchlight.

💡 Synchrotron radiation is so bright that beams in modern storage rings lose power on the order of several megawatts, which requires cooling and is taken into account in the design of spacecraft.
Synchrotron radiation is electromagnetic radiation emitted by ultra-relativistic charged particles moving in magnetic fields along curved trajectories. In astrophysics, it is the key emission mechanism of radio galaxies, supernova remnants, and accretion disks. The radiation is highly polarized and has a characteristic power-law spectrum I_ν ∝ ν^α, with typical α ≈ −0.7. The mechanism is described within classical electrodynamics for a charge moving at relativistic speeds. Important roles are played by the Doppler effect and aberration of light, which squeeze the radiation into a narrow cone.

History

Theoretically predicted in 1944 by D. Ivanenko and I. Pomeranchuk, and also by J. Schwinger. Experimentally discovered in 1947 by F. H. Pollock and a group of General Electric engineers on a 70 MeV synchrotron. Introduced into astrophysical practice in the 1950s thanks to the work of V. L. Ginzburg and others. Linked to Maxwell's equations, which laid the foundation for understanding electromagnetic radiation.

How it works

A charged particle moving in a magnetic field B with relativistic velocity v experiences the Lorentz force, producing a centripetal acceleration a = (e v B)/(γ m). According to Maxwell's equations, an accelerated charge radiates electromagnetic waves. For ultra-relativistic particles (γ ≫ 1), the radiation is strongly beamed forward due to relativistic angle transformation. The emission spectrum of a single electron peaks at frequency ν_c = (3/4π) (e B / m_e c) γ^2, and an ensemble of particles with an energy distribution yields a power-law spectrum. In astrophysical sources, synchrotron radiation is often accompanied by inverse Compton scattering.

P = \frac{2}{3} \frac{e^4 B^2}{m_e^2 c^5} E^2
P — radiated power (W), e — elementary charge (≈1.6×10^{-19} C), B — magnetic field induction (T), m_e — electron mass (≈9.11×10^{-31} kg), c — speed of light (≈3×10^8 m/s), E — kinetic energy of the electron (J).
\nu_c = \frac{3}{4\pi} \frac{e B}{m_e c} \gamma^2
ν_c — frequency (Hz) at which the spectrum cuts off; e, B, m_e, c as before; γ — Lorentz factor of the particle (γ = 1/\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}).
