A galaxy is a large-scale astrophysical system consisting of 10^7–10^12 stars, interstellar matter (gas and dust), cosmic rays, and a dark matter halo that dominates in mass. Gravitational dynamics are described by Einstein's equations on large scales and Newtonian approximation within the galactic disk. Hubble's morphological classification includes elliptical (E0–E7), lenticular (S0), spiral (Sa–Sd), and irregular (Irr) galaxies. Galaxy formation occurs in the hierarchical ΛCDM model through mergers and gas accretion, accompanied by star formation and feedback from active nuclei and supernovae.

History

The term 'galaxy' traces back to the ancient Greek 'milky circle'. In the 1750s, Immanuel Kant suggested that faint nebulae were 'island universes'. William Herschel in the late 18th century compiled the first catalog of nebulae. Decisive proof came from Edwin Hubble in 1923–1924 using the 100-inch Mount Wilson telescope, discovering Cepheids in M31 and Andromeda, which allowed distance measurements far exceeding the size of the Milky Way. Hubble classified galaxies in 1926, and later, with his redshift law, proved the expansion of the Universe. Vera Rubin in the 1970s confirmed the existence of dark matter through galactic rotation curves.

How it works

The stellar and gas components of a galaxy move in a common gravitational potential, including the contribution of visible matter and the dark halo. Rotation curves of spiral galaxies show anomalously high velocities at the periphery, indicating the existence of dark matter (the law of universal gravitation would predict a velocity drop if all mass were visible). Spiral arms are explained by the density wave theory of Lin and Shu (1964): they are quasi-stationary structures where stellar orbits intersect and compress gas, triggering star formation. The formation of large-scale galaxy structure follows from primordial density fluctuations observed in the cosmic microwave background, described by the Friedmann equations in cosmology. Hubble's law links the redshift of galaxies to distance, which underlies the Big Bang model.
