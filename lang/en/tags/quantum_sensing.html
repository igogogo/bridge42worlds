quantum sensing

12

Quantum sensing is like a super-sensitive microscope that uses the fragility of quantum states to detect the slightest changes in the environment.

In practice: Helps create ultra-precise atomic clocks and gravity sensors capable of finding underground voids without excavation.
Quantum sensing is a high-precision measurement technology based on controlling quantum states (superposition, entanglement) to register physical fields with record sensitivity. It surpasses classical methods as it uses the discreteness and nonlocality of the quantum world.

History

The idea emerged in the late 20th century when scientists realized that quantum entanglement (a connection between particles at a distance) could be used for measurements. Important contributions were made by John Bell and Alain Aspect, who demonstrated the reality of quantum wonders.

How it works

In a quantum sensor, a particle, such as an atom, is put into a quantum state and sent to 'explore' the environment. The slightest impact changes this state, and by how the particle has changed, one determines exactly what happened.

💡 A quantum magnetometer in your smartphone could sense the heart's magnetic field from across the room if not for noise interference.
Quantum sensing uses quantum particles (e.g., photons or electrons) to measure physical quantities with incredible precision. Imagine flipping a coin: in the ordinary world, it lands heads or tails, but in the quantum world, it can be in superposition (both heads and tails at the same time). This unusual ability allows detecting minute changes in temperature, magnetic field, or gravity.

Quantum sensing is a set of methods that use quantum correlations and interference to measure physical quantities with accuracy limited by fundamental limits. It is based on the creation and control of quantum states (e.g., NOON states for optical interferometry, spin-squeezed states in atomic ensembles) that allow surpassing the standard quantum limit. It is applied in gravimetry, magnetometry, thermometry, and optical clocks, achieving sensitivity close to the Heisenberg limit.

History

The concept originated in the 1980s with the development of quantum optics and quantum information theories. John Bell in 1964 formulated Bell's theorem, showing the indispensability of quantum nonlocality. Alain Aspect in the early 1980s experimentally confirmed the violation of Bell's inequalities, paving the way for technologies based on entanglement. Since the 2000s, quantum sensors based on cold atoms and NV centers in diamond have been entering applied fields.

How it works

Quantum sensing works by creating sensitive quantum states, such as squeezed or entangled states, and then measuring their response to external fields. It uses effects like quantum superposition and entanglement to amplify the measurement signal. In atomic interferometers, phase calculation is done through the de Broglie formula (λ = h/p), linking the atom's wavelength to its momentum. The standard quantum limit and the Heisenberg limit are used to estimate the precision limit. Bell's theorem provides the basis for protocols where entanglement enhances sensitivity.

\lambda = \frac{h}{p}
λ is the wavelength of matter (atom), h is Planck's constant, h ≈ 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s, p is the particle momentum
\Delta\phi \geq \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}
Δφ is the phase error, N is the average number of photons (or atoms) in a nonclassical state
