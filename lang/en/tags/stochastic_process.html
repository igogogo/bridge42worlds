Stochastic Process

📊 0 articles
This is a random process that changes over time: for example, fluctuations in stock prices or Brownian motion of pollen in water.
💡 Brownian motion is a classic example of a stochastic process, described by Einstein in 1905.
A stochastic process is a family of random variables indexed by time. It describes the evolution of a random system. Examples: stock prices, weather, queue at a store. Important types: Markov processes, Wiener process, Poisson process.

History

The foundations were laid by A.N. Kolmogorov, N. Wiener, and K. Itô in the 20th century.

How it works

The process is defined by distributions of finite-dimensional sections. For a Markov process, the future depends only on the present. The Wiener process is a continuous analogue of a random walk.

💡 Stochastic processes are used in financial mathematics to model option prices (Black-Scholes model).
A stochastic process is a parameterized family of random variables {X_t, t ∈ T} defined on a common probability space. The parameter t is usually interpreted as time. Processes are classified by: time continuity (discrete/continuous), type of distributions, Markov property, stationarity. Important examples: Wiener process (Brownian motion), Poisson process, Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process. The theory includes stochastic calculus (Itô integral, Itô formula).

History

Andrey Kolmogorov in 1931 laid the foundations of the theory of Markov processes. Norbert Wiener in 1923 gave a rigorous definition of Brownian motion. Claude Shannon used Markov chains in information theory. Kiyoshi Itô in the 1940s developed stochastic calculus. In the 1960s, the Kalman-Bucy filtering theory emerged.

How it works

A stochastic process is defined through finite-dimensional distributions. For a Markov process, transition probabilities satisfy the Kolmogorov-Chapman equation. The Wiener process W_t has independent increments, W_t ~ N(0,t). The Itô stochastic integral ∫ f dW is defined as a limit of sums. Itô formula: df(W_t) = f'(W_t)dW_t + (1/2)f''(W_t)dt.
