quantum computer

📊 18 articles

A quantum computer's qubit is like a spinning coin: while in the air, it is both heads and tails at once. Such superposition enables solving problems impossible for ordinary computers.

In practice: Simulating molecules for new drugs, financial optimization, breaking cryptographic ciphers.
A quantum computer uses qubits, which, thanks to superposition, can be in a linear combination of states (0 and 1). Entanglement creates nonlocal connections between qubits. Sequences of quantum gates — reversible logical operations — transform the states. Measurement destroys the superposition, yielding a single result; thus algorithms (such as Shor's for factorization or Grover's for search) aim to maximize the probability of the correct answer.

History

In the 1980s, physicist Richard Feynman suggested that a quantum computer could more accurately simulate natural processes. David Deutsch developed the theory, and by the late 1990s, the first working devices were built.

How it works

Qubits are made from atoms, ions, or superconducting circuits. Their state is controlled by laser pulses or microwave radiation — analogous to the logic gates of a regular processor. Superposition allows processing many possibilities in one step, and interference (like wave addition) amplifies the desired result, which is then read out.

💡 A quantum computer does not explicitly try all possibilities, as often thought. It manipulates probability amplitudes, which interfere: the waves of incorrect answers cancel each other out, while the correct one is amplified.
Bits in an ordinary computer are like switches: on (1) or off (0). Qubits in a quantum computer resemble a spinning coin: while spinning, it is both heads and tails (this property is called superposition). Multiple qubits can become entangled — a change to one instantly affects the other, no matter how far apart they are. Such entanglement allows solving some problems much faster.

A quantum computer performs computations using quantum principles — superposition, entanglement, and quantum parallelism. Qubits are built on various physical platforms: superconducting circuits (Josephson junctions), ion traps, linear optics, quantum dots, impurities in silicon, etc. Calculations are typically represented by quantum circuits, where operations are unitary transformations. Decoherence (interaction with the environment that destroys quantum properties) requires the use of quantum error correction, e.g., surface code. Quantum supremacy — performing a task practically impossible for classical supercomputers — was first claimed by Google in 2019 on a 53-qubit Sycamore processor.

History

The concept of quantum computing was independently proposed by Yuri Manin (1980) and Richard Feynman (1981). In 1985, David Deutsch created a model of a universal quantum computer. Shor's algorithm (1994) for factoring numbers attracted attention due to the threat to RSA encryption. Grover's algorithm (1995) accelerates search. Experiments evolved from 2-qubit NMR schemes to ion traps and superconducting qubits. In 2012, the Nobel Prize was awarded to Serge Haroche and David Wineland for the manipulation of individual quantum systems.

How it works

The evolution of the qubit state vector in Hilbert space is described by the Hamiltonian. The initial state (usually |0...0⟩) is sequentially transformed by unitary gates, which create superposition and entangle the qubits. Algorithms such as the quantum Fourier transform adjust the amplitudes so that interference amplifies the target state. Measurement yields one of the outcomes with probability |amplitude|². The no-cloning theorem guarantees that an unknown quantum state cannot be exactly copied, which is fundamental for protocol security.

|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle
|ψ⟩ is the quantum state of a qubit (a vector in Hilbert space), |0⟩ and |1⟩ are basis states (orthonormal basis), α and β are complex probability amplitudes, such that |α|² + |β|² = 1 (normalization condition). Measurement yields |0⟩ with probability |α|² and |1⟩ with probability |β|².
|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)
|Φ⁺⟩ is one of the maximally entangled Bell states; |00⟩ is the state where both qubits are in |0⟩; |11⟩ both in |1⟩; 1/√2 is the normalization factor. Upon measuring one qubit, the second instantly assumes a correlated state (nonlocal correlation).
i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial t}|\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{H}|\psi(t)\rangle
i is the imaginary unit; ℏ (h-bar) is the reduced Planck constant, ℏ ≈ 1.054571817×10⁻³⁴ J·s; ∂/∂t is the partial derivative with respect to time; |ψ(t)⟩ is the state vector of the system at time t; Ĥ is the Hamiltonian operator, describing the total energy of the system; the equation determines how the quantum state evolves in time, which underlies the operation of quantum gates.
