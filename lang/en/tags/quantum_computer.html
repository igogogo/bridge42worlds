Quantum Computer

📊 0 articles
A computing device that uses quantum phenomena (superposition and entanglement) to process information. Unlike classical bits (0 or 1), quantum bits (qubits) can be in superposition, allowing parallel computations. It's like being able to read all the books in a library simultaneously, rather than one at a time.
💡 In 2019, Google claimed 'quantum supremacy': their Sycamore processor solved a task in 200 seconds that would take a classical supercomputer 10,000 years.

History

The idea of quantum computing dates back to Richard Feynman (1982) and Yuri Manin. Peter Shor (1994) developed a factoring algorithm threatening RSA encryption. Lov Grover (1996) developed a search algorithm. The first experimental quantum computers appeared in the late 1990s (ion traps, superconductors). Today, companies like Google and IBM build processors with dozens of qubits.

How it works

Qubits are implemented on different physical platforms: trapped ions, superconducting circuits, photons. Operations on qubits are quantum gates, analogous to logic gates. Measuring a qubit yields 0 or 1 with probability determined by its state. Quantum algorithms use interference to amplify correct answers and suppress wrong ones.

Open problems & fun facts

Decoherence: how to maintain a quantum state long enough for computations?; Scalability: how to create thousands and millions of qubits with low error rates?
