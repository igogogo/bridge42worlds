quantum computer

A quantum computer is a computing machine that uses not ordinary bits (0 or 1), but qubits — particles that can be both 0 and 1 simultaneously, like a coin spinning in the air. Thanks to this, it can solve complex problems by iterating through many options at once, like a swarm of bees simultaneously checking all the flowers in a meadow.
💡 A quantum computer is so sensitive that the slightest vibration or heat can spoil calculations, so they are kept at a temperature colder than in outer space!
A quantum computer is a device that operates with qubits instead of classical bits. A qubit can be in a superposition of states (both 0 and 1 at the same time), and several qubits can be quantum-entangled — connected so that changing one instantly affects the other, wherever it is. It's like an orchestra, where each musician plays their own note, but together they create a most complex symphony, allowing the solving of tasks inaccessible to ordinary computers.

History

The idea of quantum computing was proposed by physicist Richard Feynman in the 1980s, realizing that to model quantum systems, a quantum computer itself is needed. Then mathematician Peter Shor in 1994 came up with an algorithm that on a quantum computer can crack modern ciphers, and in 2019 Google announced 'quantum supremacy' — solving a problem impossible for a classical supercomputer.

How it works

Imagine a maze: a classical computer checks each path in turn, while a quantum computer — like a ghost passing through all walls at once, instantly finding the exit. Qubits are implemented on superconducting circuits, ions in traps, or photons; operations on them (gates) change their states, and measurement gives the answer with high probability.

💡 Today's most powerful quantum computer has over 1000 qubits, but to outperform a regular laptop in real tasks, their number and quality must be much higher.
A quantum computer is a computing device that uses quantum mechanical phenomena (superposition, entanglement, interference) to process information. Unlike a classical bit (0 or 1), a qubit is a linear combination of basis states |0⟩ and |1⟩ with complex amplitudes, allowing simultaneous encoding of 2ⁿ states for n qubits in a Hilbert space. Computations reduce to unitary transformations (quantum gates), and the result is obtained after measurement, which collapses the state into one of the basis vectors. Potential applications include number factorization (Shor's algorithm), simulation of quantum systems, optimization, and machine learning.

History

The concept of quantum computing was proposed independently by Yuri Manin and Richard Feynman in the early 1980s. In 1985, David Deutsch formalized the model of a universal quantum computer. In 1994, Peter Shor discovered the factorization algorithm, demonstrating practical value. Experimental implementations began with nuclear magnetic resonance and ion traps; in the 2010s, superconducting qubits became the leaders. In 2019, Google's Sycamore processor solved a task in 200 seconds that would take a regular supercomputer 10,000 years, although this was later disputed.

How it works

At the basis of quantum computing lies the manipulation of quantum states described by vectors in a Hilbert space. A quantum algorithm is defined by a sequence of gates (operators) that realize unitary evolution according to the Schrödinger equation. Due to superposition, all possible combinations of input data are processed in parallel, and quantum interference amplifies correct answers and suppresses incorrect ones. Entanglement provides correlations unavailable classically. Key problems are decoherence (interaction with the environment destroying the quantum state) and gate errors, to combat which error correction codes are being developed.
