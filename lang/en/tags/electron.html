Electron

📊 1 articles
A stable elementary particle with a negative electric charge. Electrons form the electron shells of atoms and determine the chemical properties of elements. They participate in electric current, thermal conductivity, and many other phenomena. Imagine electrons as tiny balls orbiting the atomic nucleus like planets around the Sun.
💡 Electrons have no known internal structure and are considered truly elementary particles—they are not made of smaller particles.

History

The electron was discovered by Joseph John Thomson in 1897 while studying cathode rays. He determined the charge-to-mass ratio. Robert Millikan measured the electron's charge in 1909. Later, quantum mechanics described the electron as a wave rather than a particle.

How it works

Electrons occupy orbitals—regions of space around the nucleus where the probability of finding them is highest. Analogy: a bee buzzing around a flower—its position is fuzzy, but there is an area where it is most often. Electrons can transition between orbitals by absorbing or emitting photons.
