quantum state

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A quantum state is a complete characteristic of a microparticle, determining everything we can learn about it: where it can be located, with what probability, and its momentum. It is like a chess position that records all possible future moves.

In practice: Quantum states underlie lasers, transistors, MRI scanners, and quantum computers—any device that uses quantum effects operates with particle states.
A quantum state is a mathematical object (state vector) in Hilbert space (an abstract infinite-dimensional space) that contains all information about a system. It evolves deterministically according to the Schrödinger equation, but the measurement result is fundamentally random: for each observable quantity, there is a set of possible values, and measurement yields one of them with a certain probability. Moreover, the state after measurement collapses to an eigenstate of the measured observable operator. The property of superposition allows for 'ghost' states where a particle passes through two slits simultaneously.

History

The idea of quantum states was born at the beginning of the 20th century when Erwin Schrödinger wrote his famous equation describing the evolution of the wave function. Soon after, Max Born proposed a probabilistic interpretation: the square of the wave function gives the probability of finding a particle at a given point.

How it works

Just as a person's shadow on the wall does not show all the details, measuring a quantum state captures only one of the possible 'shadows' of the particle. Before measurement, the electron in an atom does not orbit like a planet but is smeared across a cloud of probabilities. When we 'look' (measure), the cloud collapses into a point—this is the collapse of the wave function.

💡 A quantum state can be entangled: two particles separated by kilometers are described by a single state, and measuring one instantly determines the result of measuring the other—Einstein called this 'spooky action at a distance.'
In the classical world, we are accustomed to objects having precise positions and speeds. In the microcosm, quantum mechanics reigns: a particle does not simultaneously have a precise location and a precise velocity. Its state is described by a wave function (a mathematical rule) that tells us with what probability we will find the particle in a particular place and with what momentum (mass times velocity). The most astonishing thing is that until we measure, the particle seems to exist in all possible states at once—this is called superposition.

In quantum mechanics, a pure state of a system is described by a vector |ψ⟩ from Hilbert space. For a system in a mixed state, the density matrix ρ = Σ p_i |ψ_i⟩⟨ψ_i| is used. The evolution of a pure state is given by the Schrödinger equation: iħ ∂/∂t |ψ⟩ = Ĥ|ψ⟩, where Ĥ is the Hamiltonian. Observables are represented by Hermitian operators, and their eigenvalues are the possible measurement results. When measuring quantity A, the state is projected onto an eigenspace with probability given by the Born rule. Quantum states can possess symmetries (e.g., bosonic/fermionic permutation properties) and can be coherent—maintaining phase relationships.

History

Key steps in understanding quantum states were taken in the 1920s. Erwin Schrödinger introduced the wave function and his equation in 1926, hoping to bring visualization back to quantum theory. That same year, Max Born proposed the probabilistic interpretation of |ψ|². Werner Heisenberg developed matrix mechanics, and Paul Dirac unified the approaches into a general theory of transformations and introduced bra-ket notation. John von Neumann formalized the concept of Hilbert space for states. Later, in 1935, Schrödinger described the 'cat' paradox and coined the term 'entanglement.' The study of quantum states continues in quantum information and quantum computing.

How it works

The state |ψ⟩ contains probability amplitudes, the squared moduli of which determine the probabilities of measurement outcomes. The Schrödinger equation iħ d|ψ⟩/dt = Ĥ|ψ⟩ ensures unitary evolution: superpositions are preserved. Measurement causes an irreversible collapse to one of the possible states with probability |⟨φ_i|ψ⟩|². For mixed states, the density matrix is used, evolving according to the Liouville–von Neumann equation. Entangled states, such as the Bell singlet (|01⟩ - |10⟩)/√2, exhibit nonlocal correlations. The superposition principle enables quantum parallelism—the basis of quantum computing. Decoherence (interaction with the environment) rapidly destroys pure states on macroscopic scales.
