transit method

📊 42 articles
Imagine looking at a light bulb, and a moth flies in front of it. The bulb dims slightly for a moment. That's exactly how scientists find planets around distant stars: they watch for tiny 'eclipses' when a planet passes across its star's disk.
💡 If aliens looked at the Sun using the transit method, they would notice Earth blocking just 0.008% of its light! Such precision is only possible with space telescopes.
The transit method is a way to detect exoplanets by the dip in a star's brightness. Just as a hand passing in front of a flashlight casts a shadow, a planet passing between us and a star blocks some of its light. By the depth and duration of the 'eclipse', astronomers estimate the planet's size and orbit.

History

The idea of using planet transits for detection originated in the mid-20th century, but the real revolution was made by the Kepler telescope, launched in 2009. Its principal investigator, William Borucki, had fought for decades to get this project approved — and ultimately discovered thousands of worlds.

How it works

Imagine a light curve of a star — a graph like a cardiogram. When a planet eclipses the star, a 'dip' appears on the graph. The depth of the dip shows how big the planet is, and the time between dips shows how fast it orbits the star. Sensitive photometers record brightness changes of hundredths of a percent.

💡 The most famous transit in history was the passage of Venus across the Sun's disk in 1769, which allowed calculation of the Earth-Sun distance. The modern transit method has found a planet resembling Tatooine from Star Wars — Kepler-16b, orbiting a binary star!
The transit method is a photometric method for detecting exoplanets based on recording periodic dips in a star's brightness as a planet passes across its visible disk. The transit depth ΔF/F is directly related to the ratio of the planet's radius to the star's radius: (R_p / R_s)². The method allows determining the planet's radius, orbital period, orbital inclination, and even atmospheric composition (via transmission spectroscopy). Combined with the radial velocity method, the planet's density can be calculated. Space telescopes such as Kepler, TESS, and CHEOPS specialize in transit surveys, while the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) and Hubble are used for detailed study of transiting planets' atmospheres.

History

The first successful detection of an exoplanet transit occurred in 1999 for the star HD 209458. The theoretical possibility of the method had been discussed since the mid-20th century, but technical difficulty required space-based surveys. The breakthrough was achieved by William Borucki, who since the 1980s had been developing the concept of the Kepler mission. Launched in 2009, Kepler observed about 150,000 stars simultaneously and discovered thousands of candidates, proving that planets are common in the Galaxy. In 2018, TESS followed, continuing transit surveys of nearby stars.

How it works

When a planet crosses a star's disk, the total radiation flux decreases by an amount proportional to the planet's disk area: ΔF/F = (R_p/R_s)². The transit light curve has a characteristic shape with gradual ingress/egress and a flat bottom (if the star is uniform). The period between transits gives the orbital period, and the transit duration gives the impact parameter and inclination. The secondary transit (when the planet passes behind the star) allows measuring the planet's own emission. Transit spectroscopy reveals absorption lines in the atmosphere — for example, water vapor, methane, carbon dioxide.

\frac{\Delta F}{F} = \left(\frac{R_p}{R_s}\right)^2
ΔF/F — relative flux drop, R_p — planet radius, R_s — star radius
