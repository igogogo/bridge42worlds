entropy

Entropy is a measure of disorder. Imagine a child's room: when toys are neatly arranged on shelves, entropy is low. But after a fun game, everything is scattered, and you can only restore order by making an effort. In the Universe, entropy always increases.
💡 If you shuffle a deck of cards, you will never return it to its original order just by shaking. That's entropy in action - disorder wins.
Entropy is a physical quantity that shows how much energy in a system is "devalued" and cannot be used for work. Like water flowing down a mountain loses its ability to turn a mill, heat dissipates, increasing entropy. In an isolated system, entropy only increases.

History

The concept of entropy was introduced by Rudolf Clausius in 1865, generalizing the second law of thermodynamics. Ludwig Boltzmann gave a statistical interpretation: entropy is related to the number of ways particles can be organized. James Maxwell proposed a thought experiment with a "demon" sorting molecules, which deepened the understanding of the connection between entropy and information. In the 20th century, Jacob Bekenstein and Stephen Hawking discovered that black holes have enormous entropy related to their area.

How it works

At the microscopic level, entropy grows because there are many more disorder states than order ones. Toss a handful of coins: almost never will all land heads up, because there are unimaginably many ways to scatter them mixed up. Similarly, gas molecules tend to fill the entire volume - that's the most probable, high-entropy state.

Entropy S is a thermodynamic state function characterizing the measure of irreversible energy dissipation. In statistical physics, it is defined as S = k ln W, where W is the number of microstates corresponding to a given macrostate. The second law of thermodynamics states that in an isolated system, entropy does not decrease. Entropy is also a measure of missing information about a system and underlies information theory. In black hole physics, Bekenstein-Hawking entropy links the entropy of the event horizon to its area, uniting thermodynamics, gravity, and quantum theory.

History

Rudolf Clausius introduced the term "entropy" in 1865, formulating the second law. Ludwig Boltzmann in the 1870s linked entropy with probability, proposing the formula S = k log W. The thought experiment of "Maxwell's demon" (1867) stimulated research into the connection between entropy and information, culminating in the works of Leo Szilard (1929) and Shannon (1948). In the 1970s, Jacob Bekenstein suggested that black holes have entropy proportional to the horizon area, and Stephen Hawking in 1974 confirmed this by discovering Hawking radiation and derived the exact formula.

How it works

According to the second law of thermodynamics, the total entropy of a closed system grows, approaching a maximum. This is due to the statistical nature of molecular motion: the system evolves from less probable states to more probable ones. Entropy is related to free energy: F = U - TS. In quantum theory, von Neumann entropy S(ρ) = -Tr(ρ ln ρ) generalizes the classical concept. For black holes, the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy S_BH = (k A)/(4 l_P²), where A is the horizon area, and l_P is the Planck length. This formula hints at the holographic principle.
