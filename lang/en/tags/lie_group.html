Lie Group

📊 0 articles
A Lie group is a mathematical concept describing symmetries that can be smoothly changed, for example, rotations of a Rubik's cube or the spinning of a wheel.
💡 Lie groups are used in particle physics to describe symmetries that determine which particles can interact.
A Lie group is a mathematical structure that is simultaneously a group (a set with an operation satisfying axioms) and a smooth manifold. This means that the elements of the group can be continuously deformed. Examples: the rotation group SO(3) (rotations in three-dimensional space) or the Lorentz group (spacetime transformations).

History

The theory of Lie groups was developed by Norwegian mathematician Sophus Lie in the late 19th century to study symmetries of differential equations. Later, Élie Cartan and Hermann Weyl developed representation theory, leading to applications in quantum mechanics and field theory.

How it works

Imagine you have a sphere and you can rotate it. All possible rotations form a Lie group: each rotation is an element, and successive rotations are the operation. Smoothness means you can rotate the sphere by any small angle, and that will be a nearby rotation. Lie groups allow studying symmetries of continuous systems.

💡 The Standard Model of particle physics is based on the gauge group SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1), which is a product of Lie groups.
A Lie group is a smooth manifold endowed with a group structure such that the group operations (multiplication and inversion) are smooth maps. Lie groups are classified by types: classical (GL(n), SL(n), O(n), SO(n), Sp(n), U(n), SU(n)) and exceptional (G2, F4, E6, E7, E8). They describe continuous symmetries and are fundamental in geometry and physics. Each Lie group has an associated Lie algebra — the tangent space at the identity, which linearizes the group.

History

Sophus Lie introduced Lie groups in the 1870s while studying continuous symmetries of differential equations. Élie Cartan in the early 20th century classified simple Lie groups and developed representation theory. Hermann Weyl applied Lie groups to quantum mechanics, showing that symmetries of atoms are described by group representations. Later, Lie groups became the foundation of gauge theories in particle physics.

How it works

A Lie group G acts on a manifold M smoothly: a map G×M→M. The Lie algebra g = T_eG is the tangent space at the identity, equipped with a Lie bracket that measures the non-commutativity of the group. The exponential map exp: g→G connects the algebra and the group. Representations of Lie groups are homomorphisms G→GL(V), allowing the study of symmetries in vector spaces.

\exp(X) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{X^n}{n!}
The exponential of an element X of the Lie algebra gives an element of the Lie group; for matrix groups, this is the usual matrix exponential.
[X, Y] = XY - YX
The Lie bracket of two elements of a Lie algebra measures their non-commutativity and defines the algebra structure.

Open problems & fun facts

Classification of all finite-dimensional representations for exceptional Lie groups.; Construction of quantum field theory with a non-trivial Lie group (e.g., SU(3) in QCD) — the confinement problem.

💡 The Standard Model of particle physics is based on the gauge group SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1), which is a product of Lie groups.
