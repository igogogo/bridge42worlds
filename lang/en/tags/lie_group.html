Lie group

📊 0 articles
A Lie group is like a round dance where the figures can change smoothly, and each figure is obtained from the previous one by a precise rule. Imagine a kaleidoscope: you turn the tube — the pattern shimmers, and all possible rotations live in a special world of symmetries that mathematicians called a Lie group.

History

Norwegian mathematician Sophus Lie created this theory in the 1870s, inspired by Galois's ideas and seeking to create for differential equations the same symmetry tool that group theory provided for algebraic equations.

How it works

Instead of studying the whole group at once, Lie looked at infinitesimal transformations near the identity — this is the tangent space with the Lie bracket operation. Like a conductor setting the tempo with a small wave of the baton, the tangent algebra determines the structure of the entire group.

A Lie group is a differentiable manifold endowed with a group structure such that the group operations of multiplication (m: G×G→G) and inversion (i: G→G) are smooth maps. The tangent space at the identity forms a Lie algebra — a vector space with a bilinear antisymmetric Lie bracket satisfying the Jacobi identity. Lie groups classify continuous symmetries and are the foundation of modern physics: from rotations in quantum mechanics (SO(3), SU(2)) to gauge theories of the Standard Model (U(1)×SU(2)×SU(3)).

History

The theory of Lie groups emerged in the works of Sophus Lie (1870s) while studying symmetries of differential equations. Later, Wilhelm Killing and Élie Cartan classified simple Lie groups. The connection to physics was established through the works of Hermann Weyl and Eugene Wigner on group representations in quantum mechanics. In the second half of the 20th century, Lie groups became the foundation of the gauge principle.

How it works

Key fact: Lie groups are locally determined by their Lie algebra via the exponential map exp: g → G. For matrix groups, exp(X) = Σ_{k=0}^∞ X^k/k!. The structure constants of the algebra determine all local properties of the group. Representation theory describes how the group acts on vector spaces: for example, SO(3) on spherical harmonics. In physics, gauge fields are connections in principal bundles with a structure group, and their curvature is the field strength (Yang-Mills equations). According to Noether's theorem, every continuous symmetry described by a Lie group leads to the conservation of some quantity.

Open problems & fun facts

Complete classification of infinite-dimensional Lie algebras; Construction of quantum groups (noncommutative deformations of universal enveloping algebras); Integrability of nonlinear systems and Kac-Moody algebras
