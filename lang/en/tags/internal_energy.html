internal energy

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

The internal energy of a body is the energy of motion and interaction of all its microparticles. It's like an invisible 'reserve of vitality' of matter, which grows upon heating and decreases upon cooling.

In practice: Understanding internal energy makes it possible to efficiently convert heat into work in engines and refrigeration units, as well as to predict chemical reactions and phase transitions.
Internal energy U is the total energy of chaotic (thermal) motion of microparticles of a system and the potential energy of their interaction. Unlike the mechanical energy of a body as a whole, internal energy does not depend on the body's speed or position in space, but is determined by its temperature, volume, and chemical composition. The first law of thermodynamics relates the change in internal energy (ΔU) to the amount of heat transferred (Q) and work done on the system (W): ΔU = Q - W.

History

The concept of internal energy was formed in the 19th century together with the science of heat — thermodynamics. Scientists like James Joule and Rudolf Clausius showed that heat is not a special substance, but a form of energy related to particle motion.

How it works

Imagine a soccer ball being inflated. By striking the ball, you compress the air inside, and the particles start moving faster — internal energy increases, the ball heats up. Heat from a campfire also makes the air molecules around it move more energetically — the air's internal energy increases, and it rises.

💡 Even ice at subzero temperature has internal energy: water molecules in the crystal lattice continue to vibrate, and only at absolute zero (−273.15 °C) does this motion theoretically cease.
Every body consists of tiny particles — molecules or atoms. They never stand still: in gases and liquids they fly freely, in solids they vibrate around their positions. The sum of the energies of all these motions (kinetic energy — the energy of motion) and interactions between particles (potential energy — the energy of position/interaction) is called internal energy. It can be changed in two ways: performing mechanical work on the body (e.g., compressing a gas) or transferring heat to it (placing it on fire).

💡 The internal energy of one liter of water at room temperature is roughly equal to the energy released by burning 10 grams of gasoline. But you cannot extract this energy 'just like that' — the second law of thermodynamics prevents it: heat spontaneously flows only from hot to cold.
In thermodynamics, internal energy is a state function determined by macroscopic parameters of the system (temperature T, volume V, number of particles N). For an ideal gas, it depends only on temperature and the number of degrees of freedom of the molecules: U = (f/2) N k_B T, where f is the number of degrees of freedom, k_B is the Boltzmann constant, T is the absolute temperature. In real systems, a contribution from intermolecular interactions is added, which leads, for example, to the existence of latent heat of phase transitions. In statistical mechanics, internal energy is expressed via the partition function: U = - ∂(ln Z)/∂β, where β = 1/(k_B T).

History

The idea that heat is related to particle motion dates back to the work of Benjamin Thompson (Count Rumford) at the end of the 18th century, but quantitative description began in the 19th century. James Joule in 1843 experimentally proved the equivalence of mechanical work and heat. Rudolf Clausius introduced the concept of internal energy in 1850, formulating the first law of thermodynamics. Ludwig Boltzmann developed a statistical interpretation, linking internal energy to the microscopic motion of particles. James Clerk Maxwell and others developed the kinetic theory of gases, which gave an explicit form of the dependence of internal energy on temperature.

How it works

The change in internal energy of a system occurs according to the first law of thermodynamics: ΔU = Q - W, where Q is heat transferred to the system, W is work done by the system on the external environment. For an ideal gas, internal energy increases with temperature: faster-moving molecules have greater kinetic energy. During phase transitions (melting, vaporization), energy is spent on overcoming intermolecular forces, not on increasing temperature — this is how latent heat arises. In the thermodynamics of non-equilibrium processes, the local change in internal energy is described by the heat conduction equation. Closely related concepts: entropy (measure of disorder), enthalpy (U + pV), free energy. Laws: first law of thermodynamics, second law of thermodynamics (establishing the direction of processes).

\Delta U = Q - W
ΔU — change in internal energy of the system, Q — amount of heat transferred to the system, W — work done by the system on the surroundings.
U = \frac{f}{2} N k_B T
U — internal energy, f — number of degrees of freedom of one molecule (for a monatomic gas f=3), N — number of gas particles, k_B — Boltzmann constant (k_B ≈ 1.38×10^{-23} J/K), T — absolute temperature in kelvins.

Open problems & fun facts

Exact calculation of internal energy for strongly interacting many-particle systems (e.g., liquids, solids with defects) is a complex theoretical task.; Reconciliation of the second law of thermodynamics with the reversibility of microscopic mechanical laws (Loschmidt's paradox).; Behavior of internal energy in extreme conditions (ultra-high pressures, strong fields) — quantum and relativistic effects must be taken into account.
