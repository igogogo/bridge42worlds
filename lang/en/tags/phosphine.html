Phosphine

📊 0 articles
Phosphine (PH₃) is a colorless, toxic gas with a smell of garlic or rotting fish. In nature, it forms under oxygen-free conditions, such as in swamps or during organic decomposition. In everyday life, it can be imagined as a 'chemical ghost' — invisible, but its presence is revealed by a sharp odor and the ability to glow in the dark upon contact with oxygen.
💡 Phosphine is so toxic that during World War I it was considered as a possible chemical weapon, but was rejected due to its high flammability.

History

Phosphine was first obtained in 1783 by French chemist Philippe Gengembre, but systematically studied later. In 2020, astronomers reported the detection of phosphine in Venus's atmosphere, sparking debates about possible biological sources, as on Earth phosphine is often associated with microbes. However, subsequent studies have cast doubt on this discovery.

How it works

Phosphine forms during the decomposition of organic matter without access to oxygen — for example, in swamps or animal intestines. Industrially, it is produced from white phosphorus and alkali. Like matches that ignite by friction, phosphine is highly flammable in air, and when burned, it emits a characteristic green light.
