Momentum is a vector quantity equal to the product of a body's mass and its velocity. It indicates how difficult it is to stop a moving body. Imagine catching a tennis ball and a cannonball at the same speed: the cannonball has much greater momentum and will knock you off your feet.

Momentum is a vector quantity equal to the product of a body's mass and its velocity. It indicates how difficult it is to stop a moving body. Imagine catching a tennis ball and a cannonball at the same speed: the cannonball has much greater momentum and will knock you off your feet.

History

The concept of momentum was introduced by René Descartes, but the law of conservation of momentum was formulated by Newton. Huygens and other scientists developed the theory of elastic collisions. In quantum mechanics, momentum plays a key role in Heisenberg's uncertainty principle.

How it works

In a closed system, the total momentum of all bodies is conserved. For example, when firing a rifle, the bullet moves forward and the rifle recoils backward — the momenta are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. This also explains how rockets move in space: by expelling gas backward, they gain forward momentum.

\vec{p} = m \vec{v}
Momentum equals mass times velocity.
\sum \vec{p}_{\text{before}} = \sum \vec{p}_{\text{after}}
The total momentum of a closed system does not change.
