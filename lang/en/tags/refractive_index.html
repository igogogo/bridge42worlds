refractive index

📊 0 articles

Light changes speed in different media, causing its path to bend — like a runner turning from asphalt onto sand.

In practice: Without accounting for refractive index, eyeglass lenses or camera objectives would not focus light, and fiber optic internet would be impossible.
The refractive index (n) is a dimensionless quantity equal to the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum (c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s) to its phase velocity in a given medium. When light transitions between media with different n, it undergoes refraction according to Snell's law: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂. The phenomenon is explained by the electromagnetic nature of light: the interaction of the wave with the electrons of the material slows its propagation.
💡 Diamond has n ≈ 2.42 — one of the highest among natural materials, so cut diamonds sparkle so intensely: light reflects many times inside the stone due to total internal reflection.
The refractive index tells how many times slower light is in a substance than in a vacuum (emptiness). When light enters water from air, it is as if stepping on sticky tape — slows down and bends. That's why a straw in a glass looks broken.

History

The law of refraction was discovered by the Dutchman Willebrord Snell in 1621, but for a long time the nature of light remained a mystery.

How it works

Light is a wave. In a dense medium (e.g., glass), the wave has a harder time 'pushing through' between atoms, so it slows down. Due to the speed difference at the interface, the wave changes direction — like a line of soldiers marching from hard ground onto a plowed field: the end file slows down first, and the whole line turns.

💡 In some transparent materials, such as Teflon, n is very close to 1.3 — like water, so they are almost invisible in benzene (n ≈ 1.50) — they become 'invisible' due to matching refractive indices.
The refractive index (n) is a fundamental optical characteristic of a medium, determining the phase velocity of electromagnetic waves. It depends on the wave frequency (dispersion), temperature, and density of the material. In anisotropic media (e.g., crystals), the refractive index differs for different polarization directions (birefringence). In metamaterials, negative n is possible, leading to inverse refraction.

History

The law of refraction was empirically discovered by Willebrord Snell (1621) and later mathematically formulated by René Descartes. Isaac Newton and Christiaan Huygens debated the nature of light, explaining refraction with corpuscular or wave theories, respectively. In the 19th century, James Clerk Maxwell derived the electromagnetic theory of light, linking the refractive index to dielectric and magnetic permittivities: n ≈ √εᵣμᵣ (for non-magnetic media n ≈ √εᵣ). This allowed quantitative prediction of n from the electrical properties of materials.

How it works

Light is an electromagnetic wave. The electric field of the wave polarizes the atoms of the medium, i.e., displaces electron shells relative to nuclei, creating oscillating dipoles. These dipoles re-emit secondary waves that interfere with the original wave, slowing its propagation. On a microscopic level, the refractive index is related to molecular polarizability through the Clausius–Mossotti equation. Dispersion (dependence of n on wavelength) is explained by resonant absorption near natural frequencies of electronic transitions; far from resonances, n decreases smoothly with increasing wavelength (normal dispersion), described by the Cauchy formula.

n = \frac{c}{v}
n — refractive index, dimensionless; c — speed of light in vacuum, c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s; v — phase velocity of light in the medium, m/s.
n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2
n_1, n_2 — refractive indices of the first and second media; θ_1 — angle of incidence, θ_2 — angle of refraction (measured from the normal to the interface).
