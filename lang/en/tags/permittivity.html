dielectric permittivity

📊 0 articles

Dielectric permittivity is a measure of how much a medium weakens an electric field, like a sponge absorbing water: the substance 'absorbs' the tension, preventing charges from feeling each other.

In practice: A capacitor in electronics stores charge better the higher the dielectric permittivity of the insulator between the plates; without it, smartphones would be the size of a suitcase.
Dielectric permittivity (ε) characterizes the ability of a medium to polarize under an electric field and weaken it. In vacuum, the electric constant ε₀ ≈ 8.85·10⁻¹² F/m. The relative permittivity εᵣ = ε/ε₀ shows how many times weaker the interaction of charges in the medium is compared to vacuum. For water, εᵣ ≈ 80, which explains water's high dissolving power for ionic compounds.
💡 Thanks to the huge dielectric permittivity of water (about 80 at room temperature), table salt readily dissolves into ions — water seems to pull the ions apart, weakening their bond by a factor of 80.
Dielectric permittivity shows how many times weaker the electric force is in a substance than in a vacuum. If two charges are placed in oil, they attract or repel each other more weakly than in air, because the oil molecules polarize (turn, forming mini-dipoles) and partially screen the field. It's like a crowd dampening a rumor by passing it inaccurately.

History

The concept arose from Michael Faraday's experiments in the 1830s, who noticed that more charge accumulated between the plates of a capacitor with a dielectric. The term 'dielectric permittivity' was introduced by William Thomson (Lord Kelvin). Later, James Clerk Maxwell included it in his equations, linking it with the refractive index.

How it works

An electric field tends to align molecules of the substance, especially polar ones (e.g., water), into chains along the field. These aligned molecules create their own weak field directed opposite to the external field, weakening it. The easier the molecules turn, the stronger the weakening, i.e., the higher the permittivity.

💡 Some ferroelectrics (e.g., barium titanate) have εᵣ up to 10000, but when heated above the Curie point, they abruptly lose this ability due to a phase transition.
Dielectric permittivity is a tensor quantity in anisotropic media and a function of frequency in alternating fields (ε(ω)). Complex dielectric permittivity ε* = ε' + iε'' accounts for energy losses: the imaginary part ε'' describes absorption. In classical electrodynamics, polarization is described by the polarization vector P = ε₀χₑE, where χₑ is the dielectric susceptibility, and εᵣ = 1 + χₑ. At high frequencies, ionic and dipolar polarization mechanisms 'lag behind' the field, and εᵣ decreases, exhibiting frequency dispersion (Debye relaxation). In the quantum picture, interband transitions and excitons determine the dielectric response.

History

Ideas about dielectric permittivity date back to Henry Cavendish's experiments (late 18th century) and systematic studies by Michael Faraday (1837), who introduced the concept of 'specific inductive capacity'. The term 'dielectric permittivity' was proposed by William Thomson in 1845. James Clerk Maxwell (1862) mathematically linked ε and the speed of light c = 1/√(ε₀μ₀), demonstrating the electromagnetic nature of light. Later developments in quantum theory of polarizability (by Hans Bethe, Lars Onsager) allowed computation of ε from molecular properties.

How it works

An applied electric field E causes displacement of charges in the dielectric: electron shells deform (electronic polarization), ionic lattices shift (ionic polarization), permanent dipoles orient (orientational polarization). Each mechanism creates a dipole moment, and the macroscopic polarization P is the sum of moments per unit volume. Dielectric permittivity ε relates electric displacement D = ε₀E + P = ε₀εᵣE. In alternating fields, a phase shift occurs between displacement and field, leading to dielectric losses. The Lorentz oscillator model or the Debye relaxation model is used for description.

\varepsilon_r = \frac{\varepsilon}{\varepsilon_0}
εᵣ — relative permittivity, dimensionless; ε — absolute permittivity of the medium, F/m; ε₀ — electric constant, ε₀ ≈ 8.854×10⁻¹² F/m.
\mathbf{D} = \varepsilon_0 \mathbf{E} + \mathbf{P} = \varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r \mathbf{E}
