Large Hadron Collider

📊 0 articles
The LHC is a huge underground particle accelerator that collides protons to find out what matter is made of.
💡 The LHC accelerates protons to 99.9999991% of the speed of light, making 11,245 revolutions per second.
The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) is the world's most powerful particle accelerator, located in a 27-kilometer tunnel under the border of France and Switzerland. It collides beams of protons or ions at energies up to 13 TeV, creating conditions similar to those just after the Big Bang. Detectors (ATLAS, CMS, ALICE, LHCb) record the collision products, allowing the study of fundamental particles and forces.

History

The idea of the LHC arose in the 1980s, construction began in 1998. The first beam was launched on September 10, 2008, but nine days later an accident occurred. Full operation began in 2010. In 2012, the Higgs boson was discovered, confirming the prediction of Peter Higgs and others.

How it works

Protons are accelerated in a chain of accelerators (linear accelerator, booster, PS, SPS) and injected into the main LHC ring. Magnets keep particles on a circular trajectory, while radiofrequency cavities accelerate them. At intersection points, beams collide, and detectors record the particles produced.

💡 The LHC uses about 9600 magnets, including superconducting ones cooled with liquid helium to 1.9 K.
The LHC is a proton-proton collider with a maximum collision energy of 14 TeV in the center-of-mass system. It is located in a 27 km tunnel at depths of 50 to 175 m. Beams consist of 2808 bunches, each containing about 1.15×10^11 protons. Luminosity reaches 2×10^34 cm^-2 s^-1. Main detectors: ATLAS and CMS (general purpose), ALICE (for quark-gluon plasma studies), and LHCb (b-quark physics). The LHC also accelerates lead ions for nuclear collision studies.

History

The concept of the LHC was proposed in 1984 at a conference in Lausanne. Construction was approved in 1994 and began in 1998. The first circulating beam was obtained on September 10, 2008, but on September 19 an accident occurred due to a faulty electrical connection, causing a helium leak. Repairs took 14 months. From 2010 to 2013, the LHC operated at 7–8 TeV, discovering the Higgs boson. After a shutdown for upgrade (LS1), operation resumed in 2015 at 13 TeV. In 2023, Run 3 began at 13.6 TeV. An upgrade to the High-Luminosity LHC is planned by 2029.

How it works

Protons from a hydrogen source pass through the linear accelerator LINAC2 (50 MeV), then the booster (1.4 GeV), PS (25 GeV), and SPS (450 GeV), after which they are injected into the main LHC ring. The ring has 1232 dipole magnets creating a field of 8.3 T to keep particles on track, and 392 quadrupole magnets to focus the beam. Acceleration is provided by 16 radiofrequency cavities (400 MHz). When protons collide, energy is released and converted into new particles according to E=mc^2. Detectors record tracks, energy, and momentum of particles, allowing their identification.

E = \gamma m c^2
E is total energy, γ is the relativistic factor, m is rest mass, c is speed of light.

Open problems & fun facts

The search for new physics beyond the Standard Model (supersymmetry, dark matter) has so far yielded no results.; Energy limitation due to ring size and magnetic field; a more powerful collider (FCC) is needed.
