Quark-Gluon Plasma

📊 0 articles
A state of matter in which quarks and gluons, normally confined inside protons and neutrons, become free. This is the hottest and densest matter, existing microseconds after the Big Bang. Imagine ice melting into water — similarly, hadrons 'melt' into plasma at extreme temperatures.
💡 Quark-gluon plasma has the lowest viscosity among known liquids, close to the theoretical limit.
A state of matter in which quarks and gluons, normally confined inside protons and neutrons, become free. This is the hottest and densest matter, existing microseconds after the Big Bang. Imagine ice melting into water — similarly, hadrons 'melt' into plasma at extreme temperatures.

History

The existence of quark-gluon plasma was predicted in the 1970s. In the 2000s, experimental evidence was obtained at the RHIC collider at Brookhaven and the Large Hadron Collider at CERN. The substance behaves as a nearly perfect fluid with very low viscosity.

How it works

At temperatures above 2 trillion degrees Celsius (100,000 times hotter than the center of the Sun), protons and neutrons 'melt', releasing quarks and gluons. Analogy: heating ice first melts it, then water evaporates — similarly, nuclear matter transitions to plasma.
