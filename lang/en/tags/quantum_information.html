quantum information

Quantum information is bits that can be zero and one at the same time, opening a new dimension for computing.

In practice: Quantum computers crack modern ciphers in minutes, and in the future will be able to design drugs by simulating molecules.
Quantum information studies methods of encoding, transmitting, and processing data using quantum systems. Unlike classical information, quantum information cannot be arbitrarily copied (no-cloning theorem) and can be compressed in new ways. The main resources are superposition and entanglement. They allow the implementation of algorithms that have no classical analogs in efficiency, for example, integer factorization (Shor's algorithm) and database search (Grover's algorithm). Quantum entanglement also provides absolutely secure key distribution (BB84 protocol).

History

The idea of quantum computing was put forward in the 1980s: Richard Feynman realized that quantum systems are hard to simulate on classical computers, and David Deutsch built the first model of a universal quantum machine.

How it works

A qubit is, for example, an atom that can be excited by a laser. The laser puts it into a superposition state (a mixture of unexcited and excited). Two atoms are entangled through interaction. Then a system of many qubits evolves according to the algorithm, and at the end a measurement yields the answer. Importantly, the computation process itself is a careful manipulation of quantum interference, like tuning a musical instrument.

💡 The no-cloning theorem states that it is impossible to create an identical copy of an arbitrary unknown quantum state — nature sets a limit on copying.
An ordinary computer stores information in bits — like a switch: on (1) or off (0). A quantum computer uses qubits (quantum bits), which can be like a coin in flight — simultaneously heads and tails. Moreover, multiple qubits become entangled (linked so that the state of one instantly determines the state of the other), allowing many possibilities to be explored in parallel. It is reminiscent of divination from a book: the classical method is to turn pages one by one, while the quantum method offers a chance to read all pages at once, though you can only obtain one.

Quantum information theory formalizes the concept of information encoded in quantum states. A qubit is a two-dimensional quantum system whose state is described by a vector in Hilbert space: |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩, with |α|²+|β|²=1. Multi-qubit systems exhibit an exponential growth in the dimensionality of the state space, which underlies the speedup of quantum computing. Key resources: entangled states (e.g., Bell states), quantum channels, and information measures (von Neumann entropy). Protocols have been developed: quantum key distribution, superdense coding (2 bits via 1 qubit), and quantum teleportation. The no-cloning theorem protects quantum information. Practical implementations face the problem of decoherence, for which quantum error correction codes are being developed.

History

The origin of the field is associated with the analysis of the EPR paradox and Bell's inequalities (1964), which pointed to the nonclassical nature of quantum correlations. In 1981, Feynman proposed using quantum systems to simulate physics. David Deutsch in 1985 described a universal quantum computer and introduced the concept of quantum parallelism. The explosion of interest was sparked by Shor's algorithm (1994) for factorization and the discovery of quantum error-correcting codes (Shor, Steane, 1995–96). Since the 2000s, experiments on ion traps and superconducting circuits have begun.

How it works

A quantum computer operates on the state of a register of N qubits, which is a superposition of 2^N basis states. The computation is specified by unitary evolution (quantum gates) that uses interference to amplify the amplitudes of correct answers and suppress incorrect ones. The states are measured at the end, yielding a result with a probability given by the Born rule. Entanglement provides nonlocal correlations, allowing, for example, in teleportation to transfer a quantum state using a previously distributed entangled pair and two classical bits. The superposition principle and interference are the basis for the speedup. The difficulty is that interaction with the environment causes decoherence, destroying quantum properties.

|\psi\rangle = \alpha |0\rangle + \beta |1\rangle
|ψ⟩ — state vector of a qubit, |0⟩ and |1⟩ — orthonormal basis states, α and β — complex amplitudes satisfying the normalization condition |α|² + |β|² = 1.
