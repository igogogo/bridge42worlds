crystal lattice

📊 0 articles
In a crystal, all particles—atoms, molecules, or ions—are arranged like rows of oranges on a fruit stand. Each orange sits exactly in its place, and this repeats layer after layer. Thanks to this strict order, crystals form smooth faces and sharp angles, like salt cubes or hexagonal snowflakes.
💡 Snowflakes grow from tiny ice crystals, and their hexagonal symmetry is embedded in how water molecules connect in the lattice. Although all snowflakes share common features, each is unique due to different growth conditions.
A crystal lattice is a three-dimensional 'packing' of atoms, ions, or molecules that repeats in all directions. Imagine honeycombs: they consist of identical hexagonal cells, and if bees built them infinitely small and in three layers, you'd get a lattice similar to a magnesium crystal.

History

The idea that crystals have a regular internal structure was already suggested by Robert Hooke in the 17th century while examining them under a microscope. The decisive moment came in 1912 when Max von Laue predicted and obtained X-ray diffraction from crystals, and William and Lawrence Bragg derived a simple equation for deciphering the lattice, for which they received the Nobel Prize in 1915.

How it works

When water evaporates from a salty solution, sodium and chloride ions stop floating separately and line up in a strict checkerboard order, but in volume. Each sodium ion is surrounded by six chloride ions, and vice versa—this way the electric forces balance each other, and we get the familiar salt cube.

💡 The repeating block of a lattice is called the unit cell. There are approximately 10¹⁸ such cells in a grain of salt. If a cell were enlarged to the size of a cherry pit, the grain would become a cube with a side over a hundred meters.
A crystal lattice is an infinite periodic structure defined by elementary translation vectors. Formally, it is described as a set of points (nodes) with an attached basis—a group of atoms repeated under all translations of the form R = n₁a + n₂b + n₃c, where a, b, c are basis vectors. The symmetry of the lattice is classified by the 14 Bravais lattices and 230 space groups. Periodicity determines diffraction patterns, phonon spectrum, and electronic band structure.

History

Robert Hooke in Micrographia (1665) first suggested that the shape of crystals reflects an ordered arrangement of particles. In the early 19th century, René Just Haüy formulated the law of rational indices, and Auguste Bravais in 1848 derived 14 lattice types. The decisive breakthrough was made by Max von Laue in 1912, proposing X-ray diffraction from crystals. William Henry Bragg and his son William Lawrence Bragg developed a method for analyzing diffraction peaks (Bragg's law) and in 1913 determined the structure of NaCl. This opened the era of structural crystallography.

How it works

The equilibrium configuration of atoms in a lattice minimizes free energy. In ionic crystals, electrostatic attraction (Coulomb's law) and short-range repulsion upon overlapping electron shells dominate. For covalent crystals (silicon, diamond), directional bonds dictate complex structures with tetrahedral coordination. The periodic potential of the lattice leads to the formation of a band structure (Bloch theorem), determining the properties of semiconductors. Diffraction of X-rays, neutrons, or electrons is described by Bragg's law nλ = 2d sin θ, where d is the interplanar spacing.

n\lambda = 2d \sin\theta
n is an integer (diffraction order), λ is the radiation wavelength, d is the spacing between atomic planes, θ is the glancing angle (between the incident beam and the plane)

Open problems & fun facts

Prediction of crystal structure from chemical composition (CSP—Crystal Structure Prediction problem) without empirical data; Nature of high-temperature superconductivity in cuprates and its connection to lattice dynamics; Complete control of defects in low-dimensional materials for quantum technologies; Creation of programmable three-dimensional crystals with specified geometry for nanoelectronics and metamaterials
