polarimetry

📊 5 articles

A lie detector for light. When light reflects or scatters, its invisible vibrations acquire a preferred direction — polarization, which tells about the shape of particles, magnetic fields, and even dark matter.

In practice: In industry, polarimetry determines sugar concentration in solutions (saccharimetry). In astronomy, it reveals dust in exoplanet atmospheres and magnetic fields of stars.
Polarimetry is a technique for measuring the polarization state of electromagnetic radiation. Light with a preferred orientation of the electric field is called polarized. The degree of polarization (from 0 to 1) and the angle of orientation provide information about the radiation sources and the propagation medium. Linear polarization arises from scattering by fine dust, reflection; circular polarization arises when passing through a medium with optical activity or anisotropic magnetic fields. In astronomical observations, polarizers and modulators, often with an analyzer, are used to construct polarization maps.
💡 Some scarab beetles see circular polarization of the sky, which helps them navigate even when the Sun is hidden by clouds.
Light is a wave (a propagating oscillation of the electromagnetic field) that oscillates perpendicular to the direction of motion. Ordinary light oscillates in all transverse directions randomly. Polarized light oscillates only in one plane, like a rope being shaken up and down. Polarimetry is the measurement of the direction and degree of this orderliness. It helps to know what the light has passed through: for example, reflection from a water surface makes it horizontally polarized, so polarizing glasses remove glare.

History

Étienne Malus discovered polarization in 1808 while looking at light reflected from a window of the Luxembourg Palace through a crystal of Icelandic spar. Later, James Maxwell and others linked polarization to electromagnetic theory.

How it works

Just as a slot in a fence allows only a rope oscillating along the slot to pass, a polarizer (a special filter) transmits light with only one direction of oscillation. By the change in intensity after passing through a rotating analyzer, the degree of polarization is determined.

💡 Iceland spar (calcite) crystals split the image due to double refraction — it was historically the first polarization analyzer, used by Malus.
Polarimetry is a set of methods for measuring the polarization of light. The polarization state is described by the Stokes vector (I, Q, U, V), where I is the total intensity, Q and U are linear polarization, V is circular. The measurement consists in determining these parameters by passing radiation through polarizers (analyzers) and phase plates (quarter-wave, half-wave) with subsequent intensity recording. In physical problems, polarimetry provides information about the asymmetry of the emitting or scattering medium: synchrotron radiation in magnetic fields is linearly polarized, Mie scattering by interstellar dust polarizes starlight, the Zeeman effect creates circular polarization in spectral lines in magnetic fields. Accuracy reaches fractions of a percent. It is used in remote sensing, astrophysics (protoplanetary disks, polars, jets), and atmospheric physics.

History

Étienne Malus in 1808 described the phenomenon he called polarization, observing reflected light through a calcite crystal. David Brewster discovered the law of the angle of complete polarization upon reflection (1815). Augustin Fresnel and Dominique Arago investigated the interference of polarized light. James Clerk Maxwell in 1865, while formulating Maxwell's equations, theoretically described electromagnetic waves and included polarization as a vector property. William Lawrence Bragg developed X-ray polarimetry. In astronomy, pioneers were Bernard Lyot (inventor of the polarizing filter for the coronagraph) and William Hiltner, who detected interstellar polarization in 1949.

How it works

Light passes through an optical path with polarization elements: first, phase-sensitive modulators (e.g., Pockels cell or rotating half-wave plate) change the polarization state, then an analyzer (polarizer) transmits the component along its axis, and a photodetector records the signal. Modulation allows separating the Stokes parameters by phase. In radio astronomy, polarization is measured by correlating orthogonally polarized channels. The degree of polarization p = sqrt(Q^2+U^2+V^2)/I. Key effects: electron scattering (linear polarization), synchrotron radiation (polarization direction perpendicular to the magnetic field), Zeeman effect (circular polarization in spectral lines), reflection from a surface (Brewster's angle). The principle of superposition and statistical signal processing are at the core.

\tan \theta_B = \frac{n_2}{n_1}
θ_B — Brewster's angle (angle of incidence), n_1 — refractive index of the medium from which light falls, n_2 — refractive index of the medium into which light enters
I = I_0 \cos^2 \phi
I — intensity of light after the polarizer, I_0 — initial intensity of polarized light entering the polarizer, φ — angle between the polarization direction of the incoming light and the transmission axis of the polarizer
