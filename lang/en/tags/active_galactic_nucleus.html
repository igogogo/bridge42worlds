active galactic nucleus

25

A cosmic beacon smaller than the Solar System, outshining the light of its entire galaxy. A supermassive black hole sucks in matter and accelerates it to near-light speeds, turning it into a glowing disk and jets.

In practice: The jets of active nuclei are natural particle accelerators, helping to study high-energy physics. Their radiation is used to calibrate space navigation systems.
An active galactic nucleus is the central region where a supermassive black hole (with a mass of millions to billions of Suns) actively consumes matter. The infalling gas forms an accretion disk, in which viscous friction converts gravitational energy into heat and radiation spanning the entire spectrum from radio to gamma rays. Powerful magnetic fields twist some of the matter into collimated jets ejected at relativistic speeds. Various types are distinguished: quasars, blazars, Seyfert galaxies — depending on the line-of-sight orientation and power.

History

The first active nucleus was noticed in 1908 as a strange bright center of a galaxy, but its nature was understood later. In 1963, astronomer Maarten Schmidt showed that quasars are distant active nuclei.

How it works

Like a whirlpool in a bathtub: water (gas) swirls around the drain (black hole), accelerates, and creates noise and splashes (radiation and jets).

💡 Some active nuclei change brightness in minutes, even though their size is like the Solar System — meaning the energy is released from a region smaller than a light-day.
At the center of almost every large galaxy hides a supermassive black hole (an object with gravity so strong that even light cannot escape). When there is a lot of gas and dust around it, they swirl into an accretion disk (a spiral of infalling matter). Friction heats the disk to millions of degrees, and it begins to shine brighter than all the stars of the galaxy combined. Often, two narrow beams — jets (streams of particles) — burst from the center, shooting out for thousands of light-years. Such a nucleus is called active.

An active galactic nucleus (AGN) is a compact region at the center of a galaxy emitting anomalously high power (up to 10^46 erg/s) across a wide range of wavelengths. The energy is released by accretion of matter onto a supermassive black hole (10^6–10^9 M☉). The infalling gas with angular momentum forms an accretion disk, where magnetorotational instability provides viscosity. The radiation is produced from gravitational potential energy: the efficiency of mass-to-energy conversion reaches 10–40%, far higher than nuclear fusion. Relativistic jets are often observed, formed by the Blandford–Znajek mechanism or through electromagnetic extraction of the hole's rotational energy. The AGN spectrum contains both thermal (disk) and non-thermal (non-thermal jet emission, synchrotron, inverse Compton) components. Classification is based on orientation, the presence of broad emission lines, and radio power. A unified scheme explains the differences between types mainly by the orientation of a dust torus that hides some regions.

History

In 1908, Edward Fath discovered emission lines in the nucleus of galaxy M87, but recognition came later. Carl Seyfert in 1943 systematized galaxies with bright nuclei. Maarten Schmidt in 1963 interpreted the spectrum of quasar 3C 273 as highly redshifted, proving its extragalactic nature and enormous luminosity. Yakov Zeldovich and Edwin Salpeter independently proposed accretion onto a black hole as the energy source. The modern picture emerged in the 1970s–80s thanks to X-ray observations and the development of disk and jet models.

How it works

Accretion of matter onto a black hole through a disk, where gravitational energy converts into heat and radiation. Some of the energy can be extracted from the hole's rotation via the Blandford–Znajek mechanism (an electromagnetic process involving the ergosphere), producing jets. The unified model assumes the central source is surrounded by a dust torus; the visible type depends on whether we see the disk directly (Seyfert I) or through the torus (Seyfert II). The beam velocity of jets is close to the speed of light, explaining superluminal motions due to relativistic aberration. Principles: accretion follows the equations of hydrodynamics and magnetohydrodynamics, radiation follows the laws of thermal radiation and synchrotron mechanism, relativistic radiation is affected by special relativity effects.
