gravitational potential

📊 0 articles
Imagine a huge trampoline with a heavy ball placed on it. The closer to the ball, the more the trampoline sags. Gravitational potential is a measure of how "deeply" you are sunk into this dip. Massive objects create a deep pit, while light ones create a barely noticeable one.
💡 If the Earth were compressed to the size of a walnut, its gravitational potential would become so deep that even light could not escape — a tiny black hole would form.
This is the energy required to pull an object out of the gravitational field, divided by its mass. In other words, how strongly gravity "holds" an object at a given point. Analogy: to lift a stone from a deep well requires more work than from a shallow one — the depth of the well is analogous to the potential.

History

The concept arose from Newton's law of universal gravitation. The potential simplified force calculations. In the mid-19th century, Gauss generalized it, and in the 21st century, the potential helps search for dark matter through gravitational lensing.

How it works

Around any body there is an invisible "potential well". Falling into it, an object accelerates, like a snowboarder on a steep slope. The more massive the body and the closer to it, the steeper the slope. In general relativity, this well is the curvature of spacetime.

💡 Due to the difference in gravitational potential, time at sea level flows slightly slower than in the mountains. On GPS satellites, this must be constantly corrected, otherwise navigation would be off by tens of meters per day.
Gravitational potential φ is a scalar function whose gradient equals the acceleration of free fall. In Newtonian mechanics, φ satisfies the Poisson equation Δφ = 4πGρ. In GR, the analog is the metric components g_{00} ≈ - (1 + 2φ/c²).

History

Isaac Newton introduced the concept of gravitational force, but the potential was later formulated by Lagrange and Laplace. Poisson's equation united the potential with mass distribution. Karl Schwarzschild in 1916 found an exact solution to Einstein's equations for a spherical mass, where the potential transitions into the Schwarzschild metric.

How it works

Gravitational potential determines how bodies move and light propagates. In general relativity, a potential difference causes gravitational redshift: light loses energy as it climbs out of the "well", expressed mathematically as Δλ/λ = Δφ/c². This effect has been verified with high precision.

\varphi = -\frac{GM}{r}
φ — gravitational potential, G — gravitational constant, G ≈ 6.674×10⁻¹¹ m³/(kg·s²), M — mass of the body, r — distance from the center of the body
\Delta \varphi = 4\pi G \rho
Δ — Laplace operator, φ — gravitational potential, G — gravitational constant, G ≈ 6.674×10⁻¹¹ m³/(kg·s²), ρ — density of matter

Open problems & fun facts

The nonlocality of gravitational energy in GR makes it difficult to define potential for strong fields.; The potential does not explain galaxy rotation curves without introducing dark matter.; Singularities inside black holes indicate the limits of the classical description of potential.
