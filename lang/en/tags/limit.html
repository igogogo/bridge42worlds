limit

📊 0 articles

A limit is the direction of mathematics' gaze toward a point toward which a function tends but does not have to reach. Like approaching the horizon: no matter how far you go, it recedes, yet its position is clearly defined.

In practice: Without the limit, it is impossible to calculate the instantaneous speed of a car from the change in its coordinates—GPS navigators and autopilots rely on this concept constantly.
The limit of a function f(x) as x approaches a equals L if for any positive ε one can point to a neighborhood of the point a such that the values f(x) differ from L by no more than ε whenever x falls into that neighborhood (except possibly the point a itself). The key point: we do not require that f(a) exists or equals L. The limit describes only the tendency, not the arrival.
💡 Long before formalization, Zeno of Elea formulated the paradox of Achilles and the tortoise: Achilles will never catch the tortoise because each time he reduces the distance by a fraction. The modern explanation of the paradox lies precisely in the concept of a limit: the sum of infinitely many segments can give a finite time.
A limit describes the behavior of a function when its argument (input) gets arbitrarily close to some number. It's like pouring water into a glass: the level approaches the brim but never reaches it unless the water overflows. In mathematics, a limit is a precise number that the function values approach, even if the function itself is not defined at that point or equals something else.

History

Intuitively, limits were used already in Ancient Greece: Archimedes' method of exhaustion calculated areas of curvilinear figures. The rigorous epsilon-delta definition of a limit appeared in the 19th century thanks to Augustin-Louis Cauchy and Karl Weierstrass.

How it works

Imagine a receding staircase: each step is half the length of the previous one. You will never touch the far wall, but with each step the distance to it becomes smaller than any predetermined number. Mathematically, this is a limit—a guaranteed closeness without the need for touching.

💡 The limit of the sequence (1 + 1/n)^n as n→∞ equals the number e ≈ 2.71828—the base of natural logarithms. This same number is the only one for which the derivative of the exponential function a^x is a^x.
A fundamental concept of mathematical analysis, underlying continuity, derivatives, integrals. One says that the limit of a function f as x approaches c equals L ( lim_{x→c} f(x)=L ) if for every ε>0 there exists δ>0 such that 0<|x−c|<δ implies |f(x)−L|<ε. This definition excludes the point c from consideration, emphasizing the local nature of the limit. A limit can be one-sided (from the left or right), infinite (x→∞ or the value tends to infinity), or not exist at all. It is generalized to metric and topological spaces via the concepts of direction and filter.

History

The intuitive concept of a limit appeared in antiquity in the works of Eudoxus and Archimedes (method of exhaustion). In the 17th century, Newton and Leibniz used infinitesimals ('fluxions' and 'differentials') to create calculus, but were criticized for lack of rigor. Augustin-Louis Cauchy in 1821 proposed a definition in the ε-δ language, later refined by Weierstrass. This ended the era of infinitesimals and provided a foundation for modern analysis.

How it works

The classical ε-δ definition formalizes the intuition of 'approaching without reaching'. In practice, limits are computed using algebraic manipulations, the squeeze theorem (two policemen theorem), L'Hôpital's rule (for indeterminate forms), or asymptotic estimates. Limits are indispensable in defining continuity (f is continuous at c if lim_{x→c} f(x)=f(c)), the derivative ( lim_{h→0} (f(x+h)−f(x))/h ), and the Riemann integral ( lim_{n→∞} Σ f(x_i^*)Δx ). In functional analysis, the limit is generalized to normed spaces.

\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L \iff \forall \varepsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0 : 0 < |x - c| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon
ε is an arbitrarily small positive number, the required precision of the approximation of the function values to L; δ is a positive number that defines a neighborhood of the point c in which the function values must be ε-close to L (excluding the point c itself); f is the function; c is the point towards which the argument tends; L is the limit value; the symbols ∀ and ∃ are the universal and existential quantifiers, respectively.
e = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n
e is Euler's number, irrational; n is a natural number increasing without bound; the limit of the sequence defined by the expression (1+1/n)^n.
