Hubble Space Telescope

25
The Hubble Space Telescope is a large camera orbiting Earth. It looks at stars and galaxies without the interference of our atmosphere, so the images are incredibly clear, as if we are in space ourselves.

History

The telescope is named after Edwin Hubble, the astronomer who proved the expansion of the Universe. The project began in the 1970s, and launch took place on April 24, 1990, aboard the Space Shuttle Discovery. The first images were blurry due to a mirror defect, but in 1993 astronauts installed corrective optics, and the telescope began working at full capacity.

How it works

Hubble uses a system of mirrors to focus light and digital sensors similar to camera matrices. It orbits Earth at about 27,000 km/h, completing a full orbit every 97 minutes. Solar panels power its instruments, and gyroscopes help it precisely point at targets, like a ship holding its course at sea.

💡 Hubble doesn't take pictures in color like our cameras; it captures light at specific wavelengths in black and white. Then scientists colorize the images so we can see space in all its glory.

The Hubble Space Telescope (HST) is an automated observatory in low Earth orbit, a joint project of NASA and ESA. It is equipped with a 2.4 m primary mirror and a suite of scientific instruments: spectrographs, wide-field cameras operating in ultraviolet, visible, and infrared ranges. The absence of atmospheric interference provides an angular resolution of about 0.05 arcseconds. The use of CCD matrices allows detection of objects down to 31st magnitude. High-resolution spectrographs provide key data on the chemical composition, temperature, and motion of celestial bodies through spectral line analysis.

History

The concept of a space telescope had been discussed since the 1940s, but full design began in the 1970s. Launch took place on April 24, 1990; spherical aberration of the primary mirror was soon discovered and corrected by servicing mission STS-61 in 1993. A total of five servicing missions were conducted (the last in 2009), upgrading instruments. The telescope is named after Edwin Hubble, whose work on redshift and Hubble's law laid the foundations of modern cosmology.

How it works

The Ritchey–Chrétien telescope collects light with a primary hyperbolic mirror and directs it to a secondary mirror, then to instruments. Stabilization is achieved with gyroscopes and magnetometers, precise pointing with guide star sensors. Scientific instruments include the Wide Field Camera 3, Cosmic Origins Spectrograph, and others. Data are transmitted via radio link through TDRS satellites. Hubble's law (v = H₀d) was empirically refined thanks to Hubble's observations of distant supernovae, leading to the discovery of accelerated expansion of the Universe. Gravitational lensing captured by the telescope has allowed mapping of dark matter in galaxy clusters. Transit spectroscopy of exoplanet atmospheres has revealed lines of water, methane, and other molecules.
