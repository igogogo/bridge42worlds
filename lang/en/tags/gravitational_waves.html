gravitational waves

📊 199 articles
Gravitational waves are like ripples on water from a thrown stone, only they travel through the entire Universe. When two huge black holes collide, they shake space-time itself, and this shudder spreads in all directions. We don't feel it, but special instruments can catch this 'tremor', just as a seismograph catches an earthquake.

Imagine that space is a stretched trampoline, and planets and stars are balls warping it. Gravitational waves are waves running across this 'fabric' when massive objects suddenly change their motion. They were predicted by Einstein as ripples in the fabric of spacetime. These waves carry information about the most powerful events in the cosmos: mergers of black holes and neutron stars.

History

The idea of gravitational waves dates back to Henri Poincaré, who proposed their existence in 1905. Albert Einstein derived them from general relativity in 1916 but doubted their reality for a long time. The first attempts at experimental detection were made by Joseph Weber in the 1960s using aluminum cylinders. The real breakthrough came with the direct observation in 2015 by the LIGO detectors.

How it works

Gravitational waves compress and stretch space in perpendicular directions, as if you were squeezing and stretching a rubber band. LIGO detectors use laser beams traveling along 4-kilometer arms. When a wave passes, the length of the arms changes ever so slightly — less than the diameter of a proton! This change is captured by an interferometer.

Gravitational waves are disturbances in the curvature of spacetime, propagating at the speed of light and described within the framework of Einstein's general relativity (GR). Their sources are the accelerated motion of massive bodies with a variable quadrupole moment: mergers of compact objects (black holes, neutron stars), supernova explosions, rotating neutron stars with deformations ('mountains'), and cosmological processes. The wave amplitude is characterized by a dimensionless strain h ≈ 10⁻²¹ for typical events. LIGO and Virgo detectors use Michelson laser interferometers with suspended mirrors to measure phase difference changes. Gravitational-wave astronomy has opened a new window on the Universe, allowing us to study black hole properties, the expansion rate of the Universe, and fundamental laws of physics.

History

Henri Poincaré, in his 1905 work 'On the Dynamics of the Electron', first suggested the existence of gravitational waves propagating at the speed of light. Albert Einstein, in 1916–1918, derived the linearized approximation of GR and obtained solutions in the form of plane gravitational waves. Their reality was long disputed due to the difficulty of separating physical effects from coordinate effects. In 1957, at the Chapel Hill conference, it was proved that gravitational waves carry energy. Joseph Weber, in the 1960s, built the first detectors based on massive bars and claimed to have detected events in 1969, but the results were not confirmed. The development of laser interferometers began in the 1970s. On September 14, 2015, the LIGO collaboration (USA) announced the direct observation of gravitational waves from the merger of two black holes (event GW150914). In 2017, the merger of two neutron stars was recorded, accompanied by electromagnetic signals.

How it works

According to Einstein's equations, accelerating masses with a non-zero quadrupole moment radiate gravitational waves. They are transverse waves with two polarizations ('plus' and 'cross'), propagating at the speed of light. Passing through a detector, the wave causes a relative change in the arm length of an interferometer: ΔL/L = h(t)/2. In a Michelson interferometer, a laser beam is split and travels along the arms, then interferes. The phase difference change is proportional to h. Sensitivity is limited by quantum and seismic noise. Matched filters and numerically calculated signal templates are used to extract signals. A black hole merger is described in three stages: inspiral, merger, ringdown. Analysis of the waveform yields masses, spins, and distance. In cosmology, gravitational waves allow testing models of the early Universe (primordial gravitational waves) through B-mode polarization of the cosmic microwave background.

E \sim \frac{G M^2}{R}
E — radiated energy, M — mass of system, R — characteristic radius (e.g., gravitational radius)
v = H_0 d
