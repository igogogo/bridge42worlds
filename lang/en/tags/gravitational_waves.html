Gravitational Waves

Ripples in spacetime propagating at the speed of light, generated by the acceleration of massive objects, such as merging black holes or neutron stars. It is like waves on water from a thrown stone, but instead of water, it is the fabric of the universe itself.

History

Albert Einstein predicted gravitational waves in 1916 as a consequence of general relativity. For a long time, their existence was questioned. Indirect evidence came from observations of a binary pulsar by Russell Hulse and Joseph Taylor (Nobel Prize 1993). Direct detection occurred on September 14, 2015, at the LIGO observatory (Nobel Prize 2017).

How it works

Gravitational waves arise from asymmetric acceleration of masses. They stretch and compress space perpendicular to the direction of propagation. LIGO detectors are giant interferometers: a laser beam is split, travels along 4 km arms, reflects, and interferes. The passage of a wave changes the arm lengths, which is registered.
