Wave-Particle Duality

📊 0 articles
The property of quantum objects to exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties depending on the experiment. Light can behave as a wave (interference) and as a stream of particles (photoelectric effect). It's as if a person were simultaneously a liquid and a solid depending on the situation.

History

Christiaan Huygens (wave theory of light) and Isaac Newton (corpuscular theory) debated the nature of light. In 1905, Einstein explained the photoelectric effect by introducing photons. Louis de Broglie (1924) proposed that particles also have wave properties. Davisson and Germer's experiments (1927) confirmed the wave nature of electrons.

How it works

In the double-slit experiment, electrons fired one by one create an interference pattern, like waves. But if a detector is placed, they behave like particles, passing through one slit. The choice of measurement determines which property appears. This is not a compromise but complementarity: both wave and particle are two sides of the same coin.
