wave-particle duality

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
14
Light is a universal actor: in the role of a wave, it bends around obstacles and paints rainbow rings; in the role of a particle, it knocks electrons out of metal, giving rise to an electric current. Any particle, even an electron or a massive molecule, possesses both roles.

History

Louis de Broglie proposed in 1924 that electrons and other particles have a wavelength related to their momentum. The idea was quickly confirmed by experiments: electron beams produced the same interference pattern as light waves. Thus quantum mechanics gained its central principle.

How it works

In the famous double-slit experiment, single electrons launched one by one still form bands on the screen—a typical wave pattern. But if you place a detector to find out which slit the electron went through, the magic disappears: each electron behaves like a tiny bullet, leaving only a single spot.

💡 Scientists passed huge fullerene molecules (almost a millimeter in size) through two slits and obtained interference patterns—the molecules behaved like waves, even though you could touch them.
Wave-particle duality is the two-faced nature of the quantum world: the same object shows particle properties in one experiment and wave properties in another. It's like a person who, depending on the situation, acts as a strict boss or a caring parent, but remains the same person.

Wave-particle duality is a fundamental property of matter and radiation, according to which all objects exhibit both wave and particle characteristics depending on the experimental conditions. Wave behavior is described by the de Broglie wavelength λ = h/p, particle behavior by energy E = hν and momentum p = h/λ. Bohr's complementarity principle states that these two descriptions are mutually exclusive in a single experiment but equally necessary for a complete picture.

History

Louis de Broglie put forward a revolutionary hypothesis in 1924 about the wave properties of matter, assigning a wavelength λ = h/p to any moving particle. Erwin Schrödinger in 1926 built on this by creating wave mechanics, describing particles as waves distributed in space. The experiments of Davisson and Germer (1927) and independently G.P. Thomson with electrons confirmed the prediction, demonstrating electron diffraction by crystals.

How it works

The duality is quantitatively expressed through Heisenberg's uncertainty principle: Δx·Δp ≥ ħ/2. The more precisely the particle characteristic (momentum) is measured, the more blurred the wave characteristic (position) becomes. The wave function ψ(x) contains complete information about the system, and its squared modulus gives the probability of finding the particle at a point. The Schrödinger equation governs the evolution of the wave pattern, and measurement collapses it into a localized particle.

\lambda = \frac{h}{p}
λ is the de Broglie wavelength (m); h is Planck's constant, h ≈ 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s; p is the momentum of the particle (kg·m/s). In the non-relativistic case, p = mv (m is rest mass, v is velocity). For particles moving at speeds close to the speed of light, p = γmv, where γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²) is the Lorentz factor, c is the speed of light, c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s.

Open problems & fun facts

Why do quantum objects exhibit duality, but macroscopic ones do not? (the decoherence problem); Are there hidden variables that predetermine particle or wave behavior?; What is the physical nature of the wave function?
