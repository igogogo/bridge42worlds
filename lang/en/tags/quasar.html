Quasar

📊 0 articles
An extremely bright active galactic nucleus powered by a supermassive black hole. Quasars are the brightest objects in the universe, outshining entire galaxies. Imagine a spotlight with the power of a trillion suns shining from the center of a distant city.
💡 Light from the brightest quasar 3C 273 takes 2.4 billion years to reach us, so we see it as it was when the first multicellular organisms appeared on Earth.
An extremely bright active galactic nucleus powered by a supermassive black hole. Quasars are the brightest objects in the universe, outshining entire galaxies. Imagine a spotlight with the power of a trillion suns shining from the center of a distant city.

History

The first quasars were discovered in the 1960s as star-like radio sources. In 1963, Maarten Schmidt determined that the spectrum of quasar 3C 273 had a huge redshift, indicating its colossal distance. This was a sensation: such bright objects must be incredibly powerful.

How it works

A supermassive black hole at the center of a galaxy attracts gas and dust, forming an accretion disk. As matter falls in, it accelerates to near-light speeds, friction heats it to millions of degrees, and it emits enormous energy, including X-rays. Analogy: it's like a waterfall where water falls from a great height and splashes, creating bright sprays of light.
