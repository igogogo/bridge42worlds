knots

📊 0 articles

A knot in mathematics is a closed loop that can be deformed arbitrarily without tearing, like a rope with joined ends.

In practice: Knots help understand how DNA and proteins are twisted, and can become a basis for super-secure quantum computing.
Knot theory classifies embeddings of a circle into three-dimensional space up to isotopy (continuous deformation without self-intersections). The key task is to find invariants that distinguish non-equivalent knots. Among them are the Alexander polynomial (1923) and the Jones polynomial (1984), which brought the author the Fields Medal. Knots naturally arise in physics: as stable field configurations (e.g., magnetic field lines or cosmic strings), and in (2+1)-dimensional systems — as world lines of anyons.
💡 There exists a knot that cannot be untied, but if you copy its mirror image and join them together, they annihilate like a particle with an antiparticle.
A piece of rope with joined ends is a mathematical knot. It can be stretched, compressed, twisted, but you cannot cut or glue parts. If one shape can be turned into another without scissors, the knots are considered the same. Knot theory is a part of topology (the branch of mathematics about properties of figures that are preserved under continuous deformations, like a rubber ball turning into a cube).

History

Knot theory emerged in the 19th century when physicist Lord Kelvin hypothesized that atoms are vortex knots in the ether. Mathematician Peter Guthrie Tait began compiling tables of knots. Later, Henri Poincaré gave a rigorous topological definition on which modern science is built.

How it works

To distinguish one knot from another, invariants are devised — characteristics that do not change under bending. For example, the minimum number of crossings: the trefoil has three, the figure-eight has four. An invariant is like a unique passport number of the knot.

💡 The Jones polynomial allows distinguishing left-handed and right-handed trefoils, which is impossible with the Alexander polynomial.
A knot in mathematics is an equivalence class of embeddings of the circle S¹ into three-dimensional Euclidean space R³ (or the sphere S³) under ambient isotopy. The unknotted circle is considered trivial. Knots that are the same under transformations are called equivalent. Classification of knots is one of the central problems of low-dimensional topology. Knot theory is closely related to braids, links, and three-dimensional manifolds, plays a role in quantum field theory, statistical mechanics, and quantum computing (topological quantum computers use anyons whose world lines form knots).

History

The birth of knot theory is associated with the hypothesis of William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) in 1867, who proposed that chemical elements are knots in the ether. Peter Guthrie Tait began compiling tables of knots, classifying all knots up to 7 crossings. With the discovery of the electron and the abandonment of the ether, knot theory receded into the background, but at the beginning of the 20th century, through the efforts of Henri Poincaré and other topologists, it received a rigorous mathematical foundation. In the second half of the 20th century, a renaissance occurred thanks to the discovery of the Jones polynomial (Vaughan Jones, 1984) and connections with quantum physics, the works of Edward Witten and others.

How it works

To classify knots, invariants are used — quantities that do not change under isotopies. The simplest is the number of crossings in a knot diagram. More complex ones include the Alexander polynomial, the Jones polynomial (related to braid group representations and quantum invariants), Vassiliev invariants. The Jones polynomial is computed via skein relations: it is recursively determined through the relationship of knot diagrams that differ only in a small neighborhood of a crossing. In physics, topological knots arise as stable solutions in field theories; for example, in Maxwell's equations there exist electromagnetic knots whose stability is guaranteed by the Hopf topological invariant. World lines of particles in (2+1)-dimensional systems can form knots, leading to fractional statistics.

t^{-1} V(L_+) - t V(L_-) = (t^{1/2} - t^{-1/2}) V(L_0)
V is the Jones polynomial, L_+, L_-, L_0 are knot diagrams differing in a small region around a crossing (positive crossing, negative, smoothed), t is a formal variable.

Open problems & fun facts

Complete classification of knots: there is no complete set of invariants that distinguishes any two non-equivalent knots.; Connection of quantum invariants with three-dimensional quantum gravity.; Efficient computation of invariants for complex knots, including cosmic and chromodynamic strings.
