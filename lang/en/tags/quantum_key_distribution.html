quantum key distribution

📊 0 articles

Quantum key — a sealed envelope with wax: any attempt to open it leaves a trace. The sender and receiver do not exchange a secret, but create it on the fly in the light of photons.

In practice: Protection of communication channels over distances of up to hundreds of kilometers: banking transactions, government lines, and the future quantum internet are built on invulnerable key distribution.
Quantum key distribution uses the properties of single photons: measuring incompatible bases (for example, horizontal-vertical and diagonal polarizations) inevitably disturbs the state. The BB84 protocol generates a raw key, then through privacy amplification and error correction, the final shared key is extracted, whose security is guaranteed by fundamental laws, not computational complexity.
💡 The record length of a terrestrial quantum key distribution link is over 500 kilometers without repeaters, achieved using ultra-sensitive detectors and quantum memory.
Alice sends Bob photons with different polarization orientations (like rotating sunglasses). Bob measures them, and then they compare over an open phone line which glasses he was wearing. The matches become the secret key. Eve, trying to eavesdrop, will inevitably alter some photons — and she will be detected by the increased error rate.

History

The first quantum key distribution protocol — BB84 — was proposed by Charles Bennett and Gilles Brassard in 1984. Later, schemes based on entangled photons appeared, strengthening security.

How it works

It's like two tin cans connected by a string: if someone presses their ear, the tension changes. Key photons are transmitted via optical fiber or even through the air, and any eavesdropping introduces noise, so the line is constantly checked for above-normal noise.

💡 The quantum satellite 'Micius' established an intercontinental key between China and Austria over a distance of 7600 km, using entanglement teleportation — an effectively instantaneous correlation even through space.
Quantum key distribution is a physical-layer cryptographic protocol in which a secret key is generated by measuring the quantum states of single-photon pulses or through correlations of entangled pairs. Security is based on Heisenberg's uncertainty principle and the no-cloning theorem: any eavesdropping attempt alters the error statistics. Classical phases over a public channel — basis sifting, estimation of the quantum bit error rate (QBER), error correction, and privacy amplification — transform partially correlated bit strings into an ideal key.

History

The concept of quantum cryptography was proposed by Stephen Wiesner in the 1970s, but the first implementable scheme — BB84 — was formulated by Bennett and Brassard in 1984. Experimental demonstrations in free space and over fiber began in the 1990s. Alain Aspect and his colleagues proved the violation of Bell's inequalities, confirming the inevitability of correlations in entangled states, which formed the basis of protocols using entangled photons (E91, BBM92). Today, QKD is a mature technology with commercial systems and satellite communication (Micius).

How it works

In BB84, Alice encodes bits in polarization states +/+ (0°/90°) or ×/× (45°/135°), randomly choosing the basis. Bob measures in a random basis. After transmission, they agree over a public channel on which shots the bases matched, forming a sifted key. Resulting errors are estimated as QBER. If QBER is below the threshold (about 11%), an error correction code and a hash function for privacy amplification are applied, eliminating Eve's potential information. Security is proven by reducing to the monotonicity of von Neumann entropy and the non-increase of quantum mutual information.

Open problems & fun facts

Realistic devices suffer from side-channel attacks (laser blinding, detector imperfections); Quantum repeaters with quantum memory are needed for a global network but are not yet developed; Integration with existing infrastructure and security certification
