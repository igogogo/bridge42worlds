stellar evolution

📊 3 articles

Stellar life — gravitational compression versus nuclear expansion. Born from gas, they shine for billions of years and die as white dwarfs, neutron stars, or black holes.

In practice: Carbon and oxygen in our bodies are the ashes of stellar fires; knowing evolution, we predict the future of the Sun and search for planets around old stars.
The evolution of a star is a sequence of states under the action of gravity and thermonuclear reactions. On the main sequence (where the star spends ~90% of its life), hydrogen burns in the core. After its exhaustion, the core contracts, the outer layers expand, and the star becomes a red giant. Stars with masses up to ~8 solar masses shed their envelope as a planetary nebula and leave a white dwarf. More massive ones reach iron synthesis, after which they collapse and explode as type II supernovae, giving rise to a neutron star or black hole.
💡 The Sun loses 240 million tons of mass every minute, converting it into light, but over its entire life it will use only 0.7% of its substance.
A star is a ball of gas where gravity pulls everything to the center, and the internal pressure of hot gas (plasma) pushes outward. In the core, at tremendous temperature, nuclear fusion reactions occur: hydrogen turns into helium and releases energy, preventing the star from contracting. When the fuel runs out, the equilibrium collapses — the star swells into a red giant, then sheds its envelope, leaving a white dwarf, or explodes as a supernova.

History

In the early 20th century, astronomers (Hertzsprung, Russell) constructed the color-magnitude diagram and noticed that stars of different types lie in certain regions. By the 1950s, physicists explained these sequences as stages of stellar life.

How it works

Like in a campfire: as long as there is wood (hydrogen), the flame is steady. When hydrogen in the core runs out, the core contracts and heats up, and the envelope expands — the star bloats. Then mass decides the fate: light stars quietly fade away, heavy ones end in a grand explosion.

💡 White dwarfs with mass close to the Chandrasekhar limit can explode as type Ia supernovae — and these explosions serve as 'standard candles' for measuring cosmological distances.
Stellar evolution is the process of changing structure and luminosity of a star under the influence of its own gravity and nuclear reactions. It begins with the collapse of fragments of a molecular cloud; the protostar heats up until hydrogen ignites in the core (PP-chain or CNO cycle). On the main sequence, hydrostatic equilibrium operates. After hydrogen in the core burns out, it contracts, and the envelope expands — the star enters the red giant stage. A helium flash initiates helium burning, then carbon, etc., up to iron in massive stars. Final stages are determined by mass: white dwarfs (mass < Chandrasekhar limit ~1.44 M☉), neutron stars (core-collapse supernova) or black holes. Mass loss, rotation, and binarity play important roles.

History

The idea of a nuclear source of stellar energy was proposed by Eddington in the 1920s. In 1938, Bethe and Weizsäcker described hydrogen fusion chains. Chandrasekhar in 1931 calculated the white dwarf mass limit (Chandrasekhar limit). Hoyle in the 1940s–50s showed how elements heavier than hydrogen are synthesized in stars, and Burbidge et al. (B²FH, 1957) presented a nucleosynthesis scheme. Modern numerical evolution models consider thousands of reactions.

How it works

The main equation of hydrostatic equilibrium: dP/dr = –G M(r) ρ / r², where P is pressure, M(r) is the mass inside radius r. Thermonuclear reactions provide energy according to the law ε ~ ε₀ ρ T^n. Evolution is traced numerically considering the equation of state (ideal gas, degenerate electron gas, etc.) and radiation transfer. The key parameter is mass. Stars with mass above ~0.4 M☉ go through the red giant stage. The Chandrasekhar limit restricts the white dwarf mass; exceeding it may lead to collapse into a neutron star.

\frac{dP}{dr} = -\frac{G M(r) \rho}{r^2}
P — pressure, r — distance from the center, G — gravitational constant (≈ 6.674×10⁻¹¹ m³ kg⁻¹ s⁻²), M(r) — mass enclosed inside the sphere of radius r, ρ — density
M_{\text{ch}} \approx 1.44 \, M_{\odot}
M_ch — maximum mass at which the pressure of degenerate electrons can support the star; M⊙ — solar mass ≈ 2×10^30 kg
