cosmic rays

📊 2
Cosmic rays are an invisible shower of particles from deep space that passes through your body every second. They are born in the most powerful explosions in the universe and travel to us like messages from the stars.

History

In 1912, Victor Hess ascended in a balloon with an electroscope and discovered that radiation increases with altitude. Thus cosmic rays—visitors from beyond Earth—were discovered.

How it works

Upon reaching the atmosphere, a particle strikes an atomic nucleus, triggering a cascade of secondary particles—an extensive air shower. Like a billiard ball breaking a rack, it creates an avalanche of millions of 'fragments' that reach the surface.

💡 The most energetic cosmic ray particle, the 'Oh-My-God particle', had the energy of a baseball thrown by a champion. Such a tiny speck could light a bulb for a second.
Like an invisible rain of tiny bullets, cosmic rays constantly bombard the Earth. Their sources are accelerators on a cosmic scale: supernova explosions and the vicinity of black holes.

Cosmic rays are a flux of relativistic particles and atomic nuclei coming from outside the Solar System. They include protons (about 90%), helium nuclei, heavier elements, electrons, and gamma rays. Their energies range from ~10^9 eV to a record 3×10^20 eV, and the spectrum follows a power law with features (the 'knee' and the 'ankle'). Acceleration occurs in astrophysical shock waves (supernova remnants, jets of active nuclei) via the Fermi mechanism.

History

In 1912, the Austrian Victor Hess proved in a balloon that the ionizing radiation had an extraterrestrial origin. In the 1930s, at the Pic du Midi, Pierre Auger discovered extensive air showers—cascades of secondary particles. Fritz Zwicky suggested that supernova explosions are the sources of the particles. In the 1960s, Arno Penzias, while working on antennas for studying cosmic rays, accidentally discovered the cosmic microwave background.

How it works

Particles are accelerated in shock waves of supernova remnants (first-order Fermi mechanism). Magnetic clouds scatter particles, causing them to cross the shock front repeatedly and gain energy. Upon collision with the atmosphere, a cascade is initiated: a proton produces pions, which produce muons, electrons, and photons. Detectors such as the Pierre Auger Observatory register either particles at ground level or the fluorescent glow of the air. Ultra-high-energy rays lose energy through photopion production when interacting with the cosmic microwave background—this creates the Greisen–Zatsepin–Kuzmin limit.

\frac{dN}{dE} = k E^{-\alpha}
dN/dE is the particle flux per unit energy interval, E is the particle energy, k is a normalization constant, α is the spectral index (α ≈ 2.7 for energies up to the knee, around 3×10^15 eV).
