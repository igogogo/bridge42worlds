Combinatorics

📊 0 articles
Combinatorics is the art of counting how many ways one can choose, arrange, or combine objects.
💡 The number of ways to shuffle a deck of 52 cards is greater than the number of atoms on Earth.
Combinatorics studies finite sets and methods of counting them. Main problems: how many subsets, permutations, combinations exist. For example, the number of ways to choose 3 books out of 10 is the number of combinations C(10,3)=120.

History

The foundations of combinatorics were laid by Blaise Pascal and Pierre de Fermat in the 17th century while studying gambling games.

How it works

Formulas are used: number of permutations of n elements = n!, number of combinations of k out of n = n!/(k!(n-k)!). These numbers appear in Pascal's triangle.

💡 Combinatorics underlies probability theory: the probability of an event is the ratio of favorable outcomes to total outcomes.
Combinatorics is a branch of mathematics that studies discrete structures and methods of enumerating them. It includes enumerative combinatorics (formulas for the number of combinations), extremal combinatorics (maximum sizes of families with given properties), and combinatorial geometry. Main objects: permutations, combinations, partitions, graphs. Important results: inclusion-exclusion principle, binomial theorem, Catalan numbers, Ramsey's theorem.

History

Blaise Pascal and Pierre de Fermat in 1654 laid the foundations of probability theory and combinatorics through correspondence. Jacob Bernoulli in 'Ars Conjectandi' (1713) systematized combinatorial rules. Leonhard Euler introduced generating functions. In the 20th century, combinatorics developed thanks to Paul Erdős, who founded extremal combinatorics and Ramsey theory.

How it works

Combinatorial problems are solved using formulas and methods: sum and product rules, permutations, arrangements, combinations. Generating functions encode sequences of numbers. Recurrence relations (e.g., Fibonacci numbers) often arise in combinatorics. The pigeonhole principle and double counting are also used.

C(n,k) = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}
The number of ways to choose k elements from n without regard to order.

Open problems & fun facts

Erdős–Szekeres conjecture on monotone subsequences; Problem of existence of finite projective planes for non-prime powers
