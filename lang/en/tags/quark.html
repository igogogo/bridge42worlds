Quarks are tiny bricks that make up everything around us. Like flour in dough, they come together to create protons and neutrons – the building blocks of atoms.

History

The idea of quarks was born in 1964 when American physicist Murray Gell-Mann (and independently George Zweig) proposed that all nuclear particles consist of smaller objects with fractional electric charge.

How it works

Quarks interact via the strong force by exchanging gluons. It's like a team ping-pong game: quarks toss 'balls' – gluons – back and forth and thereby hold together inside hadrons.

Quarks are elementary particles with half-integer spin (fermions), possessing color charge and participating in all fundamental interactions. In the Standard Model, quarks are grouped into three generations: (u, d), (c, s), (t, b). Their electric charges are fractional: +2/3 e for up-type, −1/3 e for down-type. Due to confinement, quarks are not observed free but exist only inside colorless hadrons – mesons (quark-antiquark) and baryons (three quarks).

History

The quark model was proposed in 1964 by Murray Gell-Mann and George Zweig to classify hadrons. Initially, quarks were considered a mathematical abstraction, but deep inelastic scattering experiments of electrons on protons in 1968–1973 confirmed their reality. The discovery of the J/ψ meson in 1974 (charm quark) and the Υ meson in 1977 (bottom quark) strengthened the model. The last, the top quark, was discovered in 1995 at Fermilab. The development of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) explained the dynamics of quarks through gluon exchange and the phenomenon of asymptotic freedom.

How it works

Quarks interact through the strong nuclear force, described by QCD. Each quark carries one of three color charges (red, green, blue), and the interaction is mediated by eight gluons, which themselves carry color. Due to asymptotic freedom, at short distances quarks behave almost like free particles, but as distance increases, the force field forms a 'string' with a linearly rising potential. This leads to confinement: an attempt to break the bond creates a quark-antiquark pair, and instead of a lone quark, new hadrons are born. The properties of quarks are described within Yang-Mills theory with the gauge group SU(3).
