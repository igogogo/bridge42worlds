In practice: It helps discover pulsars, capture the echo of the Big Bang, and see through dust clouds.
Radio astronomy explores the Universe in the radio wave range (wavelengths from fractions of a millimeter to tens of meters). Radio telescopes consist of an antenna and a sensitive receiver, often combined into interferometers to achieve high angular resolution. Key sources: synchrotron radiation from relativistic electrons in magnetic fields (supernova remnants, active galactic nuclei), spectral lines of hydrogen (21 cm) and molecules.

History

In 1932, Karl Jansky accidentally discovered a radio signal from the center of the Galaxy, and since then astronomers have been building radio telescopes.

How it works

Giant dish antennas, like ears, catch cosmic radio echoes, amplify them, and translate them into images—where it's brighter, the source is stronger.

💡 Jansky's first radio telescope was built from wood and wire, while today the largest radio telescope, FAST in China, has a diameter of 500 meters. It is so sensitive that it picks up signals from the edge of the observable universe.

Radio astronomy is a branch of astronomy that studies electromagnetic radiation from celestial bodies with wavelengths from ≈1 mm (submillimeter) to tens of meters (decameter). It is characterized by the use of antenna arrays and aperture synthesis methods to obtain radio images with resolution equivalent to a telescope with a diameter equal to the interferometer baseline. Receivers are cooled to cryogenic temperatures to reduce noise. Main mechanisms of radio emission: thermal (bremsstrahlung), cyclotron and synchrotron, maser, and spectral lines (HI 21 cm, CO, OH, etc.). Radio interferometry has achieved sub-millisecond resolution (VLBI networks), enabling study of galactic nuclei structure and measurement of pulsar parallaxes.

History

Radio astronomy was born in 1932 when Karl Jansky discovered radio emission from the Milky Way. In the 1940s, Grote Reber built the first parabolic radio telescope and mapped the sky. After World War II, rapid development led to the discovery of discrete sources, the synchrotron mechanism, and the 21 cm line (Ewen and Purcell, 1951). In the 1960s, Jocelyn Bell Burnell discovered pulsars, and Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson discovered the cosmic microwave background. Modern networks (ALMA, SKA) continue the tradition.

How it works

An antenna collects radio waves and focuses them onto a feed, where the signal is converted into an electric current. After amplification and frequency conversion, power is measured. In an interferometer, signals from multiple antennas are correlated, forming a visibility function. Inverse Fourier transformation using the Stokes theorem reconstructs the image. Angular resolution is determined by the diffraction limit θ ≈ λ/D. Autocorrelation and acousto-optic spectrometers are used for spectroscopy.

\theta \approx \frac{\lambda}{D}
θ — angular resolution (radians), λ — observation wavelength (m), D — antenna diameter or interferometer baseline (m)

Open problems & fun facts

Achieving angular resolution comparable to optical (requires intercontinental baselines); Protection from radio frequency interference of man-made origin; Development of efficient antennas for decameter waves
