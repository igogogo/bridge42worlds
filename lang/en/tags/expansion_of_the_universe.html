expansion of the universe

📊 153 articles
The universe expands like raisin dough in the oven: each raisin galaxy moves away from the others, and the farther away, the faster. The dough itself is space, which is getting bigger.
💡 Light from the most distant galaxies has traveled to us for billions of years, and during that time, space has stretched so much that the light has become invisible to the eye—turning into microwave radiation.
The expansion of the Universe is an observed phenomenon: galaxies move away from each other, and light from them stretches, becoming redder. Just as an inflating balloon with drawn dots moves them apart, space itself grows in size.

History

In 1927, Belgian priest and astrophysicist Georges Lemaître first suggested expansion based on Einstein's equations. In 1929, Edwin Hubble confirmed it, discovering the proportionality between the distance to galaxies and their recessional velocity (redshift).

How it works

If you throw a stone into a pond, ripples spread out at the same speed. But the expansion of the Universe is different: space between galaxies grows over time, and the speed increases with distance according to a law similar to the rule: twice as far—twice as fast receding.

💡 The most distant objects in the Universe are receding from us faster than the speed of light—this does not violate relativity, because it is space itself that moves, not objects through it.
The expansion of the Universe is a fundamental property of the Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker cosmological model, based on general relativity. Observationally, it manifests as a redshift of spectral lines from distant objects, obeying the Hubble–Lemaître law: v = H₀ D, where H₀ is the Hubble constant. Expansion means an increase in the scale factor a(t) over time, with physical distance between comoving points proportional to a(t). At the end of the 20th century, accelerated expansion was discovered, explained by dark energy or the cosmological constant Λ. The expansion has no center and occurs uniformly in all directions, being a consequence of the Big Bang about 13.8 billion years ago.

History

Theoretical foundations emerged in 1917 when Einstein added the cosmological constant for a static universe. In 1922–1924, Alexander Friedmann found non-stationary solutions to Einstein's equations, predicting expansion. In 1927, Georges Lemaître independently obtained the same solutions and compared them with Slipher's redshift data, proposing the concept of a 'primeval atom.' Edwin Hubble in 1929, based on observations by Vesto Slipher and Milton Humason, confirmed the linear relationship between redshift and distance. In 1964, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson accidentally discovered the cosmic microwave background, which became decisive evidence for a hot beginning and expansion.

How it works

The expansion is described by the FLRW metric: ds^2 = -c^2 dt^2 + a(t)^2 [dr^2/(1 - k r^2) + r^2 (dθ^2 + sin^2θ dφ^2)], where a(t) is the scale factor, k is the curvature parameter (-1, 0, +1). The evolution of a(t) is given by the Friedmann equations: (ȧ/a)^2 = (8πGρ + Λc^2)/3 - kc^2/a^2, and ä/a = -(4πG/3)(ρ + 3p/c^2) + Λc^2/3. In the current era, dark energy (Λ>0) dominates, leading to accelerated expansion. Redshift z is defined as 1+z = a(t_obs)/a(t_em).
