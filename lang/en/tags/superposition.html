Superposition

📊 0 articles
A state of a quantum system in which it simultaneously exists in all possible states until the moment of measurement. Imagine a coin spinning in the air: while it hasn't landed, it is both heads and tails. In the quantum world, a particle can be in two places at once until we look.
💡 In 1999, Anton Zeilinger's group demonstrated superposition for C60 fullerene molecules, consisting of 60 carbon atoms.

History

The concept of superposition dates back to the work of Erwin Schrödinger (1926), who formulated the wave equation. Later, Richard Feynman developed the idea in his path integrals. Superposition has been experimentally confirmed in experiments with photons and electrons.

How it works

A quantum object is described by a wave function, which is the sum of all possible states. For example, an electron in an atom can be in several orbitals simultaneously. It's like being both at the office and at the beach at the same time until a colleague calls and asks where you are.
