superposition

📊 25 articles
A spinning coin on a table is not just heads or tails—it carries both possibilities at once until it stops. An electron inside an atom is similar: it is simultaneously everywhere a little bit, and its exact location appears only at the moment of observation.
💡 A quantum computer uses superposition to try all answers at once—as if you could read all the books in a library at the same time, without opening them one by one.
Superposition is a state in which a quantum particle exists in all its possible forms at once. It's like a sounding musical chord: while it lasts, several notes live in it at once, but if you stop the sound and ask which note was last, the instrument will only show one.

History

Erwin Schrödinger gave mathematical life to superposition in 1926, writing down an equation where a cat could be both alive and dead until the box is opened. His idea grew from Louis de Broglie's earlier insight that particles behave like waves.

How it works

Before measurement, a particle resembles a smeared probability cloud. When we try to catch it, the cloud collapses into one point. An example is the double slit: a single electron flies through both at once, creating a wave pattern on the screen, but any attempt to peek destroys the pattern and leaves only a dot.

💡 Schrödinger's famous cat is not a household pet at all, but a 1935 thought experiment devised to show the absurdity of applying quantum rules to the ordinary world.
Quantum superposition is a fundamental principle by which a closed system can exist in all permissible states simultaneously. Mathematically, it is described as a linear combination of eigenstates with complex amplitudes. The measurement outcome is unpredictable but follows Born's probability rule. This property radically distinguishes the microworld from classical physics and serves as the basis for quantum computing, interference, and entanglement.

History

Erwin Schrödinger showed in 1926 that the wave function of an electron can be a superposition of several eigenstates corresponding to different energy values. Louis de Broglie had already suggested in 1924 that particles possess wave characteristics, and Max Born in 1926 gave a probabilistic interpretation of the wave function. The Schrödinger's cat paradox, proposed in 1935, still fuels debates about the boundary between the quantum and classical worlds.

How it works

The wave function ψ is a sum of possible states: |ψ⟩ = Σ c_i |φ_i⟩. The time evolution is governed by the Schrödinger equation. Upon measurement, a collapse occurs to one of the eigenstates |φ_i⟩ with probability |c_i|², and all interference patterns vanish. This principle underlies the operation of quantum logic gates and explains paradoxes such as the double-slit experiment with single particles.

|\psi\rangle = \sum_i c_i |\phi_i\rangle
|ψ⟩ is the wave function of the system; |φ_i⟩ are the eigenstates of a physical quantity; c_i are complex probability amplitudes; summation runs over all allowed states. Normalization condition: Σ_i |c_i|² = 1.
P_i = |c_i|^2
P_i is the probability of finding the system in state |φ_i⟩ upon measurement; c_i is the probability amplitude for that state, |c_i|² is the square of its modulus.
