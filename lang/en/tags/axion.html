The axion is an almost invisible particle that, like the wind, permeates everything around. It may hold the key to unraveling dark matter — the mysterious substance that binds galaxies.
💡 The name 'axion' was borrowed from the detergent brand Axion because the particle was supposed to 'wash out' one of the problems in modern physics.
The axion is a hypothetical light particle that hardly interacts with ordinary matter. Like an invisible web, it may fill the Universe, explaining the mysterious dark matter that does not emit light but manifests itself through gravity.

History

In 1977, physicists Roberto Peccei and Helen Quinn proposed a solution to the strong CP problem, and Steven Weinberg and Frank Wilczek independently realized that their theory implied the existence of a new particle — the axion.

How it works

In a strong magnetic field, axions can convert into photons, and vice versa. Experiments like the 'solar helioscope' catch axions produced in the Sun's interior, while resonant cavities try to register the 'whisper' of the axion field from space.

💡 Hypothetical axion clouds around black holes could generate powerful bursts, similar to laser pulses — this effect is called an 'axion laser'.
The axion is a hypothetical light neutral boson arising from the Peccei–Quinn mechanism to solve the strong CP problem in quantum chromodynamics. Being a pseudo-Goldstone boson, it acquires mass through instanton effects. Axions are natural candidates for cold dark matter, as they can be abundantly produced in the early Universe via the misalignment mechanism. Their interaction with ordinary matter is extremely weak, making detection difficult but rendering them ideal components of invisible mass.

History

The strong CP problem was realized after the work of Gerard 't Hooft, who showed that the QCD Lagrangian allows a CP-violating θ term, whose experimental constraint requires fine-tuning. In 1977, Roberto Peccei and Helen Quinn solved it by introducing a new global U(1) symmetry, spontaneously broken at high energies. Steven Weinberg and Frank Wilczek independently showed that this symmetry leads to a pseudoscalar — the axion. For a long time, the particle remained beyond experimental reach, until advances in detection techniques and astrophysical observations narrowed the range of its possible masses and coupling constants.

How it works

The axion couples to photons via the effective Lagrangian L ⊃ g_{aγ} a F_{μν} \tilde{F}^{μν}, which gives rise to the Primakoff effect — conversion of axions into photons in a magnetic field. In QCD, the axion field relaxes the CP-violating angle θ to zero, making it a dynamical variable. In cosmology, the axion begins to oscillate when its mass becomes comparable to the Hubble rate, after which it behaves as dust-like matter. Experiments like ADMX, CAST, and IAXO aim to detect axions through their coupling to photons, using resonant cavities, helioscopes, and strong magnets.

\mathcal{L}_{a\gamma} = g_{a\gamma} a F_{\mu\nu} \tilde{F}^{\mu\nu}
a — axion field; F_{\mu\nu} — electromagnetic field tensor; \tilde{F}^{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} F_{\rho\sigma} — dual tensor, ε — Levi-Civita symbol; g_{a\gamma} — axion-photon coupling constant.

Open problems & fun facts

The axion mass remains unknown; theoretical predictions span the range from μeV to meV, and string models allow an even wider range.; Experimental detection of QCD axions is extremely challenging due to the vanishingly small interaction probability.; The possible role of axions in the formation of large-scale structure in the Universe and their contribution to the cosmic microwave background require clarification.
