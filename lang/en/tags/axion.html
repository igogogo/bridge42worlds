axion

📊 5 articles

An elusive ultralight particle that possibly fills the Universe secretly, solving the mysteries of dark matter and matter-antimatter asymmetry.

In practice: Detecting the axion would reveal the nature of dark matter and allow the creation of super-sensitive detectors for new physics interactions.
The axion is a pseudoscalar boson arising from the Peccei-Quinn mechanism to solve the strong CP problem in quantum chromodynamics (QCD – the theory of the strong interaction). In QCD, there is a mathematical term that should violate combined parity (CP violation) and create an electric dipole moment for the neutron, but experiments show symmetry is preserved. The axion field dynamically sets this term to zero. The axion mass is inversely proportional to the symmetry-breaking energy scale and possibly lies in the microelectronvolt range. Such ultralight particles are ideal candidates for cold dark matter: they are produced non-thermally in the early Universe and practically do not interact with radiation.

History

A solution to the problem was proposed in 1977 by Roberto Peccei and Helen Quinn. The particle was later named 'axion' by Frank Wilczek and Steven Weinberg – after a laundry detergent, because it 'cleans' the theory of inconsistencies.

How it works

The symmetry required by strong interaction theory is like a bump on a smooth surface, but experiment doesn't see it. The axion acts as an invisible press, flattening this bump. Produced in the early Universe, axions would behave like a cold invisible gas, clustering around galaxies and enhancing their gravity.

💡 Axions interact so weakly with matter that they could pass through a lead wall a light-year thick without being absorbed.
The axion is a hypothetical very light particle that hardly interacts with ordinary matter. It was invented to fix a theoretical flaw in the physics of the strong interaction (the force holding nuclei together): a predicted asymmetry is not observed, and the axion field 'erases' it. If such particles were abundantly produced in the early Universe, they could make up dark matter – the invisible substance preventing galaxies from flying apart.

The axion is a pseudoscalar boson predicted by the Peccei-Quinn mechanism to solve the strong CP problem in quantum chromodynamics. The QCD Lagrangian contains a term θ (g²/32π²) G̃G, which violates CP symmetry. The experimental upper limit on the neutron electric dipole moment requires |θ| < 10⁻¹⁰, though the natural value is order unity. Introducing a global U(1)PQ symmetry, spontaneously broken at scale f_a, generates the axion field a(x). Its potential V(a) = m_a² f_a² [1 - cos(a/f_a)] after including instanton effects gives a mass m_a ≈ (f_π m_π)/f_a. For f_a ~ 10¹² GeV, the mass falls into the 10⁻⁵ eV range, making axions an excellent cold dark matter candidate. Their production is described by the misalignment scenario: the field a is displaced from the minimum after inflation, and as the Universe cools, it begins to oscillate, behaving as non-relativistic matter. The interaction with the electromagnetic field via the term g_{aγγ} a F̃F allows conversion to photons in strong magnetic fields – this principle underlies detectors like ADMX.

History

The strong CP problem was recognized in the 1970s (works by 't Hooft and others). In 1977, Roberto Peccei and Helen Quinn proposed a solution using a new symmetry. In 1978, Frank Wilczek and Steven Weinberg showed that such a model predicts a light particle, named the axion. Subsequent cosmological studies (Sikivie et al.) defined the mass window for axion dark matter. Modern experiments, such as ADMX, use microwave cavities in strong magnetic fields to search for axions.

How it works

The axion field a(x) is an angular variable corresponding to the phase of U(1) symmetry breaking. At temperatures below the QCD phase transition, a periodic potential V(a) ~ (m_a² f_a²)[1 - cos(a/f_a)] forms, giving mass m_a. In the early Universe, the field is 'frozen' at an initial misalignment angle θ, and when the expansion rate becomes comparable to the mass, the field starts oscillating, producing a density of cold dark matter Ω_a h² ~ θ² (f_a/10¹² GeV)^{7/6}. Detection is based on the inverse Primakoff effect: an axion in a strong magnetic field converts into a photon with energy equal to its total energy. The experimental setup is tuned to resonance at the frequency corresponding to the axion mass.
