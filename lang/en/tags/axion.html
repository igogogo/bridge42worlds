An elusive ghost particle: the prime candidate for dark matter, which also solves the long-standing strong interaction puzzle.

In practice: The search for axions is underway in dozens of laboratories using sensitive detectors and powerful magnets; their discovery will decipher the nature of dark matter and open the way to new physics.
The axion is a light particle predicted to solve the problem of CP violation in the strong interaction (why quarks behave symmetrically under mirror reflection). It hardly interacts with matter and could constitute cold dark matter. The search is conducted by attempting to convert an axion into a photon under the influence of a strong magnetic field.

History

In 1977, theorists Roberto Peccei and Helen Quinn proposed this particle to fix a mismatch in the theory of the force that glues protons together. Later, Frank Wilczek named it the axion after a cleaning powder that 'cleans up' the theory of the problem.

How it works

Axions, like ghosts, pass through walls and our bodies, almost without noticing ordinary matter. But if you place them in a powerful magnetic field, some of them can turn into tiny flashes of light (photons), which scientists are trying to catch with sensitive instruments.

💡 An axion could shine through a solid lead wall a light-year thick without even noticing it.
The axion is a particle invented by scientists that would eliminate a contradiction in the description of the strong nuclear interaction. It practically does not notice ordinary matter and therefore is invisible. It is assumed that axions could make up dark matter—the invisible mass that holds galaxies together.

The axion is a neutral pseudoscalar particle (spin 0) that arises as a pseudo-Goldstone boson from the breaking of the global U(1)PQ symmetry. It solves the strong CP problem: it dynamically nullifies the θ-parameter of quantum chromodynamics, explaining the absence of CP violation in the strong interaction. The axion field couples to gluons, and in the early Universe after the phase transition, it begins to oscillate, producing cold dark matter. Experiments (ADMX, CAST, ALPS) search for the conversion of axions into photons in a strong magnetic field.

History

The Peccei-Quinn mechanism was published in 1977, and soon Frank Wilczek and Steven Weinberg independently predicted a light boson. Wilczek named it the axion, by analogy with a cleaning agent that 'cleans up' QCD from the strong CP problem. In the 1980s, the first experiments were proposed, including a 'magnetic sequester.' In the 21st century, axion searches have become one of the main directions of particle physics, intertwined with astrophysics and cosmology.

How it works

The axion field a(x) interacts with electromagnetism through the term (g_{aγγ}/4) a F_{μν} ilde{F}^{μν} in the Lagrangian. In an external magnetic field, this leads to the conversion of axions into photons. Cosmologically, axions are born at the spontaneous breaking of symmetry, and their field is frozen until the moment when the mass becomes comparable to the Hubble rate; then it begins to oscillate, behaving as cold dark matter. For detection, resonance in microwave cavities is used (frequency is related to the axion mass) with sensitivity g_{aγγ} ~ 10^{-16} GeV^{-1}.

m_a \approx 6\,\mu\text{eV} \left( \frac{10^{12}\,\text{GeV}}{f_a} \right)
m_a is the axion mass, f_a is the Peccei-Quinn symmetry breaking scale (energy scale), μeV is microelectronvolt (1 μeV = 10⁻⁶ eV), GeV is gigaelectronvolt (1 GeV = 10⁹ eV). At f_a=10¹² GeV, the mass is approximately 6 μeV.
