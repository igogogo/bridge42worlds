Nitrogen

📊 1 articles
Nitrogen (N₂) is a colorless, odorless, and tasteless gas that makes up 78% of Earth's atmosphere. It is like an invisible 'background player': it does not support combustion or respiration, but is vital for plants in the form of compounds (e.g., ammonia). In everyday life, liquid nitrogen is used for freezing food, and the gas is used to create an inert atmosphere in welding.
💡 Liquid nitrogen at −196°C is used in cryotherapy to remove warts and in cooking for instant freezing of ice cream.

History

Nitrogen was discovered in 1772 by Scottish physician Daniel Rutherford, who called it 'noxious air'. Almost simultaneously, it was studied by Antoine Lavoisier and other scientists. Lavoisier proposed the name 'azote' (from Greek 'lifeless'), because animals died in pure nitrogen. Later, it was found that nitrogen is a component of proteins and DNA, making it a key element of life.

How it works

Nitrogen in the atmosphere is chemically inert due to the strong triple bond between atoms. For plants to absorb it, nitrogen must be 'fixed' — converted into ammonia or nitrates. This is done by bacteria in the soil or industrial processes (e.g., the Haber-Bosch process). Like a safe with valuable contents: nitrogen is everywhere, but to use it, you need the right key.
