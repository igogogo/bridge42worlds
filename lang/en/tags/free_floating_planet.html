free-floating planet

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A wanderer planet, ejected from its home system, silently drifting through interstellar darkness, betrayed only by a fleeting amplification of a distant star's light.

In practice: Revising theories of planetary system formation and estimating the population of potentially habitable worlds hidden in interstellar space.
Free-floating planets are not gravitationally bound to any star. Their discovery was made possible by two methods: direct observation in the infrared if the planet is young and hot, and gravitational microlensing — when the planet focuses the light of a background star into a characteristic symmetric brightness spike. The light curve conveys information about mass and distance, and the absence of repeated events indicates its isolation.

History

The first reliable candidates for free-floating planets were discovered in 2004 from their faint infrared glow in the Orion cluster and from microlensing events.

How it works

Planets are born in gas and dust disks around stars. If another massive planet is nearby or a star flies by, gravitational slingshot can tear a smaller planet from its parent star's embrace, turning it into an eternal wanderer. To find it, astronomers look for "bumps" on light curves — a momentary amplification of light predicted by relativity.

💡 According to one estimate, there could be up to 20 billion free-floating planets in the Milky Way — more than stars — and some of them can hold onto moons.
Imagine a Jupiter-sized ball that lost its Sun. Such solitary planets form like ordinary planets, but during a game of "gravitational checkers" with their siblings, they get flung out. We notice them when they pass in front of a distant star and briefly magnify its brightness with their gravity, like a lens.

A free-floating planet (also rogue planet) is a planetary-mass object not gravitationally bound to any star and drifting through the galaxy as an independent body. Such objects form in protoplanetary disks similarly to ordinary planets, but subsequent dynamical instability in the system (planet-planet scattering, tidal interaction with the star, external perturbation from a passing star) leads to their ejection. A less common channel is the collapse of an isolated gas-dust cloud, similar to star formation but at the limit of the smallest possible mass.

History

The first candidates were substellar-mass objects in the Orion cluster, recorded in deep infrared surveys in the late 1990s – early 2000s. A key breakthrough came from gravitational microlensing experiments (OGLE, MOA) starting in the 2000s, revealing many short, star-unassociated lensing events. In 2011, a statistically significant population of invisible planetary masses in the Galaxy was announced.

How it works

The main detection method is gravitational microlensing. When a planet passes near the line of sight to a background star, its gravitational field, according to general relativity, curves spacetime and focuses light, creating two images that are individually unresolved but produce a spike in total brightness. The characteristic angular scale is the Einstein radius: θ_E = √((4GM)/(c²) · (D_ls)/(D_l D_s)), where M is the lens mass, D_l, D_s, D_ls are distances (observer–lens, observer–source, lens–source). The event duration t_E = θ_E/μ_rel, where μ_rel is the relative proper motion, is limited to a few hours for Earth-mass planets. Free-floating planets produce simple, symmetric curves without additional caustics, distinguishing them from lensing campaigns for exoplanets in bound systems.
