A city-sized magnetic star has the power to rip the keys from your hand from a hundred thousand kilometers away. A magnetar is a neutron star with a field billions of times stronger than the most powerful magnet on Earth.
💡 If a magnetar were at the distance of the Moon, it would wipe the information from all credit cards on Earth.
A neutron star is ordinarily a rapidly spinning lighthouse, but when its magnetic field is amplified to cosmic extremes, the lighthouse becomes a destructive vortex. Like a giant dynamo, a magnetar stores energy in twisted magnetic field lines and suddenly releases it in the form of gamma-ray flares.

History

Astrophysicists began to talk about neutron stars with the strongest magnetic fields in 1992, when Robert Duncan and Christopher Thompson proposed the magnetar model to explain the sources of soft gamma repeaters.

How it works

Inside a magnetar, superfluid and superconducting layers of matter interact with the magnetic field, causing sudden reconfigurations—starquakes. The star's crust cracks, and the twisted field straightens out like a snapped spring, releasing a monstrous amount of energy into the surroundings.

💡 On December 27, 2004, the magnetar SGR 1806-20 produced a gamma-ray flare that released more energy in a tenth of a second than the Sun will emit in 150,000 years, and ionized the upper layers of Earth's atmosphere for several seconds.
A magnetar is a neutron star with an extreme dipole magnetic field on the order of 10¹³–10¹⁵ G. Such a field can deform the crust, generate a continuous magnetosphere, and cause soft gamma-ray bursts (SGRs) and anomalous X-ray pulsars (AXPs). The energy of a magnetar is powered by magnetic field decay rather than rotation, so their rotation periods are longer than those of radio pulsars, and they spin down rapidly.

History

The first hints of magnetars appeared with the detection of soft gamma-ray bursts in 1979, but their nature remained unclear until the magnetar hypothesis was advanced in 1992 by Robert Duncan and Christopher Thompson. Observational confirmation came from space observatories such as RXTE and Chandra, which discovered anomalous pulsar braking and giant flares. Today, about 30 confirmed magnetars are known.

How it works

According to the dynamo mechanism, the magnetic field of a magnetar is amplified in the first seconds of the neutron star's life through convection and rapid rotation. A field of 10¹⁵ G turns the vacuum around the star into a nonlinear quantum electrodynamic medium, where electron-positron pair production and photon scattering can occur. Sudden field changes cause magnetic reconnection, producing powerful flares visible in the gamma-ray and X-ray bands. The overall field evolution is described by Maxwell's equations within relativistic magnetohydrodynamics.

B_c = \frac{m_e^2 c^3}{e \hbar}
B_c is the critical field; m_e is the electron mass (≈ 9.11×10⁻³¹ kg); c is the speed of light (≈ 3×10⁸ m/s); e is the elementary charge (≈ 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C); ħ is the reduced Planck constant (≈ 1.05×10⁻³⁴ J·s). The value B_c ≈ 4.4×10¹³ G.
