turbulence

📊 0 articles

Vortex chaos in a flow: when a liquid or gas loses smoothness and breaks down into a cascade of eddies, like milk foam in morning coffee.

In practice: Affects wing lift, stability of gas pipelines, mixing of ocean currents, and design of low-drag automobiles.
Turbulence is a regime of motion of a fluid or gaseous medium characterized by irregular fluctuations in velocity, pressure, and density fields. Flow becomes turbulent when a critical value of the Reynolds number (Re) is exceeded, which expresses the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces. For pipe flows, Re_cr ≈ 2300. In a developed turbulent flow, an energy cascade from large scales to small scales is observed; in the inertial subrange, the Kolmogorov–Obukhov law for the energy spectrum E(k) ∝ k^{-5/3} holds. Turbulence increases frictional drag but enhances heat and mass transfer.
💡 The flight of the bumblebee was long considered paradoxical: according to steady aerodynamics, its wings do not generate enough lift. Actually, the bumblebee uses turbulent vortices created by sharp wing flaps.
Turbulence is such a flow of liquid or gas where particles move not in an orderly, layered manner, but chaotically, with eddies. In a calm (laminar) flow, a stream of water is smooth, but in a turbulent one it breaks into swirling vortices. The faster it flows and the lower the viscosity (flow resistance), the easier turbulence arises. An example is water in a river at a rapid: near stones it boils and foams, mixing with air.

History

In 1883, English engineer Osborne Reynolds conducted an experiment: he introduced a thin stream of dye into a pipe with water and saw that at slow flow the dye traveled as a smooth thread, while at fast flow it blurred into chaos. Thus he described the transition to turbulence and introduced a number that helps predict this transition.

How it works

Large vortices are unstable and break down into smaller ones, those into even smaller ones, and so on until friction grinds the motion into heat. This process resembles a cascade: the energy of a waterfall splits into jets, spray, and foam until the water calms down.

💡 Even in fully developed turbulent flow, there exist thin regions with very strong vorticity fluctuations — vortex filaments — which may be the cause of anomalous dissipation.
Turbulence is a complex nonlinear phenomenon described by the Navier–Stokes equations for viscous fluid motion. At Reynolds numbers above critical, the laminar solution loses stability, and small perturbations grow, generating chaotic motion. Turbulence energy is produced at large scales (integral scale L) by shear stresses and cascades down the spectrum to the dissipative scale (Kolmogorov scale η), where viscosity turns kinetic energy into heat. In the inertial subrange (η ≪ r ≪ L), the only governing parameter is the energy dissipation rate ε, and dimensional analysis yields the famous '–5/3' law. Direct numerical simulation (DNS) requires resolving all scales, making calculations extremely expensive at high Re; hence, large-eddy simulation (LES) and Reynolds-averaged methods (RANS) are used. In astrophysics, turbulence plays a key role in accretion disks, star formation, and the interstellar medium.

History

Osborne Reynolds in 1883 performed the famous experiment with a dyed stream and introduced the dimensionless parameter (Reynolds number) that determines the transition to turbulence. In 1941, Andrey Kolmogorov created the statistical theory of locally isotropic turbulence, postulating the universality of the inertial subrange. Experimental confirmations of the k^{-5/3} spectrum were obtained in the 1950s–60s.

How it works

The mechanism of the turbulent cascade: energy is injected into the flow at large scales (e.g., by obstacle flow) and through a sequence of vortex breakdowns is transferred to ever smaller scales. In the inertial subrange, far from both source and dissipation, statistics are homogeneous and isotropic, and the energy spectrum depends only on wavenumber k and dissipation rate ε: E(k) = C ε^{2/3} k^{-5/3}, with constant C ≈ 1.5. Dissipation occurs at scales on the order of the Kolmogorov length η = (ν^3/ε)^{1/4}, where ν is kinematic viscosity. Transition to turbulence is associated with instability of the laminar profile: at Re > Re_cr, small perturbations grow exponentially, forming secondary flows and ultimately chaos. Mathematical difficulties arise because the Navier–Stokes equations are nonlinear and do not admit a general analytical solution; the problem of turbulence closure remains one of the most important unsolved problems of classical physics.

Re = \frac{\rho u L}{\mu}
Re — Reynolds number, ρ — fluid density, u — characteristic flow velocity, L — characteristic linear dimension, μ — dynamic viscosity of the fluid. Transition to turbulence usually occurs at Re > 2300 in pipes.
E(k) = C \varepsilon^{2/3} k^{-5/3}
E(k) — spectral density of kinetic energy per unit mass as a function of wavenumber k (k = 2π/λ, where λ is the vortex size), ε — energy dissipation rate per unit mass, C — Kolmogorov constant (usually about 1.5).

Open problems & fun facts

General analytical solution of the Navier–Stokes equations for turbulent flows remains unknown (millennium problem); Closure problem: in averaged models, correlations arise that require semi-empirical hypotheses; The huge gap between the Kolmogorov length scale and the integral scale makes DNS impractical at high Re
