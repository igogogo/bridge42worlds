When an ambulance passes by, its siren changes tone: higher when approaching, lower when receding. The same happens with light from distant galaxies: if a galaxy is moving away, its light becomes slightly redder — this is redshift. The faster the galaxy recedes, the stronger the shift.

History

The Doppler effect for sound was described by Christian Doppler in 1842. Astronomer Vesto Slipher began measuring shifts of lines in the spectra of nebulae back in the 1910s. But the decisive step was made by Edwin Hubble in 1929, discovering the proportionality of redshift to the distance of galaxies.

How it works

Atoms emit light at specific wavelengths — like fingerprints. When a galaxy recedes, the wave crests arrive less frequently, and the entire spectrum shifts toward longer wavelengths — to the red. From the amount of shift, the recession velocity is calculated. It's like a comb with sparse teeth: if the source moves, the 'teeth' of the spectrum become even sparser.

💡 Thanks to redshift we know the Universe is expanding. Edwin Hubble noticed that almost all galaxies are redshifted, and the farther they are, the redder they appear.
Redshift is the stretching of light waves from a receding source, analogous to how waves from a stone thrown into water become longer if we move away in a boat. In space, the expansion of space itself stretches photons on their way to us.

Redshift (denoted z) is defined as z = (λ_observed – λ_emitted)/λ_emitted. In cosmology, it is caused by the expansion of the Universe according to General Relativity; the scale factor a(t) relates the wavelength at emission to today's: λ_obs / λ_em = a_today / a_then. The shift also includes the Doppler effect from peculiar velocities and gravitational redshift from spacetime curvature. For z<0.1 the linear Hubble law approximation v = H₀ D works; for larger z, the cosmological model (ΛCDM) must be taken into account.

History

The idea of an expanding Universe arose from Friedmann's equations (1922) and Georges Lemaître's observations (1927), but gained wide acceptance after Edwin Hubble's work (1929) showing a linear relationship between redshift and distance. Lemaître was the first to interpret this as an expansion of space. Later, measurements of type Ia supernovae in 1998 led to the discovery of accelerated expansion (dark energy).

How it works

Redshift is measured spectroscopically: lines of known elements (e.g., hydrogen) are shifted by a factor (1+z). The Hubble law v = H₀ d relates recession velocity and distance. For large z, the relationship becomes nonlinear and is determined by the cosmological model. Peculiar velocities of galaxies make an additional, often negligible contribution. There is also gravitational redshift, predicted by General Relativity: photons lose energy when escaping a gravitational well. Redshift measurements rely on the Doppler effect and Hubble's law.
