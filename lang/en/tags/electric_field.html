electric field

📊 0 articles
An electric field is an invisible force around charged objects. Like water around a magnet creating circles, the electric field draws lines along which a charge will move. When you touch synthetic clothing and hear a crackle, you are feeling the electric field.
💡 Lightning is a giant discharge that occurs due to the huge difference in electric fields between clouds and the ground.
An electric field is a spatial network of forces created by electric charges. It is like an invisible hand that pushes or pulls other charges. The closer you are to the source, the stronger this field, like the heat from a campfire intensifies as you approach.

History

The concept of the field was introduced by Michael Faraday in the 19th century, and James Clerk Maxwell later described it mathematically in his famous equations.

How it works

Imagine that around each charge - like around a pebble thrown into water - ripples spread. These ripples show where another charge will move. Positive and negative charges create fields that either merge or cancel each other out.

💡 If we could see electric fields, every object would turn into a glowing jellyfish of shimmering lines.
The electric field is a vector field created by charged particles and changing magnetic fields. It describes the force acting on a test charge at each point in space. In classical electrodynamics, electric and magnetic fields are combined into the electromagnetic tensor. Coulomb's law, Gauss's law, and Maxwell's equations define its structure and evolution. In quantum physics, the electric field is considered as a manifestation of the exchange of virtual photons.

History

Michael Faraday in 1831 introduced the concept of lines of force to visualize electric and magnetic interactions. James Clerk Maxwell in 1864 generalized the discoveries into equations, predicting electromagnetic waves. Hendrik Lorentz introduced the concept of the electromagnetic field as a separate entity and the Lorentz force for moving charges. Henri Poincaré studied the symmetries of Maxwell's equations and pointed out relativistic effects. The development of quantum electrodynamics in the works of Dirac and others built a bridge to the microworld.

How it works

The electric field E is defined by the force per unit test charge and obeys superposition. Maxwell's equation ∇·E = ρ/ε₀ (Gauss's law) relates the field to charge density. For electrostatics, the field is conservative, and the potential φ satisfies Poisson's equation. In dynamics, the electric field is also generated by a changing magnetic field (Faraday's law). In the relativistic formulation, the electromagnetic tensor F^{μν} combines the fields E and B. The quantum limit is described by Feynman diagrams with the photon as the U(1) gauge boson. Coulomb's law for a point charge gives E = k q / r².
