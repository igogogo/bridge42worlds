Planck Length

📊 0 articles
The Planck length is the smallest possible distance, about 1.6×10⁻³⁵ meters, below which the concept of length loses meaning.
💡 If an atom were the size of Earth, the Planck length would be the size of a grain of sand.
The Planck length is a fundamental unit of length in quantum gravity, equal to √(ħG/c³). At such scales, quantum effects of gravity become important, and the classical description of spacetime breaks down.

History

The Planck length was introduced by Max Planck in 1899 as a combination of fundamental constants.

How it works

At distances on the order of the Planck length, gravity becomes quantum, and spacetime may be discrete, like pixels on a screen.

💡 The Planck length is 10²⁰ times smaller than the size of a proton.
The Planck length l_P = √(ħG/c³) ≈ 1.616×10⁻³⁵ m is the scale at which quantum effects of gravity become significant. It is believed that at this scale, spacetime ceases to be smooth and acquires a discrete or foamy structure.

History

Max Planck in 1899 proposed a system of units based on fundamental constants: ħ, G, and c. The Planck length is one of them. In modern physics, it is considered the scale where a quantum theory of gravity is needed.

How it works

The Planck length arises from the combination of constants when gravitational effects become comparable to quantum ones. For example, the de Broglie wavelength of a particle with Planck energy equals its gravitational radius. At smaller distances, quantum fluctuations of the metric become strong.

l_P = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar G}{c^3}}
The Planck length is expressed in terms of Planck's constant, the gravitational constant, and the speed of light.
