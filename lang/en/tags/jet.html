An astrophysical jet is a narrow stream of plasma shooting out from the poles of black holes or young stars at near-light speed. Like from a garden hose, but instead of water — superheated gas flying through a galaxy.

In practice: Jets help us understand how black holes regulate galaxy growth, and their nature can be used to test physics of extreme states.
A jet is a collimated stream of plasma, moving at relativistic speeds and emitting radiation from radio to gamma rays. It forms in the vicinity of compact objects — black holes, neutron stars, or protostars — due to the accretion of matter and the twisting of magnetic fields. Jets carry enormous kinetic energy and can influence the interstellar medium, creating giant bubbles and shock waves.

History

The first jets were noticed in galaxy M87 in 1918, but their nature was not understood for a long time. In the 1970s, with the development of radio astronomy, it became clear that they are associated with black holes.

How it works

Around a black hole, a disk of gas (accretion disk) rotates. Due to strong magnetic fields and rotation, some of this gas does not fall into the hole but is ejected outward along the rotation axis, like spinning pizza dough — it flies apart.

💡 The longest known jet (in galaxy J1420-0545) stretches for nearly 16 million light-years — greater than the distance from us to the nearest large galaxy cluster.
A jet is a long and very narrow stream of plasma (electrically charged gas) that is ejected from the center of some galaxies or young stars. Imagine a fire hose that blasts out glowing matter with tremendous force, but aimed not in one direction but in two opposite ones, like two sharp beams. These streams can stretch for millions of light-years.

A relativistic jet is a narrowly collimated bipolar outflow of plasma, formed in an 'accretion disk — compact object' system. The launching mechanism involves magnetohydrodynamic processes: the rotation of the magnetized disk twists the field lines, creating toroidal fields that accelerate plasma along the rotation axis to relativistic speeds (Lorentz factor up to 10–100). Jets exhibit superluminal motion, emit synchrotron and inverse Compton components, and are also sources of neutrinos and high-energy cosmic rays.

History

An extended stream in galaxy M87 was first described by Heber Curtis in 1918. For a long time, jets were considered rare, but with the development of radio interferometry in the 1970s, they were discovered in many active galactic nuclei and quasars. Theoretical understanding advanced thanks to the work of Roger Blandford and Roman Znajek (the Blandford-Znajek mechanism for extracting energy from a rotating black hole) and John Wheeler, who coined the term 'black hole' and promoted recognition of their astrophysical role. Space observatories (Hubble, Chandra, Event Horizon Telescope) have allowed observation of jets at high resolution.

How it works

Gas falling onto a compact object forms a rotating accretion disk. The frozen-in magnetic field of the disk is amplified by differential rotation. Part of the rotational energy is transferred to the plasma via magnetic stresses, and it is ejected as two oppositely directed streams. For black holes, an additional mechanism is possible: the rotating hole interacts with the magnetic field, inducing an electric potential and accelerating charges along the axis — the Blandford-Znajek process. Conservation of angular momentum plays a key role.
