planetary migration

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
6

Planets are not rigidly fixed to their orbits: gravitational tides from the gas disk can drag them away from or toward the star, like a boat changing course under the influence of a current.

In practice: Explanation of the architecture of exoplanetary systems (hot Jupiters, compact super-Earth systems); assessment of the habitability of planets that ended up in the habitable zone due to migration.
Planetary migration is a change in the orbital distance of a planet from its star due to gravitational torques from the protoplanetary disk. For terrestrial-type planets (mass up to ~10 M⊕), type I migration is typical: the body excites spiral density waves in the disk, creating a braking torque. More massive giant planets clear a ring-shaped gap in the disk and migrate via type II, following the viscous evolution of the gas. An additional mechanism is dynamical scattering of planets by each other after the disk dissipates.

History

Scientists suspected migration back in the 1970s, but the decisive moment came with the discovery in 1995 of the planet 51 Pegasi b — a hot Jupiter that could not have formed so close to the star. Since then, migration has become a key part of planet formation theories.

How it works

A planet's gravity perturbs the surrounding gas, generating spiral waves, like the wake of an oar in water. These waves sap energy from the planet and force it to migrate inward or outward. In a viscous disk, the planet drifts along with the gas, like a chip carried by a current.

💡 Simulations show that in the Solar System, Jupiter first migrated inward to the orbit of Mars and then turned back due to resonance with Saturn — this 'stirred up' the asteroid belt and likely prevented super-Earths from forming.

Planetary migration is a fundamental process in the evolution of planetary systems, whereby a planet changes its orbital semi-major axis due to angular momentum exchange with the gas-dust disk or other bodies. Mechanisms: type I migration (Lindblad and corotation torques from spiral waves excited by the planet; leads to rapid inward migration for bodies up to 10 M⊕); type II migration (a planet more massive than ~1 MJup opens a gap and migrates along with the accreting gas of the viscous disk); fast type III migration (in massive disks for intermediate-mass planets). Gravitational interaction of planets after disk dissipation also leads to orbital evolution (planet-planet scattering, secular resonances, Kozai-Lidov effect for inclined orbits). Observational evidence: hot Jupiters, resonant chains, misaligned orbits.

History

The concept of migration dates back to the 1970s: Goldreich and Tremaine proposed it to explain the dynamics of giant planets. Wide acceptance came after the discovery of exoplanets, primarily 51 Pegasi b (1995). Key researchers include Papaloizou, Lin, Mordasini, Kley, and others. Modern hydrodynamic simulations, developing since the 2000s, have allowed a detailed description of migration types.

How it works

It is based on the gravitational interaction of the planet with the disk. The planet excites spiral density waves in the disk at inner and outer Lindblad resonances. The transfer of angular momentum by these waves leads to braking of the planet (inward migration). Corotation torques (from gas on orbits close to the planet) can slow or reverse migration. Once the planet opens a gap (criterion: its tidal force exceeds viscous stresses), viscous evolution of the disk comes into play, and the planet drifts with the accretion flow. After the gas disappears, planet-planet scattering dominates. The law of conservation of angular momentum is key. The process is described by the equations of hydrodynamics and gravity, often using numerical modeling due to nonlinearity.
