Manifold

📊 0 articles
A manifold is a space that near each point looks like ordinary flat space, but overall may be curved, like the surface of the Earth.
💡 The surface of a donut (torus) is also a manifold, and mathematicians can study its shape without cutting the donut.
A manifold is a topological space that is locally homeomorphic to Euclidean space ℝ^n. This means that every point has a neighborhood that looks like a 'piece' of the plane or space. Examples: sphere, torus, Möbius strip. Manifolds can have a boundary (e.g., disk) or be without boundary. They are the main object of study in differential geometry and topology.

History

The concept of a manifold was introduced by Bernhard Riemann in 1854 in his lecture 'On the Hypotheses which lie at the Bases of Geometry'. Later, Henri Poincaré developed the topology of manifolds, and in the 20th century they became central in general relativity and string theory.

How it works

Imagine you live on the surface of the Earth. Around you, the terrain appears flat like a sheet of paper. But if you go far, you will notice curvature. A manifold is a mathematical model of such a surface: locally it looks like a plane, but globally it may be curved.

💡 The universe in general relativity is modeled as a four-dimensional manifold (spacetime), curved by masses.
A manifold is a Hausdorff topological space with a countable base, each point of which has a neighborhood homeomorphic to an open subset of ℝ^n. A smooth manifold is additionally equipped with a smooth atlas — a collection of charts with smooth transition functions. Manifolds come in various dimensions: 1-manifolds (curves), 2-manifolds (surfaces), 3-manifolds, etc. They can be orientable or non-orientable (e.g., Möbius strip). Important examples: sphere S^n, torus T^n, projective space ℝP^n.

History

Bernhard Riemann in 1854 introduced the concept of a multidimensional manifold in his habilitation lecture. Henri Poincaré in the late 19th century laid the foundations of algebraic topology by studying the fundamental group of manifolds. In 1915, Einstein used a four-dimensional manifold to describe spacetime. In the mid-20th century, Morse theory, cobordism theory, and classification of manifolds developed.

How it works

An n-dimensional manifold M is covered by charts (U_i, φ_i), where φ_i: U_i → ℝ^n is a homeomorphism. The transition functions φ_j ∘ φ_i^{-1} on overlaps of charts must be smooth (for a smooth manifold). Additional structures can be introduced on a manifold: Riemannian metric, connection, tensor fields. The tangent space T_pM at a point p is an n-dimensional vector space consisting of velocities of curves passing through p.

\phi_j \circ \phi_i^{-1}: \phi_i(U_i \cap U_j) \to \phi_j(U_i \cap U_j) \text{ — smooth}
Transition functions between charts must be smooth (infinitely differentiable) for a smooth manifold.

Open problems & fun facts

Poincaré conjecture (proved by Perelman) — classification of three-dimensional manifolds.; The problem of existence and uniqueness of smooth structures on topological manifolds (e.g., ℝ^4 admits infinitely many exotic smooth structures).
