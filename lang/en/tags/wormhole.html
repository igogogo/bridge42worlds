Wormhole

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
18

A short tunnel in spacetime connecting distant corners of the Universe, like a burrow piercing a folded sheet.

In practice: So far pure theory, but wormhole research advances quantum gravity and may one day underlie superluminal communications or travel.
A wormhole is a hypothetical structure allowed by general relativity that connects two points in spacetime like a handle. It could link distant regions of the same universe or different universes. To keep the hole open for passage, exotic matter with negative pressure is needed, violating the usual energy conditions.

History

The idea appeared almost immediately after the creation of general relativity: in 1916, Ludwig Flamm found something like a tunnel in Einstein's equations. Later, Einstein and Rosen described a 'bridge', and the name 'wormhole' was given by physicist John Wheeler in 1957.

How it works

Imagine a two-dimensional world as a sheet of paper. Curving it, you can punch a hole and connect two distant points with a short tube — that's how a wormhole creates a tunnel in spacetime. To prevent the tunnel walls from collapsing, exotic matter with negative pressure (pushing apart rather than attracting) is needed.

💡 If you looked through a wormhole, you could see a distorted, lens-like but recognizable image of the other end — perhaps a star or galaxy that would otherwise take millions of years to reach.
A wormhole is like a tunnel through a mountain that connects two sides directly, without a detour. In space, such a tunnel would link distant points of spacetime (the unified fabric of space and time). The entrances would look like black spheres, but inside would be the shortest route.

In general relativity, a wormhole is a non-singular solution with a throat connecting two asymptotically flat regions. The Einstein–Rosen bridge is not traversable due to the event horizon. Traversable wormholes were constructed by Morris and Thorne in 1988: their metric contains a shape function b(r) and a redshift function Φ(r), with b(r₀)=r₀ at the throat. Matter that violates the weak energy condition is required; this is impossible in classical physics, but quantum effects (e.g., vacuum fluctuations) could provide negative energy.

History

In 1916, Ludwig Flamm, interpreting the Schwarzschild solution, proposed the idea of a 'wormhole'. Einstein and Rosen in 1935 described the bridge as a geometrized model of an elementary particle. The term 'wormhole' was introduced by John Archibald Wheeler in 1957 in the context of quantum foam. Modern interest revived after the paper by Morris and Thorne, which showed the possibility of travel provided exotic matter exists.

How it works

Einstein's equations link geometry (the Einstein tensor Gμν) to matter (the energy-momentum tensor Tμν). For a wormhole, the metric is given as ds² = -e^{2Φ(r)}c² dt² + dr²/(1 - b(r)/r) + r² dΩ². The absence of a horizon requires Φ(r) to be finite everywhere. Traversability imposes conditions on b(r) and Φ(r), and also requires violation of the null energy condition (negative energy). Quantum effects, such as the Casimir effect, provide such a possibility.

ds^2 = -c^2 e^{2\Phi(r)} dt^2 + \frac{dr^2}{1 - b(r)/r} + r^2 (d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta d\phi^2)
ds — spacetime interval, c — speed of light in vacuum (≈3×10⁸ m/s), Φ(r) — redshift function (determines gravitational time dilation), b(r) — shape function (b(r₀)=r₀ at the throat), r — radial coordinate (circumference 2πr), t — time coordinate, θ and φ — angular coordinates on the sphere

Open problems & fun facts

The possible form of exotic matter suitable for stabilizing the wormhole is unknown; Travel through the wormhole could lead to causality violation (a time machine); Quantum instability and Hawking radiation could destroy the wormhole
