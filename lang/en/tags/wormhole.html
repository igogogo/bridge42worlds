A wormhole is a hypothetical object predicted by general relativity. Just as a worm bores through an apple, creating a direct path instead of going around the surface, a wormhole pierces the fabric of spacetime, connecting distant regions.

History

First mathematically described in 1916 by Karl Schwarzschild (as a solution to Einstein's equations), but the idea of tunnels in spacetime was developed by Einstein and Rosen in 1935, and the term 'wormhole' was coined by John Wheeler in 1957.

How it works

A thrown stone creates ripples on a lake's surface — that's a disturbance in spacetime. A wormhole is a sudden whirlpool, sucking in and ejecting elsewhere. To prevent such a whirlpool from collapsing, a special 'anti-gravity' matter is needed.

💡 Through a wormhole, one could theoretically travel in time: if one end is accelerated to near-light speeds, time there slows down, and you'd exit before you entered.
A wormhole (Einstein-Rosen bridge) is a topological feature of spacetime, representing a 'handle' in the manifold that connects two distant regions or different universes. In general relativity, it is described as a solution to Einstein's equations, usually with a non-zero stress-energy tensor. The simplest metric is the Morris-Thorne metric, which requires exotic matter with negative energy density to keep the throat open. Wormholes can be traversable or non-traversable; the latter, like the Einstein-Rosen bridge, have event horizons and resemble two connected black holes. The main problem is stability: without exotic matter, the throat collapses on a time scale of the order of the Planck time.

History

The concept emerged after the publication of the Schwarzschild solution in 1916, which contains an inner tunnel, but it is hidden behind an event horizon. In 1935, Albert Einstein and Nathan Rosen showed that the equations could describe a 'bridge' between two regions, avoiding singularities; later, John Wheeler connected such bridges with particles and coined the term 'wormhole' in 1957. In 1988, Kip Thorne and Michael Morris proposed a traversable wormhole without a horizon, stimulating research into exotic matter. Modern efforts include attempts to construct wormholes in quantum gravity.

How it works

A wormhole acts as a spacetime tunnel described by the metric ds^2 = -e^{2Φ(r)} dt^2 + (1 - b(r)/r)^{-1} dr^2 + r^2 (dθ^2 + sin^2θ dφ^2), where Φ(r) is the redshift function and b(r) is the shape function. For traversability, the throat must have no horizon, i.e., e^{2Φ} must remain finite, and tidal forces must be tolerable. The averaged null energy condition must be violated, which is possible due to quantum effects like the Casimir effect. Traveling through the wormhole means an observer moving along a timelike geodesic through the throat.

Open problems & fun facts

Stability of the throat without exotic matter; Existence of macroscopic exotic matter with negative energy density; Time travel paradoxes and causality violation; Lack of observational evidence or ways to search for it; Quantum corrections and possible destruction at Planck scales
