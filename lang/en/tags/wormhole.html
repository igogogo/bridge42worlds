wormhole

📊 10 articles

A shortcut through curved space, like a wormhole in an apple—directly connecting two distant points.

In practice: Wormholes could theoretically become portals for interstellar travel; currently they serve as a testing ground for thought experiments in quantum gravity and verification of fundamental laws.
A wormhole is a solution to Einstein's equations describing a topological 'handle' in spacetime. It can be traversable if the throat does not collapse and does not contain an event horizon. Stabilization requires matter that violates the null energy condition (with negative energy density), which is impossible in classical physics. However, quantum effects such as the Casimir effect demonstrate the possibility of negative energy densities at microscopic scales, but macroscopic sources have not yet been found.

History

The mathematical foundation was laid by Albert Einstein and Nathan Rosen in 1935 (Einstein–Rosen bridge). The term 'wormhole' was coined by John Wheeler in 1957.

How it works

Imagine the surface of water—that is space. If you create depressions at two distant points and connect them with an underwater channel, you get a shortcut. But to prevent the tunnel from collapsing, its walls must be made of a special material that can hold shape despite pressure. In the case of a wormhole, this role must be performed by hypothetical exotic matter with negative pressure.

💡 If you could look into a traversable wormhole, you would see the back of your own head—light curving around the throat would return to you.
A wormhole is a hypothetical bridge between two distant regions of space (or even different universes). General relativity allows that spacetime can bend so much that it creates a tunnel—like folding a sheet of paper and poking a pencil through. A wormhole has two mouths (entrances) and a narrow passage (throat). To prevent the wormhole from collapsing instantly, exotic matter with negative energy is needed, which repels rather than attracts.

A wormhole is a hypothetical spacetime topology equivalent to S² × R¹, with two asymptotically flat regions connected by a throat of minimal radius. The simplest static spherically symmetric Morris–Thorne metric: ds² = -c²dt² + dl² + (b₀² + l²)(dθ² + sin²θ dφ²), where b₀ is the throat radius. Traversability requires the absence of a horizon and matter that violates the null energy condition (ρ + pᵣ < 0). Quantum fields can create local negative energy densities (Casimir effect), but a stable macroscopic wormhole requires global violation, which has not yet been found. The ER = EPR hypothesis links wormholes to quantum entanglement.

History

The first solution to Einstein's equations containing a bridge was given by Karl Schwarzschild in 1916, but it was hidden behind a horizon. In 1935, Einstein and Rosen proposed a model to eliminate singularities, but the passage was impassable. The term 'wormhole' was introduced by John Wheeler in 1957. In 1988, Kip Thorne and his students formulated the conditions for traversability, showing the necessity of exotic matter, which spurred modern research.

How it works

In general relativity, gravity is the curvature of spacetime. A wormhole appears when the metric allows a nontrivial topology with a throat of radius r₀. To prevent the throat from collapsing, negative pressure is needed, which could be provided by exotic matter (e.g., a quantum field in a special state). The Casimir effect creates negative energy density, but only in microscopic volumes. By violating the null condition, the wormhole is formally traversable, but the backreaction of vacuum fluctuations may destabilize it. Moving the mouths creates a time machine, which questions the existence of traversable wormholes due to causality violation.
