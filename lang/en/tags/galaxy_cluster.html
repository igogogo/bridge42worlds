Galaxy cluster

📊 0 articles
A gravitationally bound group of hundreds or thousands of galaxies, permeated by hot gas and dark matter. These are among the most massive structures in the universe. Imagine a metropolis where each galaxy is a building, and the space between them is filled with invisible dark matter.
💡 The Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-558) provided direct evidence for dark matter: after two clusters collided, the gas remained in the center while dark matter passed through.

History

The first galaxy clusters were discovered in the 18th century, but their nature became clear only in the 20th century. In 1933, Fritz Zwicky measured the velocities of galaxies in the Coma Cluster and found that visible mass was insufficient to hold them together — this was the first evidence of dark matter. Modern surveys like the Sloan Digital Sky Survey catalog thousands of clusters.

How it works

Galaxy clusters are held together by gravity, with most of the mass in dark matter. Hot gas between galaxies emits X-rays, allowing clusters to be detected. Gravitational lensing bends light from distant galaxies, creating a clue. Analogy: a cluster is like a swarm of bees, where each bee is a galaxy, and the invisible hive is dark matter.
