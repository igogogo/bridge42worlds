galaxy cluster

8

Galaxy clusters are giant metropolises of the Universe: thousands of galaxies bound by gravity and enveloped in hot gas, like an invisible web.

In practice: Studying clusters helps to 'weigh' invisible dark matter, test theories of gravity, and understand how the large-scale structure of the cosmos formed.
Galaxy clusters are gravitationally bound systems containing hundreds to thousands of galaxies. They are immersed in a vast halo of hot plasma with a temperature of 10–100 million degrees, emitting in the X-ray range. Approximately 85% of the total mass is dark matter, 10–12% is hot gas, and only a few percent are stars. Clusters form at the nodes of the cosmic web—the large-scale structure of matter distribution in the Universe.

History

Astronomers noticed clusters as early as compiling catalogs of nebulae. In the 1930s, Fritz Zwicky measured the velocities of galaxies in the Coma cluster and found that the visible mass was insufficient to keep them from flying apart. Thus arose the hypothesis of dark matter—invisible substance.

How it works

Just as water droplets in a cloud coalesce into larger drops, galaxies attract each other and form clusters. Gravity holds them together, but there is not enough visible mass, so additional invisible mass—dark matter—is assumed to exist.

💡 The galaxy cluster El Gordo (ACT-CL J0102-4915) is the most massive known, with a mass exceeding 3 quadrillion solar masses.
A galaxy cluster is a large number of galaxies located close together and held by gravity. They are the largest stable objects in the Universe. At the center of a cluster there is often a giant elliptical galaxy, and the space between galaxies is filled with superheated gas that glows in X-rays.

A galaxy cluster is the largest gravitationally bound system in the Universe with a mass on the order of 10¹⁴–10¹⁵ solar masses and a size of several megaparsecs. It includes hundreds to thousands of galaxies, the intracluster medium (hot gas with temperatures of tens of millions of kelvins, emitting in X-rays), and dark matter, which dominates and manifests through gravitational lensing. The distribution and evolution of clusters depend on cosmological parameters such as the matter density Ω_m and the fluctuation amplitude σ_8. Clusters serve as natural laboratories for studying dark energy via counting their numbers at different epochs of cosmic history and through observations of the Sunyaev–Zeldovich effect—distortion of the cosmic microwave background by hot gas.

History

The Coma cluster was catalogued by Charles Messier in 1781 as a nebulous object. In 1933, Fritz Zwicky, applying the virial theorem to the velocity dispersion of galaxies in this cluster, showed that the dynamical mass far exceeds the visible mass and proposed the term 'dark matter'. In the 1970s, X-ray satellites (Uhuru, Einstein) discovered the hot intergalactic gas, which became an important tool for studying clusters. By the 1980s, it was clear that the baryonic component (stars and gas) is only a fraction of the total mass, with dark matter dominating. Vera Rubin earlier confirmed the need for dark matter on galactic scales.

How it works

The cluster is held together by gravity, described by Newton's law of universal gravitation and Einstein's general relativity. The velocity dispersion of galaxies (about 800 km/s) and the temperature of hot gas measure the depth of the gravitational potential: kinetic energy of gas particles ~ kT is comparable to potential, kT ~ μ m_p σ², where μ is the mean molecular mass, m_p the proton mass. The gas radiates via bremsstrahlung and spectral lines of highly ionized ions (e.g., iron Fe XXV–XXVI), allowing determination of temperature and chemical composition. Gravitational lensing of background galaxies provides an independent method to map the mass distribution. Cosmological simulations (Illustris, Millennium) within the cold dark matter model (ΛCDM) reproduce cluster formation.

M = \frac{5\sigma^2 R}{G}
M — cluster mass, σ — velocity dispersion of galaxies, R — characteristic radius (e.g., half-mass radius), G — gravitational constant (G ≈ 6.674×10⁻¹¹ m³ kg⁻¹ s⁻²)
