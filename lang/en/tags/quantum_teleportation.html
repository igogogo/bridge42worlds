Quantum Teleportation

📊 0 articles
Quantum teleportation is the transfer of a quantum state from one particle to another without moving the particle itself. The original state is destroyed in the process, as if a 'Xerox' burns the original. Not the light itself is transmitted, but the instructions for its assembly.
💡 Even the state of a whole atom can be teleported, but the current record is a few hundred meters via optical fiber.
Quantum teleportation works on the 'twin' principle: two partner particles (entangled) feel each other at a distance. If a measurement is performed on one, the other instantly takes on the specified state, but this requires a classical signal that travels no faster than light.

History

The idea was proposed in 1993 by Charles Bennett and colleagues. Theoretically, it relied on the Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen paradox and the work of John Bell. The first experiment was conducted in 1997 by Anton Zeilinger and his team at the University of Innsbruck.

How it works

Two friends have 'magic' coins that always land opposite. To transmit your coin, you compare it with one of the pair and report the result over the phone. The friend turns their coin into a copy, and your original loses its special properties.

💡 Teleportation does not violate the Heisenberg uncertainty principle because the measured particle loses its original state, and the copy is created indirectly.
Quantum teleportation is a process of transferring a quantum state over a distance using a separated entangled pair and a classical communication channel. It does not imply the transfer of matter or energy in the manner of science fiction 'transporters'. It is based on nonlocality, the no-cloning theorem, and the Bell protocol. State A is sent to B via a Bell measurement on A and one part of the entangled pair; the result is transmitted over a classical channel, after which B applies the corresponding unitary transformation to their particle, restoring the original state. The original particle irrevocably loses its state.

History

The concept was proposed in 1993 by Charles Bennett, Gilles Brassard, Claude Crépeau, Richard Jozsa, Asher Peres, and William Wootters. The theoretical foundations were the works of Erwin Schrödinger, who coined the term 'entanglement', and John Bell, who proved the inequality confirming nonlocality. The first experimental demonstration with photons was carried out in 1997 by Anton Zeilinger's group (Innsbruck) and independently by Francesco De Martini's team (Rome). Alain Aspect in the 1980s performed crucial tests of Bell's inequalities, strengthening confidence in nonlocality.

How it works

The protocol relies on the principle of superposition and the effect of entanglement. Sender and receiver use a pair of maximally entangled qubits in a Bell state. The sender's measurement in the Bell basis on their half of the pair and the teleporting qubit projects the system onto one of the four Bell states, destroying the original qubit. The result (two classical bits) is transmitted over a normal channel. The receiver applies one of four unitary transformations (identity, X, Z, or XZ) depending on the received bits, and their qubit takes on the original state. The process is consistent with the theory of relativity, since the classical signal is limited by the speed of light. The no-cloning theorem guarantees transfer, not copying of information. Initial state |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩, entangled pair — e.g., |Φ+⟩ = (|00⟩+|11⟩)/√2. The total system: |ψ⟩⊗|Φ+⟩. After the receiver's measurement, their qubit collapses to a state related to |ψ⟩ by a unitary operation depending on the outcome.

Open problems & fun facts

Long-distance teleportation over great distances due to decoherence in optical fiber or the atmosphere; Creation of reliable quantum repeaters for long-range action; Teleportation of massive particles and complex states; Integration into scalable quantum networks
