Quantum Teleportation

📊 0 articles
The transfer of a quantum state of a particle over a distance using quantum entanglement and classical communication. The particle itself does not move, but its state is 'copied' onto another particle, destroying the original. It's like scanning a document, printing a copy, and burning the original—information is transferred, but not matter.
💡 Quantum teleportation does not violate relativity because classical information is transmitted at or below the speed of light, and the state itself is teleported instantly but cannot be read without the classical key.

History

Theoretically proposed by Bennett et al. in 1993. The first experimental realization was by Anton Zeilinger's group in 1997 for photons. Later, states of atoms, ions, and even qubits in superconducting circuits were teleported. The distance record is over 1400 km (Micius satellite, 2017).

How it works

Alice has a particle A in an unknown state. She and Bob share an entangled pair of particles B and C. Alice performs a measurement on A and B, which destroys A's state and entangles B and C. Alice sends the measurement result to Bob via a classical channel. Bob applies a corresponding transformation to C, and C takes on the original state of A. Without classical communication, teleportation is impossible.
