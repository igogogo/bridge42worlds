coherent state

📊 0 articles

Light in which photons march synchronously, like a drill column. The coherent state is the most predictable of all quantum objects, almost without surprises.

In practice: Without coherent states, there would be no lasers, optical interferometers, and holograms—it is a fundamental tool of modern optics.
A coherent state is a family of quantum states of the harmonic oscillator (e.g., an electromagnetic field mode) that minimize the Heisenberg uncertainty relation. Mathematically, it is an eigenfunction of the annihilation operator a, a|α⟩ = α|α⟩, where the complex number α specifies the mean amplitude and phase. Expanding in number (Fock) states reveals a Poissonian photon statistics: the probability of detecting exactly n photons is e^{-|α|²} |α|^{2n}/n!. The average energy is ħω|α|². Such states do not spread over time and preserve the shape of the wave packet, which is why they often serve as a bridge between quantum and classical descriptions.
💡 When reflecting coherent light from an ordinary mirror, it remains coherent—losses only reduce α. That's why laser pointers work even after hundreds of kilometers in optical fiber.
Ordinary light from a bulb is chaotic: photons scatter in different directions without agreement. In a laser, however, all photons oscillate in sync, creating a perfectly smooth wave—such a coherent state resembles a choir singing in unison. In it, quantum uncertainty (the inevitable imprecision in knowing properties) is reduced to the minimum possible, like a well-oiled pendulum.

History

Erwin Schrödinger discovered this state in 1926 while trying to find a quantum description as close as possible to the familiar motion of a pendulum. Later, with the invention of the laser, it became a practical tool.

How it works

Imagine a swing: if you push it rhythmically, the amplitude grows and the oscillation becomes smooth. In the quantum world, the 'swing' is the electromagnetic field. A coherent state arises when the field is pumped by a laser—it oscillates with a well-defined amplitude (extent) and phase (shift). In this case, the uncertainty is evenly distributed: we are slightly wrong in both amplitude and phase, but the total error is minimal.

💡 Coherent states form a so-called 'overcomplete' family: any two are not orthogonal, but the set of all states on the complex α-plane allows expansion of any operator. This is used in quantum tomography.
Coherent states |α⟩, also known as Glauber states, form an overcomplete basis in Fock space. They are obtained by the action of the displacement operator D(α) = exp(α a† − α* a) on the vacuum: |α⟩ = D(α)|0⟩. The mean values of the quadratures X₁ = (a + a†)/√2 and X₂ = (a − a†)/(i√2) are Re(α) and Im(α) respectively, and the variances of both quadratures are equal: ⟨(ΔX₁)²⟩ = ⟨(ΔX₂)²⟩ = 1/2 (in photon units), which gives the minimum possible product allowed by the Heisenberg uncertainty principle. Under free evolution with the Hamiltonian H = ħω(a†a + 1/2), the state remains coherent, only the phase α rotates: α(t) = α(0) e^{-iωt}. This makes them ideal information carriers in quantum communications.

History

Schrödinger (1926) first formulated the concept of non-spreading wave packets for the oscillator. In 1963–65, Glauber, Sudarshan, and Klauder independently rediscovered coherent states in the context of quantum optics, showing that they describe ideal laser radiation. Glauber received the 2005 Nobel Prize for this work. In subsequent decades, coherent states found applications in atomic physics (coherent population trapping), condensed matter physics (Bose-Einstein condensates), and quantum information.

How it works

Generation of coherent states is achieved by a classical current interacting with the quantum field. The Schrödinger equation with the interaction Hamiltonian H_int = iħ(ϵ a† − ϵ* a) yields the evolution of the displacement operator, creating a coherent state from the vacuum. The stability under time evolution is due to the preservation of quadrature variances. However, interaction with a thermal bath leads to loss of coherence (damping)—the average photon number decreases exponentially, but the state remains coherent. This robustness is used in laser interferometry: even after traveling long distances, the interference pattern is determined by the phase α.

a | \alpha \rangle = \alpha | \alpha \rangle
a is the photon annihilation operator (lowers the photon number in the mode by 1), |α⟩ is a coherent state (vector in Fock space), α is a complex number whose modulus determines the mean field amplitude (mean photon number ⟨n⟩ = |α|²), and the argument determines the mean phase.

Open problems & fun facts

Can coherent states of the gravitational field be created to test quantum gravity?; Experiments with optical coherent states are limited by losses at high intensities (photon noise).; Discrimination of a coherent state with an unknown phase is an NP-hard problem.
