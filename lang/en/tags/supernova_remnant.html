supernova remnant

2

A stellar explosion scatters matter, leaving behind an expanding cloud — a cosmic jellyfish glowing for thousands of years.

In practice: Supernova remnants are natural accelerators of cosmic rays and factories of heavy elements; their emission helps study the interstellar medium and explosion dynamics.
A supernova remnant is an expanding shell of ejected material and swept-up interstellar matter. Inside lies a hot, tenuous bubble bounded by a shock wave that heats gas to X-ray emitting temperatures. Several morphological types exist: shell-type (like Cassiopeia A), plerionic (powered by a pulsar wind, like the Crab), and mixed. Supernova remnants enrich the interstellar medium with heavy elements and launch cosmic rays.

History

Supernova remnants have been observed since antiquity: the Crab Nebula is the result of an explosion recorded by Chinese astronomers in 1054. In the 20th century, Fritz Zwicky and Walter Baade linked them to neutron stars.

How it works

The shock wave pushes matter at thousands of kilometers per second, sweeping up interstellar gas. Collisions heat the gas to millions of degrees, making it glow in X-rays — much like a supersonic aircraft's trail turns into a cloud, but much hotter.

💡 The Crab Nebula expands at about 1,500 km/s — while you read this sentence, its diameter increases by a distance comparable to Earth's size.
When a massive star exhausts its fuel, it collapses and explodes as a supernova. The outer layers are blown off, forming an expanding cloud of gas and dust — a supernova remnant. It's like a burst balloon with glitter: the shell flies outward in all directions, leaving a compact object (neutron star or black hole) inside.

A supernova remnant is the product of the interaction between ejected stellar material and the surrounding interstellar medium. Evolutionarily, stages include free expansion, Sedov-Taylor (adiabatic), thin shell formation, and transition to a turbulent cloud. Typical explosion energy ~10⁵¹ erg, ejecta mass several solar masses. In plerions, a central pulsar injects energetic particles, creating synchrotron radiation. Supernova remnants are the primary sources of cosmic rays up to the knee energy (~3×10¹⁵ eV).

History

Fritz Zwicky in 1934 predicted neutron stars as supernova remnants. The discovery of pulsars by Jocelyn Bell Burnell in 1967 confirmed this connection. Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar earlier calculated the white dwarf mass limit, indicating the inevitability of collapse for more massive stars. Advances in X-ray astronomy (Chandra, XMM-Newton) have allowed detailed study of remnant dynamics and chemical composition.

How it works

The shock wave propagates through the interstellar medium according to momentum and energy conservation. The Sedov-Taylor solution for a point explosion in a uniform medium is applied: radius depends on time as R ~ t^{2/5}. High-energy electrons in magnetic fields produce synchrotron emission, explaining radio glow. Heavy elements (oxygen, silicon, iron) synthesized in the star are ejected and mixed with interstellar gas, influencing subsequent star formation.

R \approx \left(\frac{E t^2}{\rho}\right)^{1/5}
R — shock wave radius, E — kinetic energy of the explosion (~10^{51} erg), t — time after explosion, ρ — density of the interstellar medium
