Helium (He) is the second element of the Periodic Table, a noble gas with atomic number 2. It makes up about 24% of the baryonic mass of the Universe, second only to hydrogen. It is formed mainly in stellar nucleosynthesis and during primordial nucleosynthesis after the Big Bang. On Earth, helium is extracted from natural gas, where it accumulates due to radioactive decay of heavy elements. Unique properties of helium, such as superfluidity at temperatures below 2.17 K, make it an ideal laboratory for studying quantum macroscopic phenomena.

History

Helium was first detected in 1868 during a solar eclipse by Frenchman Jules Janssen and Englishman Norman Lockyer by a bright yellow line in the spectrum of the chromosphere. Lockyer named the element after Helios, the Greek god of the sun. Only in 1895 did William Ramsay isolate helium from the mineral cleveite, proving its earthly existence. Johann Balmer made an important contribution to spectral methods, whose formula for hydrogen stimulated the search for new elements in stars. Later, Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin showed that helium is one of the main elements of stellar matter.

How it works

Helium is formed as a result of thermonuclear fusion: in the proton-proton chain, four protons merge into a helium nucleus, releasing energy according to the mass-energy equivalence E=mc². As a noble gas, it is chemically inert. Superfluidity of helium-4 at temperatures below the lambda point (2.17 K) is explained by the bosonic nature of atoms and is described by quantum mechanics equations (the Schrödinger equation for a macrosystem), where the macroscopic wave function leads to absence of viscosity and quantized vortices.
