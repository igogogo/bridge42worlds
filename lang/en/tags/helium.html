Helium is the second lightest and second most abundant element in the universe. It is an inert gas with no color or odor. Imagine inflating a balloon: if filled with helium, it rises because helium is lighter than air.

History

Helium was first detected in 1868 by astronomer Pierre Janssen during a solar eclipse as an unknown spectral line in the Sun's spectrum. The name comes from the Greek 'Helios' — Sun. On Earth, helium was isolated from uranium ore in 1895 by William Ramsay.

How it works

Helium is produced in stars through nuclear fusion of hydrogen. It is also a product of radioactive decay of uranium and thorium in the Earth's crust. Helium is used in cryogenics (cooling superconductors), in breathing mixtures for deep-sea diving, and as a shielding gas in welding.
