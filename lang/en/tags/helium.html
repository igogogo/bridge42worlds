Helium — the second lightest element: an invisible gas from stellar furnaces that makes voices squeaky and cools the most powerful magnets.

In practice: Used in breathing mixtures for deep-sea diving, in helium-neon lasers, and for cooling superconducting magnets in medical tomographs.
Helium is the second element of the periodic table, a colorless and odorless noble gas. Its atom contains two protons (the He-4 isotope has two neutrons, He-3 has one). The filled electron shell makes helium chemically inert. Under normal conditions, it is a monatomic gas (atoms do not form molecules) with very low density. When liquid helium-4 is cooled to 2.17 K, it transitions to a superfluid state: it flows without friction and has enormous thermal conductivity.

History

Helium was first noticed not on Earth but on the Sun: in 1868, astronomers discovered a bright yellow line in the solar spectrum that did not belong to any known element. It was attributed to a new element, named helium after Helios, the god of the Sun. On Earth, helium was isolated only in 1895 from uranium ore.

How it works

Helium is extracted from natural gas, where it accumulates over millions of years as a product of radioactive decay of heavy elements. The unusual properties of helium at low temperatures are explained by quantum effects: its atoms are so light that when cooled they behave like waves, creating superfluidity — frictionless flow.

💡 Helium is the only simple substance that remains liquid at absolute zero temperature (at normal pressure); quantum fluctuations prevent it from freezing.

Helium is the second element of the periodic system, an inert noble gas. Its nucleus (α-particle) is exceptionally stable, making helium the end product of thermonuclear reactions in stars. The He-4 isotope is a boson and below the lambda point (2.17 K) transitions to a superfluid state, described by Landau's two-fluid model. Quantized vortices appear and the fountain effect is possible. He-3, being a fermion, becomes superfluid only at millikelvin temperatures due to the formation of Cooper pairs, similar to superconductivity.

History

In 1868, Pierre Janssen and Norman Lockyer independently discovered a bright yellow line in the solar spectrum; Lockyer suggested it belonged to a new element — helium. In 1895, William Ramsay isolated helium from the mineral cleveite, and Per Teodor Cleve and Nils Langlet determined its atomic mass. In 1908, Heike Kamerlingh Onnes first obtained liquid helium; superfluidity in He-4 was discovered in 1937 by Pyotr Kapitsa, with theoretical explanation provided by Landau and others.

How it works

Superfluidity of helium-4 is explained by Bose-Einstein condensation: a macroscopic fraction of atoms transitions to the ground state with zero momentum. Quantum coherence of the condensate over large distances leads to dissipationless flow. For He-3, superfluidity arises from the formation of pairs with antiparallel spins (analogous to Cooper pairs), described by the Ginzburg–Landau equation. In astrophysics, helium is synthesized in stellar interiors (proton-proton and CNO cycles) and during primordial nucleosynthesis after the Big Bang.
