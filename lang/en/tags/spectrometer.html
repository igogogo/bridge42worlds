spectrometer

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

Rainbow meter: breaks light into colored stripes and uses them to determine the composition of distant stars.

In practice: In medicine, mass spectrometers detect traces of drugs in blood, while optical spectrometers test water quality by identifying contaminants.
A spectrometer is an instrument for measuring the distribution of electromagnetic radiation by wavelength or frequency. Main components: an entrance slit, a dispersive element (prism or diffraction grating), focusing optics, and a detector (CCD array, photomultiplier). Spectrometers are distinguished by range: ultraviolet, visible, infrared, X-ray, etc. A spectrograph is an instrument that records the entire spectrum simultaneously. Key characteristics are spectral resolution and light throughput.

History

The first prototype of a spectrometer was a glass prism used by Isaac Newton in 1666 to decompose sunlight. Later, Joseph von Fraunhofer added a diffraction grating and saw dark lines in the Sun's spectrum.

How it works

Light passes through a narrow slit and hits an element that separates it by wavelength—this could be a prism (refracts violet more strongly) or a diffraction grating (reflects different colors at different angles). The detector records how much light arrives at each microscopic section. This constructs the spectrum—a graph of brightness versus wavelength.

💡 Every chemical element leaves a unique “fingerprint” in the spectrum—a set of bright lines. This is how astronomers determined that the Sun and other stars are composed mainly of hydrogen and helium.
A spectrometer is an instrument that, like a prism, splits light into colors. But it doesn't just show a rainbow: it precisely measures how much of each color is in the light. This way we can find out what the light source is made of—sodium gives yellow, hydrogen gives red and blue-violet.

A spectrometer is a measuring instrument that allows quantitative analysis of the spectral composition of electromagnetic radiation. In general, it includes a radiation source (external or built-in), a dispersive system, an optical scheme for forming a parallel beam and focusing, a detector, and a data processing system. The basis of operation is the conversion of the intensity-wavelength dependence into a spatial distribution, which is recorded by a multi-element photodetector. Dispersion can be achieved by refraction in a prism (refractive) or by diffraction on a periodic structure. Modern spectrometers use concave holographic gratings, which allow combining dispersion and focusing onto a flat detector. Absolute wavelength calibration is performed using reference sources (gas-discharge lamps with known lines). Spectrometers are classified by detection principle (scanning, with a stationary detector; single-beam, double-beam), by modulation type (dispersive, interference—Fourier spectrometers), by spectral region. Mass spectrometers measure not wavelengths but masses of charged particles, but also belong to analytical instruments. Astronomical spectrometers are often coupled with telescopes and have high resolution (R = λ/Δλ up to 100,000 and above) for studying stellar atmospheres, the interstellar medium, and the redshift of galaxies.

History

The history of the spectrometer began with the experiments of Isaac Newton in 1666, who decomposed sunlight with a prism. Newton introduced the term “spectrum,” calling the colored stripe a “ghost” (spectrum). In 1814, Joseph von Fraunhofer, replacing the prism with a diffraction grating of his own making, discovered dark lines in the solar spectrum (Fraunhofer lines) and measured their wavelengths. This laid the foundation for astrospectroscopy. In 1859, Gustav Kirchhoff and Robert Bunsen created the first spectroscope with a gas burner and prism, linking spectral lines to chemical elements. In the 20th century, the development of electronics led to photoelectric registration. Today, space observatories are equipped with spectrometers operating from X-ray to infrared ranges (for example, the STIS instrument on the Hubble telescope).

How it works

The principle of an optical spectrometer: the radiation under study passes through an entrance slit to a collimator, which forms a parallel beam. Then the beam hits a dispersing element—for example, a reflective diffraction grating with rules. According to the diffraction condition d(sin α ± sin β) = mλ, where d is the grating period, α and β are the angles of incidence and diffraction, m is the order, light of different wavelengths is deflected at different angles. Then a focusing mirror forms images of the slit in monochromatic light on the detector (CCD line or array). The signal intensity from each pixel is proportional to the photon flux of the corresponding wavelength. Calibration converts pixel number into wavelength. An alternative type is the Fourier spectrometer: it uses a Michelson interferometer, where the interferogram (intensity dependence on path difference) is measured, and the spectrum is recovered by Fourier transformation. The advantage is high resolution and light throughput.
