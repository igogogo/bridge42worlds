Xenon

📊 0 articles
PopularSimpleAdvanced
Xenon (Xe) is a heavy, inert, colorless, odorless gas that produces a bright bluish-white light in an electrical discharge. It is like an 'exotic' guest: the rarest of the stable noble gases in the atmosphere (0.0000087%). Used in powerful flash lamps, anesthesia, and ion thrusters for spacecraft.
💡 Xenon ion thrusters are used on many spacecraft, including the Dawn probe, which visited Vesta and Ceres, and operated continuously for years.

History

Xenon was discovered in 1898 by William Ramsay and Morris Travers, who isolated it from the residue after evaporating liquid air. The name comes from the Greek 'xenos' (stranger). In 1962, the first chemical compound of xenon — xenon hexafluoroplatinate — was obtained, disproving the notion of complete inertness of noble gases.

How it works

Xenon has a high atomic mass and is easily ionized, making it ideal for ion thrusters: accelerated xenon ions create thrust. In medicine, xenon is used as an inhalational anesthetic with minimal side effects. In everyday life, xenon lamps produce light close to daylight and are used in car headlights and movie projectors.
