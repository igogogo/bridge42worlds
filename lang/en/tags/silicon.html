silicon

📊 0 articles

Hidden in sand and rocks, and in the form of the thinnest wafers it serves as the 'brain' of every digital device.

In practice: Microchips in every phone and laptop; solar panels on roofs; glass and concrete around us.
Silicon (Si) is a metalloid, i.e., an element with properties intermediate between metals and nonmetals. Its atoms are arranged in a diamond-like lattice. It is the second most abundant element in the Earth's crust, found in quartz and silicates. A bandgap of 1.1 eV makes it a classic semiconductor: doping with phosphorus creates free electrons (n-type), and with boron—holes (p-type). The contact of p- and n-regions forms a p–n junction, the basis of diodes, transistors and solar cells.
💡 The whole Silicon Valley is named after this element. The first silicon transistor was made in 1954, and since then the number of transistors on a chip has been doubling approximately every two years (Moore's law).
Silicon is the main element of sand. It is a semiconductor: in pure form it hardly conducts current, but small additions turn it into a controlled conductor. It is this property that allows creating transistors and microchips.

History

Swedish chemist Jöns Jacob Berzelius first obtained pure silicon in 1824. But its electronic talents were discovered much later. In the 20th century, scientists learned to purify silicon and add impurities (doping), creating the transistor—the digital age began.

How it works

Pure silicon blocks the movement of charges, like a roadblock. A small addition of boron creates vacant spots—'holes', through which current is passed along a chain. The addition of phosphorus, conversely, gives extra electrons. By combining regions with different dopants, we get switches that process information.

💡 Pure silicon shines like a metal, but in powder form it looks dark grey. Silicon wafers for chips are polished to atomic smoothness—they are flatter than any mirror.
Silicon crystallizes in the diamond structure (lattice constant 0.543 nm). Its bandgap is indirect: an electron transition between bands requires the participation of a phonon, so silicon emits light weakly but absorbs it effectively. Electronic properties are explained by band theory, based on the Schrödinger equation and the Pauli principle. Silicon chip technology (CMOS) dominates thanks to the high-quality native oxide SiO₂, used as a gate insulator in MOSFETs. Doping introduces impurity levels into the bandgap, controlling the concentration of charge carriers.

History

Jöns Jacob Berzelius isolated amorphous silicon in 1824, and Henri Sainte-Claire Deville obtained crystalline silicon in 1854. The first point-contact transistor (1947) worked on germanium, but silicon quickly replaced it. In 1954, Morris Tanenbaum created the first silicon transistor at Bell Labs. Leo Esaki contributed to the understanding of junctions by investigating quantum tunneling and inventing the tunnel diode. Since then, miniaturization has progressed exponentially, approaching physical limits.

How it works

When n- and p-regions come into contact, a depletion region arises, creating a potential barrier. Under forward bias, the current increases exponentially; under reverse bias, it is blocked. This rectifying property is described by the diode equation. In metal-oxide-semiconductor field-effect transistors (MOSFETs), the voltage on the gate controls the conductivity of the channel. Operation is based on electrostatics, the Poisson equation, and carrier statistics (Fermi–Dirac distribution). In solar cells, the p–n junction separates photogenerated electron-hole pairs, generating an electric current.

SiO₂ — silicon dioxide (quartz, sand), C — carbon (coke), Si — elemental silicon, CO — carbon monoxide (carbon monoxide).

Open problems & fun facts
