Knot theory

📊 0 articles
It is the science of how to distinguish a knot on a rope from a simple tangle — can it be untied without cutting?
💡 The simplest nontrivial knot is the trefoil: it cannot be untied without cutting the rope.
Knot theory studies embeddings of a circle into three-dimensional space up to isotopy. Two knots are considered equivalent if one can be smoothly deformed into the other without self-intersections. The main task is to classify knots using invariants (polynomials, knot groups).

History

The first knot tables were compiled by Peter Tait in the 19th century. The mathematical theory began with the works of Max Dehn and James Alexander in the early 20th century.

How it works

Knots are represented by diagrams — projections onto a plane with crossings indicated. Invariants, such as the Jones polynomial, are computed from the diagram and do not change under isotopy. This allows distinguishing knots.

💡 The Jones polynomial was discovered in 1984 and led to a Nobel Prize in Chemistry for the discovery of topological enzymes.
Knot theory is a branch of topology studying embeddings of the circle S^1 into R^3 (or S^3) up to isotopy. A knot is a closed non-self-intersecting curve. Combinatorially, knots are given by diagrams with crossings. Invariants: knot group (fundamental group of the complement), Alexander polynomial, Jones polynomial, HOMFLY polynomial, linking numbers. Knots can be trivial (circle) or nontrivial. There is a knot sum operation giving a semigroup structure.

History

Peter Tait in the 1870s compiled knot tables up to 10 crossings, attempting to explain atoms (Kelvin's vortex atom theory). Max Dehn in the 1910s solved the problem of recognizing the trivial knot. James Alexander in 1928 introduced the Alexander polynomial. In 1984, Vaughan Jones discovered the Jones polynomial, leading to rapid development. In the 1990s, Vassiliev invariants and knot theory in physics (topological field theory) appeared.

How it works

A knot is represented by a diagram — a projection onto a plane with a finite number of crossings, indicating which strand is on top. Isotopy of a knot corresponds to a sequence of Reidemeister moves on the diagram. Invariants are computed from the diagram and do not change under these moves. For example, the Jones polynomial V_K(t) is a rational function in t satisfying the Kauffman bracket relation.

V_{L_+}(t) - t V_{L_-}(t) = (1 - t^2) V_{L_0}(t)
Relation between Jones polynomials of three links differing at one crossing.

Open problems & fun facts

Problem of recognizing the trivial knot (algorithmically solvable but practically difficult); Conjecture that the Jones polynomial distinguishes all knots (open)
