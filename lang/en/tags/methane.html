Methane (CH₄) is the simplest alkane, a saturated hydrocarbon with an sp³-hybridized carbon atom, forming a tetrahedral structure with an H-C-H angle of ≈ 109.5°. It is the main component of natural gas (70–90%), a powerful greenhouse gas with a global warming potential 28 times higher than CO₂ over 100 years, and an important player in atmospheric chemistry, including the formation of tropospheric ozone and stratospheric water vapor. In astrophysics, methane is a biomarker: its simultaneous presence with oxygen in exoplanet spectra could indicate life.

History

The history of methane begins with alchemical observations of 'flammable air' in swamps. In 1776, Alessandro Volta scientifically described it, isolating it from bottom sediments. In the 19th century, Michael Faraday liquefied methane, and John Tyndall in 1859 proved its greenhouse properties. The modern era is associated with the study of methane hydrates on the ocean floor and its role in the climate system.

How it works

Methane traps heat through vibrational-rotational transitions: its molecule has several normal modes (symmetric and asymmetric C-H stretching, H-C-H bending vibrations) that actively absorb infrared radiation at wavelengths of about 3.3 μm and 7.7 μm. In the atmosphere, it is oxidized by hydroxyl radicals (OH•) over ~9 years, turning into CO₂ and water vapor. According to Planck’s radiation law, the absorption intensity depends on temperature, and the Doppler effect broadens spectral lines in a turbulent atmosphere.
