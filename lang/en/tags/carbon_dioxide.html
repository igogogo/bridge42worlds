Carbon dioxide is an invisible cloud that escapes from the lungs when exhaling. Plants eagerly catch it and, with the help of the sun, turn it into greenery and the sweetness of fruits, like cooks preparing a delicious meal from invisible ingredients.

History

In 1756, Scotsman Joseph Black called CO₂ 'fixed air,' showing that it is released from limestone and absorbed by alkalis. Later, Lavoisier identified it as a compound of carbon and oxygen. In 1859, Irish physicist John Tyndall measured the heat absorption of CO₂, laying the foundations of the greenhouse effect theory.

How it works

In a plant leaf, CO₂ enters through microscopic pores into green cells, where sunlight splits water and forces carbon dioxide to turn into sugars. This process—photosynthesis—feeds almost all living things. In the ocean, CO₂ dissolves, but excess acidifies the water, threatening corals and shells, like a weak acid shower eroding calcareous shells.

💡 The bubbles in soda are carbon dioxide, which fizzes and tickles the tongue. And dry ice, used to cool ice cream in cinemas, is solid carbon dioxide; it doesn't melt into a puddle but sublimates directly into vapor, enveloping everything in fog.
Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is a colorless, odorless gas (in small concentrations) exhaled by all animals. In the atmosphere, it acts like a greenhouse film: it freely transmits solar rays but traps the heat radiated by the Earth. This natural effect makes the planet habitable, but its intensification due to fuel burning threatens overheating.

Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is a linear triatomic molecule with C=O double bonds. At standard conditions, density is 1.98 kg/m³, solubility in water is 1.45 g/L (forming weak carbonic acid). It is the main anthropogenic greenhouse gas; its concentration has risen from 278 ppm (pre-industrial level) to 421 ppm in 2023. It absorbs IR radiation in the bands at 4.3 µm and 15 µm, creating radiative forcing.

History

Black (1756) isolated CO₂ from limestone; Lavoisier (1770s) determined its composition. Tyndall (1859) measured heat absorption. Arrhenius (1896) quantitatively predicted global warming from rising CO₂. Keeling (from 1958) began monitoring at Mauna Loa, proving the trend. The carbon in CO₂ is formed in stars through the triple-alpha process, explained by Fred Hoyle and Margaret Burbidge in 1957.

How it works

The greenhouse effect of CO₂ is based on its ability to absorb Earth's infrared radiation. The Earth radiates as a blackbody with a maximum near 10 µm (Wien's displacement law). Vibrations of the CO₂ molecule (antisymmetric stretching with a dipole moment) resonate with IR photons, re-emitting some energy back to the surface. Radiative forcing depends logarithmically on concentration: ΔF = 5.35·ln(C/C₀). According to the second law of thermodynamics, heat flow from a cold atmosphere to a warm surface is impossible without an external source, so re-emission merely slows cooling.

\Delta F = 5.35 \ln\left(\frac{C}{C_0}\right)
\Delta F is radiative forcing (W/m²), 5.35 is an empirical coefficient, C is the current CO₂ concentration (ppm), C₀ is the pre-industrial concentration (≈ 278 ppm)
