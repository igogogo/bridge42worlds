tidal disruption

📊 0 articles
When a star, wandering in the darkness, gets too close to a mighty black hole, tidal forces tear it to shreds, like a child ripping apart a fluffy toy. Half of the matter flies away, while the other half swirls into a fiery ring around the cosmic predator.
💡 The brightness of such a flare can exceed the luminosity of the entire galaxy where it occurred for several months. But the star itself was ordinary — it was just unlucky with its orbit.
Tidal disruption is a cosmic 'death spiral': passing by a supermassive black hole, a star experiences such different gravitational forces on its near and far sides that it is literally stretched into a thread. Like a plasticine ball brought too close to a powerful fan.

History

The idea was suggested by general relativity: as early as the 1970s, John Wheeler discussed the possible destruction of stars by black holes. The first real candidate — galaxy NGC 5905 with an X-ray flare — was noticed in 1990 by the ROSAT satellite.

How it works

Near the black hole, the tidal acceleration — the difference in gravity on the ends of the star — exceeds its own gravity. The star is 'spaghettified': part of the matter is ejected in a hypervelocity tail, and part quickly falls back, forming an accretion disk and a bright flare.

💡 Sometimes a black hole 'hiccups': some events show periodic repetition of X-ray flares — possibly, stellar debris on an elongated orbit repeatedly passes through periapsis.
A tidal disruption event (TDE) is an astrophysical event in which a star, approaching a supermassive black hole within a distance less than the Roche limit, is destroyed due to the difference in gravitational potential. Part of the matter is ejected into hyperbolic orbits, while the remainder forms an accretion disk, generating a transient flare in UV, X-ray, and optical with a characteristic light curve decay t^{-5/3}.

History

The theoretical prediction appears in the works of John Archibald Wheeler (1970s), who discussed the fate of matter near black holes. In the 1980s, Martin Rees described the phenomenology of the event in detail. The first convincing observations in X-ray (galaxy NGC 5905, ROSAT satellite, 1990) and in UV/optical came at the end of the 20th century. Modern surveys (ZTF, eROSITA) discover dozens of events per year.

How it works

Tidal disruption is initiated when a star of mass M_* and radius R_* approaches a black hole of mass M_BH to a distance less than the tidal radius r_t = R_* (M_BH / M_*)^{1/3}. Tidal forces overcome self-gravity, and the star is stretched into a long stream. About half of the mass escapes beyond gravitational capture and flies away, while the remaining debris rapidly falls back at an accretion rate declining as t^{-5/3}. A compact accretion disk forms, sometimes with a jet; emission is observed in many bands. The law of universal gravitation is applicable here for calculating tidal forces.

r_t = R_* \left(\frac{M_\mathrm{BH}}{M_*}\right)^{1/3}
r_t — tidal radius, R_* — star radius, M_BH — black hole mass, M_* — star mass.
