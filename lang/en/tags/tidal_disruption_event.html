tidal disruption event

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

A star, torn apart by tidal forces of a supermassive black hole. Its material spirals into a disk, producing a bright flare visible even in distant galaxies.

In practice: Tidal disruption events help detect dormant black holes, study their population, and accretion processes in extreme gravity.
A tidal disruption event (TDE) is an astrophysical transient that occurs when a star passes too close to a supermassive black hole. When the tidal radius (the distance where the star's self-gravity balances the tidal force) becomes smaller than the pericenter (closest orbital point), the star is disrupted. About half of the mass falls onto the black hole, forming an accretion disk with a characteristic fallback rate ∝ t^{−5/3}. The disk emits in soft X-rays and ultraviolet, reaching a peak luminosity of ~10⁴⁴ erg/s, then fading over months to years.

History

First described in detail by astrophysicist Martin Rees in 1988. The first candidates were discovered in the 1990s by X-ray telescopes.

How it works

When a star approaches closer than the Roche limit (the minimum distance at which it can hold itself by gravity), the black hole's powerful gravity starts stretching it. Half of the material is flung away, and half is pulled in, forming an accretion disk (a whirlpool of gas). Friction in the disk heats the gas, and it emits in X-rays and ultraviolet.

💡 One of the brightest recorded TDE flares, ASASSN-15lh, was initially thought to be a record supernova, but later turned out to be a star torn apart by a rapidly spinning black hole.
A tidal disruption event is when a star comes too close to a supermassive black hole (an object with such strong gravity that even light cannot escape). Tidal forces (the difference in attraction between the near and far sides of the star) tear it apart. The star's debris begins orbiting the black hole, colliding and heating to millions of degrees – a bright glow appears, which gradually fades.

Tidal disruption events are unique tools for detecting quiescent supermassive black holes in the centers of galaxies. The theoretical model predicts that after disruption, the accretion rate (mass infall speed) follows a t^{−5/3} law, confirmed by observations of optical and X-ray transients. The luminosity can exceed the Eddington limit for a black hole of that mass, leading to outflows and the formation of broad emission lines in the spectrum. Some of the energy may go into relativistic jets, making TDEs sources of high-energy neutrinos and cosmic rays. TDEs also allow studying intermediate-mass black holes and the dynamics of star clusters.

History

The concept of tidal disruption was detailed in the works of Martin Rees (1988), although tidal forces near black holes were considered as early as the 1970s. The first observational confirmation came with the ROSAT mission in the 1990s, detecting soft X-ray flares from inactive galaxies. Modern surveys, such as ASAS-SN and ZTF, find dozens of candidates in the optical, while Swift and XMM-Newton study them in X-rays. Significant contributions came from accretion disk theory and hydrodynamic simulations of disruption.

How it works

A tidal disruption is initiated when the tidal radius R_t ≈ R_* (M_BH/M_*)^{1/3} exceeds the pericenter distance. The star is disrupted, and its material is distributed along orbits with different eccentricities, forming an elliptical stream. The gas, losing energy via shock waves and self-gravity, accumulates, and its return rate to the black hole follows Ṁ ∝ (t/t_fall)^{−5/3}, where t_fall is the fallback time of the most bound material. Accretion may be super-Eddington, generating powerful winds and, in some cases, relativistic jets, explaining radio-loud TDEs. Spectroscopy reveals broad hydrogen and helium lines, as well as blue-shifted absorption lines indicating outflows.

L(t) = L_0 \left(\frac{t}{t_0}\right)^{-5/3}
L(t) – luminosity at time t after peak; L_0 – peak luminosity; t_0 – characteristic time on the order of months, depending on the mass of the black hole and star.
