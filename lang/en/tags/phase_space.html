phase space

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
Phase space is a map of all possible states of a system: where each object is and how fast it is moving. For a pendulum, it is a circle: height and speed always change together, tracing a closed loop.

History

The concept evolved in the works of Hamilton and Jacobi in the 19th century, but Henri Poincaré gave it real depth while studying the stability of the Solar System. He was the first to glimpse the chaotic intertwining of trajectories in phase space.

How it works

The trajectory of a system in phase space never crosses itself—like a thread laid without knots. This fundamental property, discovered by Liouville, means that clouds of states change shape but not volume, like kneading dough.

💡 The phase space of a single particle has six dimensions (three coordinates and three velocities), and a dust speck dancing in a light beam already wanders in a twelve-dimensional world.
Like a weather map where each point is a combination of temperature and pressure, phase space encompasses all conceivable positions and momenta of a system. The motion of planets, the oscillations of molecules—all are paths in a multidimensional landscape.

Phase space is a multidimensional symplectic manifold whose points correspond one-to-one to all possible micro- or macrostates of a classical (or quantum in the limit) dynamical system. It is parameterized by generalized coordinates and their conjugate canonical momenta; dimension 2n for a system with n degrees of freedom. The Hamiltonian flow determines evolution, and Poincaré sections reveal chaos.

History

The geometric interpretation of mechanics goes back to Lagrange and Hamilton (early 19th century). The concept of phase space was formalized by Liouville (1838), who proved the theorem on conservation of phase volume. Henri Poincaré (1890s) applied it to the three-body problem and discovered homoclinic tangles—the seed of chaos theory. In the 20th century, the Krylov–Bogolyubov school and Kolmogorov–Arnold–Moser developed ergodic theory and KAM theory.

How it works

The Hamiltonian vector field is generated by the Poisson bracket with the scalar Hamiltonian H(q,p). Liouville's theorem (a direct consequence of Hamilton's equations) asserts the incompressibility of the phase flow: ∂ρ/∂t + {ρ,H} = 0, where ρ is the probability density. Boltzmann's ergodic hypothesis assumes that the trajectory eventually covers the energy surface uniformly, justifying the replacement of time averages with phase averages in statistical mechanics.

Open problems & fun facts

Quantum phase space: due to the uncertainty principle, the concept of a point in phase space has no exact analogue, and the quasi-probability representation (Wigner function) can take negative values.; Ergodicity and chaos: a rigorous proof of ergodicity for physically realistic systems with long-range forces (gravity, plasma) remains open.; Many-body quantum tomography: reconstructing the high-dimensional phase distribution of entangled states becomes exponentially complex with increasing particle number.
