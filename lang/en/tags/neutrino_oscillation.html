Neutrino oscillation

📊 0 articles

A neutrino, like an actor changing masks, on its way from the Sun transforms from an electron neutrino into a muon neutrino—a quantum transformation. This proves that the particle has mass and allows us to see through the solar core.

In practice: Oscillations confirm that neutrinos have mass and enable studying processes in the center of the Sun and detecting bursts from distant supernovae.
Neutrinos come in three flavors: electron, muon, and tau. Each flavor is not a particle with a definite mass but a mixture of three neutrino states with slightly different masses. Because of the mass difference, these states evolve in time at different rates, so during the neutrino's flight, its flavor periodically changes—we see sometimes electron, sometimes muon, sometimes tau. The amplitude and frequency of such oscillations are determined by the mixing angles and the squared mass differences.
💡 The Sun emits only electron neutrinos, but detectors on Earth catch only about a third of them—the rest transformed into muon and tau neutrinos along the way. Thus oscillations resolved the long-standing 'solar neutrino puzzle'.
Neutrinos come in three flavors—electron, muon, and tau—associated with corresponding charged particles. Like a spinning coin that appears as heads or tails while spinning, a neutrino can spontaneously change its flavor. This is possible only because neutrinos have a very small but nonzero mass.

History

The idea of the neutrino was born in 1930 when Wolfgang Pauli postulated this particle to explain energy loss in beta decay. Oscillations were predicted by Bruno Pontecorvo in 1957, who suggested that neutrinos of different flavors could transform into each other. Definitive confirmation came in 1998 from the Super-Kamiokande experiment in Japan, which detected the transformation of atmospheric neutrinos.

How it works

Each neutrino flavor is a mixture of three quantum states with different masses. As they travel, these states accumulate different phase shifts—like two pendulums of different lengths that start in sync but gradually lose synchronization. As a result, the composition of the mixture changes, and we see one flavor then another.

💡 A beam of muon neutrinos, passing through the Earth, transforms into electron and tau neutrinos, which is recorded by distant detectors—this is how long-baseline experiments such as T2K and DUNE work.
Neutrino oscillations are a quantum effect in which the flavor of a neutrino (electron, muon, or tau) changes as it propagates. The flavor states in which neutrinos participate in weak interactions are superpositions of mass states (ν₁, ν₂, ν₃) with different masses. The connection between them is given by the unitary PMNS matrix. Since the masses of the states are unequal, they acquire different phase advances, leading to periodic changes in the probability of detecting a particular flavor as a function of the distance traveled and the neutrino energy. Key parameters are the squared mass differences Δm²₂₁ and Δm²₃₁, mixing angles θ₁₂, θ₂₃, θ₁₃, and the phase δ, responsible for CP symmetry violation.

History

The existence of the neutrino was hypothesized by Wolfgang Pauli in 1930 to explain the beta decay spectrum. The possibility of oscillations between neutrino flavors was first considered by Bruno Pontecorvo in 1957. Experimental indications appeared in the 1960s in the Homestake experiment, which recorded a deficit of solar neutrinos. Conclusive evidence was obtained in 1998 at the Super-Kamiokande facility (for atmospheric neutrinos) and in 2001–2002 at the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (for solar neutrinos). For the discovery of oscillations, Takaaki Kajita and Arthur McDonald were awarded the 2015 Nobel Prize.

How it works

Flavor and mass states are related by the unitary matrix U (PMNS): |ν_α⟩ = Σ_i U_{αi} |ν_i⟩. In vacuum, the probability of transition from flavor α to β is given by: P = | Σ_{i} U_{βi} U^*_{αi} exp(-i Δm_{i1}² L / 2E) |². In the two-neutrino approximation, P = sin²(2θ) sin²(1.27 Δm² [eV²] L [km] / E [GeV]). When traveling in matter, such as the Sun, elastic scattering on electrons adds an effective potential for electron neutrinos (the Mikheyev–Smirnov–Wolfenstein effect), leading to resonant enhancement of oscillations. The existence of oscillations proves a nonzero neutrino mass and violation of lepton flavor number. The value of the CP-violating phase δ is still unknown; its nonzero value could explain the observed matter–antimatter asymmetry.

P_{\alpha \to \beta} = \sin^2(2\theta) \sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta m^2 L}{4 E}\right)
θ — mixing angle; Δm² = m₂² − m₁² — squared mass difference (eV²); L — distance from source to detector; E — neutrino energy (eV). In practical units, the argument is often written as 1.27 · Δm² · L / E, where Δm² in eV², L in km, E in GeV.

Open problems & fun facts

Measurement of the absolute neutrino mass scale (oscillations give only squared mass differences).; Determination of the CP-violating phase δ to test the hypothesis about the origin of baryon asymmetry.; Establishment of the mass hierarchy (normal or inverted).; Search for sterile neutrinos—particles that do not participate in weak interactions.
