Chaos theory

📊 0 articles
Chaos theory studies systems where small changes lead to huge consequences — like the flap of a butterfly's wing causing a hurricane.
💡 The butterfly effect is a term from chaos theory, introduced by Edward Lorenz in the 1960s.
Chaos theory describes deterministic systems that are extremely sensitive to initial conditions. A small difference in initial data quickly leads to completely different trajectories. Examples: weather, turbulence, population fluctuations.

History

Foundations were laid by Edward Lorenz in 1963 while studying convection models. Also important are Henri Poincaré's works on the three-body problem.

How it works

The system is described by nonlinear equations. Due to sensitivity to initial conditions, long-term prediction is impossible, although the system itself is deterministic. The Lorenz attractor is a classic example of a strange attractor.

💡 Chaos theory is used for encryption: chaotic systems can generate pseudorandom numbers.
Chaos theory is a branch of mathematics and physics that studies dynamical systems exhibiting deterministic chaos. Key properties: sensitivity to initial conditions, topological transitivity, density of periodic points. Chaotic systems are nonlinear and often have strange attractors — fractal sets in phase space. Lyapunov exponents measure the rate of divergence of trajectories. Examples: Lorenz system, logistic map, double pendulum.

History

Henri Poincaré in the 1890s discovered chaotic behavior in the three-body problem. Edward Lorenz in 1963, working with a convection model, discovered the strange attractor. In the 1970s, Mitchell Feigenbaum discovered universality in the period-doubling scenario. Benoit Mandelbrot introduced fractals, closely related to chaos. In the 1980s, chaos theory became popular and found applications in biology, economics, meteorology.

How it works

A dynamical system is defined by a map or differential equation. For chaos, the system must be nonlinear and have at least three degrees of freedom (for continuous systems). Lyapunov exponents λ: if λ>0, trajectories diverge exponentially. Strange attractor — an attracting set with fractal dimension. Bifurcations — qualitative changes in behavior as parameters vary.

\lambda = \lim_{t\to\infty} \frac{1}{t} \ln \frac{|\delta x(t)|}{|\delta x(0)|}
Average rate of exponential divergence of nearby trajectories.

Open problems & fun facts

Development of prediction methods in chaotic systems on short timescales; Understanding the connection between chaos and quantum mechanics (quantum chaos)

💡 Chaos theory is used for encryption: chaotic systems can generate pseudorandom numbers.
