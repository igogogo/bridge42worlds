💡 The weak interaction is the only force that can change the type (flavor) of quarks, which is necessary for thermonuclear reactions in stars.
The weak interaction is one of the four fundamental forces, responsible for beta decay and other processes involving neutrinos. It acts over very short distances (10⁻¹⁸ m) and is mediated by W and Z bosons.

History

The weak interaction was first described by Fermi in 1934 to explain beta decay. The electroweak theory unified it with electromagnetism in the 1960s.

How it works

Particles exchange heavy W or Z bosons, leading to the transformation of one particle into another, for example, a neutron into a proton.

💡 The weak interaction violates parity (P-symmetry) and CP-symmetry, which explains the predominance of matter over antimatter.
The weak interaction is a fundamental interaction responsible for processes involving changes in quark and lepton flavor. It is mediated by massive W⁺, W⁻, and Z⁰ bosons. It has a small coupling constant and a very short range. It plays a key role in nuclear reactions in the Sun.

History

In 1934, Fermi created the first theory of beta decay. In 1956, Wu experimentally discovered parity violation. In the 1960s, Glashow, Salam, and Weinberg unified the weak and electromagnetic interactions. W and Z bosons were discovered in 1983.

How it works

The weak interaction occurs through the exchange of virtual W or Z bosons. For example, in beta decay, a neutron (udd) transforms into a proton (uud), emitting an electron and an antineutrino. The W boson carries charge, the Z boson mediates neutral currents.

\mathcal{M} \propto \frac{g^2}{q^2 - M_W^2}
The amplitude is proportional to the square of the coupling constant and inversely proportional to the square of the transferred momentum minus the W mass.

Open problems & fun facts

The origin of CP violation is not fully explained in the Standard Model.
