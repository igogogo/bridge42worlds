A meteorite is a messenger from deep space, a stone that survived a fiery flight through the atmosphere and holds the secrets of the Solar System's birth.

In practice: Meteorites are free samples of ancient material, allowing us to study the composition of the protoplanetary cloud without space missions and find organic molecules—the building blocks of life.
Meteorites are solid extraterrestrial bodies that have fallen onto a planet's surface. Sizes range from millimeter-sized dust to blocks several meters across. The main source is asteroids, but fragments from the Moon and Mars are also found. By composition, they are classified as stony meteorites (including chondrites and achondrites), iron meteorites, and stony-iron meteorites. Chondrites contain chondrules—millimeter-sized spheres formed by rapid cooling of matter in the protoplanetary disk.
💡 The Allende meteorite, which fell in Mexico in 1969, is 4.567 billion years old—older than Earth.
A meteorite is a piece of cosmic rock or metal that has landed on Earth's surface. Entering the atmosphere, it heats up and produces a bright flash—a meteor, or 'shooting star'. What does not burn up and reaches the ground is called a meteorite. They come in stony, iron, and stony-iron varieties.

History

People have long found unusual stones, but for a long time they did not believe in their celestial origin. In the early 19th century, a major meteorite shower in France and a thorough investigation by scientist Jean-Baptiste Biot convinced the scientific world that stones indeed fall from the sky.

How it works

Meteorites are fragments of asteroids, less often comets or other planets. As a result of collisions, they are ejected into interplanetary space and, after millions of years, may cross Earth's orbit. Just as a river brings stones from the mountains, cosmic trajectories deliver these celestial wanderers to us.

💡 The Murchison meteorite contains over 70 different amino acids, many of which are absent in terrestrial life, providing direct evidence of their extraterrestrial synthesis.
A meteorite is extraterrestrial material delivered to Earth upon impact. Sources include asteroids (fragments from collisions), comets (rarely), and planets (ejecta from the Moon and Mars). Meteorites are valuable objects for studying the chemical and mineralogical composition of the early Solar System, processes of planetary differentiation, and age determination via isotopic systems (e.g., uranium-lead, potassium-argon). Carbonaceous chondrites are especially important, being rich in water and organics, including amino acids, which is relevant for the panspermia hypothesis—the possible transfer of life through space.

History

The decisive moment in the recognition of meteorites was the event of 1803: after the meteorite shower in L'Aigle (France), Jean-Baptiste Biot collected eyewitness accounts and samples, convincing the Academy of Sciences. Meteoritics has developed at the intersection of astronomy, geology, and physics. X-ray diffraction and mass spectrometry have enabled detailed study of minerals and isotopic composition. The discovery of presolar grains—microscopic dust particles that survived supernova explosions before the birth of the Sun—opened a new chapter in the study of the history of matter.

How it works

Entering the atmosphere at speeds from 11 to 72 km/s, a meteoroid undergoes strong aerodynamic heating, melting, and mass loss (ablation). The glow is caused by the shock wave and ionization of air. Equations of motion considering drag and ablation allow calculation of trajectory and final mass. The annual influx of meteoritic material is about 40,000 tons, predominantly in the form of micrometeorites. Networks of observatory cameras (e.g., the Fireball Network) record bright fireballs, enabling reconstruction of the meteoroid's orbit. Mineralogical analysis relies on X-ray diffraction and Bragg's law: nλ = 2d sinθ.
