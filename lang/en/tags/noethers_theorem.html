Noether's theorem

📊 0 articles
Noether's theorem says: if there is a symmetry in nature, then there is also something that is conserved.
💡 Thanks to this theorem, we know that energy is conserved because the laws of physics do not change over time.
Noether's theorem is one of the deepest statements in physics: every continuous symmetry of a physical system corresponds to a conservation law. For example, symmetry under rotations leads to conservation of angular momentum. This links the beauty of symmetry to practical laws.

History

The theorem was proved by Emmy Noether in 1918 while working on problems of general relativity. It became one of the most important results in mathematical physics.

How it works

If the action of a system does not change under some continuous transformation (e.g., time shift), then it implies the existence of a conserved quantity (energy). Mathematically, this is expressed through the Noether current, whose divergence is zero.

💡 Emmy Noether was a female mathematician in early 20th century Germany, and she had to lecture under a man's name to be allowed to do so.
Noether's theorem establishes an exact correspondence between continuous symmetries of a physical system and conservation laws. If the action is invariant under a one-parameter group of transformations, then there exists a conserved quantity (first integral). The theorem applies both in classical mechanics and in field theory. It explains the origin of conservation laws of energy, momentum, angular momentum, electric charge, and others.

History

Emmy Noether proved the theorem in 1918 in Göttingen, helping David Hilbert and Felix Klein understand conservation laws in general relativity. The result was published in the paper 'Invariante Variationsprobleme'. The theorem became the foundation for understanding the role of symmetries in physics and inspired the development of gauge theories.

How it works

Consider an action S = ∫ L d^4x invariant under transformations with parameter ε. The variation of the action is zero, leading to the equation ∂_μ j^μ = 0, where j^μ is the Noether current. The integral of j^0 over space gives the conserved charge Q. For example, for translational symmetry, j^μ = T^{μν} δx_ν, and Q is energy-momentum.

\partial_\mu j^\mu = 0
The divergence of the current is zero, which means the corresponding charge is conserved.

Open problems & fun facts

Noether's theorem does not work for discrete symmetries (e.g., time reversal)—there are no conservation laws for them.; In quantum field theory, anomalies can occur—symmetry breaking by quantum effects, which modifies conservation laws.
