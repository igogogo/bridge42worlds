superfluidity

📊 0 articles

Superfluid helium seeps through microscopic slits, as if friction vanished entirely — the fluid flows without energy loss.

In practice: Superfluidity helps study quantum effects in the macroscopic world and cool superconducting magnets.
Superfluidity is a macroscopic quantum effect where a fluid moves without dissipation, has zero viscosity, and can flow through capillaries without resistance. It is observed in liquid helium-4 (bosons) at 2.17 K and in helium-3 (fermions) at about 2.6 mK.

History

Discovered in 1937 by Pyotr Kapitsa (not in the list) and independently by John Allen and Don Misener. Lev Landau explained it with a quantum theory. Later, scientists understood that superfluidity is linked to the condensation of atoms into a single quantum state.

How it works

At ultra-low temperatures, helium atoms behave like one giant quantum particle (Bose-Einstein condensate), moving coherently without collisions that cause friction.

💡 Superfluid helium-4 creates a fountain up to 40 cm high at the slightest thermal disturbance — this thermomechanical effect looks like a sudden geyser in a laboratory vessel.
Superfluidity is a state of certain liquids at very low temperatures (below –271°C for helium-4) where viscosity (internal friction) becomes zero. The fluid flows without resistance, can 'creep' out of a container by climbing up the walls as a thin film, and can pass through the tiniest pores.

Superfluidity arises below the lambda point (2.17 K for He-4) and is described by Landau's two-fluid model: the normal component carries entropy, while the superfluid component moves without friction. For He-3, superfluidity appears at ~2.6 mK due to Cooper pairing of atoms (fermions form pairs — bosons). Superfluidity also manifests in dilute atomic Bose condensates held in optical traps. Quantized circulation (vorticity) and the existence of a critical velocity are fundamental properties of the superfluid component.

History

In 1937, Pyotr Kapitsa in Cambridge discovered almost complete disappearance of viscosity in helium-4 below 2.17 K. Simultaneously, John Allen and Don Misener observed the fountain effect. In 1941, Lev Landau built a theoretical model, introducing the concepts of superfluid and normal components. The idea of connection with Bose-Einstein condensation, predicted by Satyendra Nath Bose and Albert Einstein, was proposed by Fritz London (not in the list). Superfluidity of He-3 was discovered in 1972 by Douglas Osheroff, Robert Richardson, and David Lee, and explained via Cooper pairing with anisotropy.

How it works

Superfluidity of helium-4 is due to the bosonic nature of atoms. At low temperatures, a macroscopic fraction of atoms occupies a single quantum state — a Bose-Einstein condensate forms. Elementary excitations — phonons and rotons — define the spectrum. According to Nöther's theorem, spontaneous breaking of gauge symmetry leads to a gapless mode of oscillations, providing superfluidity. The superfluid component flows without viscosity until the velocity exceeds a critical value determined by Landau's criterion. Superfluidity in helium-3 is more complex: atoms are fermions, they form Cooper pairs, turning into bosons, which then condense. This is analogous to superconductivity but with p-wave pairing, leading to anisotropic properties.
