color charge

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
Color charge is a special property of quarks and gluons, like paint on balls. Only instead of paints there are three colors: red, green, and blue. It helps particles 'communicate' via the strong interaction.

History

Oscar Greenberg in 1964 invented color charge to solve the puzzle: how can three identical quarks be in one proton? It turned out they have different 'colors', which saves the Pauli exclusion principle.

How it works

Quarks bombard each other with gluons, swapping colors. The simplest way to stick together is for three quarks with different colors (as in a proton) or a quark and antiquark with opposite colors (as in a pion) to come together. The force between them grows with distance: the further they fly apart, the stronger the attraction.

💡 Quarks constantly change their color, like chameleons in the jungle. But in nature we see only 'colorless' combinations — like light from three colored flashlight beams.
Color charge works like electric charge, but for the strong interaction. Imagine that quarks are fish of three colors (red, green, blue), and the gluon is a fisherman who carries colored worms. Scientists agreed that mixing all three colors gives 'white', neutral — such particles survive.

Color charge is a quantum number characterizing the transformations of quark and gluon fields with respect to the gauge group SU(3) in quantum chromodynamics. For quarks in the fundamental representation, three values are possible (colors: red, green, blue); for antiquarks, anticolors. Gluons are in the adjoint representation and are described by an octet of color states (e.g., red-antigreen). Color charge is strictly conserved in all interactions, but the confinement mechanism prohibits the existence of free colored objects in the low-energy limit.

History

The concept was introduced by Oscar Greenberg in 1964 to explain the symmetry of baryon wave functions. Soon Yoichiro Nambu and Han Moo-Young proposed using a triplet structure. The final formulation in the framework of QCD was completed in the 1970s, when asymptotic freedom was discovered and the role of SU(3) as an exact gauge symmetry was understood. Experimentally, the existence of color charge is confirmed by analysis of hadron production cross sections in e⁺e⁻ collisions (ratio R = σ(e⁺e⁻ → hadrons)/σ(e⁺e⁻ → μ⁺μ⁻)).

How it works

In QCD, each quark current changes color by emitting or absorbing a gluon. The interaction Lagrangian has the form g_s \bar{ψ} γ^μ t^a ψ A_μ^a, where t^a are the generators of SU(3) in the fundamental representation. Gluons themselves carry color, so their self-interaction leads to charge anti-screening: the effective coupling constant α_s increases with decreasing energy scale. This leads to confinement at distances of about 1 fm: the potential V(r) ≈ -4α_s/(3r) + σ r, where σ is the string tension. At high energies (small distances) α_s decreases (asymptotic freedom). Observed hadrons are always white (singlet under SU(3)).

Open problems & fun facts
