quantum algorithm

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
📊 4

A recipe for solving a problem that uses quantum tricks (simultaneous existence of many states and instantaneous connection of particles) to answer faster than any classical computer.

In practice: Shor's algorithm cracks modern encryption systems; Grover's algorithm speeds up search in unordered databases; quantum computing promises breakthroughs in modeling materials and drugs.
Quantum algorithms exploit three key resources: quantum parallelism (superposition), quantum entanglement, and amplitude interference. Instead of enumerating possibilities, they transform the superposition of all input data so that in the end, constructive interference amplifies the amplitude of the correct answer, while incorrect information cancels out. Efficiency is often measured by the number of queries to an oracle — a 'black box' that checks the solution.

History

The first quantum algorithm was devised by David Deutsch in 1985, demonstrating that a quantum computer can solve problems more efficiently than a classical one.

How it works

Qubits become entangled so that a change in one instantly affects another, even at a distance. The algorithm processes the superposition of all input numbers simultaneously, as if computing on an infinite number of calculators in parallel. At the end, measurement collects the result — as if extracting the desired sound from a chorus of voices.

💡 Simon's problem (finding a hidden pattern in a black box), which is solved exponentially faster by a quantum computer, directly inspired Peter Shor to create the factorization algorithm — breaking down a number into prime factors.
A classical program processes data step by step, like a cook following one recipe. A quantum algorithm runs all possible computational paths at once — as if you simultaneously try all moves in a chess game. Then it cleverly discards the wrong options and keeps the correct answer. At the core are qubits (quantum bits) — particles that can be both zero and one at the same time (superposition).

A quantum algorithm is a sequence of unitary operations on a quantum register, which is initialized in a superposition of basis states. Due to the linearity of unitary transformations, the values of a function are computed for all arguments simultaneously. The key technique is amplitude interference: the probabilities of wrong answers cancel out, while the probability of the correct one increases to nearly 1. Algorithms often use the quantum Fourier transform (Shor's algorithm) or iterative amplitude amplification (Grover's algorithm).

History

The theoretical foundations were laid by Richard Feynman in 1982, who pointed out that quantum systems are hard to simulate classically. David Deutsch in 1985 formulated the concept of a quantum Turing machine and gave the first example of a quantum algorithm that requires fewer oracle queries. In 1992, Deutsch and Richard Jozsa generalized it, demonstrating exponential advantage. In 1994, Peter Shor developed the factorization algorithm, and in 1996, Lov Grover proposed the algorithm for quadratic speedup in search.

How it works

A typical quantum algorithm consists of four stages: initialization of the register in a superposition of all input states (e.g., Hadamard transform), application of an oracle (quantum function evaluation), amplification of the correct solution (e.g., Grover iteration or quantum Fourier transform), and measurement. The speedup arises because quantum operations act on amplitudes rather than probabilities, and the number of possible states grows exponentially with the number of qubits.

k = \frac{\pi}{4} \sqrt{N}
k — required number of oracle queries, N — total number of elements.

Open problems & fun facts

Construction of practically useful quantum algorithms that outperform classical ones for real-world problems (optimization, machine learning).; Development of quantum algorithms robust to noise without full error correction.; Achieving quantum supremacy in problems of commercial or scientific value.
