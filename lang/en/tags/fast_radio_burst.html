fast radio burst

📊 2

A cosmic radio pulse lasting a few milliseconds but with energy that the Sun emits over days.

In practice: Used for probing the intergalactic medium and testing cosmological models.
Fast radio bursts — millisecond radio transients (temporary sources) of cosmological origin. Their dispersion measure (the degree of signal stretching by free electrons in the intergalactic medium) indicates enormous distances. A typical burst releases as much energy in a couple of milliseconds as the Sun emits over several days, suggesting the involvement of compact objects — neutron stars or magnetars (neutron stars with record magnetic fields).

History

First discovered in 2007 by Duncan Lorimer and his student David Narkevic while analyzing archival data from the Parkes radio telescope (Australia).

How it works

Most likely, the bursts originate from magnetars — neutron stars (ultra-dense remnants of exploded stars) with exceptionally strong magnetic fields. When this field rearranges, it ejects a powerful radio pulse that then travels for billions of years.

💡 Some fast radio bursts repeat at irregular intervals, like cosmic 'breathing'. The first such repeater, FRB 121102, emitted hundreds of flashes, prompting scientists to build special telescopes to search for them.
A fast radio burst is a sudden flash of radio waves (light invisible to the eye) from distant space. It lasts only a few milliseconds (thousandths of a second) but packs as much energy as the Sun emits over several days. Due to interaction with the rarefied gas between stars (the interstellar medium), signals at different frequencies arrive with different delays — this is called dispersion (pulse stretching). The source is far beyond our Galaxy, often billions of light-years away.

Fast radio bursts (FRBs) are transient radio phenomena with characteristic durations on the order of milliseconds, possessing high peak fluxes (up to several Janskys) and significant dispersion measures (DM), which correlate with redshift, confirming their cosmological nature. FRB spectra often show a steep frequency dependence, consistent with synchrotron radiation or radiation from charged particles in strong magnetic fields. The emission mechanism may involve coherent radiation (where particles emit in phase, amplifying the signal), explaining the observed brightness temperature exceeding 10³⁵ K. The scattering and scintillation properties of the signals indicate plasma turbulence in the propagation medium, allowing indirect investigation of the intergalactic medium and the baryon fraction of the Universe.

History

The first detection of FRB 010724 (also known as the Lorimer burst) occurred in 2007 during analysis of data from the Australian Parkes Observatory radio telescope from July 24, 2001. Duncan Lorimer and David Narkevic identified a single pulse with DM=375 pc/cm³, significantly exceeding the Milky Way contribution, indicating an extragalactic origin. Subsequent discoveries with the Arecibo and CHIME telescopes, including the repeating FRB 121102, confirmed the class of phenomena. Theoretical models include explosive scenarios on magnetars (as in the giant flare model), neutron star mergers, or evaporation of primordial black holes. The discovery of FRB-like emission from the galactic magnetar SGR 1935+2154 in 2020 strengthened the magnetar origin hypothesis.

How it works

FRB emission is probably generated by a sudden release of magnetic energy in the magnetosphere of a neutron star, leading to a relativistic plasma flow that produces coherent radio emission via cyclotron maser-type instabilities or a synchrotron maser. Alternative models consider shock waves from compact object mergers. Dispersion and Faraday rotation show that the radiation passes through magnetized plasma, imposing constraints on electron density and magnetic fields in the intergalactic medium. The exact emission mechanism is not yet established; key challenges include explaining the coherence of the emission and extreme brightness temperatures.
