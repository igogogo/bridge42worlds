fast radio bursts

📊 0 articles
Lightning in the sky flashes for an instant but carries enormous energy. Fast radio bursts are similarly flashes of radio waves from distant space, lasting fractions of a second. One such pulse can release as much energy in a brief moment as the Sun emits over several days.
💡 The first fast radio burst was discovered in archival data years after it was captured by a telescope—so long did it take to find the cause of the strange signal.
Just as a bat’s ultrasonic echolocation bounces off obstacles, a radio burst passing through intergalactic space carries information about the composition of the intergalactic medium. A short radio pulse captured on Earth tells us about the number of electrons along a path of billions of light-years.

History

The precursor to the discovery was a 2007 paper in which Duncan Lorimer and his student reported an anomalous pulse captured by the Australian Parkes radio telescope back in 2001.

How it works

A burst of radiation is born when, for example, the ultra-strong magnetic field of a magnetar suddenly reconfigures, like a spring suddenly uncoiling, releasing stored energy in the form of radio waves.

💡 Some fast radio bursts repeat like a cosmic lighthouse, while others are unique and have never been observed again, hinting at different birth mechanisms.
Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs) are brief (lasting from fractions of a millisecond to several milliseconds) intense radio flashes with enormous energy up to 10^39 ergs. Their dispersion measure significantly exceeds the contribution of the Milky Way, indicating an extragalactic origin. Some sources show repeating activity, while others are single events. The spectral structure often shows frequency drift, and the polarization suggests a synchrotron mechanism in a magnetized environment.

History

The first publication about a mysterious radio pulse, now known as the 'Lorimer burst', appeared in 2007 after analyzing archival data from the Parkes telescope. Until 2013, there was skepticism about the astrophysical nature of FRBs, until the detection of several new events and, crucially, the repeating source FRB 121102 confirmed their reality. The CHIME, ASKAP, and VLA observatories made significant contributions to identification and cataloguing. More than 600 events are now known, and research continues.

How it works

The leading hypothesis links FRBs to magnetars—neutron stars with extreme magnetic fields (up to 10^15 G). A sudden reconfiguration of the magnetic field (a starquake) or flare activity in the magnetosphere leads to a shock wave and synchrotron radiation. Another possible mechanism is coherent emission from bunches of charged particles, similar to a maser effect. Sources of single FRBs could include neutron star mergers or the collapse of unusual supernovae.

\Delta t = k \cdot DM \cdot (\nu_1^{-2} - \nu_2^{-2})
Δt is the delay between frequencies ν₁ and ν₂, k = e²/(2π m_e c) ≈ 4.15×10⁻³ s·MHz²·pc·cm⁻³ (dispersion constant, e is the electron charge, m_e is the electron mass, c is the speed of light), DM is the dispersion measure (pc/cm³), ν is the radio wave frequency.
