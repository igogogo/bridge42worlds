eigenvalue

📊 0 articles
Imagine stretching a rubber band. It lengthens but stays straight — that's the direction in which the band 'obeys' the stretch. The eigenvalue is exactly how much it stretched in that direction. In mathematics, eigenvalues show how a transformation (e.g., stretching or compression) affects an object along special 'compliant' directions.
💡 Eigenvalues explain why bridges don't collapse from wind: engineers calculate at what frequency the bridge will start to oscillate and ensure that these 'eigenfrequencies' do not match the wind frequency.
In linear algebra, an eigenvalue is a number characterizing how many times a vector is stretched or compressed under a linear transformation if the vector remains on the same line. It's like the shadow of a rotating stick: there are directions where the shadow only changes length but not direction — those are eigendirections, and the length change coefficients are eigenvalues.

History

The idea of eigenvalues emerged in the 19th century in the works of Augustin Cauchy and Charles Hermite while studying quadratic forms. But true fame came to them from quantum mechanics, where the Schrödinger equation is an eigenvalue problem: the energy of a particle turns out to be an eigenvalue of the Hamiltonian operator.

How it works

In quantum mechanics, a particle's state is described by a wave function. When measuring energy, we apply an operator to it, like a template that 'reveals' specific values — eigenvalues. Just as a sieve sorts sand by particle size, the energy operator 'sifts' through possible states and yields the allowed energy levels of an atom.

💡 Eigenvalue search is used by Google's search engine: the PageRank algorithm is based on computing the eigenvector of the web page link matrix. And facial recognition systems turn photos into eigenvectors, extracting key features.
An eigenvalue of a linear operator A is a number λ such that there exists a nonzero vector v (eigenvector) satisfying the equation A v = λ v. The spectrum of an operator (the set of eigenvalues) plays a key role in functional analysis and quantum mechanics. For Hermitian operators, eigenvalues are real, which physically is interpreted as possible measurement results of an observable. Generalization to infinite-dimensional spaces leads to self-adjoint operators with a continuous spectrum.

History

The roots of the concept go back to the 18th century: Euler studied the rotation of a rigid body, identifying the principal axes of inertia. In the 19th century, Cauchy developed the theory of eigenvalues of symmetric matrices. A decisive contribution was made by David Hilbert, who developed the spectral theory of operators, paving the way for quantum mechanics. In 1926, Erwin Schrödinger formulated his famous equation Ĥψ = Eψ, where Ĥ is the Hamiltonian operator and E is the energy eigenvalue. Since then, eigenvalue problems have become the main tool for calculating electronic structures, vibrations, and stability of systems.
