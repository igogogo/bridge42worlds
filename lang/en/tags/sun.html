The star at the center of the Solar System, the source of light and heat for Earth. The Sun is a yellow dwarf, composed mainly of hydrogen and helium. Imagine a giant thermonuclear reactor that has been operating for 4.6 billion years and will continue for another 4.6 billion.

History

The Sun has been revered since antiquity as a deity. In the 16th century, Copernicus placed the Sun at the center of the universe. In the 1920s, Arthur Eddington suggested that the Sun's energy source is thermonuclear fusion. In 1938, Hans Bethe described the proton-proton chain in detail, for which he received the Nobel Prize.

How it works

In the Sun's core, at a temperature of 15 million degrees, hydrogen is converted into helium through thermonuclear reactions, releasing energy. This energy gradually makes its way to the surface, radiating as light and heat. Solar activity, including sunspots and flares, is related to the magnetic field. Analogy: the Sun is like a huge furnace where hydrogen burns, and we receive heat and light.
