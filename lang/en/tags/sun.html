Sun

📊 83 articles
The Sun is the star that gives us light and heat. It is like an eternally boiling cauldron where hydrogen turns into helium, and every second it releases as much energy as humanity consumes in a million years.
💡 The Sun is so huge that 1.3 million Earth-sized planets could fit inside it. And if we weighed the whole Sun, it would be 333,000 times heavier than our planet.
The Sun works like a natural thermonuclear reactor. Under tremendous gravity, hydrogen is compressed so much that its nuclei fuse, forming helium and releasing enormous energy. It's similar to how pressed hay can self-ignite, but billions of times more powerful.

History

People have known about the Sun since antiquity, but its nature was understood only in the 20th century. In 1925, astronomer Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin showed that the Sun is composed mainly of hydrogen and helium. Later, physicists like Hans Bethe explained that its energy comes from thermonuclear fusion reactions.

How it works

The Sun shines thanks to thermonuclear fusion in its core. It's like billions of hydrogen bombs exploding every second, but gravity holds everything together like an invisible shell. The energy takes hundreds of thousands of years to reach the surface, and we see it as sunlight.

💡 The Sun loses about 4 million tons of mass every second through energy radiation, but its reserves will last another 5 billion years. It also emits the solar wind—a stream of charged particles that causes auroras on Earth.
The Sun is a G2V yellow dwarf, the central body of the Solar System, providing Earth with energy. Its mass is 1.989×10³⁰ kg, radius about 696,000 km, effective surface temperature 5778 K. In the Sun's interior, in the radiative zone and convective zone, complex energy transfer processes occur. Energy release is provided by the proton-proton chain and, to a lesser extent, the CNO cycle, leading to the fusion of helium from hydrogen. The Sun also has a complex magnetic field that gives rise to solar activity, including sunspots, flares, and coronal mass ejections.

History

Since ancient times, the Sun was deified, but scientific study began with Galileo's telescopic observations and the discovery of sunspots. In 1925, Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin, analyzing the Sun's spectrum, first established that it is predominantly composed of hydrogen, contradicting the prevailing views. In 1938, Hans Bethe and Carl von Weizsäcker independently described the chains of thermonuclear reactions that power the star. Modern helioseismology, developed since the 1960s, has allowed us to look inside the Sun, confirming theoretical models.

How it works

The Sun's energy is generated in the core through thermonuclear fusion, mainly via the pp-chain, where four protons turn into a helium nucleus, releasing two positrons and two neutrinos. According to Planck's empirical radiation law, the Sun's spectrum is close to blackbody radiation with a peak in the visible range (Wien's displacement law). The balance between gravitational contraction and radiation pressure is maintained by the condition of hydrostatic equilibrium. Radiative transfer and convection equations describe energy transport from the core to the surface. Neutrinos produced in the chains freely leave the Sun, allowing their detection on Earth and testing theoretical models (solar neutrino problem). The Sun's outer layers—photosphere, chromosphere, and corona—are heated to millions of degrees by mechanisms of magnetic reconnection and Alfvén waves.
