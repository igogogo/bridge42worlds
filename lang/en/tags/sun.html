Sun

117

A giant thermonuclear reactor 150 million kilometers from Earth; its radiation is the source of almost all energy on our planet.

In practice: The basis of climate, photosynthesis, and all renewable energy; solar panels convert its light into electricity.
The Sun is a yellow dwarf of spectral class G2V, the central star of our system. Its mass is about 2×10³⁰ kg. In the core, at a temperature of 15 million kelvin, thermonuclear fusion occurs: the proton-proton chain turns hydrogen into helium-4, releasing neutrinos and gamma rays. Energy is transferred first by radiation, and closer to the surface by convection. The visible surface (photosphere) has a temperature of 5778 K and is covered with granules — convective cells. Above it are the chromosphere and corona, heated to millions of degrees (the heating mechanism is not fully clear). The Sun's activity follows an 11-year cycle, which affects the number of sunspots and flare frequency.

History

The Sun was worshipped since ancient times. In the 19th century, Joseph Fraunhofer discovered dark lines in its spectrum, and in 1925, Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin proved that the Sun consists mainly of hydrogen. In the 20th century, physicists uncovered the source of its energy — thermonuclear fusion.

How it works

In the Sun's core, under enormous pressure and temperature, hydrogen nuclei fuse into helium, releasing energy. This energy slowly pushes through the star's bulk to the surface and is radiated into space. Thus the Sun has been shining for 4.6 billion years and will last about as long again.

💡 The Sun loses mass at a rate of about 4 million tons per second due to radiation and the solar wind, but even over billions of years this will amount to a minuscule fraction of a percent.
The Sun is a star, a hot gas ball shining due to thermonuclear conversion of hydrogen into helium in its core. Its surface temperature is about 5500 °C, in the interior — millions of degrees. By diameter it is 109 times larger than Earth, and by mass it accounts for 99.8% of the entire Solar System. Sunlight reaches Earth in 8 minutes. Without the Sun, there would be no day and night, no weather, no life.

The Sun is a main-sequence star with an age of 4.57 billion years. Hydrostatic equilibrium is maintained by a balance of gravity and gas and radiation pressure. Thermonuclear reactions take place in the core: the proton-proton chain provides 99% of the energy, the CNO cycle about 1%. The central density is about 150 g/cm³, temperature 15.7 million K. The radiative zone extends from the core to 0.7 solar radii, where the tachocline is located — a layer of sharp change in rotation velocity, important for magnetic field generation. Above is the convective zone, reaching the photosphere and manifesting as granulation. Above the photosphere — the chromosphere about 2000 km thick and the corona with a temperature around 2 million K, whose heating is likely provided by magnetic waves and field line reconnection. The solar wind carries away mass at a rate of about 10⁻¹⁴ solar masses per year. The star's activity is described by the 11-year Schwabe cycle, governed by magnetic dynamo processes. The spectrum is close to blackbody radiation with an effective temperature of ~5777 K, but contains Fraunhofer absorption lines. The total luminosity is 3.828×10²⁶ W. The Sun is located in the Gould Belt, about 8 kiloparsecs from the Galactic center, and moves in orbit at a speed of about 220 km/s.

History

The Sun has been an object of worship and study since ancient times. A scientific breakthrough occurred in 1814 when Joseph von Fraunhofer systematically described the dark lines in the solar spectrum. In 1925, Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin, applying ionization theory, proved that the Sun and stars consist mainly of hydrogen. The mechanism of thermonuclear fusion was revealed by Hans Bethe and Carl von Weizsäcker in the 1930s. In the second half of the 20th century, helioseismology allowed probing the Sun's interior, and spacecraft studied the solar wind and corona.

How it works

The Sun's equilibrium is described by the hydrostatic equation: dP/dr = -G M(r) ρ / r². The rate of thermonuclear reactions strongly depends on temperature: for the proton-proton chain ε ∝ ρ T⁴. The energy release in the core is about 0.3 μW/cm³. In the radiative zone, energy is transported by radiation (opacity coefficient κ), in the convective zone by convection, according to the Schwarzschild criterion. The magnetic field is generated in the tachocline through the Ω-effect, and the α-effect is realized by turbulent convection. Flares are explained by magnetic reconnection — the rapid release of energy, described by magnetohydrodynamics. Doppler measurements allow studying surface motion and internal oscillations. The radiation spectrum is modeled by Planck's law with corrections for chemical composition and opacity. Detection of solar neutrinos confirms the models of thermonuclear burning.
