quantum decoherence

📊 20 articles

A quantum system loses its superposition (the ability to be in multiple states at once) due to inevitable interaction with the environment — like a whisper drowned out by the noise of a crowd.

In practice: Key to understanding the transition from the quantum to the classical world; critical for building quantum computers, where decoherence must be suppressed.
Decoherence explains why we don't see superpositions of large objects: interaction with the environment (atoms, photons) extremely quickly converts the quantum state into a classical one. It is a process by which, from the possible states, only what looks like classical definiteness survives. The decoherence rate increases with the distance between superposed states and with the strength of interaction.
💡 Decoherence can occur even in a perfect vacuum — due to quantum fluctuations that act as a weak but inevitable noise.
In the quantum world, a particle can be in multiple states at once (called superposition), but when it interacts with surrounding particles or even light, this ability vanishes. It's like a transparent drawing: if you press a colorful chaotic background behind it, the original image is no longer distinguishable.

History

The idea arose from reflections on Schrödinger's cat paradox (a cat that is simultaneously alive and dead until measured). The modern understanding was developed by physicists like John Archibald Wheeler, who investigated the role of the observer.

How it works

Any environment constantly 'measures' a quantum object, destroying its delicate quantum properties. Just as bubbles in a freshly opened soda quickly turn into a uniform liquid, the quantum 'fizz' goes flat under the onslaught of the environment, leaving the familiar definiteness.

💡 For a dust grain with a mass of 1 microgram, a superposition of two positions 1 micrometer apart is destroyed in about 10⁻²¹ seconds under the influence of the cosmic microwave background — the remnant radiation of the Big Bang.
Quantum decoherence is an irreversible process of forming classical properties from quantum ones due to the entanglement of the system with the environment. It is described by the decrease of off-diagonal elements of the density matrix in the basis of the measured quantity. The decoherence rate depends on the distance between superposed states and the coupling strength to the environment. On macroscopic scales, decoherence is practically instantaneous, which is why superpositions of large bodies are not observed.

History

The quantum measurement paradox was illustrated by Erwin Schrödinger in 1935 (Schrödinger's cat). John Archibald Wheeler contributed to understanding the role of the observer and the informational aspect of quantum phenomena. The formal theory of decoherence was developed in the 1970s–80s, primarily by Hans Dieter Zeh and Wojciech Zurek, who showed that the inevitable interaction with a macroscopic environment destroys quantum interferences in an extremely short time.

How it works

The environment continuously 'measures' the position or other property of the system, entangling with it. In the density matrix, this is reflected by exponential decay of off-diagonal elements: |ρ_ij| ~ exp(-γ t (x_i - x_j)^2). Interference terms vanish, and effective classical behavior emerges. This process aligns with the second law of thermodynamics: information about coherence is dispersed into the environment, increasing entropy.

\rho(x,x',t) = \rho(x,x',0) \exp(-\gamma (x - x')^2 t)
ρ(x,x',0) — initial matrix element, γ — decoherence rate (depends on temperature and coupling to environment), t — time, x and x' — positions.
