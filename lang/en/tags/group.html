A group in mathematics is like a set of movements you can make with an object, and which can be combined. For example, rotations of a Rubik's cube: if you rotate one face, then another, you get a new movement. And you can always go back by making the reverse move. Importantly, these movements are closed: a combination of two always yields a movement from the same set.
💡 Groups help understand why snowflakes always have six arms, and why some tilings can be made without gaps. They hide behind any symmetry in nature.
A group is a set of elements with an operation of combining them, similar to how we combine rotations of a square so that it coincides with itself. Four rotations (0°, 90°, 180°, 270°) form a group: their combinations don't go beyond these angles, and each rotation can be undone by the reverse one.

History

The concept of a group originated in the work of Évariste Galois in the 1830s, when he was seeking a solution to algebraic equations in radicals. Galois showed that the group of permutations of the equation's roots determines its solvability. Later, group theory became the language of symmetries in physics and crystallography.

How it works

A group operates by rules: closure (the result of an operation remains in the group), associativity (the order of parentheses doesn't matter), existence of a neutral element (doing nothing), and for each element an inverse (returning to the original state). Like in a kaleidoscope: combining reflections, we get all patterns, and any reflection can be reflected back.

💡 The largest simple group ever described—the 'monster'—has an order of about 8×10⁵³ elements. Its existence was predicted by mathematician Bernd Fischer, and constructed by the joint efforts of many scientists, including Robert Griess.
A group in mathematics is a set G with a binary operation · satisfying the axioms: closure (a·b ∈ G), associativity (a·(b·c) = (a·b)·c), existence of a neutral element e (e·a = a·e = a), and an inverse element a⁻¹ (a·a⁻¹ = a⁻¹·a = e). Group theory studies algebraic structures that arise as sets of symmetries of objects. Groups are classified into types: finite, infinite, Abelian, solvable, simple, and serve as the foundation for algebra, geometry, topology, and physics (gauge theories, crystallography, quantum mechanics).

History

The roots of group theory go back to the permutation theory of Lagrange and Ruffini, but its birth is associated with Évariste Galois (1830), who introduced the term 'group' to describe symmetries of polynomial roots. Henri Poincaré applied groups to topology, laying the foundations of algebraic topology. In the 20th century, the classification of finite simple groups became a monumental project, completed by 2004. Lie groups linked symmetries with differential geometry, becoming the apparatus of the Standard Model.

How it works

A group acts on a set, moving its elements while preserving structure. This action gives orbits and stabilizers. Composition laws are expressed through Cayley tables or graphs. Representation theory translates abstract groups into linear algebra, which proves crucial in elementary particle physics. For example, SU(3) describes quark symmetries. According to Noether's theorem, every continuous symmetry (Lie group) corresponds to a conserved quantity.

For any elements a, b, c of group G, the result of composition does not depend on the placement of parentheses.
\exists e \in G: e \cdot a = a \cdot e = a; \forall a \in G \exists a^{-1} \in G: a \cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1} \cdot a = e
e is the neutral element of the group (often 0 or 1), a^{-1} is the inverse element of a.
