galactic evolution

📊 0

A galaxy grows like a city: it merges with neighbors, redevelops quarters of stars, and extinguishes the fountains of youth at its center.

In practice: By reconstructing galaxy evolution, astronomers predict the fate of the Milky Way and refine the distribution and role of dark matter.
Galactic evolution studies how galaxies—systems of dark matter, gas, and stars—grow and change. Small galaxies merge into larger ones, and supermassive black holes and supernova explosions regulate star birth. A galaxy's shape (spiral or elliptical) reflects its merger history and the availability of cold gas.

History

In the 1920s, Edwin Hubble classified galaxies by their appearance, and later Vera Rubin discovered hidden mass (dark matter) in them, without which their fast rotation cannot be explained.

How it works

Just as a river carries silt and builds islands, gas flows into galaxies and fuels star formation. When galaxies collide, stars rarely hit each other, but gas clouds get compressed, triggering a burst of new star birth.

💡 In 4.5 billion years, our Milky Way will collide with the Andromeda Galaxy and transform into a giant elliptical galaxy.
Galaxies are not static: they change over billions of years. Small clouds of gas (mostly hydrogen) contract and give birth to stars. Large galaxies collide—like two crowds merging into one—and change their shape. Supermassive black holes at the centers of galaxies heat up the gas, halting the birth of new stars.

Galactic evolution encompasses the formation and transformation of galaxies over cosmological time. Within the ΛCDM model, primordial density fluctuations collapse into dark matter halos, into which baryonic gas flows (accretes), cooling and forming stars. Hierarchical mergers produce ever more massive systems. Key physical processes include: stellar feedback from supernovae, nuclear activity (accretion onto a supermassive black hole), morphological transformation (e.g., disk disappearance), quenching of star formation, and enrichment of the interstellar medium with heavy elements. Observations at high redshifts (early universe) show that galaxies were then more compact and more active.

History

In 1926, Edwin Hubble proposed a classification of galaxies by shape, suggesting an evolutionary sequence (later rejected). In the 1970s, Vera Rubin and Kent Ford measured rotation curves, demonstrating the necessity of a dark halo. Advances in computational cosmology (e.g., Illustris and EAGLE simulations) and deep surveys (Hubble Deep Field, JWST) have made it possible to trace evolution from the early universe to the present day.

How it works

Evolution is governed by gravitational instability in an expanding universe, described by general relativity and hydrodynamics. The hierarchical growth of the dark matter halo mass function is described by the Press–Schechter formalism. Gas cools by radiating in atomic and molecular lines, and forms a rotating disk. Star formation follows the empirical Kennicutt–Schmidt law. Feedback from stars and active nuclei (heating, gas outflows) can suppress further star formation. Mergers excite non-axisymmetric structures (bars, spiral arms) and redistribute angular momentum. Interpretation uses Hubble's law (linking redshift to distance) and spectroscopy.
