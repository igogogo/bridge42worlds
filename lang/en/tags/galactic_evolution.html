galactic evolution

Galaxies grow and change like cities: they merge, build up outskirts, experience bursts of star formation — booms of construction. This is galactic evolution.

In practice: Understanding galaxy evolution helps predict the future of the Milky Way and search for life in mature systems.
Galactic evolution describes how galaxies change structure and composition over billions of years. Key processes: gas accretion, star formation, mergers, and tidal interactions. For example, spiral galaxies can transform into ellipticals during major collisions, and supermassive black holes form at the centers of many galaxies.
💡 The Milky Way is currently absorbing a dwarf galaxy — its stellar stream stretches across the sky.
Galaxies are huge collections of stars, gas, and dust. Over time, they change: they merge with each other (like streams into a river), new stars ignite, and old ones fade. This process is called galaxy evolution.

History

Edwin Hubble in the 1920s first showed that galaxies come in different shapes, hinting at their development. Later, astronomers like Vera Rubin found that dark matter (invisible mass holding galaxies together) governs their rotation and mergers.

How it works

Galaxies change because gas inside compresses into stars, and gravity attracts neighboring galaxies. Large galaxies 'eat' small ones, and the shape changes — spirals become ellipticals.

💡 Colliding galaxies can create rings of stars and gas — for example, Hoag’s Object.
Galactic evolution is the sum of physical processes that determine the formation, growth, and transformation of galaxies from the early Universe to the present. It includes hierarchical merging (small galaxies merge into larger ones), cold gas accretion from the intergalactic medium (fueling star formation), feedback from supernovae and active nuclei (regulating heating and gas expulsion), and dynamical relaxation. The final appearance of a galaxy — spiral, elliptical, or irregular — depends on its merger history and star formation rate.

History

The concept emerged after Edwin Hubble classified galaxies by shape in the 1920s, and James Jeans and others suggested gravitational instability as a formation mechanism. Decisive contributions came from Vera Rubin's 1970s work on galaxy rotation, which pointed to dark matter, and deep field observations (Hubble Deep Field) showing galaxy evolution over time. Margaret Burbidge and Fred Hoyle developed nucleosynthesis theory, important for chemical evolution.

How it works

Hierarchical growth: small density fluctuations in the early Universe grow into galaxies under gravity (described by Friedmann equations and ΛCDM cosmology). Gas cools and fragments into stars; massive stars explode, enriching the medium with heavy elements. Mergers disrupt ordered rotation, increasing velocity dispersion and turning disks into spheroids. Dark matter provides the gravitational scaffolding.
