The Higgs field is an invisible field that permeates the entire universe, giving mass to elementary particles. Imagine trying to walk through a pool of molasses: the stronger a particle interacts with the field, the greater its mass, as if it gets stuck in the molasses.

History

In 1964, Peter Higgs and other physicists independently proposed a mechanism explaining the origin of particle mass. In 2012, the Higgs boson was discovered at the Large Hadron Collider, confirming the existence of the field. This was a triumph for the Standard Model of particle physics.

How it works

The Higgs field exists everywhere, and particles such as electrons and quarks interact with it, acquiring mass. Analogy: if you walk through a room full of people, those who interact with you will slow you down — similarly, the Higgs field slows particles, creating inertia.
