quantum thermodynamics

📊 0

If classical thermodynamics is the traffic rules for cars, then quantum thermodynamics tries to guess the path of a bicycle in fog: here there are no clear trajectories, but strange detours are possible. It describes how heat and work behave in a world where particles can be in several states simultaneously.

In practice: Used in the design of quantum engines and nanorefrigerators, as well as for understanding energy exchange in quantum computers.
Quantum thermodynamics extends classical thermodynamics to systems where quantum effects dominate: discrete energy levels, coherence (synchronization of wave functions), and entanglement. Individual quantum systems (electron, photon, molecule) can exhibit fluctuations of work and heat comparable to average values, and temporarily violate the second law of thermodynamics (law of entropy increase). However, for large ensembles or long time intervals, statistical regularities are restored. Central concepts include quantum fluctuation theorems linking forward and reverse processes, and generalization of entropy through the density matrix (description of quantum state).

History

The foundations of ordinary thermodynamics were laid by Ludwig Boltzmann in the 19th century. Quantum thermodynamics began to develop from the mid-20th century when scientists started combining quantum mechanics (discovered by Erwin Schrödinger and others) with ideas about heat.

How it works

Two connected vessels with one gas atom each — this is how quantum heat exchange can be seen. The atom tunnels (passes through an invisible barrier, as if seeping) from one vessel to another, transferring energy in jumps rather than smoothly. Quantum thermodynamics describes such jumps using mathematics where probabilities and special states play a key role.

💡 In quantum thermodynamics, one can create a "quantum Maxwell's demon" — a device using quantum information to extract work from a system, seemingly violating the second law. But, as theorems have shown, the cost of acquiring and erasing information compensates for the gain, preserving overall entropy.
In ordinary thermodynamics (the science of heat and energy), particles move along definite trajectories, like billiard balls. Quantum thermodynamics looks at a world where particles behave like waves (quantum mechanics) and can be in superposition (in several states at once, like a coin while it's spinning in the air), and their properties are blurred by the uncertainty principle (the more precisely you know the position, the less you know the momentum). It studies how in such a world heat is transferred, work is done, and entropy (a measure of disorder) increases. Tiny quantum systems, like a single atom, can temporarily violate the usual laws of thermodynamics, but on average they still obey them.

Quantum thermodynamics is an interdisciplinary field at the intersection of quantum mechanics, statistical physics, and information theory. It studies the laws of energy and entropy conversion in systems described by quantum states (pure or mixed). Thermodynamic quantities — work, heat, and free energy — are defined through the evolution of the density matrix ρ under the action of external parameters and interaction with reservoirs. A key result is fluctuation theorems, for example, the Jarzynski equality: ⟨e^{-βW}⟩ = e^{-βΔF}, where the averaging is done over many realizations of a non-equilibrium process. This allows obtaining equilibrium properties from non-equilibrium measurements. The role of quantum coherence and entanglement as resources for performing thermodynamic tasks is also actively investigated, for example, in quantum heat engines or refrigerators based on individual ions.

History

Thermodynamics as a phenomenological science emerged in the 19th century thanks to the works of Sadi Carnot, Rudolf Clausius, and Lord Kelvin. Statistical justification was provided by Ludwig Boltzmann, linking entropy to the number of microstates (formula S = k log W). The quantum era required a revision of these ideas, starting with the work of von Neumann, who introduced the entropy S(ρ) = −Tr(ρ ln ρ) and showed its role in measurement. In the 1950s, the development of the theory of open quantum systems and lasers stimulated interest in the thermodynamics of small systems. The field acquired its modern form in the 1990s–2000s after the proof of fluctuation theorems (Jarzynski, Crooks) and their quantum generalizations. Today, quantum thermodynamics is actively developing in the context of quantum computing and nanotechnology.

How it works

Quantum thermodynamics is based on the description of a system through the density matrix ρ, which generalizes the classical probability distribution. The equilibrium state is given by the Gibbs distribution: ρ_eq = e^{-βH} / Z, where Z = Tr(e^{-βH}) is the partition function, β = 1/(k_B T). Entropy is given by the von Neumann entropy S_vN = −k_B Tr(ρ ln ρ). Work and heat are defined as changes in the internal energy U = Tr(ρH) with the separation of contributions from changes in the Hamiltonian H (work) and from changes in the density matrix (heat). In non-equilibrium processes, the quantum fluctuation theorem applies: the probability of obtaining work W in the forward process and −W in the reverse are related by P_f(W)/P_r(−W) = e^{β(W−ΔF)}. This expresses the universality of fluctuations and allows testing the laws of thermodynamics at the level of single quantum transitions. Additionally, quantum correlations (entanglement) can enhance the efficiency of heat engines by using non-classical states of the working body.

\rho = \frac{e^{-\beta H}}{Z}, \quad Z = \operatorname{Tr}(e^{-\beta H})
ρ — density matrix of the system, e — Euler's number (≈2.718), β = 1/(k_B T) — inverse temperature, k_B — Boltzmann constant (≈1.38×10⁻²³ J/K), T — absolute temperature in kelvins, H — Hamiltonian operator (energy of the system), Z — partition function (normalization factor), Tr — trace of a matrix (sum of diagonal elements).
S_{vN} = -k_B \operatorname{Tr}(\rho \ln \rho)
S_vN — von Neumann entropy, k_B — Boltzmann constant (≈1.38×10⁻²³ J/K), ρ — density matrix, ln ρ — natural logarithm of the matrix ρ (defined via eigenvalues), Tr — trace of the matrix. For pure states S_vN = 0, for maximally mixed states S_vN = k_B ln N, where N is the dimension of the Hilbert space.
