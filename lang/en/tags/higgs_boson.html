Higgs boson

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
2

The Higgs boson is a ripple in a field that fills space. This field slows particles like water slows a swimmer, thereby giving them mass. Without it, electrons would be massless, and atoms could not exist.

In practice: Understanding the Higgs mechanism is the foundation of the Standard Model, used in medical technologies (PET scanning, proton therapy) and in the construction of new accelerators.
The Higgs boson is a quantum manifestation of the Higgs field, a fundamental scalar field (its value is given by a single number rather than a vector) that permeates the entire Universe. In the Standard Model, the mass of elementary particles arises from their interaction with this field: the stronger the coupling, the greater the mass. The boson itself is a localized excitation of the field, detected in 2012 at the Large Hadron Collider at an energy of about 125 GeV.

History

The idea was proposed in 1964 by Peter Higgs and other scientists. After nearly half a century, in 2012, physicists at the Large Hadron Collider (a huge device for colliding protons) detected a particle matching the predictions.

How it works

A fish in water is slowed by the resistance of the medium — similarly, the Higgs field slows particles, giving them mass. The stronger a particle interacts with the field, the more massive it is. Vibration of this field manifests as the Higgs boson, like a sound wave in water.

💡 Due to a popular science book, the Higgs boson is sometimes called the 'God particle', but physicists avoid this term: it creates a false impression of the particle's role and evokes inappropriate religious associations.
The Higgs boson is a tiny particle whose discovery confirmed the existence of the Higgs field (an invisible medium filling all space). The field interacts with other particles, slowing them down and giving them mass — the property that makes objects heavy. Particles that feel the field more strongly are more massive; photons (light particles) do not sense it and have no mass. The boson itself is a brief excitation of the field, like a splash in water from a thrown stone.

The Higgs boson is a quantum of a doublet scalar field that spontaneously breaks the electroweak gauge symmetry SU(2)×U(1) down to electromagnetic U(1), giving masses to W and Z bosons via the Higgs mechanism. Fermions acquire masses through Yukawa interactions with the field. It was experimentally discovered in 2012 at the LHC by the ATLAS and CMS collaborations with a mass of about 125 GeV/c², becoming the last confirmed element of the Standard Model.

History

The Higgs mechanism was independently proposed in 1964 by Peter Higgs, Robert Brout, and François Englert; similar ideas were expressed by Philip Anderson in the context of superconductivity. Years of searching required the construction of the most powerful accelerator — the Large Hadron Collider at CERN. In 2012, the ATLAS and CMS collaborations reported a particle with the expected properties; in 2013, Englert and Higgs were awarded the Nobel Prize.

How it works

The Higgs field has a non-zero vacuum expectation value (v ≈ 246 GeV). Its potential takes the shape of a 'Mexican hat' due to a negative mass term, leading to spontaneous symmetry breaking. The strength of a particle's interaction with the field (coupling constant) determines its inertial mass. Gauge bosons absorb components of the Higgs field as longitudinal degrees of freedom, becoming massive. Quantum fluctuations around the vacuum state manifest as the Higgs boson. The masses of W and Z bosons are expressed in terms of gauge couplings and v, while fermion masses are expressed through Yukawa couplings and v.

V(\phi) = -\mu^2 \phi^2 + \lambda \phi^4
V(φ) is the potential energy of the Higgs field, φ is the field strength, μ is the mass parameter (μ² > 0), λ is the self-coupling constant (λ > 0). The minimum of the potential is at a non-zero φ, which gives the vacuum expectation value.
M_W = \frac{g v}{2}
M_W is the W boson mass, g is the weak coupling constant, v is the vacuum expectation value of the Higgs field (v ≈ 246 GeV). Similarly for the Z boson: M_Z = √(g²+g'²) v / 2, where g' is the hypercharge constant.
