Higgs boson

📊 2 articles
Imagine an invisible field permeating space like a thick syrup. Some particles glide through it easily, others get stuck, becoming 'heavy'. The Higgs boson is a bubble that proves the syrup exists.
💡 The Higgs boson is sometimes called the 'God particle', although the scientist Peter Higgs joked that it would be more accurate to call it the 'damned particle' — it was so difficult to detect.
The Higgs boson is a special particle associated with a field that gives mass to other particles. Just as water gives weight to a sponge by soaking it, the Higgs field makes particles massive.

History

In 1964, three groups of physicists (Peter Higgs, Robert Brout, and François Englert) proposed a mechanism explaining the origin of mass. Half a century later, in 2012, the boson itself was finally captured at the Large Hadron Collider at CERN.

How it works

In the first moments after the Big Bang, the Higgs field 'turned on' and stopped the free flight of particles, giving them inertia. Without it, electrons could not stay bound around nuclei, and the familiar world would not have assembled.

💡 The mass of the Higgs boson is about 125 GeV, which is roughly 133 times heavier than a proton, but it lives only a tiny fraction of a second, decaying into lighter particles.
The Higgs boson is the quantum of the scalar Higgs field, a neutral particle with spin 0. In the Standard Model, it appears as a result of spontaneous breaking of electroweak symmetry. By interacting with the Higgs field, W and Z bosons, as well as charged fermions, acquire mass. The Higgs boson ensures unitarity of scattering of longitudinal modes of massive gauge bosons.

History

The mechanism of mass generation for gauge bosons was independently proposed in 1964 by Robert Brout and François Englert, Peter Higgs, and also the group of Guralnik, Hagen, and Kibble. The discovery of the particle took place in 2012 at the LHC through the analysis of events with two photons and four leptons. This was a triumph of the Standard Model and earned Englert and Higgs the 2013 Nobel Prize.

How it works

The Higgs field potential has the shape of a 'Mexican hat', so that in vacuum the field takes a non-zero value (~246 GeV). Particles interact with this vacuum condensate, leading to the appearance of mass terms in the Lagrangian. The strength of interaction determines the magnitude of the mass. Experimentally, the boson is produced in proton collisions and decays through several channels, such as H→γγ, H→ZZ*→4ℓ, H→WW*.
