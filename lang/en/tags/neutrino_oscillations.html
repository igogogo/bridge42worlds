neutrino oscillations

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

Neutrinos are ghostly particles that, on their way from the Sun to the Earth, change their disguise, transforming from one type into another, like a spy constantly changing their appearance.

In practice: Understanding neutrino oscillations proves that neutrinos have mass, which means they can play a role in dark matter and influence the evolution of the Universe.
Neutrino oscillations are a quantum mechanical phenomenon where a neutrino initially possessing a certain lepton flavor (electron, muon, or tau) can later be detected as a neutrino of a different flavor. This is explained by the fact that flavor states are superpositions of mass states differing in mass. The interference of these mass waves during propagation leads to a periodic change in the probability of detecting different flavors.

History

Scientists long could not understand why fewer neutrinos come from the Sun than theory predicts. It turned out that they simply turn into another type on the way, which old detectors could not see. This discovery was confirmed at the end of the 20th century.

How it works

A neutrino is born with a certain 'flavor' (type), but its physical nature is a mixture of three different 'mass states.' As it moves, these states lag behind each other differently due to quantum effects (like three runners at different speeds), and eventually the probability of registering a particular flavor changes. It’s like a traffic light that appears either red or green from a distance because of flickering.

💡 Solar neutrinos pass through the Earth completely, and only one in a hundred billion interacts along the way, so detectors are built deep underground or underwater — to filter out cosmic noise.
There are three types of neutrinos (light invisible particles). Flying through space, a neutrino can turn from one type into another — this is called oscillation. It's like if a car brand changed on the go: a Lada is driving, but a Ferrari arrives — only quietly and unnoticed.

Neutrino oscillations are the periodic transformation of neutrinos of one generation (νe, νμ, ντ) into neutrinos of another generation during free propagation. The mechanism is based on the mismatch between states of definite flavor (weak eigenstates) and states of definite mass. The evolution is described by the Schrödinger equation in the basis of three neutrinos, leading to the Pontecorvo–Maki–Nakagawa–Sakata (PMNS) mixing matrix. The oscillation probability depends on the mass squared differences Δm²ij, mixing angles θ12, θ23, θ13, the distance traveled L, and the energy E.

History

The idea of oscillations was proposed by Bruno Pontecorvo in 1957, by analogy with kaon oscillations. The effect was first experimentally detected in 1998 at the Super-Kamiokande facility (Japan) through the deficit of muon neutrinos from atmospheric showers. Solar neutrino anomalies, previously recorded by Ray Davis's chlorine-argon experiment, were explained as oscillations. Modern data from KamLAND, Daya Bay, and others have confirmed all three mixing angles.

How it works

Neutrino propagation is described by a superposition of mass states |ν_i⟩ with definite mass mi. Flavor states |ν_α⟩ are related to mass states through the unitary mixing matrix U_αi. The Hamiltonian in vacuum is diagonal in the mass basis, so each mass state evolves with a phase exp(-i m_i² L/(2E)). The interference of these phases gives rise to an oscillating probability P(να → νβ). In matter, an effective potential for interaction with electrons is added (the Mikheyev–Smirnov–Wolfenstein effect), enhancing oscillations (e.g., in the Sun).

P_{\nu_\alpha \rightarrow \nu_\beta} = \sin^2(2\theta) \, \sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta m^2 L}{4 E}\right)
θ — mixing angle; Δm² = m_1² - m_2² — neutrino mass squared difference (in eV²); L — distance from source to detector (in meters); E — neutrino energy (in eV). For practical units: P = sin²(2θ) sin²(1.27 Δm²(eV²) L(km) / E(GeV)).
