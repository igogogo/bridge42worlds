inflation

📊 9 articles

Cosmic inflation is an instantaneous, ultra-fast stretching of space right after the birth of the Universe, smoothing it to homogeneity like taut tension straightens a crumpled sheet.

In practice: Inflation explains why the Universe is so flat and homogeneous, and predicts specific temperature patterns in the cosmic microwave background, detected by the COBE and WMAP satellites.
Cosmic inflation is a hypothetical stage in the early Universe's history when its size grew exponentially: over equal tiny time intervals, the scale increased by the same factor. It lasted from roughly 10⁻³⁶ to 10⁻³² seconds after the Big Bang, and during this time the Universe expanded by at least a factor of 10²⁶. The cause of such rapid growth was a special field (inflaton) with negative pressure — it created not attraction but powerful repulsion, similar to antigravity.

History

The idea of inflation was proposed by American physicist Alan Guth in 1980. He sought to explain the mysteries of the Big Bang — for example, why the Universe is so homogeneous in all directions.

How it works

A special field arose in space, similar to a magnetic field but causing repulsion instead of attraction. It forced space to expand faster than light (the expansion speed of space itself is not limited by the speed of light). Later this field decayed, and its enormous energy turned into particles, from which galaxies later formed.

💡 Thanks to inflation, opposite points on the sky, separated by billions of light-years, have the same temperature: before inflation they were close together and had time to reach thermal equilibrium.
In the very first moments after the Big Bang, a tiny region of space suddenly began expanding with colossal acceleration. In a minuscule fraction of a second, it inflated to sizes exceeding the entire currently visible Universe. This process is called inflation. It made space very flat and homogeneous — like the surface of a huge balloon appears flat to an ant.

Cosmological inflation is a stage of exponential expansion of the early Universe, proposed to explain its large-scale homogeneity, geometric flatness, and the absence of relic magnetic monopoles. It solves the horizon problem (why distant regions look the same, though they could never have exchanged light) and the flatness problem (why the curvature of space is close to zero). Before inflation, all currently causally disconnected regions were within a single horizon, and inflation smoothed out the curvature. Moreover, quantum fluctuations of the inflaton field, stretched to cosmological scales, gave rise to the seeds of future galaxy clusters.

History

The concept of inflation was put forward by Alan Guth in 1980, but his initial model encountered the graceful exit problem (inflation couldn't end smoothly). Soon Andrei Linde, as well as Paul Steinhardt and Andreas Albrecht independently proposed the slow-roll model, solving this difficulty. Later Linde developed the theory of chaotic inflation. The ideas of inflation build on earlier work by Alexei Starobinsky and on the Big Bang theory of Georges Lemaître.

How it works

Inflation is driven by the inflaton — a scalar field slowly rolling toward the minimum of its potential. According to Einstein's equations, a field with negative pressure generates accelerated expansion. Quantum fluctuations of this field during inflation turn into perturbations of the spacetime metric. After inflation ends, they become adiabatic density perturbations with a nearly scale-invariant spectrum (n_s < 1), which is confirmed by CMB observations. Inflation also predicts primordial gravitational waves.
