A method of studying matter by its spectrum—splitting light into its constituent colors. Like fingerprints: each chemical element leaves a unique set of lines in the spectrum.

History

Beginnings with Newton (splitting light with a prism) and Fraunhofer (dark lines in the Sun's spectrum). In the 19th century, Kirchhoff and Bunsen linked lines to elements. Spectroscopy is now the foundation of astrophysics.

How it works

Light from a star passes through gas and is absorbed at specific wavelengths. The positions of lines reveal the composition, temperature, density, and motion of the object. The Doppler effect shifts lines, allowing velocity measurements.
