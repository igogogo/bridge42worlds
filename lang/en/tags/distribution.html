Distribution

📊 0 articles
A distribution is a mathematical concept that generalizes functions, allowing one to work with objects such as a point impulse (delta function), which is infinitely thin and tall.
💡 Distributions are used in quantum mechanics to describe particles: the wave function can be singular, like the delta function.
A distribution (generalized function) is a linear continuous functional on the space of smooth compactly supported functions (test functions). It generalizes the notion of a function: any locally integrable function generates a distribution. Distributions allow differentiation of functions that are not differentiable in the classical sense. Examples: Dirac delta function, principal value integral.

History

Distribution theory was created by French mathematician Laurent Schwartz in the 1940s. He received the Fields Medal in 1950 for this work. Earlier, Sergei Sobolev introduced generalized functions in 1936.

How it works

Imagine you have a function that is zero everywhere except at one point, where it is infinite, but its integral is 1. This is the delta function. It cannot be described by an ordinary function, but as a distribution it works: it 'picks out' the value of a function at a point. Distributions can be differentiated, yielding, for example, a 'double peak'.

💡 Distributions underlie the theory of partial differential equations: fundamental solutions (Green's functions) are often distributions.
A distribution is a continuous linear functional on the space D(ℝ^n) of infinitely smooth functions with compact support (test functions). The space of distributions D' is the strong dual of D. Operations: differentiation (∂^α T), multiplication by a smooth function, convolution. Distributions are divided into regular (generated by a locally integrable function) and singular (e.g., δ-function). Distribution theory allows solving linear differential equations in a generalized sense.

History

Sergei Sobolev introduced generalized functions in 1936 to solve hyperbolic equations. Laurent Schwartz created a rigorous theory of distributions in 1945, publishing the monograph 'Théorie des distributions' (1950-1951). For this he received the Fields Medal. The theory became the foundation for functional analysis and partial differential equations.

How it works

A distribution T acts on a test function φ as ⟨T, φ⟩. For example, the delta function: ⟨δ, φ⟩ = φ(0). The derivative of a distribution is defined as ⟨∂^α T, φ⟩ = (-1)^{|α|} ⟨T, ∂^α φ⟩. Convolution of distributions is defined if at least one has compact support. A fundamental solution of an operator L is a distribution E such that L E = δ.

\langle \delta, \phi \rangle = \phi(0)
The Dirac delta distribution assigns to a test function its value at zero.
\langle \partial^\alpha T, \phi \rangle = (-1)^{|\alpha|} \langle T, \partial^\alpha \phi \rangle
The derivative of a distribution is defined by transferring the derivative to the test function.

Open problems & fun facts

Classification of distributions with given properties (e.g., positive distributions).; Development of nonlinear generalized function theory (e.g., Colombeau algebras).
