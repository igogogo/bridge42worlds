globular cluster

📊 0 articles

Gravity has woven hundreds of thousands of stars into a tight glowing ball – like a swarm of bees held together by mutual attraction.

In practice: Globular clusters are natural laboratories: they allow us to calibrate the age of the Universe and test theories of stellar evolution.
Globular clusters are spherical stellar systems containing tens of thousands to millions of stars, gravitationally bound. They reside in the galactic halo and belong to the oldest population: some ages reach 12–13 billion years, nearly as old as the Universe itself. Their stars are poor in elements heavier than helium (low metallicity), indicating birth before supernovae enriched the interstellar medium. The orbits of stars within the cluster are chaotic, but the system remains stable for billions of years.
💡 In the center of some globular clusters, the density of stars is so high that on a hypothetical planet, the sky would be lit by hundreds of suns brighter than the full Moon – there is no eternal night, but twilight never comes.
A globular cluster is a huge family of stars held together by gravity (the force of attraction) in the shape of a ball. Inside are up to a million suns, born almost simultaneously from the same gas cloud. The stars orbit a common center, like bees around a hive. In the middle, they are packed so tightly that on an imaginary planet there, the night sky would shine with hundreds of moons. Such clusters are the oldest objects in the Galaxy, over 10 billion years old.

History

The first globular cluster was noticed in 1665 by astronomer Abraham Ihle, but it was only in the 20th century that Harlow Shapley used them to determine that the Sun is not the center of the Galaxy but a remote outskirts.

How it works

The cluster holds together thanks to mutual attraction of all stars. In the tight space, they exchange energy: more massive stars gradually 'sink' to the center, while lighter ones are pushed to the periphery. This resembles a crowd in a closed room, where slow-moving people accumulate in the middle and nimble ones stay at the edges, but no one flies out.

💡 If the human eye could detect faint objects, the center of globular cluster 47 Tucanae would appear from Earth the size of the full Moon, even though it is nearly 15,000 light-years away.
A globular cluster is a gravitationally bound system with approximate spherical symmetry and a sharp increase in density towards the center. A typical cluster contains 10⁴ to 10⁶ stars within a radius of 10–30 parsecs. The stars belong to population II (old, low metallicity), their orbits do not lie in the disk plane but fill the halo, moving at high speeds. Internal dynamics are described by models of self-gravitating systems: two-body encounters lead to mass segregation – massive stars sink to the center, light ones migrate to the periphery. Mass loss occurs due to tidal forces of the Galaxy and evaporation of high-velocity stars. Age is determined by the main-sequence turn-off point on the Hertzsprung–Russell diagram; the oldest clusters are over 12 billion years old, serving as important indicators of the Galaxy's age and constraining cosmological models.

History

In 1665, Abraham Ihle recorded M22, but telescopes of the time did not resolve individual stars. William Herschel in the late 18th century cataloged many clusters. A breakthrough came in 1918 when Harlow Shapley, studying their distribution, showed that the Sun is far from the Galactic center. With the development of photometry, globular clusters became standards for measuring distances and ages.

How it works

The gravitational binding of a cluster is described by the virial theorem: 2T + U = 0 in a steady state. Stars orbit in a common potential that slowly evolves due to collective effects. Two-body encounters lead to relaxation: the core contracts, the envelope expands. Tidal stripping by the Galaxy accelerates mass loss. In dense cores, star collisions can produce blue straggler stars and intermediate-mass black holes. The presence of multiple stellar populations in some clusters indicates several star formation episodes or chemical inhomogeneities in the initial cloud.
