globular cluster

📊 0 articles
A globular cluster is a swarm of ancient stars that whirls around the center of a galaxy like a swarm of fireflies around a lantern. The stars in it are held together by gravity, forming a dense sphere.
💡 In some globular clusters, there are more stars than grains of sand on a beach — up to a million in a single object.
Like a school of fish moving through the ocean as a single cloud, held together by the school, the stars in a globular cluster are bound by gravity and travel through the galaxy together. These are very old and dense groups, where stars can exchange energy and even collide.

History

The first globular cluster was noticed in the 17th century, but their nature was revealed only in the 20th century. Edwin Hubble used globular clusters in the Andromeda Nebula to prove that it is a separate galaxy, not part of the Milky Way.

How it works

Gravity acts as an invisible shepherd, gathering stars into a tight ball. The stars orbit a common center of mass, and those in the core can get so close that they form binary systems, blue stragglers, or even collide.

💡 Globular clusters are cosmic centenarians: their age reaches 13 billion years, older than many galaxies.
A globular cluster is a gravitationally bound system of tens of thousands to a million stars, spherically symmetric and with strong concentration toward the center. Stellar orbits are anisotropic, and the cluster as a whole hardly rotates. In the core, density reaches 10^4 stars/pc^3, leading to frequent dynamical interactions, energy exchange, and gradual core collapse. Clusters are populated by old low-mass stars, but some show blue stragglers — products of mergers. Chemical composition indicates formation in the early Universe from primordial gas enriched only with light elements.

History

Although globular clusters were recorded by telescopes since the 18th century, their extragalactic nature was understood later. In 1918, Harlow Shapley determined distances to remote clusters and estimated the size of the Galaxy. Edwin Hubble in the 1920s found globular clusters in the Andromeda Nebula, confirming the existence of other galaxies. Modern cosmology uses globular clusters as indicators of early stages of galaxy formation and to test dark matter models.

How it works

The dynamical equilibrium of the cluster is described by the virial theorem: 2⟨T⟩ + ⟨U⟩ = 0, where T is the average kinetic energy and U is the average potential energy. Orbital relaxation occurs over a time on the order of t_relax ≈ (N / 8 ln N) t_cross, where N is the number of stars. Massive stars through mass segregation sink to the center, accelerating core collapse. Post-core-collapse clusters have a dense core and a sparse halo. Observed anomalies in velocity dispersion are sometimes attributed to the presence of dark matter or intermediate-mass black holes.

T — average kinetic energy of all cluster stars, U — average gravitational potential energy; brackets ⟨⟩ denote time-averaging.
