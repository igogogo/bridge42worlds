globular cluster

📊 0

Gravity has woven hundreds of thousands of ancient stars into a dazzling ball—a true cosmic hive of shining bees.

In practice: These clusters serve as natural laboratories of stellar evolution and beacons for measuring cosmic distances: the variable stars within them act as standard candles.
Globular clusters are gravitationally bound systems of tens of thousands to millions of stars, aged 10–13 billion years. Their nearly perfect spherical shape is due to mutual attraction, and the core has an extremely high density of stars. The chemical composition is poor in elements heavier than helium (astronomers call this low metallicity), which indicates their birth in the early Universe.

History

In 1746, Jean-Dominique Maraldi first distinguished a globular cluster among other objects. Much later, in the early 20th century, astronomer Harlow Shapley used their distribution to realize that the Sun is far from the center of the Milky Way.

How it works

The stars in the cluster attract each other and revolve around a common center of mass. In the middle, they are packed more tightly and exchange gravitational kicks, changing their velocities and trajectories. This does not destroy the cluster, allowing it to live for billions of years.

💡 Some clusters, such as Omega Centauri, may hide a black hole of intermediate mass at their center—a 'missing link' between light and supermassive black holes, the key to understanding black hole growth.
A globular cluster is a huge group of very old stars bound by gravity. They orbit a common center, like bees around a hive, and never fly apart. Such clusters reside on the outskirts of galaxies.

Globular clusters are dense spherical clusters of stars inhabiting the halos of galaxies. The age of their stars is comparable to the age of the Universe, and low metallicity indicates formation from primordial gas. Spectroscopy reveals several chemical populations, testifying to a complex star formation history. Dynamical evolution leads to mass segregation: massive stars migrate to the center, lighter ones outward. The study of clusters sheds light on the assembly of galaxies and the distribution of dark matter.

History

The globular cluster M22 was first discovered by Johann A. Ihle in 1665. Systematic catalogs were compiled by Charles Messier and William Herschel in the 18th century. In the early 20th century, Harlow Shapley, studying the distribution of clusters, proved that the Solar System is located on the periphery of the Galaxy. Later, using Hertzsprung–Russell diagrams, the ages of clusters were determined, confirming cosmological time scales. In the 1990s, the Hubble Space Telescope revealed multiple stellar populations, complicating the picture of their formation.

How it works

The gravitational interaction of stars is described by the law of gravitation, and the statistical properties of the system follow from the virial theorem (twice the average kinetic energy equals the average potential energy). This allows measuring the total mass of the cluster from the velocity dispersion of stars. The tidal forces of the Galaxy strip away outer stars, and internal processes (evaporation of stars from the core and gravitational contraction) lead to slow evolution. Some clusters contain central intermediate-mass black holes or blue straggler stars—the result of mergers.

M \approx \frac{5\sigma^2 R}{G}
M is the total mass of the cluster, σ is the velocity dispersion of stars along the line of sight, R is the effective radius (half-light radius), G is the gravitational constant (6.67×10⁻¹¹ m³/(kg·s²))
