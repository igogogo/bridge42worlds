Neon

📊 0 articles
Neon (Ne) is an inert, colorless, odorless gas that glows bright red in an electrical discharge. Imagine a 'noble' gas that does not participate in chemical reactions but can spectacularly light up in advertising signs. In nature, neon is scarce — it makes up only 0.0018% of Earth's atmosphere.
💡 Neon signs often actually contain argon with a phosphor — pure neon only gives a red color.

History

Neon was discovered in 1898 by Scottish chemist William Ramsay and English chemist Morris Travers. They isolated it from liquid air, noticing a bright red glow in a spectroscope. The name comes from the Greek 'neos' (new). Soon, neon was used in gas-discharge tubes, and by the 1910s, neon signs had conquered the world.

How it works

When an electric current is passed through a tube containing neon, the gas atoms become excited, and upon returning to the ground state, they emit photons of characteristic red color. Different gases produce different colors: neon — red, argon — blue, helium — yellow. In everyday life, neon is used not only in advertising but also in lasers and as a cryogenic refrigerant.
