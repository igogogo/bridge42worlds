Homology

📊 0 articles
Homology is a way to count holes in a space: how many one-dimensional (like in a donut), two-dimensional (like in a sphere), and so on.
💡 Homology can distinguish a sphere from a torus, but does not distinguish a cup from a donut — they have the same homology.
Homology is an algebraic tool that assigns to a topological space a sequence of abelian groups reflecting its 'holes' of different dimensions. For example, a circle has a one-dimensional hole, a sphere has a two-dimensional hole.

History

Homology originated from the works of Henri Poincaré and Emmy Noether, who formalized homology groups in the 1920s.

How it works

A chain complex is built from simplices (triangles, tetrahedra, etc.), then boundaries and cycles are taken. Homology groups are the quotient groups of cycles by boundaries. The dimensions of these groups (Betti numbers) indicate the number of holes.

💡 Homology is used in data analysis: persistent homology helps find structure in point clouds.
Homology is a functor from the category of topological spaces to the category of graded abelian groups, which measures the 'holes' of a space. For a space X, a singular chain complex is constructed: groups C_n(X) of formal linear combinations of continuous maps of the n-dimensional simplex into X. The boundary operator ∂: C_n → C_{n-1} satisfies ∂^2=0. Homology H_n(X) = Ker ∂_n / Im ∂_{n+1}. Betti numbers b_n = rank H_n(X) give the number of n-dimensional holes.

History

Henri Poincaré introduced the concept of homology in 1895 to classify manifolds. Emmy Noether gave homology an algebraic form using groups in 1925. In the 1930s, Leopold Vietoris and Solomon Lefschetz developed the theory. Later, homology with coefficients, cohomology, and homological algebra emerged.

How it works

For a space, a chain complex is built, where the boundary of a simplex is an alternating sum of its faces. Cycles are chains with zero boundary, boundaries are chains that are boundaries of higher-dimensional chains. Homology is cycles modulo boundaries. Homology is invariant under homotopy equivalence. It is computed using cellular decompositions or spectral sequences.

Open problems & fun facts

Poincaré conjecture (solved for dimension 3, but generalizations to higher dimensions partially open); Computing homology for arbitrary spaces remains a difficult problem

💡 Homology is used in data analysis: persistent homology helps find structure in point clouds.
