Renormalization

📊 0 articles
Renormalization is a mathematical trick that removes infinities from calculations to obtain finite answers.
💡 Without renormalization, quantum field theory would give infinite values for the mass and charge of the electron.
Renormalization is a procedure in quantum field theory that eliminates infinities arising in calculations. Physical parameters (mass, charge) are redefined to match experimental values. It is like calibrating scales: you remove systematic error to get the correct result.

History

Renormalization was developed in the 1940s by Richard Feynman, Julian Schwinger, and Sin-Itiro Tomonaga for quantum electrodynamics. They received the Nobel Prize in 1965.

How it works

When calculating particle interactions, integrals diverge (give infinity). Renormalization introduces counterterms that cancel these divergences, and then the parameters of the theory (mass, charge) are replaced by physical ones measured in experiment.

💡 Renormalization works only for 'renormalizable' theories, such as the Standard Model, but not for gravity.
Renormalization is a method to eliminate ultraviolet divergences in quantum field theory. Divergences arise from integrals over high momenta of virtual particles. The procedure includes regularization (cutting off integrals) and renormalization of parameters (mass, charge, wave function). A theory is called renormalizable if all divergences can be absorbed into a finite number of parameters. The Standard Model is renormalizable, while gravity is not.

History

The first problems with divergences arose in quantum electrodynamics in the 1930s. In 1947, Bethe calculated the Lamb shift using renormalization. In 1948-1949, Feynman, Schwinger, and Tomonaga developed a systematic procedure. In the 1970s, 't Hooft and Veltman proved the renormalizability of non-Abelian gauge theories, for which they received the Nobel Prize in 1999.

How it works

Consider the electron propagator. The bare propagator contains a divergence. Introduce counterterms: δ_m, δ_2. The full propagator: S_F = i/(p̸ - m_0 - Σ(p) + iε), where Σ(p) is the self-energy. Renormalization: m_0 = m + δ_m, and choose δ_m so that the pole of the propagator is at the physical mass m. Similarly for charge: e_0 = Z_3 e, where Z_3 is the photon field renormalization.

m_0 = m + \delta m
The bare mass m_0 is expressed in terms of the physical mass m and the counterterm δm, which removes the divergence.

Open problems & fun facts

Gravity is non-renormalizable—a quantum theory of gravity is needed (strings, loop).; Triviality problem: φ^4 theory in 4D may be trivial (interaction vanishes when regularization is removed).

💡 Renormalization works only for 'renormalizable' theories, such as the Standard Model, but not for gravity.
