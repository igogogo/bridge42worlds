band structure

📊 0 articles
Imagine that electrons in a solid are like bees in a large hive. They can only fly on certain 'floors' or 'combs'. These allowed levels are called bands. The band structure is a map showing which energy floors electrons can occupy, and where they are forbidden to appear—these are forbidden bands. It's like in a multi-story building, some floors are occupied, and others are completely empty.
💡 Thanks to band structure, some materials shine like metals (they have no forbidden band), while others are transparent, like glass (the forbidden band is too large for visible light). That's why metals conduct electricity, and plastic doesn't.
Band structure is a graph of electron energy versus its momentum in a crystal. Due to the periodic arrangement of atoms, electrons can only occupy certain allowed bands, not arbitrary energies. Between them are forbidden bands. It's like a musical instrument: a string can only produce certain notes (fundamental tone and overtones), not any sound. So an electron in a crystal 'sounds' at certain energies.

History

The idea of band structure was first proposed by Felix Bloch in 1928, solving the Schrödinger equation for an electron in a periodic lattice field. Erwin Schrödinger, the creator of wave mechanics, thereby laid the foundations for this discovery.

How it works

In an isolated atom, electrons occupy discrete orbitals. When atoms come together to form a crystal, their orbitals overlap and split into many levels, forming almost continuous bands. The distance between atoms determines whether the bands will overlap or a forbidden band remains. It's like placing many identical tuning forks in a row: each gives one note, but together they create a whole chord with new frequencies.

💡 In graphene, the band structure has the shape of cones touching at a single point—this makes it a semimetal with unique properties, for the study of which the Nobel Prize was awarded in 2010.
Band structure is the dispersion relation E(k) for electronic states in a periodic crystal potential. According to Bloch's theorem, the electron wave function has the form ψ_k(r)=e^{ik·r}u_k(r), where u_k is a periodic function. The energy spectrum consists of allowed bands separated by forbidden gaps. The band structure determines the electrical, optical, and thermal properties of solids. Metals have a partially filled conduction band, dielectrics have a completely filled valence band with a large band gap (E_g>3 eV), and semiconductors have a small band gap (E_g<3 eV). The complex behavior of E(k) near high-symmetry points in the Brillouin zone allows the introduction of the concept of effective mass.

History

Felix Bloch in 1928, applying the Schrödinger equation to a periodic potential, derived the form of the wave function and laid the foundations of band theory. In the 1930s, Brillouin developed the concept of Brillouin zones. Later, W. Shockley, J. Bardeen, and others used band structure to explain semiconductor properties. Erwin Schrödinger, whose equation underlies the calculations, is a key figure.

How it works

Band structure arises from solving the stationary Schrödinger equation for an electron in a periodic crystal potential V(r)=V(r+R). The energy spectrum E(k) is a multivalued function in reciprocal space. Near band extrema, the energy is often approximated by a quadratic dependence: E(k) ≈ (ħ^2/2m*)(k - k0)^2, where m* is the effective mass. According to the Pauli principle, electrons fill states from low energies up to the Fermi level. Optical properties are determined by direct and indirect transitions across the band gap with absorption or emission of photons, which is described by conservation of energy and momentum.

E(k) = \frac{\hbar^2 k^2}{2m^*}
E is the electron energy; k is the magnitude of the wave vector; ħ is the reduced Planck constant (ħ = h/(2π) ≈ 1.0546×10^{-34} J·s); m* is the effective mass of the electron in this band

Open problems & fun facts

Accurate calculation of the band structure of strongly correlated systems (e.g., high-temperature superconductors); Prediction of the band structure of new materials (topological insulators) without complex calculations; Accounting for many-body interaction effects (excitons, polarons) in band calculations; Development of materials with a given band structure (band engineering) for photonics
