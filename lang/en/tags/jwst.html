Imagine a cosmic eye that sees in the dark. The James Webb Space Telescope is a giant mirror in space that catches light invisible to our eyes, just as a cat sees in the dark. It looks back in time to see how the first stars and galaxies were born, like flipping through an old photo album of the Universe.
💡 Webb's mirror is made of gold and is the size of a tennis court. Its sunshield is as big as a bus stop, and it always keeps the telescope in the shade, like a beach umbrella.
JWST is a space observatory that operates in the infrared range. Just as a snake senses the heat radiation of its prey, the telescope detects heat emitted by ancient objects. This allows it to peer through dust clouds and see processes hidden from ordinary telescopes.

History

Launched on December 25, 2021, after decades of planning. Named after James Webb, NASA administrator during the Apollo program. Developed by NASA in collaboration with the European and Canadian space agencies.

How it works

A huge segmented mirror collects infrared light and focuses it onto detectors. Just as a sunflower turns toward the sun, the thermal shield always blocks sunlight and heat from the Sun and Earth, cooling the instruments to cryogenic temperatures for maximum sensitivity.

💡 JWST is so sensitive it could see the faint glow of a bumblebee at the distance of the Moon. And its orbit is at the L2 Lagrange point, 1.5 million kilometers from Earth – farther than any previous space telescope.
The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is a next-generation infrared space observatory with a 6.5-meter segmented mirror. It is equipped with four main instruments operating in the 0.6 to 28 μm range, enabling the study of the early Universe, formation of galaxies, stars, and planetary systems. Infrared astronomy is critical for observing high-redshift objects, whose visible light has shifted into the infrared due to the expansion of the Universe.

History

The concept for JWST emerged in 1996 as the 'Next Generation Space Telescope' (NGST). The launch, initially planned for 2007, was delayed due to technical challenges and budget overruns. Named after James Webb, NASA administrator (1961–1968). The project involved NASA, ESA, and CSA. The main science goals were defined by the Decadal Survey of Astronomy and Astrophysics.

How it works

The telescope is placed at the Sun-Earth L2 point, providing a stable thermal environment. A five-layer sunshield made of Kapton passively cools the instruments to about 40 K. The segmented beryllium primary mirror with gold coating corrects wavefront errors using adaptive optics systems. Detectors register infrared radiation, which is then analyzed by spectrometers and cameras. Observations rely on spectroscopy and gravitational lensing (for amplifying distant objects).
