Laplace Transform

📊 0 articles
The Laplace transform is a mathematical tool that turns complex differential equations into simple algebraic ones, as if you translated the problem into another language where it is easier to solve.
💡 The Laplace transform is used in electrical engineering for circuit analysis: it turns Kirchhoff's laws into algebraic equations.
The Laplace transform is an integral transform that converts a function f(t) (usually defined for t≥0) into a function F(s) of a complex variable s: F(s) = ∫_0^∞ f(t) e^{-st} dt. It is especially useful for solving linear differential equations with constant coefficients, as it replaces differentiation by multiplication by s and integration by division by s.

History

The Laplace transform was introduced by Pierre-Simon Laplace in 1782 in his work on probability theory. However, it gained wide application in engineering in the early 20th century thanks to the work of Oliver Heaviside, who used operational calculus.

How it works

Imagine you have an equation describing the motion of a pendulum with friction. Solving it directly is difficult. The Laplace transform converts this equation into an algebraic one in terms of F(s). You solve it, then apply the inverse transform to obtain the answer in the time domain. It is like using a dictionary to translate from English to mathematical language.

💡 The Laplace transform is the foundation of control theory: transfer functions of systems are described in the s-domain.
The Laplace transform is an integral transform of the form F(s) = ∫_0^∞ f(t) e^{-st} dt, where s = σ + iω is a complex variable. It is defined for functions that grow no faster than an exponential. Properties: linearity, differentiation (L{f'(t)} = sF(s) - f(0)), integration, convolution. The inverse Laplace transform is computed via the Mellin integral: f(t) = (1/2πi) ∫_{c-i∞}^{c+i∞} F(s) e^{st} ds. Tables of Laplace transforms are widely used.

History

Pierre-Simon Laplace introduced the transform in 1782 in the context of probability theory. Oliver Heaviside independently developed operational calculus in the late 19th century for solving differential equations in electrical engineering. Rigorous justification was given by Thomas Bromwich and Gustav Doetsch in the early 20th century. Today, the Laplace transform is a standard tool in engineering and physics.

How it works

The Laplace transform converts differential equations into algebraic ones due to the property: L{f^{(n)}(t)} = s^n F(s) - s^{n-1}f(0) - ... - f^{(n-1)}(0). Initial conditions are automatically incorporated. To solve an equation, first apply the transform, solve the algebraic equation, then find the inverse transform (often using tables). The method is also used for stability analysis of systems: the poles of F(s) determine the behavior of the solution.

F(s) = \int_0^\infty f(t) e^{-st} dt
Transforms a function f(t) (t≥0) into a function F(s) of a complex variable s.
\mathcal{L}\{f'(t)\} = sF(s) - f(0)
Differentiation in the time domain corresponds to multiplication by s in the s-domain minus the initial condition.
