An hourglass on a spaceship shows that the sand falls more slowly than on Earth. This is not an optical illusion—time really stretches when you move very fast or are near a massive body like a black hole.
💡 GPS satellites fly at about 14,000 km/h, so their clocks fall behind by 7 microseconds every day. If engineers didn't correct for time dilation, your navigator would be off by several kilometers after just a couple of minutes.
Time dilation is an effect whereby clocks that are moving or in a strong gravitational field run slower compared to stationary ones. Imagine that speeding on the highway compresses not only distance but also the very flow of minutes: the more fiercely the engine roars, the slower the hands on the dashboard creep for an outside observer.

History

Hendrik Lorentz discovered the mathematical recipe for time stretching back in the late 19th century while trying to explain the mysteries of light. Later, in 1905, Albert Einstein turned this recipe into the elegant theory of relativity. The effect was first measured in practice in 1971 by sending ultra-precise atomic clocks on round-the-world flights.

How it works

A space traveler accelerates to 99% of the speed of light and heads for the stars. For him, only a few years pass, but on Earth decades go by. The closer the speed to light's, the bigger the gap. And if you hover near the event horizon of a black hole, where gravity is immense, one hour could stretch into eternity for an outside observer.

💡 In the movie "Interstellar," the characters return from a planet near a black hole and find that their colleague has aged 23 years. This is not fantasy—gravitational time dilation works exactly like that, and astrophysicists confirm the filmmakers' calculations.
Time dilation is a relativistic effect manifesting as an increase in the measured time interval between two events for an observer moving relative to the events, compared to the proper time. There are two types: kinematic time dilation (special relativity) and gravitational time dilation (general relativity). The magnitude of the effect depends on the relative velocity or gravitational potential and is described by Lorentz transformations and the Schwarzschild metric, respectively.

History

Hendrik Lorentz in the 1890s–1904 derived transformations describing length contraction and time dilation for moving systems, trying to reconcile electrodynamics with the results of the Michelson–Morley experiment. In 1905, Albert Einstein gave these transformations physical meaning in his special theory of relativity. Karl Schwarzschild in 1916 found an exact solution to the field equations for a spherical mass, which directly leads to the conclusion of gravitational time dilation. Observationally, the effect is confirmed by the increased lifetime of fast muons in the atmosphere and by direct experiments with transported atomic clocks.

How it works

In special relativity, the time interval Δt measured by a stationary observer is related to the proper interval Δτ of a moving object as Δt = γ Δτ, where γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²) is the Lorentz factor. As v → c, γ grows without bound, and time for the moving object "freezes." In general relativity, gravitational time dilation is described by the expression Δτ = Δt √(1 − r_s/r), where r_s = 2GM/c² is the Schwarzschild radius; clocks located closer to a massive body run slower compared to distant ones.

\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}

The expression breaks down for r ≤ r_s = 2GM/c² (event horizon).
