Time dilation

29

A speedometer is a time machine: at 100 km/h, each second is slightly shorter for the driver than for the pedestrian; the effect is truly noticeable only near the speed of light.

In practice: GPS satellites travel at tremendous speeds and are farther from Earth, where gravity is weaker. Without relativistic corrections, their clocks would diverge from Earth clocks by 38 microseconds per day, and navigation would be off by kilometers daily.
Time dilation is an effect of relativity theory in which time intervals stretch in a moving reference frame or in a gravitational field compared to a stationary observer. In special relativity, time slows down by a factor of γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²), where v is the speed of motion and c is the speed of light. Gravitational time dilation is predicted by general relativity: clocks in a stronger gravitational potential run slower. Both effects have been precisely measured and they add together.

History

The idea emerged from the Lorentz transformations (late 19th century), and in 1905, Albert Einstein derived it from the postulate of the constancy of the speed of light within the framework of special relativity.

How it works

Special relativity states that the speed of light is the same for all observers. If a spaceship moves at high speed, its light signals still travel away at 300,000 km/s. To maintain this, from the perspective of an outside observer, time on the ship stretches — each tick of the clock seems longer. Gravitational time dilation: a massive body warps spacetime (the unified arena of three spatial dimensions and time), and clocks near it run slower — as if gravity slows down time itself.

💡 On GPS satellites, time runs faster by 45 microseconds per day due to weaker gravity (they are farther from Earth), but slower by 7 microseconds due to speed; the net correction is 38 microseconds, without which the navigation error would reach tens of kilometers after one day.
Time dilation is a phenomenon in which time flows more slowly in a rapidly moving object or in a strong gravitational field (the space around massive bodies, where their attraction is felt) than for a distant stationary observer. For example, a sprinter's watch momentarily lags behind the spectator's watch, but this is noticeable only at speeds of tens of thousands of kilometers per second. The reason is the constancy of the speed of light: since its value is the same for all observers, space and time must adjust to preserve this rule. A fast-moving train shortens in length, and time inside it stretches.

Time dilation is a kinematic and gravitational effect arising from the principles of relativity and the constancy of the speed of light. In special relativity, the Lorentz transformations transform time coordinates: Δt' = γ Δt, where γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²) is the Lorentz factor; as v approaches c, time in the moving system virtually stops. In general relativity, gravitational time dilation is described by the metric: for a stationary field, a clock at a point with gravitational potential φ runs slower by a factor of (1 + φ/c²) compared to distant clocks; near the event horizon of a black hole, dilation tends to infinity.

History

The foundations were laid in the works of Hendrik Lorentz (1892, 1895) and Henri Poincaré (1905), who introduced 'local time' and coordinate transformations. In 1905, Albert Einstein showed that time dilation is a direct consequence of two postulates: the equivalence of inertial frames and the constancy of the speed of light. Experimental confirmation came from the observation of atmospheric muons (1941): the lifetime of fast muons was extended, and they reached the surface. Later, the effect was tested with atomic clocks on airplanes (Hafele–Keating, 1971) and is used daily in GPS.

How it works

Time dilation preserves the invariance of proper time dτ² = dt² − dx²/c² in flat spacetime. In a moving system, worldlines maximize proper time, leading to the 'twin paradox.' Gravitational time dilation arises from the metric: the component g₀₀ = (1 + 2φ/c²) determines the rate of clocks. The equivalence principle connects both types: acceleration is indistinguishable from gravity, so clocks in an accelerating rocket also slow down.
