Time Dilation

📊 0 articles
PopularSimpleAdvanced
An effect of relativity theory where time for a moving object or an object in a strong gravitational field passes slower than for a stationary observer. It is as if clocks on a spaceship traveling at enormous speed run slower than on Earth.
💡 Due to time dilation, astronauts on the ISS age slightly slower than Earthlings: over six months, the difference is about 0.007 seconds.
An effect of relativity theory where time for a moving object or an object in a strong gravitational field passes slower than for a stationary observer. It is as if clocks on a spaceship traveling at enormous speed run slower than on Earth.

History

Albert Einstein predicted time dilation in special relativity (1905) for moving systems and in general relativity (1915) for gravitational fields. Experimentally confirmed: in 1971, Hafele and Keating flew around the Earth on airplanes with atomic clocks, and also in GPS satellites where time is corrected.

How it works

The faster an object moves, the slower time passes for it compared to a stationary observer. Formula: Δt' = Δt / √(1 - v²/c²). Time also slows down near massive objects (gravitational time dilation). For example, on the surface of a neutron star, time passes significantly slower than on Earth.
