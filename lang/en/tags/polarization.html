polarization

📊 0 articles
Light is a wave, and it can be imagined as a rope being shaken. Usually the rope oscillates in all directions, but if passed through a narrow fence, only oscillations along the slits remain. Polarized light is like that: it 'vibrates' strictly in one plane. Polarizing glasses work like such a fence: they block glare from water or asphalt—those horizontal oscillations that interfere with vision.
💡 Bees and many insects see the polarization of the sky even in cloudy weather and use it for navigation. And mantis shrimp distinguish far more polarization shades than humans, helping them hunt and communicate.
Light is an electromagnetic wave with oscillating electric and magnetic fields. Polarization describes the direction of oscillation of the electric field. In sunlight, the directions are mixed randomly, but after reflection from smooth surfaces or passing through special materials, the oscillations align in one plane—the light becomes polarized.

History

Polarization was discovered by the French engineer Étienne-Louis Malus in 1808, while looking through a calcite crystal at light reflected from a window. Later, James Clerk Maxwell in 1865 created the unified theory of the electromagnetic field, showing that light is waves with vector fields, and laid the precise foundation for understanding polarization.

How it works

Light reflected from a horizontal surface is strongly horizontally polarized. Polarizing glasses contain a filter that only passes vertical oscillations. It's like microscopic blinds that cut out stray light—glare disappears, while the rest of the scene remains bright.

💡 The screen of a liquid crystal display (LCD) manipulates polarization. Each pixel contains a liquid crystal that, under voltage, rotates the polarization of backlight. The system of two polarizers at the input and output turns this rotation into a change in brightness.
Polarization is a fundamental property of transverse waves, primarily electromagnetic, where the electric field strength vector oscillates in a specific direction perpendicular to the propagation direction. In the quantum picture, polarization corresponds to the spin state of a photon: circular polarization rightfully corresponds to a projection of +ħ or -ħ onto the momentum direction. The space of polarization states forms a two-dimensional Hilbert space, with the Poincaré sphere used for visualization. Polarization is applied in optics, telecommunications, and quantum informatics as a carrier of a qubit.

History

Étienne-Louis Malus discovered polarization by reflection in 1808, while observing light through a calcite crystal from the window of his home in Paris. In the 1810s, Augustin Fresnel and François Arago supplemented the wave theory of light with transverse oscillations and investigated the interference of polarized rays. James Clerk Maxwell in 1865 derived equations that explained polarization as the directionality of the electromagnetic field vectors. Quantum electrodynamics later linked polarization to photon spin, and Alain Aspect in 1982 conducted the famous experiment with polarization-entangled photons, violating Bell's inequalities.

How it works

According to Maxwell's equations, an electromagnetic wave consists of mutually perpendicular electric and magnetic fields oscillating in phase. The type of polarization is determined by the trajectory of the end of the E vector: if it oscillates along a line—linear polarization; if it rotates with constant amplitude—circular; the general case is elliptical. The intensity of light transmitted through a polarizer is described by Malus's law: I = I₀ cos²θ. Wave plates introduce a phase shift between orthogonal components, changing the polarization type. In quantum optics, a single photon exists in a superposition of two basis states, and measurement is projective and described by density matrices.

I = I_0 \cos^2\theta
I is the output intensity, I₀ is the initial intensity, θ is the angle between the polarization plane of the incident light and the transmission axis of the polarizer
