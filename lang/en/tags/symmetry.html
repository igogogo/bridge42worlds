Symmetry

📊 0 articles
Symmetry is when something does not change if you rotate, shift, or reflect it.
💡 Symmetries in physics are directly linked to conservation laws: for example, symmetry under time translation leads to the law of conservation of energy.
Symmetry is a property of a system to remain unchanged under certain transformations. For example, if you rotate a perfect sphere, it looks the same. In physics, symmetries determine fundamental laws and are related to conserved quantities.

History

The idea of symmetry in physics dates back to antiquity, but it was mathematically formulated by Sophus Lie and Emmy Noether in the early 20th century. Noether's theorem (1918) established the connection between symmetries and conservation laws.

How it works

Symmetry means that the laws of physics do not depend on the reference point. For example, a shift in time does not change the laws—hence conservation of energy. Symmetries can be continuous (rotations) or discrete (reflections).

💡 Symmetry under a 360° rotation for fermions gives a minus sign to the wave function—this is related to spin 1/2.
Symmetry in physics is the invariance of a system (or its laws) under a certain group of transformations. There are spacetime symmetries (translations, rotations, boosts) and internal symmetries (gauge, isospin). Symmetries play a key role in the classification of elementary particles and the construction of field theories. Symmetry breaking (spontaneous or explicit) explains many phenomena, from particle masses to phase transitions.

History

The concept of symmetry in physics evolved from geometric considerations (Plato, Kepler) to mathematical groups. In 1918, Emmy Noether proved her famous theorem, linking symmetries to conservation laws. In the 20th century, symmetries became the foundation of quantum field theory: gauge symmetries (Yang, Mills, 1954) and spontaneous breaking (Nambu, Goldstone, Higgs).

How it works

Symmetry is described by a group of transformations that leave the action invariant. By Noether's theorem, each continuous symmetry corresponds to a conserved quantity. In quantum field theory, symmetries are implemented by unitary operators on Hilbert space. Gauge symmetries are local symmetries that require the introduction of interaction-mediating fields.

\partial_\mu j^\mu = 0
The divergence of the Noether current is zero, which corresponds to a conservation law.
