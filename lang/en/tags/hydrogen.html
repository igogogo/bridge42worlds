hydrogen

📊 164 articles
Hydrogen is the simplest and lightest atom. Imagine a tiny solar system: at the center, a nucleus consisting of a single proton, and around it, like a planet, orbits a single electron. Most of the visible Universe is built from such 'bricks' — stars, gas clouds, and even the water in your glass.
💡 Every second, inside the Sun, 500 million tons of hydrogen are converted into helium, releasing energy that warms our planet. And your body is about 10% hydrogen — mostly in the form of water!
Hydrogen is the fuel of stars. Just as wood burns in a campfire, hydrogen 'burns' in stellar cores through thermonuclear reactions, turning into helium and emitting light. Without hydrogen, there would be no Sun or life.

History

Hydrogen as a chemical element was discovered by Henry Cavendish in 1766. However, its stellar role was first recognized by Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin in 1925 — she showed that stars consist mostly of hydrogen. The spectral lines of hydrogen had been studied earlier by Johann Balmer in 1885.

How it works

The hydrogen spectrum is like a unique barcode. When electricity or heat excites the atom, the electron jumps to a higher 'floor', then falls back, emitting light of strictly defined colors. By these colors, astronomers can tell that a distant star contains hydrogen.

💡 Hydrogen is the only element that has distinct names in scientific jargon for its isotopes: protium (regular hydrogen), deuterium (heavy hydrogen), and tritium (super-heavy, radioactive). Moreover, hydrogen in the metallic state could be a room-temperature superconductor!
Hydrogen (H) is the first element of the periodic table; its atom consists of one proton and one electron. It is the most abundant element in the Universe, making up about 75% of baryonic mass. In molecular form, H₂ is a light, colorless, odorless, flammable gas. Hydrogen is a key element in stellar nucleosynthesis: in the interiors of stars, it is converted into helium via the pp-chain or CNO cycle, releasing energy. The spectral lines of hydrogen (Lyman, Balmer, Paschen series, etc.) played a fundamental role in the development of quantum mechanics, as their explanation was a triumph of the Schrödinger equation. In cosmology, the recombination of hydrogen led to the formation of neutral atoms and the appearance of the cosmic microwave background.

History

Hydrogen was first obtained and described by Henry Cavendish in 1766, but its spectral patterns remained a mystery until 1885, when Johann Jakob Balmer empirically derived a formula for the visible lines. In 1913, Niels Bohr proposed a quasi-modern atomic model that explained the origin of the spectra, but the complete quantum mechanical description was given by Erwin Schrödinger in 1926. The astrophysical significance of hydrogen became clear thanks to Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin, who in 1925, analyzing stellar spectra, proved that stars predominantly consist of hydrogen, which changed our understanding of the evolution of the Universe.

How it works

The hydrogen atom is the simplest quantum system, obeying the Schrödinger equation, which predicts discrete energy levels: E_n = -13.6 eV / n². Transitions of the electron between levels produce characteristic spectral lines. In astrophysics, hydrogen lines (e.g., the 21-cm line of hyperfine splitting) are used for mapping galaxies. In stellar interiors, hydrogen participates in fusion reactions, releasing energy according to the mass-energy equivalence E=mc². Big Bang nucleosynthesis, occurring in the first minutes after the Big Bang, explains the observed abundance of hydrogen.
