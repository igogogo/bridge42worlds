History

Hydrogen was discovered by English scientist Henry Cavendish in 1766 as 'flammable air.' Antoine Lavoisier named it hydrogène (water-builder) because burning hydrogen produces water. In 1931, Harold Urey discovered deuterium — an isotope of hydrogen.

How it works

Hydrogen is the primary fuel of stars. In stellar cores at millions of degrees, hydrogen fuses into helium through nuclear fusion, releasing enormous energy. On Earth, hydrogen is used in the chemical industry and is considered an eco-friendly fuel.
