Strong Interaction

📊 0 articles
The strong interaction is the most powerful force in nature, gluing quarks together inside protons and neutrons.
💡 The strong interaction is 100 times stronger than electromagnetism, but acts only over distances smaller than the size of an atomic nucleus.
The strong interaction is a fundamental force that holds quarks together inside hadrons (protons, neutrons) and binds protons and neutrons in the nucleus. Its carriers are gluons.

History

The concept of the strong interaction arose in the 1930s to explain nuclear forces. Quantum chromodynamics (QCD) was developed in the 1970s.

How it works

Quarks exchange gluons, creating a color field. The farther apart the quarks, the stronger the attraction, like a rubber band being stretched.

💡 Gluons themselves carry color charge, so they interact with each other, unlike photons.
The strong interaction is one of the four fundamental interactions, described by quantum chromodynamics (QCD). It acts between quarks and gluons, which carry color charge. The strong interaction is responsible for binding quarks into hadrons and nucleons into the nucleus.

History

After the discovery of the neutron (1932), it became clear that a new force was needed to hold the nucleus together. Yukawa proposed the meson theory (1935). In the 1960s, the quark model and QCD were developed by Gell-Mann and others. Asymptotic freedom was discovered in 1973 by Gross, Politzer, and Wilczek.

How it works

The strong interaction is mediated by gluons. Quarks have three colors (red, green, blue), gluons carry color-anticolor. The interaction exhibits confinement: quarks cannot be isolated. At high energies, the interaction weakens (asymptotic freedom).

\alpha_s(Q^2) = \frac{1}{\beta_0 \ln(Q^2/\Lambda^2)}
The coupling constant α_s decreases with increasing momentum transfer Q.

Open problems & fun facts

Quark confinement has not been proven analytically; the proton mass problem.
