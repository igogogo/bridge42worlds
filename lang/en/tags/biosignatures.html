biosignatures

📊 0 articles

Biosignatures are clues of life far from Earth: spectral fingerprints of gases like oxygen or methane that are hard to explain without living organisms. Just as the smell of smoke indicates fire, these gases hint at something alive.

In practice: Analyzing exoplanet atmospheres for biosignatures is the main path for modern astronomy to search for extraterrestrial life.
Biosignatures are a set of chemical compounds, isotopic anomalies, or structures that indicate the presence of life. In the astronomical context, the main targets are gases in exoplanet atmospheres: dioxygen (O₂), ozone (O₃), methane (CH₄), nitrous oxide (N₂O). They are detected by transmission spectroscopy: the planet partially eclipses the star, and its atmosphere absorbs some radiation at specific wavelengths. Contextual factors are also important — for example, abundant methane in an atmosphere without signs of volcanism could be a sign of microbes.
💡 Oxygen on Earth appeared only thanks to cyanobacteria 2.4 billion years ago — before that, the atmosphere was unbreathable.
When scientists search for life on other planets, they look for special substances — biosignatures. These can be gases in the atmosphere, like oxygen (which we breathe) or methane. On Earth, these gases are produced by living beings: plants release oxygen, bacteria produce methane. If we see the same gases on a distant planet, it could mean that there is life there.

History

The idea emerged in the mid-20th century when astronomers pondered how to search for life. In the 1990s, the first exoplanets were discovered, making it a reality.

How it works

Starlight passes through the planet's atmosphere. Different gases absorb light in their own way, and dark lines appear in the spectrum (rainbow band). That's how we learn what the air is made of.

💡 In 2023, JWST found possible traces of dimethyl sulfide in the atmosphere of planet K2-18 b—on Earth, it is produced exclusively by living organisms.
A biosignature is any observable feature (chemical, spectroscopic, morphological) that can be produced by life and has no plausible abiotic source. In exoplanet research, the focus is on atmospheric biosignatures: gases in thermodynamic disequilibrium (e.g., simultaneous presence of O₂ and CH₄), organic compounds (chlorophyll-like pigments), or isotopic shifts (e.g., depletion of carbon-13 in biological tissues). Besides gases, biosignatures such as the "red edge" of vegetation—a sharp jump in reflectivity around 0.7 µm—are considered.

History

The concept was formulated by Carl Sagan and other pioneers of exobiology in the 1960s–1970s. In the 1990s, the discovery of exoplanets (Mayor, Queloz, 1995) turned biosignatures from hypothetical into observable. Modern instruments — James Webb (JWST) and the future Extremely Large Telescope (ELT) — will enable detailed study of terrestrial planet atmospheres in habitable zones.

How it works

Transmission spectroscopy: during a planet's transit, some starlight is filtered by its atmosphere. Subtracting the star's spectrum during transit from the out-of-transit spectrum yields the transmission spectrum, where molecular absorption lines are visible. Interpretation is based on laboratory spectra and photochemical models. For biosignatures, context is critical: for example, oxygen can form without life (photodissociation of water with hydrogen escape), so combinations of gases unlikely without biota are sought.
