biosignatures

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
2

Biosignatures are cosmic fingerprints of life: chemical substances in planetary atmospheres that betray the presence of organisms, like fossilized footprints on an ancient beach.

In practice: The search for biosignatures in exoplanet atmospheres is the main method for finding extraterrestrial life, driving the development of cutting-edge telescopes.
A biosignature is any detectable phenomenon (gas, mineral, color) that can only be explained by biological activity. The most compelling biosignatures are gases in chemical disequilibrium; for instance, the simultaneous presence of oxygen (O₂) and methane (CH₄). It is crucial to rule out non-biological sources (volcanoes, photochemical reactions) that can produce false signs of life.

History

The idea of searching for life-related gases in planetary atmospheres took shape in the 1960s. Astrobiologist Carl Sagan demonstrated that Earth's atmosphere with oxygen and methane would be a clear sign of life to a distant observer.

How it works

Just as the smell of exhaust fumes indicates a car, a combination of gases in a planet's air can reveal its inhabitants. If oxygen and methane—gases that quickly react and vanish—are seen simultaneously in the spectrum, it means there is a source constantly replenishing them, possibly living organisms.

💡 Earth, with its oxygen-methane atmosphere, would have shone like a beacon for hypothetical alien astronomers for two billion years already.
Signs of life that scientists search for on other planets. Living organisms change their environment: on Earth, plants and forests release oxygen (O₂), and some bacteria emit methane (CH₄). Telescopes capture light passing through a distant planet's atmosphere and break it into a spectrum to find the characteristic traces of these gases.

Biosignatures are classified as atmospheric (gases—O₂, O₃, CH₄, N₂O, (CH₃)₂S), surface (pigments like chlorophyll, vegetation reflectance, 'red edge'), and temporal (seasonal gas cycles). The main criterion is thermodynamic disequilibrium: a mixture that, without a constant source, would quickly reach chemical equilibrium. Detection is performed via transit spectroscopy and direct imaging. Future missions (JWST, LUVOIR, HabEx) will search for biosignatures in the habitable zones of nearby stars. The main difficulty is false-positive scenarios, requiring detailed climate and photochemical modeling.

History

The first ideas about possible signs of life on Mars were voiced back in the 19th century (Schiaparelli's canals). A systematic approach emerged in the 1960s, when James Lovelock analyzed Earth's atmosphere as a product of the biosphere. Carl Sagan and others developed the concept of thermodynamic disequilibrium. With the discovery of exoplanets in the 1990s, the search for biosignatures became a central goal of astrobiology.

How it works

During a transit of an exoplanet in front of its star, part of the starlight is filtered by its atmosphere, and absorption lines of molecules appear in the spectrum. Each molecule has a unique spectral 'fingerprint'. By measuring the depth of lines at different wavelengths, the gas composition is determined. A biosignature is considered reliable if a combination of gases is detected that cannot be maintained without a biological source, and all known abiotic pathways under the given conditions (temperature, radiation, geology) are ruled out. Principles of spectroscopy and photochemical modeling are used.
