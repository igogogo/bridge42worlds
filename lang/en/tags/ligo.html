LIGO

# tag · @ author · ! scientist
33
LIGO is a giant ruler that measures how gravitational waves stretch and squeeze space. Two long arms of a laser beam are compared: if a wave passes, one arm becomes slightly longer than the other.

History

The idea of the detector goes back to the pioneering work of Joseph Weber in the 1960s, but it was only in 2015 that the advanced LIGO interferometer, built under the leadership of Rainer Weiss and others, first detected gravitational waves from the merger of two black holes.

How it works

A laser beam is split into two beams traveling along perpendicular 4-kilometer arms. Reflecting off mirrors, the beams meet and cancel each other out, but the slightest asymmetry in lengths — due to a gravitational wave — disrupts the perfect cancellation, producing a signal.

💡 LIGO's sensitivity is such that it detects length changes ten thousand times smaller than the size of a proton — like measuring the distance to the nearest star with the accuracy of a human hair's thickness.
Like an ear catching the faintest rustle in silence, LIGO listens to the tremor of spacetime itself. The interwoven laser beams in the interferometer turn the elusive gravitational ripple into an electrical signal.

LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory) is a complex of ground-based detectors using the principle of Michelson laser interferometry with Fabry-Perot cavities in 4 km long arms to register gravitational waves — perturbations of the spacetime metric predicted by general relativity. An incoming wave induces a phase difference in the two orthogonal arms, measured by the shift of the interference pattern.

History

Attempts to detect gravitational waves began in the 1960s with Joseph Weber's solid-state resonators. In the 1970s, the idea of a laser interferometer was proposed by Rainer Weiss and further developed with the participation of Kip Thorne and Ronald Drever. Key funding for the project was obtained in the 1990s, construction was completed in the early 2000s. The first direct detection — event GW150914 — occurred on September 14, 2015.

How it works

In each arm, the laser beam is reflected many times (about 280 times) between mirrors, effectively increasing the length to 1120 km and enhancing sensitivity. The combined beam is sent to a photodetector. The gravitational wave, by changing the proper length of the arms in opposite directions according to the metric h = ΔL/L, modulates the brightness of the interference spot. The noise reduction system (seismic, thermal, shot) allows measuring relative strains on the order of 10⁻²¹.

h = \frac{\Delta L}{L}
h — dimensionless strain amplitude; ΔL — change in arm length; L — original arm length (4 km for LIGO).

Open problems & fun facts

Limited frequency range: the detector is insensitive to gravitational waves with frequencies below ~10 Hz due to seismic noise, and above ~10 kHz due to shot noise.; Cosmological sources: predicted gravitational waves from inflation remain inaccessible to ground-based facilities, requiring space interferometers like LISA.; Nature of compact objects: it is not always possible to unambiguously distinguish black holes, neutron stars, or exotic objects based on the signal shape.
