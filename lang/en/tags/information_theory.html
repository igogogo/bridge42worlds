Information Theory

📊 0 articles
Information theory studies how to measure and transmit information: how many bits are needed to send a message, and how to protect it from noise.
💡 The unit of information — the bit — was introduced by Claude Shannon in 1948.
Information theory is a mathematical theory of communication developed by Claude Shannon. It provides ways to measure the amount of information (entropy), data compression, and coding for error protection. For example, the entropy of Russian text is about 1.5 bits per character.

History

Founded by Claude Shannon in 1948 in his article 'A Mathematical Theory of Communication'. Later developed in the works of Norbert Wiener and Andrey Kolmogorov.

How it works

Information is measured through uncertainty: the less probable an event, the more information it carries. Entropy H = -∑ p_i log p_i is the average amount of information per symbol. Communication channels have a capacity that limits the transmission rate.

💡 Information theory underlies all modern communication systems: from Wi-Fi to satellite television.
Information theory is a branch of mathematics that studies quantitative aspects of information. Basic concepts: Shannon entropy, mutual information, channel capacity. Entropy H(X) = -∑ p(x) log p(x) measures the uncertainty of a random variable. Shannon's source coding theorem states that data can be compressed to the entropy without loss. The Shannon-Hartley theorem for a noisy channel gives the maximum transmission rate. Information theory is closely related to statistical physics (Boltzmann entropy) and machine learning.

History

Claude Shannon in 1948 published 'A Mathematical Theory of Communication', laying the foundations. In 1949, the book by Shannon and Weaver appeared. Ralph Hartley in 1928 introduced a logarithmic measure of information. In the 1950s, error-correcting coding developed (Hamming, Reed-Solomon). In the 1960s, algorithmic information theory emerged (Kolmogorov, Solomonoff, Chaitin).

How it works

Information is measured in bits. Entropy is the average number of bits needed to encode a symbol from a given distribution. Mutual information I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X|Y) measures the amount of information about X contained in Y. Channel capacity C = max_{p(x)} I(X;Y). Error-correcting codes add redundancy to protect against noise.
