solar corona

# tag · @ author · ! scientist

The solar corona is the Sun's outer atmosphere, visible during eclipses as a pearly halo. It is hundreds of times hotter than the star's surface, which still surprises scientists.

In practice: Studying the corona helps predict solar flares and ejections that affect satellites, communications, and power grids on Earth.
The solar corona is the outer layer of the solar atmosphere, extending millions of kilometers. It consists of rarefied plasma (about 10^15 particles/m^3) with a temperature from 1 to 3 million kelvins, significantly hotter than the underlying photosphere (about 5800 K). The corona radiates mainly in the ultraviolet and X-ray ranges due to the high ionization of atoms (e.g., iron ions Fe IX–Fe XIV). The brightness of the corona is a million times less than the solar disk, so it is observed with coronagraphs or during eclipses. The coronal heating problem remains central in solar physics: mechanisms of magnetic reconnection and wave heating by Alfvén waves are likely at work.

History

Even in ancient times, people noticed the glow around the Sun during eclipses, but they didn't understand its nature until the invention of the spectroscope. In the 19th century, spectral analysis revealed mysterious green lines in the corona, attributed to a new element called 'coronium'. It turned out to be radiation from highly ionized iron, indicating temperatures in the millions of degrees.

How it works

The heating of the corona is a mystery. The main hypothesis: solar magnetic fields emerging from the depths become tangled and reconnect, releasing enormous energy, like a short circuit. This heats the plasma and also generates the solar wind—a stream of particles flowing from the Sun.

💡 During solar eclipses, the corona appears to be several solar diameters in size, but in reality it extends far beyond Earth's orbit, gradually transitioning into the solar wind.
The solar corona is a rarefied but incredibly hot gas enveloping the Sun. Although the Sun's surface (photosphere) has a temperature of about 5500°C, the corona is heated to millions of degrees. The solar corona is a fully ionized plasma with a complex magnetic field topology. Coronal temperatures reach ~10^6 K in quiet regions and ~10^7 K in active regions. Energy transfer from the convective zone to the corona occurs via magnetic fields. Heating mechanisms include nanoflares (small-scale reconnection) and dissipation of Alfvén waves. The coronal spectrum contains emission lines of highly ionized elements, as well as a continuum from Thomson scattering of photospheric radiation by free electrons (K-corona). Coronal loops, arcades, and streamers are observed in X-rays and extreme ultraviolet (EUV). The solar wind flows from coronal holes—regions with open magnetic field. Coronal dynamics are responsible for space weather.

History

The first mentions of the corona appear in chronicles of ancient civilizations describing solar eclipses. Systematic observations began with the invention of the telescope. In 1868, Pierre Janssen and Norman Lockyer independently discovered helium in the chromosphere spectrum. The mysterious green line at 530.3 nm, discovered in 1869, was identified in 1939-1942 as Fe XIV emission—iron stripped of 13 electrons, indicating high temperatures. Space missions like Skylab (1970s), SOHO, TRACE, and the Solar Dynamics Observatory revolutionized understanding of the corona, revealing its dynamics and connection to magnetic fields.

How it works

Coronal heating is explained by two classes of models: direct heating through current dissipation (alternating current models) and wave heating (direct current). Magnetic reconnection, described by magnetohydrodynamic equations, converts magnetic energy into thermal and kinetic energy. Energy conservation (first law of thermodynamics) in plasma accounts for heating, radiation, and thermal conduction. The corona loses energy through radiation, thermal conduction into the chromosphere, and solar wind acceleration. Maxwell's equations combined with hydrodynamic equations form the basis of MHD description of the corona. Spectral analysis allows diagnosing temperature and density.
