Antimatter

📊 0 articles
Matter composed of antiparticles, which have the same mass but opposite charge and other quantum numbers. When a particle meets its antiparticle, they annihilate, releasing energy. Imagine that every person has an exact copy with opposite qualities — upon meeting, they vanish in a flash of light.
💡 In the movie 'Angels & Demons', antimatter is used as a bomb — in reality, producing even a gram of antimatter would cost billions of dollars and take years.

History

Paul Dirac predicted the existence of the antielectron (positron) in 1928 by combining quantum mechanics and special relativity. The positron was discovered by Carl Anderson in 1932. Later, the antiproton and antineutron were discovered.

How it works

In laboratories, antiparticles are created in accelerators during high-energy particle collisions. Analogy: if you strike an anvil with a hammer, sparks may fly — similarly, collisions produce particle-antiparticle pairs. In nature, antimatter is rare because it quickly annihilates with ordinary matter.

E = mc^2
When mass m annihilates, energy E is released, equal to mass times the speed of light squared.

Open problems & fun facts

Why is there so little antimatter in the universe? This is called baryon asymmetry — one of the greatest mysteries in physics.
