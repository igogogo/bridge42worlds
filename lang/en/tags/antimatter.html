Imagine that every object has an invisible twin. As soon as they meet, both disappear in a flash of pure energy.

History

Antimatter was predicted by British physicist Paul Dirac in 1928, while solving an equation that united quantum mechanics and relativity. In 1932, Carl Anderson first saw the positron in cosmic rays.

How it works

When a particle collides with its antiparticle, their mass completely converts into energy according to E=mc². This principle works in a PET scanner: a patient is injected with antimatter, and it 'lights up' tumors.

Antimatter is the mirror reflection of ordinary matter: particles with the same mass but opposite charge. On contact, they annihilate, turning into radiation, just as a drop of water and a drop of oil in a fire flare up with light.

Antimatter consists of antiparticles, each having the same mass but opposite quantum numbers (charge, lepton or baryon number) compared to the corresponding particle. Annihilation of a particle-antiparticle pair results in the production of photons or lighter pairs, with conservation of energy, momentum, and quantum numbers.

History

The concept of antiparticles arose from Dirac's equation (1928), which allowed solutions with negative energy. Interpreting such states as particles with positive energy and opposite charge led to the prediction of the positron, discovered by Anderson in 1932. Later, accelerators produced antiprotons, antineutrons, and even antihydrogen. Today, antimatter is studied as a tool to test fundamental symmetries of nature.

How it works

In quantum field theory, each field describing particles corresponds to a conjugate field of antiparticles. The annihilation process is described with high probability by Feynman diagrams and obeys the Dirac equation. Near black holes, in Hawking radiation processes, pairs of particle-antiparticle can be born, one of which falls into the hole, and the other flies away.

E = mc^2
E — released energy, J; m — total mass of annihilating particles, kg; c — speed of light in vacuum, c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s

Open problems & fun facts

Why does the observable Universe have a predominance of matter (baryon asymmetry)?; How long can antimatter be stored and transported over long distances?; How does gravity act on antimatter: does it fall down or up? Experiments (AEGIS, ALPHA) are looking for the answer.
