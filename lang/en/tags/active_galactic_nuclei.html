active galactic nuclei

📊 0 articles
At the center of almost every galaxy resides an insatiable giant—a supermassive black hole. Sometimes it consumes so much matter that its surroundings shine brighter than all the stars in the galaxy, as if a quiet lamp suddenly turned into a searchlight.

History

The first to notice unusual galactic nuclei: in 1908, Edward Fath recorded emission lines in NGC 1068, and Carl Seyfert in 1943 identified an entire class of such objects. The revolution came in 1963 when Maarten Schmidt realized that the quasar 3C 273 was a distant and record-bright active nucleus.

How it works

Gas falls toward the black hole, forming a rapidly rotating accretion disk. Friction heats it to monstrous temperatures, and it radiates light, often ejecting two thin jets along the rotation axis. If the jet points toward Earth, we see an exceptionally bright blazar; if not, a Seyfert or radio galaxy.

💡 The most powerful active nuclei—quasars—shine as brightly as hundreds of trillions of suns, yet the size of their source is no larger than the Solar System. More compact, yet incomparably brighter than the entire galaxy.
An active galactic nucleus is a region the size of the Solar System at a galaxy's center, shining like a billion stars. All its power arises from gas swirling in a vortex around a supermassive black hole—like water frantically draining from a bathtub, but heated to millions of degrees.

An active galactic nucleus (AGN) is a compact central region whose luminosity significantly exceeds the total light of the host galaxy's stars. The energy source is accretion of matter onto a supermassive black hole with a mass from millions to billions M_⊙. Typical components: accretion disk (optical/UV/X-ray), broad-line region (fast-moving clouds), narrow-line region (further out), dusty torus, collimated relativistic jets. Classification depends on viewing angle: Seyfert 1/2, quasars, radio galaxies, blazars—a unified scheme explains the differences by the orientation of the torus.

History

The roots of the discovery go back to 1908, when E. A. Fath at Lick Observatory detected emission lines in NGC 1068. In 1943, Carl Seyfert systematized galaxies with bright nuclei—Seyfert galaxies. In 1963, Maarten Schmidt, studying the spectrum of the radio source 3C 273, measured a redshift z=0.158 and discovered quasars as distant 'star-like' objects. In the 1970s, Roger Blandford and Martin Rees developed a model of an accretion disk with jets.

How it works

The gravitational energy of matter falling into the black hole's potential is converted to heat in the accretion disk. Viscous friction heats the gas to T~10^5–10^6 K for the disk and up to 10^8–10^9 K in the corona, producing thermal and Compton radiation. Particles can be accelerated in magnetohydrodynamic processes and form jets—collimated outflows of plasma moving at relativistic speeds. The Eddington limit plays a key role: the luminosity cannot exceed L_Edd, otherwise radiation pressure stops accretion. Jets can extract rotational energy from the black hole via the Blandford–Znajek mechanism.

L_\mathrm{Edd} = \frac{4\pi G M m_{p}}{\sigma_T}
L_Edd is the Eddington luminosity, M is the black hole mass, m_p is the proton mass (≈ 1.67×10^{-27} kg), σ_T is the Thomson scattering cross-section (≈ 6.65×10^{-29} m²), G is the gravitational constant (≈ 6.67×10^{-11} m³/(kg·s²)).
