active galactic nucleus

📊 0

A galactic nucleus blazing brighter than all its stars combined, powered by matter falling into a supermassive black hole.

In practice: Quasars — the most powerful active nuclei — serve as beacons for studying the Universe in its infancy. Their jets heat intergalactic gas, influencing the growth of entire galaxies.
An active galactic nucleus is a compact central region whose luminosity far exceeds the total radiation of all the galaxy's stars. The energy comes from accretion (infall) of gas and dust onto a supermassive black hole (millions to billions of solar masses). The material spirals into a disk, heating to millions of degrees, and shines brightly in X-ray and ultraviolet. Powerful magnetic fields sometimes create jets — relativistic plasma streams ejected over tens of thousands of light-years.

History

The first active nuclei — quasars — were discovered in the early 1960s. Radio astronomers found point-like sources resembling stars, but their spectra matched no known object. In 1963, Maarten Schmidt realized they were distant galaxies with an unusually bright center.

How it works

Gas and dust around the black hole form a rapidly rotating accretion disk — a flattened cloud like a whirlpool. Friction and gravity convert rotational energy into heat and light. Some material is ejected outward as narrow jets — along the black hole's rotation axis.

💡 Light from the most distant quasar traveled 13 billion years to reach us — it existed when the Universe was only 700 million years old.
At the heart of most large galaxies lies a supermassive black hole — an object with gravity so strong that even light cannot escape. When gas and dust fall into the hole, they swirl into a flattened disk, collide, and heat to incredible temperatures, producing a dazzling glow. This turns an ordinary galactic center into an active source, shining like a cosmic spotlight.

An active galactic nucleus (AGN) is the central region of a galaxy exhibiting non-stellar electromagnetic radiation with significant variability on timescales from hours to years. The model includes a supermassive black hole (mass 10^6 to 10^9 solar masses) and an accretion disk, where material heats through viscous friction to temperatures from tens of thousands to millions of degrees, producing a characteristic 'ultraviolet bump'. The disk's ionizing radiation excites gas clouds, generating broad and narrow emission lines. Bipolar jets often form, radiating through synchrotron and inverse Compton processes. Depending on jet power and orientation to the observer, AGNs are classified as radio-loud (quasars, blazars) or radio-quiet (Seyfert galaxies).

History

The first signs of nuclear activity were noticed by E. Fari in 1908, who detected bright lines in the spectrum of NGC 1068. In 1943, Carl Seyfert systematized such objects. Powerful radio sources of the 1950s, looking like stars but with strange spectra, led to the discovery of quasars. In 1963, Maarten Schmidt deciphered the spectrum of 3C 273, realizing the lines were highly redshifted due to cosmic expansion. Ya. B. Zeldovich and E. Salpeter independently proposed accretion as the energy source, and in 1969, D. Lynden-Bell proved that supermassive black holes hide in galactic centers. Modern telescopes — from Hubble to the Event Horizon Telescope — continue to unveil AGN structure.

How it works

Accretion theory explains AGN energy output. Gas captured from the surroundings has angular momentum and forms a rotating disk around the black hole. Viscosity caused by magnetorotational instability (MRI) allows matter to lose energy and gradually approach the hole, heating and radiating. As it falls into the black hole, gravitational energy is released: up to about 10% of rest mass is converted to radiation (E=mc²), tens of times more efficient than thermonuclear fusion. Rotating magnetized black holes can also tap their spin energy via the Blandford–Znajek mechanism, launching relativistic jets. The non-thermal continuum arises from synchrotron radiation of electrons and inverse Compton scattering of photons on accelerated particles. The spectrum of radio-quiet AGNs is well described by the standard disk model of Shakura and Sunyaev.

L_{\mathrm{Edd}} = \frac{4\pi G M m_p c}{\sigma_T}
L_Edd is the Eddington luminosity (maximum luminosity for stable accretion), G is the gravitational constant (≈6.67×10⁻¹¹ m³ kg⁻¹ s⁻²), M is the black hole mass, m_p is the proton mass (≈1.67×10⁻²⁷ kg), c is the speed of light (≈3×10⁸ m/s), σ_T is the Thomson scattering cross-section of the electron (≈6.65×10⁻²⁹ m²)
