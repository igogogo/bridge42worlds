thermal conductivity

📊 0 articles

Thermal conductivity is the ability of a substance to transmit the 'tremor' of atoms from hot to cold: metal is like a nimble baton, foam plastic is an awkward sloth.

In practice: Copper pipes in a radiator quickly release heat into the room, while a wool sweater retains body heat due to low thermal conductivity of air in fluffy fibers.
Thermal conductivity (k) is the proportionality coefficient in Fourier's law: heat flux density q⃗ = –k ∇T, where ∇T is the temperature gradient. It characterizes the ability of a substance to transfer the kinetic energy of microscopic particles without macroscopic movement of the substance. In metals, heat transfer is mainly by conduction electrons; in dielectrics, it is by phonons (quanta of crystal lattice vibrations). Silver has the highest thermal conductivity among pure metals (k ≈ 430 W/(m·K)).
💡 Diamond has a thermal conductivity five times higher than copper, although diamond is an electrical insulator. The reason is a rigid crystal lattice and light carbon atoms, along which phonons travel quickly.
Thermal conductivity shows how quickly heat flows through a material when one end is hot and the other cold. Imagine a line where each person whispers a message to their neighbor: in metals, 'neighbors' (atoms) are tightly packed, and energy hops quickly; in wood, atoms are loose, so transfer is slow. That's why a metal spoon in hot tea heats up, but a wooden stick does not.

History

Early experiments were conducted by Jan Ingenhousz in the 18th century, comparing how different metals conduct heat. But the scientific foundation was laid by Jean Baptiste Joseph Fourier in 1822, who derived the law of heat conduction — the main equation of heat transfer.

How it works

Heat is the jiggling of atoms. In a solid, atoms are tightly bound by springs (chemical bonds). If you heat one end, the atoms there start jiggling more and shake their neighbors, passing vibrations along the chain. In metals, there are also free electrons (tiny carrier particles) that, like agile couriers, distribute energy much faster than lattice vibrations. That's why metals conduct heat superbly.

💡 In superfluid helium-4, thermal conductivity does not obey the classical Fourier law: heat propagates as 'second sound' — a temperature wave moving at about 20 m/s.
Thermal conductivity is a tensor quantity in anisotropic crystals. In general, it depends on temperature, density, structure, and impurities. The microscopic theory for phonons is based on the Boltzmann kinetic equation for the phonon distribution function. In the Debye model, the lattice thermal conductivity k ~ C_v v_s l, where C_v is heat capacity, v_s is speed of sound, l is the phonon mean free path. The main phonon scattering mechanisms are phonon-phonon collisions (normal and Umklapp processes), scattering on defects and sample boundaries. In metals, the electron contribution dominates, described by the Wiedemann–Franz law: k/σ = (π²/3)(k_B/e)² T, where σ is electrical conductivity.

History

The law of thermal conduction was formulated by Joseph Fourier in 1822 in his 'Analytical Theory of Heat'. For over a century, thermal conductivity remained a phenomenological constant. The kinetic theory of gases (James Clerk Maxwell, Ludwig Boltzmann) made it possible to relate it to molecular motion. In the 20th century, the development of quantum solid-state theory (Peter Debye, Rudolf Peierls) led to the phonon picture and the prediction of lattice thermal resistance. At the end of the century, materials with extreme thermal conductivity (diamond, graphene, nanotubes) were discovered.

How it works

In solids, heat transfer occurs via two main carriers: conduction electrons and phonons. Phonons — collective lattice vibrations — behave like a gas of quasiparticles and obey Bose–Einstein statistics. The distribution of phonons over frequencies and wave vectors is described by dispersion relations depending on crystal symmetry. Thermal resistance arises because the phonon mean free path is finite, limited by anharmonicity of interatomic potentials (three-phonon processes), isotopic disorder, defects, and grain sizes. At high temperatures, scattering on other phonons (so-called Umklapp processes) dominates. In nanostructures, the mean free path can be limited by geometric dimensions, leading to reduced thermal conductivity (phonon ballistic regime).
