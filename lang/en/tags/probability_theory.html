Probability Theory

📊 0 articles
Probability theory helps estimate chances: what is the probability that it will rain tomorrow, or that you will win the lottery.
💡 If you flip a coin 10 times, the probability of getting 10 heads in a row is about 0.1%.
Probability theory studies random events and their quantitative characteristics. The probability of an event is a number between 0 and 1 indicating how likely it is. For example, the probability of rolling a six on a die is 1/6.

History

The foundations were laid by Pascal and Fermat in the 17th century, then Laplace, Gauss, and Kolmogorov in the 20th century provided the axiomatics.

How it works

Probability is calculated as the ratio of favorable outcomes to total outcomes (classical definition). For complex events, the addition and multiplication rules of probability are used.

💡 The Monty Hall problem shows that intuition often errs in estimating probabilities.
Probability theory is a mathematical discipline that studies the laws of random phenomena. Basic concepts: probability space (Ω, F, P), random variable, distribution, mathematical expectation, variance. There are discrete and continuous distributions. Important laws: law of large numbers, central limit theorem. Probability theory underlies statistics, information theory, financial mathematics.

History

Blaise Pascal and Pierre de Fermat in 1654 laid the foundations by solving problems about division of stakes. Christiaan Huygens published the first textbook in 1657. Pierre-Simon Laplace in 'Théorie analytique des probabilités' (1812) systematized knowledge. Andrey Kolmogorov in 1933 gave an axiomatic definition via measure theory. In the 20th century, the theory of stochastic processes and stochastic calculus (Itô) developed.

How it works

Probability is formally defined as a measure on a sigma-algebra of events satisfying axioms: non-negativity, normalization, countable additivity. Random variables are measurable functions. Distribution is given by a distribution function or density. Mathematical expectation is an integral with respect to the measure. Independence is factorization of the joint distribution.

P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)}
The probability of event A given B is expressed in terms of the prior probability of A and the likelihood.

Open problems & fun facts

Riemann hypothesis and its connection to the distribution of prime numbers (probabilistic approach); Problem of existence and uniqueness of infinite-dimensional distributions
