semiconductor

📊 0 articles
A semiconductor is a material that can conduct electricity not always, but only when heated or illuminated. Imagine a drawbridge that is usually raised, but when a signal flag is raised (energy is given), it lowers and allows cars to pass. In a semiconductor, electrons sit in 'locked' places, but with a little heat or light, they gain enough strength to break free and turn the material into a conductor.
💡 Your smartphone and computer work thanks to billions of tiny semiconductor switches—transistors. If they were made of ordinary conductors, the phone would heat up like an iron and discharge in seconds!
A semiconductor is a material with conductivity between a conductor and an insulator. It has a unique ability: its resistance drops sharply when heated or illuminated. Imagine a crowd of people on an escalator. In the normal state, everyone stands still—no current. But if given a little energy (a push), they start moving, and a flow arises. In a semiconductor, 'electrons' sit in the valence band, and to make them move, they need to be transferred to the conduction band across a forbidden band.

History

The first experiments with semiconductors began in the 19th century: Faraday noticed that silver sulfide conducts current better when heated. In 1947, Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley created the first germanium transistor. Since then, silicon has become the basis of electronics. Shuji Nakamura in the 1990s developed bright blue LEDs based on gallium nitride—a semiconductor—for which he received the Nobel Prize.

How it works

In a semiconductor, electrons are in energy bands. There is a valence band, where electrons are tightly bound to atoms, and a conduction band, where they are free. Between them is a forbidden band. An electron can jump across it if it receives energy greater than the band gap width. This is like jumping over a ditch: without a running start (energy), it won't work. By adding impurities (doping), additional steps can be created to facilitate the jump—this is how transistors are made.

💡 Semiconductors are used not only in electronics but also in solar cells: photons of light knock electrons from the valence band into the conduction band, creating an electric current. The most common semiconductor—silicon—makes up almost a third of the Earth's crust, literally the sand under our feet!
A semiconductor is a material whose resistivity at room temperature ranges from 10^-4 to 10^8 Ω·m, intermediate between conductors and dielectrics. The main property of semiconductors is the strong dependence of conductivity on temperature, illumination, electric field, and impurities. The electronic structure is described by band theory: the valence band and the conduction band are separated by a forbidden band of width E_g (for silicon ~1.1 eV). At zero temperature, a semiconductor is a dielectric. Conductivity appears when carriers (electrons and holes) are excited by heat or radiation. Doping with donor or acceptor impurities allows control of the type and concentration of carriers.

History

Semiconductor properties were first observed by Michael Faraday in 1833 in silver sulfide. In 1874, Karl Braun discovered the rectifying effect at a metal-semiconductor contact. The decisive breakthrough came in 1947 with the invention of the transistor at Bell Labs (Bardeen, Brattain, Shockley). Shuji Nakamura in 1993 made a breakthrough by creating a bright blue LED based on gallium nitride, opening the way to white LED lighting. For this achievement he was awarded the Nobel Prize.

How it works

The conductivity of a semiconductor is determined by the concentration and mobility of free charge carriers. In an intrinsic semiconductor at T>0, electrons transition from the valence band to the conduction band, leaving holes. The carrier concentration obeys the law of mass action: n_i^2 = N_c N_v exp(-E_g/kT). When doped, donors create levels near the conduction band, facilitating thermal ionization. The principle of operation of the p-n junction, the basis of diodes and transistors, relies on diffusion and drift of carriers in an electric field. An important role is played by Coulomb's law, which determines potential barriers, and the Schrödinger equation, which describes the wave functions of electrons in a periodic potential.

n_i^2 = N_c N_v \exp\left(-\frac{E_g}{k T}\right)
n_i is the electron concentration (equal to the hole concentration) in an intrinsic semiconductor; N_c is the effective density of states in the conduction band; N_v is the effective density of states in the valence band; E_g is the band gap width; k is the Boltzmann constant (k ≈ 1.38×10^{-23} J/K); T is the temperature in kelvins
