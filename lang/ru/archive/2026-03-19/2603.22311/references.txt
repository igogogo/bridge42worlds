A. Rolling Hough Transform
Every line in two dimensional (2D) space may be uniquely parameterized according to:
ρ=xcos(θ) +ysin(θ),(1)
whereθis the angle from normal andρis the minimum Euclidean distance between the line
and the origin in Cartesian space. The HT converts an image from Cartesian space (x,y)
to Hough space (ρ,θ) by counting the number of sufficiently bright pixels along each line
parameterized byρandθthrough an image. Bright lines within an image will appear as
peaks in Hough space. The RHT method can detect straight line segments as well as curved
filaments. In our application, the image is first convolved with a tophat filter (diameter =
41 pixels in our case) to remove sharp features. This smoothed image is then subtracted
from the original image to remove large-scale structures. On this new image containing only
sharp edges, the RHT selects a circular region of radius (r= 6) centered on a pixelx,y. A
HT is performed forρ= 0, centered on the central pixel of this region. This HT returns a
score,R(θ,x,y), for the degree of linear structure at eachθthrough that pixel. The process
is repeated for every pixel (x,y) in a image, generating a map of linear structure, as shown
in the top row of Figure 1.

We optimize theAstro-BEATSparameters as follows: for a subset of the DBSCAN parame-
ters with the range defined in Supplementary Table 1. We optimize the parameters on one
video in a set of 10 test videos. We find the parameter combination that yields the greatest
F1 score on this video, as defined in Equation 5). These parameters are next used to assess
the performance ofAstro-BEATSon the other 9 videos.
We see that the F 1 score remains above 0.7 for a range of parameters, indicating that
Astro-BEATSperformance is not strongly dependent on parameter choice (Supplementary Fig-
ure 2).Astro-BEATSyields an average F 1 score of aroundF 1 = 0.78±0.07 across the 10
videos used for optimization (Supplementary Figure 2A). This shows thatAstro-BEATSper-
formance is robust when it is optimized on a subset of observations. TheAstro-BEATSpa-
rameters used in this paper were optimized using Video 5. This is because the F 1 score
performance obtained using these parameters is closest to the median F 1 score across all
candidate optimization videos, reducing the risk of over or underfitting.
D. Reverification
We manually inspected the FP and FN detections fromAQuA,IDT, andAstro-BEATSto iden-
tify two types of miss-match between expert and machine annotations. Firstly, alignment
errors occur when evaluating detection performance using euclidean distance (see Supplemen-
tary Material E). For large transients or events containing multiple subdomains, the center
identified by an algorithm may be sufficiently displaced from the human-annotated center
3
that the two detections are no longer considered the same event.Detections misclassified due
to alignment error are manually marked as TP for the performance evaluation of each algo-
rithm. Examples of this type of alignment error are illustrated in Supplementary Figure 4A.
Secondly, small or dim transients may be missed during human annotation, but picked up
by an algorithm. These transients are later marked by a second round of human inspec-
tion as TP. Examples of these smaller transients are shown in Supplementary Figure 4B.
A catalogue of revised manual detections for each tested algorithm are included with this
publication.26
Manual re-verification is only conducted to gain a more accurate measurement of algorithm
performance for this work. Comparisons of algorithm performance to manual annotations
before and after verification are shown in Supplementary Figure 3. We present re-verified
results throughout this paper for all algorithms in order to give the most accurate assessment
of their detection Precision and Recall. We note however, that this second manual re-
verification will in general not be possible whenAstro-BEATSis applied to larger datasets
for which we do not have manual annotations.

To assess annotation performance, we calculate a number of TP, FP and FN events for
each algorithm using the re-verified dataset. Annotations are matched to manually defined
transient centers using the Hungarian algorithm44 and thelinear sum assignmentfunction
from the Python libraryScipy. In this implementation, the Hungarian algorithm assigns
centroids detected by each algorithm to manually annotated transient centers with the cost
of assignment being the Euclidean distance between the centroids. 9 We set a maximum
allowable separation of 6 pixels in space or 6 frames in time between a centroid identified by
the tested algorithm and the corresponding ground-truth centroid for them to be considered
a valid match.
To assess segmentation quality, we compare algorithm segmentation masks to those gen-
erated by two experts 9 using the Diverse Counterfactual Explanations (DiCE) 41 similarity
5
score, which evaluates the pixel-by-pixel similarity between the masks generated by a given
algorithm and the ground truth annotations. TP pix, FPpix, and FN pix denote the number
of TP (overlapping), FP (algorithm only), and FN (ground truth only) pixels. The DiCE
value ranges from 0 (no overlap between ground truth and algorithmic segmentation) to 1
(perfect overlap).
F. Statistical analysis
We employ the Student’st-test and F-test to assess whether the central tendency (t-test) or
variance (F-test) of one algorithm differs significantly from another. A difference in model
performance is considered statistically significant when the correspondingp-value is less than
0.05. In cases where data is not normally distributed, we use the Mann-Whitney test 42 to
compare the differences between two groups of observations. To test whether or not the
distributions are normally distributed, we use the Shapiro-Wilk statistical test. 45 We do not
report the results of this test, as it was performed solely to determine whether at-test or a
Mann–Whitney test was appropriate for comparing the groups.
