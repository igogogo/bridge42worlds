Appendix B: Fluorescence model : three-level versus multi-level
Manfroid et al. (2021) showed that the simple three-level at omic model used by Preston (1967) and Arpigny (1979) yields N iI/FeI
column density (abundance) ratios similar to those provide d by the more realistic multi-level ﬂuorescence model, part icularly when
the excitation temperature of NiI is increased by 180 K relat ive to the temperature derived from the analysis of the FeI li nes. While
the ratios are similar, the column densities themselves, an d consequently the production rates, are not; the latter can be overestimated
by as much as one order of magnitude. For example, using the th ree-level atomic model as detailed in Manfroid et al. (2021) , we
found a FeI excitation temperature of approximately 4500 K f or the data set obtained for comet 3I on December 21 with setti ng 348.
This temperature is typical of that found in solar system com ets (Manfroid et al. 2021). In this framework, we obtained lo g10 Q(FeI)
= 24.06, log 10 Q(NiI) = 24.41, and log 10 [Q(NiI)/Q(FeI)] = 0.35. While the NiI /FeI abundance ratio agrees with the value obtained
from the multi-level model within the uncertainties (T able 2), the production rates are 0.6 dex too large. As shown in Fig . B.1, this
discrepancy can be explained by the fact that the blackbody u sed in the three-level model underestimates the true solar ﬂ ux seen by
the NiI and FeI atomic transitions. Therefore, for the same o bserved emission line intensities, more atoms are needed to compensate
for the fewer solar photons.

Appendix C: Comparison with other measurements
Belyakov et al. (2026) discovered a forbidden NiI line at 7.507 µm in the spectrum of comet 3I obtained on December 16, 2025,
using the JWST /MIRI medium-resolution spectrometer. Assuming ﬂuorescen ce emission, they derived log 10 Q(NiI) = 23.72±0.12
and 23.89 ±0.15, with di ﬀerent assumptions about the parent. Q is in s −1 . This independent measurement is in excellent agreement
with our measurement of log 10 Q(NiI) = 23.89±0.03, which was obtained on December 15, 2025. These results support their
hypothesis that the NiI forbidden line at 7.507 µm is primarily due to ﬂuorescence. These authors also uncove red a fainter NiI line
at 11.307 µm, from which they derived slightly higher values, log 10 Q(NiI) = 24.0±0.2 and 24.2 ±0.2, under the same hypotheses.
Roth et al. (2026c) reported the detection of two NiI lines at 3.119 and 3.915 µm on December 23, 2025, using the JWST
NIRSpec IFU. Using the same ﬂuorescence model as Belyakov et al. (2026), they derived log 10 Q(NiI) in the range 24.4 −24.9, with
diﬀerent assumptions about the parent. They assumed an expansi on velocity of 0.31 km s −1 which is smaller than the velocity of
0.54 km s −1 used by Belyakov et al. (2026). Rescaling to the higher veloc ity would give log 10 Q(NiI) in the range 24.6 −25.1, which
is higher than the value measured on December 16. These value s are also higher than the values measured with UVES on Decemb er
21, log 10 Q(NiI) = 23.79±0.04 (T able 2).
Zhao et al. (2026) obtained multi-epoch low-resolution ( R ∼ 300) spectra of comet 3I from December 2, 2025 to January 20,
2026. During this period, the comet was at heliocentric dist ances ranging from 1.8 to 3.3 au. The production rates they de rived
for FeI are in excellent agreement with ours, while their NiI production rates are about a factor of two higher. The variat ion of the
production rates of both FeI and NiI with heliocentric dista nce was found to be shallower post-perihelion than pre-peri helion, as
conﬁrmed by our observations (Fig. 2). Zhao et al. (2026) als o found a high CO abundance in comet 3I, which, if related to th e
high metal abundance, could suggest that metals are linked t o a CO-bearing reservoir. This was also observed in comet 201 6/R2
(Manfroid et al. 2021).
Post-perihelion observations of comet 3I were obtained by H oogendam et al. (2026) with the Keck Cosmic W eb Imager on
November 16, 2025. With a resolving power of R ∼ 1800, several NiI and FeI lines were detected, and productio n rates computed
using a simpliﬁed three-level model. As explained in Append ix B, these production rates are likely overestimated. On th e other hand,
the Q(NiI) /Q(FeI) ratio is less sensitive to the atomic model. Hoogenda m et al. (2026) measured log 10 Q(NiI)/Q(FeI) =−0.16 ±0.03
at rh = 1.51 au. This value is in excellent agreement with the rh dependence shown in Fig. 4, extrapolated at rh = 1.51 au.
