A.METHODS
A.1.Photometric Data Reduction
This work uses short-cadence (SC) photometric data of V783 Cyg from theKeplerspace telescope, covering
BJD 2455372–2455462 (Quarter 6). We used the publicly available Pre-search Data Conditioning (PDC) light curves
(Stumpe et al. 2012; Smith et al. 2012), retrieved from the Mikulski Archive for Space Telescopes (MAST) 3. The light
curves are provided in barycentric Julian date (BJD) andKeplermagnitudes; for each sub-quarter, we normalized the
flux and converted it to theKeplermagnitude system. Figure A1 presents an overview of the SC photometric data.
Quarter 6 was selected because it provides the longest contiguous segment of short-cadence data for V783 Cyg with
minimal instrumental gaps, covering approximately 3.25 Blazhko cycles (the Blazhko period of V783 Cyg is∼27.67
days (Plachy et al. 2014)).

Following the methodology established in our previous work (Niu et al. 2023; Niu & Xue 2024; Xue & Niu 2024;
Niu & Xue 2025; Niu 2026), we extracted the amplitudes and frequencies of the harmonicsf 0–47f0 (Table A1) and
their temporal variations over∼90 days (Figures A2–A4). To extract the amplitudes and frequencies of the harmonic
pulsation modes, we performed a standard iterative pre-whitening procedure, continuing until the signal-to-noise ratio
(S/N) fell below 7.0 (Table A1).
To investigate temporal variations in the amplitudes and frequencies of the harmonics, we employed a short-time
Fourier transform (STFT) approach (Niu et al. 2023; Niu & Xue 2024; Xue & Niu 2024; Niu & Xue 2025; Niu 2026). A
sliding window of 15 days was advanced across the full dataset (∼90 days) in increments of 1.5 days (Figures A2–A4).
The window length of 15 days represents a compromise between frequency resolution (needed to resolve individual
harmonics at high orders) and temporal resolution (needed to track variations on the Blazhko timescale); it yields
approximately 18 sampling points per Blazhko cycle.
A.2.Quantifying Variability and Correlations
To quantify the amplitude and frequency variations and their interrelations, we computed the following metrics:
•Relative amplitude variation: ∆A/ ¯A≡(A max −A min)/ ¯A, whereA max andA min are the maximum and
minimum amplitude values from the STFT, and ¯Ais the mean amplitude over the full dataset. This metric
normalizes the amplitude range, allowing comparison across harmonics of vastly different mean amplitudes.

•Absolute frequency variation: ∆f≡f max −f min, wheref max andf min are the maximum and minimum
frequency values. This is reported in absolute terms (c/d) because the frequency variations are of similar
magnitude across all harmonics.
•Pearson correlation coefficientρ A,f: quantifies the linear correlation between the amplitude and frequency
variations. A value of +1 indicates perfect correlation,−1 indicates perfect anti-correlation, and 0 indicates no
