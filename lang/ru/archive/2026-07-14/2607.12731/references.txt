Appendix A: Multiple-shell interaction
Here we summarise the numerical procedure used to follow the
dynamical evolution of multiple shells.
We consider an outflow composed of N shells, labelled by
an index i =1 ,...,N , according to their emission order from
the central engine. Within this framework, the outermost shell
corresponds to the first one emitted, while the innermost shell is
the most recently emitted. The shell-ejection process is described
as a stochastic Poisson process with a time-dependent rate, as
introduced in Sect. 3, resulting in a higher concentration of shells
in the outer regions of the outflow.
Each shell, emitted at a time ti, is characterised by four quan-
tities: the Lorentz factor γi, the mass mi = pγ−1
i , the initial radial
position Ri = cβiti, and the width li. The separation from the
previously emitted shell Li is given by Li = Ri −R i+1 −l i+1, where
the position of the last-emitted shell is set toR N =0.
The dynamical evolution of the shell train proceeds as follows.
Let j denote the index of the jth collision, occurring at time tj
and radius Rc(tj). Given the shell velocities βi and separations Li
at time tj−1, the collision time for each adjacent pair satisfying
βi+1 >βi is δti,i+1 = Li/[c(βi+1 −βi)]. Defining δtj = min(δti,i+1),
the jth collision occurs at tj = tj−1 + δtj. Let s and r denote
the indices of the slow and rapid shells involved in the collision
at time tj. The collision radius is Rc(tj)= Rr(tj)= Rr(tj−1)+
cβrδtj. The radiation produced in this interaction is detected by
an observer located at a distance R0 from the central engine at
time tobs,j =[ R0 −R c(tj)]/c + tj, where R0 is taken to be equal to
the total radial extent of the outflow. This time corresponds to the
onset of the pulse described by Eq. (5).
After the collision, each shell advances from its previous
position Ri(tj−1) to Ri(tj)= Ri(tj−1)+ cβiδtj. All shells retain
their Lorentz factor, mass, and width, except for the s and r
shells, which merge into a single shell labelled by index r, with
properties determined as described in Sect. 3. The s shell is then
removed from the system.
The above procedure is iterated until either all shells have
merged into a single shell or the shells become ordered in such a
way that their Lorentz factors increase monotonically outward,
preventing further collisions.
Appendix B: Spectral parameters
The spectral parameters α, β, and Ep,i of the Band function (Band
et al. 1993), used to compute the flux correction factor kflux de-
scribed in Sect. 3, are assigned as follows.
The values of the spectral indicesαand βwere retrieved from
the publicly available Fermi GBM Burst Catalog 8. We down-
loaded all GRBs detected between 14 July 2014 and 14 October
2025, and discarded entries that do not satisfy the following con-
straints (Poolakkil et al. 2021):
–A : positive relative error < 0.44 and negative relative error
<0.83;
–α: positive error<0.37 and negative error<0.51;
–β: positive error<1.0 and negative error<0.53;
–E p: positive relative error <0.35 and negative relative error
<0.43;
where A denotes the spectral normalisation. This selection
yields a total of 990 sets of spectral parameters, which we refer
to as the good sample.
8 https://heasarc.gsfc.nasa.gov/w3browse/fermi/
fermigbrst.html
For each simulated GRB, the values of αand βare randomly
drawn from the good sample. The intrinsic peak energy Ep,i is
assigned differently. Simply setting Ep,i = Ep(1+ zGRB), with
Ep randomly extracted from the good sample, would neglect the
observed Ep,i-Liso correlation (Amati 2006; Yonetoku et al. 2004),
where Liso is the bolometric isotropic-equivalent peak luminosity.
We therefore proceed as follows. Starting from the value of
Liso derived from the theoretical LC, we compute the correspond-
ing Ep,i using the Ep,i-Liso relation reported in GS22, including
the intrinsic scatter of the correlation.
The final values of α, β, and Ep,i obtained through this proce-
dure are those assigned to each simulated GRB.
Appendix C: Genetic algorithm and model
parameter optimisation
Genetic algorithms (GAs) are machine-learning algorithms in-
spired by evolutionary processes, in which a population of can-
didate solutions evolves over successive generations towards an
optimal configuration, as measured by a predefined set of metrics.
Within this framework, each candidate solution, or ‘individual’,
is represented by a set of eight model parameters (Table C.1),
analogous to a genetic chromosome, with each parameter corre-
sponding to a ‘gene’. The evolution of the population is driven
by the relative fitness of individuals, which determines their like-
lihood of contributing to subsequent generations. Evolutionary
processes, such as crossover between parent individuals and ran-
dom mutations of individual genes, are implemented to promote
exploration of the parameter space and prevent premature conver-
gence.
Each individual encodes a specific combination of model
parameter values, sampled within the ranges reported in Table C.1.
The optimisation proceeds through a fixed sequence of steps,
identical to those described in M25, which we summarise here
for completeness:
– the algorithm was run for 30 generations, each consisting of
a population ofN pop =2000 individuals.
– For each individual, NGRB =2000 synthetic LCs were gener-
ated using the corresponding parameter set. The same selec-
tion criteria applied to the real data (Sect. 2.1) were applied,
and simulated LCs failing to meet these requirements were
discarded.
– The metrics described in Sect. 2.2 were then computed for the
selected simulated LCs and compared with the corresponding
metrics derived from the real datasets. The comparison was
quantified through an L2 loss function (see M25 for details).
The final loss assigned to each individual was defined as the
average of the five metric losses, while the fitness score was
taken as the inverse of this quantity.
– At each generation, individuals were ranked according to their
loss values. New offsprings were produced by combining the
genes of the top 15% fittest individuals. In addition, random
mutations were introduced by resampling individual genes
from their allowed ranges, with a mutation probability of 4%
per gene.
