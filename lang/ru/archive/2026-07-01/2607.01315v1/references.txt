A.NUMERICAL SIMULATIONS
We follow a similar procedure to G. C. Weldon et al. (2026) to solve for the coupled planetary interior and dynamical
evolution. The key difference here is the extension of planetary structure models from 0.3M J to 0.05MJ to study sub-
Jovian planets. We also include the effect of tidal heating, which inflates the planet during late times in the migration.
A.1.Secular evolution
We numerically solve the octupole-level secular equations for the hierarchical three-body system following S. Naoz
et al. (2011, 2013a). We also include general relativistic precession of the inner and outer orbit (S. Naoz et al. 2013b).
Equilibrium tides are included following the formalism of P. P. Eggleton et al. (1998) and P. P. Eggleton & L. Kiseleva-
Eggleton (2001). We fix the viscous timescalet v,∗ = 50 years for the star andtv,p = 0.05 years for the planet (consistent
with intermediate values in e.g., C. Petrovich 2015a). The stellar and planetary spins are initialized to the solar (24.47
days) and Jovian (0.41 days) values, respectively. The code follows the precession of the spin vector (see for full set of
equations, S. Naoz 2016). Furthermore, we model the stellar evolution using the SSE evolution code of J. R. Hurley
et al. (2000). The combined code with secular evolution, general relativity, tides, and stellar evolution has been tested
and applied to various astrophysical systems in the literature (e.g., S. Naoz 2016; S. Naoz et al. 2016; A. P. Stephan
et al. 2016, 2018, 2019, 2020, 2021; I. Angelo et al. 2022; C. Shariat et al. 2023, 2024; G. C. Weldon et al. 2025; L.
Holzknecht et al. 2025; G. C. Weldon et al. 2026).
The upper limit for each system’s integration time in our simulations of initial Jovians is 10 Gyr. We stop the
simulation if a planet is engulfed during stellar evolution, or if the planet’s mass falls below 0.05M J during mass loss.
If a hot planet forms, the orbit circularizes until the integration is stopped oncee p <10 −4.

A.3.Planetary structure modeling
A.3.1.Grid construction
To account for the planetary response to mass loss, we use Modules for Experiments in Stellar Astrophysics (MESA)
(e.g., B. Paxton et al. 2011, 2013, 2015, 2018, 2019), which has the ability to model the evolution of giant planets
using themake planetsmodule. While it is possible to evolve MESA and the secular code simultaneously (e.g., J.
Gao & G. Li 2025), such simulations are computationally prohibitive for large population synthesis studies, especially
when accounting for mass loss on an orbit-by-orbit basis. Therefore, a pre-calculated grid allows us to approximate
the structural evolution for many systems over a large parameter space.
We construct MESA models for planets with masses 0.3MJ < mp <10M J, and analytically extend these models into
the sub-Jovian regime wheremake planetshas difficulty converging, while ensuring physical quantities are consistent
at the transition. The planets in this work have a 10M ⊕ core, corresponding to the minimum mass for runaway gas
accretion (e.g., J. B. Pollack et al. 1996). The remainder of the planet has a solar composition envelope. In MESA,
we vary the total planet mass, using masses of 0.3-0.9M J (spaced in increments of 0.1MJ) and 1.0-10.0MJ (spaced in
increments of 0.5MJ). The initial planet radius set inmake planetsis 2.0R J. To access the mass-radius relations at
various entropies, we vary the stellar irradiation levels, considering fluxes of 10 5, 106, 107, and 5×10 7 erg s−1 cm−2.
We also vary the starting radius of the planet in the 5×10 7 erg s−1 cm−2 run from 2.0-6.0RJ (in increments of 1.0RJ)
to access higher entropies.
