This parameterization allows the data to determine both the shape and support of the effective-spin distribution
on either side of the transition mass, without imposing a Gaussian, spline, or other fixed functional form. Because
the density is constructed through an exponentiated Gaussian process multiplied by a variable-support window, the
inferred distribution may become very small over selected regions ofχ eff space. This flexibility is useful for identifying
sharp features, extended tails, or disconnected support in the spin distribution.
The priors adopted for all hyper-parameters are summarized in Table 2. Figure 5 shows representative posterior
predictive distributions for the spin model.
5.2.Simulated data
We simulate 100 realizations of 256 gravitational wave observations. These simulations are drawn from a skew-normal
distribution withµ eff =−0.075, σeff = 0.17, ϵeff = 0.9, consistent with findings from theBivariate Skewedmodel
in the analysis of the LVK ( The LIGO Scientific Collaboration et al. 2026b). The mass model and redshift models
are imported fromGWPopulation(C. Talbot et al. 2025) and are aSinglePeakSmoothedMassDistribution
(α= 3.14,m min = 4.56 M ⊙, ∆m= 5.96 M ⊙,m max = 81.08 M ⊙,β= 1.7) and aPowerLawRedshift(κ= 2.7).
Each event posterior has 1000 samples.
We also generate 5×10 7 found injections from the same mass and redshift models with some different parameters
(α= 3,m min = 3 M⊙,m max = 130 M⊙), and a truncated Normal distribution inχ eff (µeff = 0, σeff = 0.5, χeff,min =
−1, χeff,max = 1).
In Fig. 6 we show the simulated dataset used in our mock analysis, and in Fig. 7 we show the recovered posterior
predictive distributions ofχ eff and of the transitional mass parameter in two cases.
