Hidden Gas Clumps Trick Astronomers in Early Galaxies ⚡ экспресс

Original: "One cloud is not enough: extreme conditions bias chemical abundances in high-redshift galaxies"
· Bianca Moreschini, Francesco Belfiore, Alessandro Marconi, Elisa Cataldi, Mirko Curti, Amirnezam Amiri, Anna Feltre, Filippo Mannucci, Elena Bertola, Caterina Bracci, Matteo Ceci, Avinanda Chakraborty, Giovanni Cresci, Quirino D'Amato, Enrico di Teodoro, Michele Ginolfi, Isabella Lamperti, Cosimo Marconcini, Martina Scialpi, Lorenzo Ulivi, Maria Vittoria Zanchettin
arXiv:2601.08939v1 · 2026-01-13 · CC BY 4.0 · ⏱ 1 мин · Galaxies
James Webb Space Telescope reveals that unseen dense gas knots can throw off measurements of ancient galaxies’ ingredients.
Связи в графе знаний 1

Tiny, dense gas clumps—like blueberries hidden in a muffin—can trick our best telescopes. In early galaxies, these knots shine so brightly they make the whole galaxy seem chemically poorer. This cosmic illusion means we might be misreading the history of stars and elements.

🎯 The ‘auroral lines’ used to measure temperature in these galaxies glow in the same green and red colors as the aurora on Earth, but they come from oxygen and nitrogen atoms in space, not from solar wind hitting our atmosphere.
