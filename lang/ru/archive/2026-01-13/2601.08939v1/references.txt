Appendix A: Modelling assumptions and chemical
abundance determinations
Although oxygen is the primary coolant in HII regions, abundant
elements like nitrogen, sulfur, and carbon can also influence ISM
cooling and, consequently, the predicted fluxes of single-cloud
models. The predicted flux in these models may no longer scale
linearly with the abundance of the corresponding element for
significant variations in its abundance. However, we verified that
the final results does not change when adopting instead model
grids with N and S abundances rescaled by¬Ī0.6 and¬Ī0.3 dex,
respectively, and limiting the scaling factors to vary within small
amounts (as in Marconi et al. 2024).
Similarly, these scaling factors can also effectively absorb
deviations in depletion factors from those assumed in the mod-
els. For instance, we verified that the iron abundance inferred
by combining depleted and undepleted models is consistent with
that obtained using a single model and allowing the iron line flux
to vary by up to¬Ī1 dex. In Table A.1 we list for reference all the
parameters of the single-cloud models described in Sec. 2.1.
For comparison, we computed abundances for each object
with theT e-method following standard practice, and summarise
the detailed below. First, we corrected the observed fluxes for
reddening as derived from the HOMERUN best-fit model, adopt-
ing the attenuation curve detailed in the text: Cardelli et al.
(1989) for M 4327, and Calzetti et al. (2000) for the Sunburst
Arc and RXJCJ2248-ID. To simplify the analysis, in the case of
the Sunburst Arc we adopted zero attenuation for the UV lines,
and used the attenuation from the JWST-only fit for the optical
lines, as the latter is also consistent with the value obtained by
Welch et al. (2025) from the Balmer decrements. To derive tem-
peratures and abundances via theT e-method, we used thePyNeb
package, adopting the same transition proabilities and collisional
excitation rates as in our photoionisation modelling, summarised
in Table A. This ensures the best possible consistency between
the abundances inferred through different approaches.

Appendix D: Other element abundances
So far, we limited our analysis of chemical abundances in the
Sunburst Arc and RXCJ2248-ID to oxygen and nitrogen. How-
ever, investigating relative abundances of elements with differ-
ent nucleosynthetic origins can provide valuable insights into
chemical enrichment processes (e.g., Kobayashi 2025). A de-
tailed analysis will be presented in future works, but for com-
pleteness we report in Table D.1 the best-fit model abundances
for all the elements not discussed before, along with a few con-
siderations below.
Helium.Previous studies suggested a correlation between ni-
trogen and helium enrichment. If nitrogen excess originates from
CNO processing in massive stars, and hydrogen is simultane-
ously burned into helium in outer layers, then stellar winds may
eject both into the surrounding ISM, producing regions enriched
in both N and He. This is consistent with observations of second-
generation stars in globular clusters, which often show helium
enrichment, making helium a possible tracer of clustering effi-
ciency (e.g., Ji et al. 2025, and references therein).
Intriguingly, if we also allow helium abundance to vary
freely in the fit of RXCJ2248-ID, we found that the nitrogen-
rich component shows a helium abundance approximately twice
the solar value, while the other component have normal levels of
both N and He. However, leaving He abundance free does not
significantly improve the fit quality, and is not required, but only
represents an additional degree of freedom.
In general, Ji et al. (2025) do not find a clear trend of He en-
richment in N-rich galaxies within their sample. High He abun-
dance can be mimicked by assuming emission predominantly
arises from very-dense clouds (n e ‚ąľ10 5 cm‚ąí3), where colli-
sional excitation significantly contributes to He line fluxes (e.g.,
Thuan et al. 1996; Izotov et al. 2014; Yanagisawa et al. 2024b;
Katz et al. 2024; Ji et al. 2025; Berg et al. 2025). While HOME-
RUN self-consisently considers such high densities, our best-fit
models for both the Sunburst Arc and RXCJ2248-ID (regard-
less of the adopted assumptions) yield high helium abundances
compared to the solar value 12+log(He/H)=10.914¬Ī0.013

Appendix E: Correcting for temperature
fluctuations
To address the issue of temperature fluctuations both
observational- and simulative-based prescriptions have been de-
veloped. While HOMEURN allows a self-consistent treatment
of ISM complexity, here we briefly discuss the approaches by
Mendez-Delgado et al. (2023b) and Cameron et al. (2023a).
Recently, Mendez-Delgado et al. (2023b) proposed an em-
pirical calibration to recover the correct metallicity when the
right combination of diagnostics is available. As an exam-
ple, we follow their prescription for M 4327. Specifically, we
extract the flux of [N II]őĽ5755 from the HOMERUN fidu-
cial model described in Sec. 3.1 (this line is undetected in
the JWST data). We use this model-predicted value to de-
rive bothT(N II) andn e usinggetCrossTemDen, adopting [O
II]őĽőĽ3727,3729/őĽőĽ7323,7332 as a density-sensitive diagnos-
tic over then e ‚ąľ10 3‚Äď106 cm‚ąí3 range (Mendez-Delgado et al.
2023a). We obtainn e =682 cm ‚ąí3 andT(N II)=9903 K. We
then follow Mendez-Delgado et al. (2023b) to account for tem-
perature fluctuations and adopt their relation 12+log(O/H)=
‚ąí1.19√ó10 ‚ąí4 T(N II)+9.68, which was calibrated on a sample
of galactic and extragalactic HII regions, and ring nebulae. With
this correction we obtain 12+log(O/H)=8.50, in agreement
with the best-fit metallicity from our fiducial HOMERUN model
(Table 1).
Cameron et al. (2023a) used high-resolution radiation-
hydrodynamic simulations of a dwarf galaxy to calibrate lin-
ear relations between the auroral-to-nebular line ratios and ac-
tual gas temperatures for a given transition. However, their cal-
ibrations are only valid for densities‚Č≤10 3 cm‚ąí3, and therefore
should not be directly applicable to high-redshift systems, where
denser environments are common (e.g., Sec. 1).
As a qualitative test, we follow a similar approach to that
of the simulation, computingT([O III]őĽ5007) andT([O III]
őĽ4363) from our grid of single-cloud models, weighting each
by the respective emitted line flux. We find differences of‚ąľ
1000‚ąí2000 K between the two temperatures, in agreement with
thet 2 estimates reported in Sec. 6.1.1.
Lastly, we also consider our assumption of negligible tem-
perature fluctuations within each single-cloud model (Sec.
6.1.1). Applying Cameron et al. (2023a) corrections to mod-
