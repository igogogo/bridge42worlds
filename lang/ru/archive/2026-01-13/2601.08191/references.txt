The phonon properties within the harmonic approximation were calculated using
density functional perturbation theory (DFPT) [43] in QE. We used aq-grid of 8×

8×8 to obtain the corresponding dynamical matrix elements for eachqpoint, which
were Fourier-transformed to obtain the interatomic force constants. The interatomic
force constants were then used to interpolate the harmonic phonon dispersion for an
arbitraryqpoint. The anharmonic phonons were obtained by the anharmonic special
displacement method (A-SDM) [44] implemented in EPW [45]. We used a 2×2×2

an 8×8×8q-grid, and then Fourier-transformed the resulting dynamical matrices
to get the anharmonic interatomic force constants for an 8×8×8 grid in real space,
which were used for subsequent electron-phonon coupling calculations.
To reduce the computational cost of electron-phonon coupling calculations, we
used the Wannier interpolation technique [46]. We used a coarse 8×8×8k-grid to
construct the Wannier functions in Wannier90 [47], for which the initial projections
were chosen as the 2porbitals for nitrogen and 4dorbitals for niobium. We computed
the electron–phonon couplings in the Bloch basis on the coarsek-grid andq-grid using
EPW [45, 48], and Fourier-transformed them into the Wannier basis. With electron–
phonon couplings in the Wannier basis, we were able to obtain any electron-phonon
coupling at arbitrarykandqpoints via the inverse Fourier transformation. To obtain
the superconducting gap, we solved the anisotropic Eliashberg equations using EPW
with a finek-grid of 72×72×72 points and a fineq-grid of 36×36×36. The Coulomb
pseudopotential is set toµ ∗ = 0.14.
To include electron–photon interactions from cavity photons, we used quantum
electrodynamical density-functional theory, which included an additional electron–
photon exchange potential in the Kohn-Sham Hamiltonian [15]. Our simulations
utilized two cavity modes with polarization along thexandydirections. The ratio
of the mode strengthλ α to the bare frequencyω α for the two modes was chosen as
λα/ωα = 0.1. We fixed the lattice constant and relaxed the atomic positions in the
unit cell, but the atomic positions remained the same as in the outside cavity case.
Other computational conditions were the same as those outside the cavity.

Next, we used another more accurate but computationally expensive approach,
anisotropic Eliashberg equations, to estimate how the superconducting gaps andT c
are modified for NbN inside a cavity. Figure S11 shows the temperature-dependent
superconducting gap obtained from solving the Eliashberg equations. TheT c can be
estimated at the temperature where the superconducting gap vanishes. TheT c’s are
about 32 K for both cases where the NbN is outside and inside a cavity with the
ratio ofλ α/ωα = 0.1. The superconducting gap for NbN is computed down to 10 K.
Our computedT c was overestimated, compared to the experimental one,T c = 13.1K.
The discrepancy between the computational and experimental values may be due to
impurity effects [50], phonon anharmonicity [51], or non-adiabatic effects [52], which
were neglected in the calculations. When the anharmonic phonons are considered,T c
reduces to 27 K for the case without the cavity. We focus more on whether the cavity
modifies the superconducting gap, rather than on the absolute value ofT c = 13.1 K.
The harmonic phonon results are sufficient to demonstrate that the change in the
superconducting gap inside the cavity is only slight compared to the case without
a cavity at low temperatures. When NbN is placed inside a cavity with two photon
modes polarized along thexandydirections, the electron density of thenormal
phasedecreases in thexy-plane and accumulates along thezdirection; see the inset
of fig. S11. These results indicate that the cavity modifies the electron density, even
though the superconducting gap shows only minimal changes.

5 Temperature dependence of the cavity modes
parameter of the NbN-cavity system
The results of the Lorentzian fitting of peaks I, II, and III at various temperatures in
the superconducting state of NbN are shown in fig. S12. Note that the peak amplitude
decreases at high temperature, hence the larger fitting error bars, which represent the
uncertainties associated with the Lorentzian fitting procedure. When comparing peak
frequencies (fig. S12(a,d,f)) and linewidths (fig. S12(c,f,i)) between the simulation
(red) and the experiment (black), the differences are minor. However, the peak ampli-
tudes show the most prominent changes, which are indicated by the shaded regions in
fig. S12(b,e,h). Additionally, it can be observed that the amplitude differences set in
at different temperatures for the three peaks, as related to the change in the optical
gap with temperature.
