A.RESOLVING EMISSION LINES WITHSEDONA
For modeling emission lines, it is necessary to resolve the bound-bound transitions within the nLTE framework implemented
inSEDONA. During each iteration of the radiative transfer simulation, the radiation field and gas temperature are updated. These
quantities are then used to determine the ionization and excitation states of the gas, which in turn set the frequency-dependent
opacity and emissivity. In the nLTE regime, the radiation field influences transition rates between excitation states through both
bound-bound and bound-free processes. Assuming statistical equilibrium, these transition rates are computed to estimate the
level populations.
SEDONAtreats bound-bound processes using an indirect approach to reduce computational cost. Based on the opacity at the
frequency of a photon packet, the code determines whether an interaction corresponds to electron scattering or effective scattering
(i.e., absorption followed by re-emission). In the case of effective scattering, a new photon frequency is drawn from the local
emissivity distribution. As a result, a photon packet initially associated with one transition (e.g., hydrogen) can be re-emitted at
a different frequency (e.g., oxygen). When averaged over a large ensemble of photon packets, this method reproduces the results
of more explicit line-transfer approaches while requiring significantly less computational time and memory.
Finally, to accurately simulate line processes, it is essential to resolve the intrinsic width of the line, which is determined by
thermal velocity broadening:

B.THE CHOICE OF THE ATOMIC LIBRARY
When performing Monte Carlo radiative transfer simulations, the choice of atomic data is critical. While a more comprehensive
atomic library includes a larger number of transitions, it also substantially increases computational cost. For this reason, we adopt
a simplified atomic library for the main simulations in this work, consisting of 1,982 energy levels and 13,024 line transitions.
We also perform comparison runs using a more extensive library with 7,591 energy levels and 82,446 transitions.
As shown in Figure A1, both atomic libraries produce consistent results for the continuum and the primary emission features.
However, differences arise in the treatment of weaker lines. In particular, the simplified atomic dataset artificially enhances
certain transitions, such as OIIIλ5592 (solid line). This enhancement is caused by the absence of recombination pathways from
OIVto OIIIin the reduced atomic model. These features disappear when using the more complete atomic dataset (dashed line),
indicating that they are artifacts of the simplified treatment.
C.EFFECT FROM THE COMPACTNESS OF THE ENVELOPE
Building on the work of Roth et al. (2016), we examine how the compactness of the reprocessing envelope influences the He-
to-H line ratio. We find that reducing the outer radiusR out produces a more compact photosphere, which significantly enhances
the HeIIλ4686/Hαflux ratio, as shown in Figure A2. This behavior arises from the different formation depths of these lines:
HeIIλ4686 originates closer to the inner, hotter regions of the envelope, whereas Hαis produced predominantly at larger radii.
AsR out decreases, the outer layers are truncated, preferentially suppressing Hαemission while leaving HeIIλ4686 relatively
unaffected, thereby increasing the He-to-H ratio.
